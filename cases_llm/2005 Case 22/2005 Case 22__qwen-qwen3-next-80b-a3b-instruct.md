# Case 22-2005 —An 81-Year-Old Man with Cough, Fever, and Altered Mental Status

## Presentation of Case



   *Dr. Sherry Chou* (Neurology): An 81-year-old man was admitted to the Massachusetts General Hospital in September because of fever, chills, productive cough, and diffuse weakness.



Two weeks before admission, a cough developed, with small amounts of yellowish sputum. The patient was treated with azithromycin for five days, without improvement. Five days before admission, amoxicillin was begun. The cough continued to worsen, with increased production of sputum that was tinged with blood. High fevers and chills developed, with progressive anorexia, diffuse weakness, and mild confusion. He was brought to the emergency department and admitted.



A diagnosis of chronic lymphocytic leukemia (CLL) had been made six years earlier. The patient had been treated for two years with fludarabine, which was stopped because anemia and thrombocytopenia had developed. For three years before admission, he received no treatment for his leukemia and had been clinically stable, with slowly rising peripheral lymphocyte counts and falling serum immunoglobulin levels. He had had several episodes of viral and pneumococcal pneumonia in the two years before admission. Other medical problems included atrial fibrillation, diabetes mellitus, hypertension, prostatic adenocarcinoma (which had been treated with radiation therapy seven years earlier), multiple cutaneous squamous-cell carcinomas (treated with surgical excision), and three melanomas on the face and neck. The most recent melanoma had been treated within the six months before admission with excision and skin grafting.



The patient lived at home with his wife in the Boston area. He had traveled to California six months before and to New York one month before becoming ill. He had no known contacts with sick people or recent exposures to animals or insects. He did not have nausea, vomiting, or a change in bowel habits. His medications on admission included potassium chloride, digoxin, hydrochlorothiazide, atenolol, warfarin, glyburide, and amoxicillin.



On examination, the patient appeared tired but was able to converse appropriately. The axillary temperature was 39.6°C. The pulse was irregular at 100 beats per minute, the blood pressure 170/90 mm Hg, and the respiratory rate 24 breaths per minute. Oxygen saturation was 98 percent with the patient breathing 2 liters of supplemental oxygen delivered by nasal cannula. The pupils were equal and reactive to light. The neck was supple. There was no palpable cervical lymphadenopathy, but slightly enlarged lymph nodes were present in the axillae. The chest was clear to auscultation. There was a 2/6 systolic ejection murmur at the right upper sternal border. The abdomen was nontender, nondistended, and soft. The tip of the spleen was palpable. The remainder of the examination revealed no abnormalities. On neurologic examination, the patient was mildly confused and was not oriented to place or time. He did not follow commands. The strength in the arms and legs was 3/5 bilaterally. There was marked truncal weakness, and he was unable to sit up in bed.



A chest radiograph showed prominence of the right paratracheal stripe and right hilum and bilateral small pleural effusions. Blood levels of electrolytes and serum levels of urea nitrogen, creatinine, glucose, albumin, bilirubin, alkaline phosphatase, and aminotransferase were normal. Flow cytometry of peripheral-blood lymphocytes revealed that they were CD19+ and CD20+ B cells coexpressing CD5 and CD23 and monotypic lambda immunoglobulin light chains. Specimens of urine, blood, nasal secretions, and sputum were sent for culture and viral testing. Ampicillin, gentamycin, and metronidazole were administered.



On the second hospital day, the patient remained febrile, with a temperature that peaked at 39.1°C. He continued to be confused, and by the third day he was somnolent. His degree of arousal waxed and waned. Right-sided weakness developed. Computed tomographic (CT) scanning of the head after the administration of intravenous contrast material revealed no abnormalities except for mild mucosal thickening of the ethmoid air cells. CT scanning of the chest showed mediastinal and axillary lymphadenopathy that had enlarged since a study obtained five months earlier. There was no hilar lymphadenopathy or pulmonary abnormalities. A lumbar puncture was performed. Flow cytometry showed that the lymphocytes were T cells (90 percent) with a normal immunophenotype and a normal CD4:CD8 ratio; there were a small number of B cells with an immunophenotype identical to that of cells associated with CLL.



Acyclovir and ceftriaxone were begun. On the fourth hospital day, the patient became progressively more somnolent, and hypoxemic respiratory failure developed (the partial pressure of oxygen was 63 mm Hg with the fraction of inspired oxygen at 40 percent). The trachea was intubated, and the patient was transferred to the medical intensive care unit. He had only minimal motor response to pain. He had a weak grimace in response to nasal tickle and a cough reflex. Nuchal rigidity was present. Repeated CT scanning of the head showed no changes. By the fifth hospital day, the patient was comatose. Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) of the brain showed a region of restricted diffusion in the left thalamus with surrounding T    hyperintensity that involved most of the left thalamus. There was a 5-mm-long, enhancing focus, T    isointense and T    hyperintense, that was located just inferior to the left hypoglossal canal and thought to be a schwannoma. After discussion with his family, ventilator support was withdrawn on the 12th hospital day, and the patient died. An autopsy was performed.

## Differential Diagnosis

The clinical course of this 81-year-old man with chronic lymphocytic leukemia (CLL) presents a complex, multi-systemic deterioration characterized by progressive respiratory infection, systemic inflammatory response, neurological decline, and ultimately fatal encephalopathy. The constellation of fever, productive cough with hemoptysis, worsening confusion, focal neurological deficits, and eventual coma in the setting of profound immunosuppression demands a meticulously structured differential diagnosis that integrates the interplay between his underlying hematologic malignancy, prior infections, immunoglobulin deficiency, and the acute infectious and inflammatory processes that culminated in central nervous system (CNS) pathology. The key diagnostic challenge lies in distinguishing between primary CNS infection, paraneoplastic or autoimmune encephalopathy, metastatic infiltration, and systemic sepsis with secondary CNS complications—all occurring against a backdrop of profound B-cell immunodeficiency and recurrent bacterial pneumonias.

The initial presentation—fever, cough, purulent and later hemoptoic sputum—strongly suggests a lower respiratory tract infection, and the isolation of *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* from sputum culture confirms a significant bacterial pathogen. *P. aeruginosa* is a classic opportunistic pathogen in immunocompromised hosts, particularly those with CLL who have hypogammaglobulinemia, impaired neutrophil function due to prior fludarabine therapy, and recurrent infections. In this patient, the failure of azithromycin and amoxicillin to improve symptoms is entirely consistent with the intrinsic resistance of *P. aeruginosa* to these agents. The radiographic findings—right paratracheal stripe prominence, hilar and axillary lymphadenopathy, and bilateral pleural effusions—suggest a process not limited to the lung parenchyma but involving lymphatic drainage and possibly mediastinal spread. However, the CT chest showed no parenchymal infiltrates, which is unusual for typical bacterial pneumonia. This raises the possibility that the pulmonary findings were secondary to lymphatic obstruction from lymphadenopathy or that the infection was primarily a bronchitis or endobronchial process with secondary bacteremia, rather than true lobar pneumonia. The absence of parenchymal consolidation on imaging, despite clinical and microbiologic evidence of infection, underscores the atypical presentation of infection in CLL patients, whose immune response is blunted and who may fail to mount a classic inflammatory lung reaction.

The progression to altered mental status and neurologic decline is the critical pivot in this case. The initial mild confusion, evolving to somnolence, then to right-sided weakness, nuchal rigidity, and finally coma, points to a process involving the CNS. The normal initial head CT, followed by the development of restricted diffusion in the left thalamus on MRI, is highly significant. Restricted diffusion in the thalamus is a hallmark of acute ischemic stroke, but in the context of systemic infection and immunosuppression, it is far more suggestive of infectious or inflammatory encephalitis, particularly viral or fungal. The thalamus is a common site for infection in immunocompromised hosts due to its rich vascular supply and the tendency of certain pathogens to preferentially target deep gray matter structures. The presence of surrounding T2 hyperintensity suggests vasogenic edema, consistent with an active inflammatory or infectious process. The enhancing 5-mm focus near the left hypoglossal canal was interpreted as a schwannoma—a benign nerve sheath tumor—which is likely an incidental finding, given its chronicity, lack of growth since prior imaging, and absence of clinical signs of cranial nerve VIII or IX/X dysfunction. The MRI findings, therefore, are most consistent with an acute, focal, infectious or inflammatory process in the thalamus.

The cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) analysis is pivotal. The presence of T cells with a normal phenotype (CD4:CD8 ratio preserved) is expected in the context of normal immune surveillance. However, the detection of a small number of B cells with the identical immunophenotype as the patient’s CLL—CD19+, CD20+, CD5+, CD23+, lambda-restricted—constitutes a critical diagnostic clue. This finding is not merely a reflection of passive leakage from the bloodstream; it indicates that CLL cells have infiltrated the CNS, a phenomenon known as CNS involvement by CLL. While rare, CNS infiltration by CLL occurs in approximately 1–2% of patients, typically in advanced, high-risk disease with prolonged immunosuppression, hypogammaglobulinemia, and prior cytotoxic therapy. The fact that these cells were detected in CSF and not in the blood at the time of lumbar puncture suggests a compartmentalized process—CLL cells have crossed the blood-brain barrier and established a localized proliferation within the CNS. This is distinct from leukemia cutis or lymphomatous meningitis; it represents true parenchymal infiltration, likely seeding the thalamus and contributing to the restricted diffusion lesion. The thalamus, with its dense vascular network and proximity to the third ventricle, is a favored site for CLL infiltration due to its role as a lymphoid trafficking hub.

This leads to the central diagnostic dilemma: Is the encephalopathy primarily due to direct CNS infiltration by CLL cells, or is it secondary to an infectious agent that has taken advantage of the immunosuppressed state? The isolation of *P. aeruginosa* from sputum, combined with the patient’s bacteremia likely secondary to pneumonia, raises the possibility of septic emboli or meningitis. However, *P. aeruginosa* meningitis is exceedingly rare, and when it occurs, it typically presents with purulent CSF, markedly elevated neutrophils, low glucose, and high protein—findings not reported here. The CSF showed no pleocytosis, no elevated protein, and no organisms on Gram stain or culture. The absence of neutrophilic pleocytosis argues strongly against acute bacterial meningitis. The patient’s clinical deterioration occurred over days to weeks, not hours, further arguing against acute pyogenic meningitis.

Another critical consideration is viral encephalitis. The patient had no prodrome of headache, photophobia, or seizure, which are typical of herpes simplex virus (HSV) or varicella-zoster virus (VZV) encephalitis. However, immunocompromised patients may present atypically. HSV encephalitis most commonly affects the temporal lobes, not the thalamus, though it can involve the thalamus secondarily. VZV vasculopathy can cause thalamic infarcts, but typically presents with headache, cranial neuropathies, and vasculitic changes on angiography—not seen here. Cytomegalovirus (CMV) and Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) are more likely in profoundly immunosuppressed patients. CMV can cause ventriculitis, meningoencephalitis, and even thalamic lesions, particularly in patients with low CD4 counts or B-cell dysfunction. However, CMV typically causes multifocal lesions, not a single dominant thalamic focus. EBV can cause lymphoproliferative disorders and CNS lymphoma in immunocompromised hosts, but again, the CSF findings show no atypical lymphocytes, no elevated EBV DNA, and no evidence of lymphoma on imaging. The presence of monoclonal lambda B cells in CSF, identical to the CLL clone, makes a primary CNS lymphoma (PCNSL) less likely, as PCNSL is typically diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL), not CLL, and rarely presents with isolated thalamic involvement. Moreover, PCNSL typically shows ring-enhancing lesions on MRI, not the described T2 hyperintensity with restricted diffusion.

Fungal infections must also be considered. Cryptococcus neoformans is a classic cause of subacute meningitis and encephalitis in immunocompromised patients, particularly those with B-cell defects. However, cryptococcal meningitis usually presents with chronic headache, elevated opening pressure, and CSF findings of lymphocytic pleocytosis, low glucose, and positive India ink or cryptococcal antigen. None of these were reported here. *Aspergillus* or *Mucor* species can cause angioinvasive disease with infarction and hemorrhage, particularly in neutropenic patients, but this patient’s neutrophil count was not reported as low, and there was no evidence of sinusitis or pulmonary angioinvasion on imaging. The calcified spleen and renal cysts suggest chronic, possibly fungal, granulomatous disease, but again, no evidence of disseminated fungal infection was documented.

The possibility of autoimmune encephalitis must be entertained, particularly in the context of CLL. Paraneoplastic syndromes can occur in CLL, though they are exceedingly rare. Anti-NMDA receptor encephalitis, for example, typically presents with psychiatric symptoms, seizures, and movement disorders—not isolated thalamic lesions. Paraneoplastic limbic encephalitis is associated with antibodies like anti-Hu, anti-Yo, or anti-Ri, which are more commonly linked to small cell lung cancer or ovarian cancer, not CLL. The absence of any known paraneoplastic antibodies, and the fact that the CSF contained CLL cells, makes a purely autoimmune etiology less likely. However, CLL can be associated with autoimmune phenomena, including autoimmune hemolytic anemia, immune thrombocytopenia, and, rarely, autoimmune encephalitis. The patient had no history of such autoimmune disorders, and the timing of neurological decline—immediately following a severe bacterial infection—suggests a more direct pathogenic mechanism.

Another important consideration is septic embolization. The patient had atrial fibrillation, a known risk factor for cardioembolic stroke. However, emboli typically cause cortical infarcts, not deep thalamic lesions. Moreover, embolic strokes do not typically cause progressive somnolence over days; they are usually acute and maximal at onset. The patient’s clinical course was insidious and progressive, consistent with an infiltrative or inflammatory process rather than an embolic event. The MRI findings of restricted diffusion in the thalamus with surrounding edema are not typical of embolic stroke, which usually shows more circumscribed, wedge-shaped infarcts.

The most compelling explanation is a triad of events: (1) a severe, treatment-resistant *P. aeruginosa* pneumonia leading to bacteremia and systemic inflammation; (2) concomitant CNS infiltration by the patient’s own CLL clone, which had been quiescent for years but became active due to the inflammatory milieu of systemic infection; and (3) secondary infectious encephalitis, likely viral, triggered by the profound immunosuppression. The presence of CLL cells in CSF confirms CNS involvement, and the thalamic lesion is most consistent with direct tumor infiltration causing local inflammation, edema, and disruption of neural circuits. The fever, leukocytosis (implied by the clinical course), and systemic inflammatory markers suggest a cytokine storm from the pneumonia, which may have disrupted the blood-brain barrier and facilitated CLL cell trafficking into the CNS. This is a well-described phenomenon in CLL: systemic inflammation can trigger proliferation and migration of the leukemic clone into sanctuary sites like the CNS.

The failure of antibiotics (ampicillin, gentamicin, metronidazole, ceftriaxone, ceftazidime) to improve neurological status is not surprising if the primary driver is not bacterial meningitis but CNS infiltration by CLL or a viral encephalitis. The administration of intravenous immunoglobulin on day 7 may have been an attempt to bolster humoral immunity, but in CLL, the defect is not merely antibody deficiency—it is also impaired T-cell function and immune dysregulation. IVIG would not reverse CLL infiltration.

The autopsy findings, though not detailed, would have been critical. If the thalamus showed sheets of monoclonal B cells with the same immunophenotype as the peripheral CLL, that would confirm CNS infiltration as the primary cause of encephalopathy. If, however, there was evidence of fungal hyphae, viral inclusions, or vasculitis, the diagnosis would shift. Given the clinical and CSF data, the most likely primary diagnosis is **CNS infiltration by chronic lymphocytic leukemia**, complicated by **bacteremia from *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*** and possibly **viral encephalitis** (e.g., EBV or CMV) as a contributing factor. The thalamic lesion represents the anatomical correlate of CLL cell infiltration, not a stroke or primary infection. The patient’s prolonged immunosuppression, prior fludarabine therapy, hypogammaglobulinemia, and history of recurrent infections created the perfect storm for this rare but devastating complication. The progression from pneumonia to coma was not merely a progression of infection—it was the manifestation of a malignant process escaping immune surveillance and colonizing the central nervous system, ultimately leading to fatal neurological dysfunction.
