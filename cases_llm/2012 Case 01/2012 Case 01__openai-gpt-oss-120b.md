*Evidence For.*  
- Presence of purulent drainage from a perineal abscess shortly before the hand lesions, suggesting a source of bacteremia or contiguous spread.

*Evidence Against.*

*Conclusion.*  A primary bacterial cellulitis or necrotizing fasciitis is **unlikely** to be the primary driver of the ongoing ulcerations, though a superimposed bacterial colonisation cannot be completely excluded. ## Recommendations for Further Evaluation  

1. **Confirm the Diagnosis of PG**  
   - Apply the **Su et al. criteria** for PG (major criteria: neutrophilic infiltrate on histology, exclusion of infection; minor criteria: pathergy, associated systemic disease, rapid progression, undermined violaceous border, response to steroids). The patient meets several major and minor criteria.  

2. **Search for Underlying Systemic Triggers**  
   - **Gastrointestinal Evaluation**: Colonoscopy with biopsies to rule out ulcerative colitis or Crohn’s disease; stool studies (fecal calprotectin) to assess intestinal inflammation.  
   - **Hematologic Assessment**: Complete blood count with differential, peripheral smear review, serum protein electrophoresis, and, if indicated, bone marrow biopsy to assess for MDS, AML, or lymphoma.  
   - **Autoimmune Panel**: Repeat ANCA, complement levels, and rheumatoid factor; consider anti‑neutrophil cytoplasmic antibodies if vasculitis still in differential.  

3. **Therapeutic Optimization**
