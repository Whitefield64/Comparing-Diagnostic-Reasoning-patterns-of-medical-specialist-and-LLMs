# Case 1-2012 —An 82-Year-Old Man with Persistent Ulcers on the Hands

## Presentation of Case



   *Dr. Sarah Gee* (Dermatology): An 82-year-old man was admitted to this hospital because of persistent skin lesions on the hands.



The patient had a history of diabetes mellitus and recurrent hidradenitis suppurativa. Five weeks before admission, he was admitted to another hospital because of drainage from a perineal abscess that was unresponsive to cephalexin. On examination, he reportedly had a small abrasion on his left hand, which he thought was caused by a dog bite. The administration of cephalexin was discontinued, and piperacillin–tazobactam and vancomycin were begun. During the following week, increasing erythema and an enlarging hemorrhagic bulla developed on the dorsum of the left hand and a diffuse, urticarial, and purpuric rash developed on the torso. Laboratory testing reportedly revealed thrombocytopenia. Débridement of the lesion of the left hand was performed; pathological examination of a specimen reportedly showed acute inflammation and granulation tissue. After débridement, the lesion worsened and similar lesions developed on the right hand, on the metacarpophalangeal joints. The lesions were reportedly painful to the touch and to manipulation, with ulceration extending to the tendons. Glucocorticoids were administered. On the 10th hospital day, a biopsy specimen of the skin of the right abdomen was obtained; pathological examination reportedly showed leukocytoclastic vasculitis and no microorganisms. The administration of piperacillin–tazobactam and vancomycin was stopped, as was subcutaneous heparin (begun during admission), and ciprofloxacin and linezolid were begun, in conjunction with a topical antimicrobial solution for the hands. The lesions expanded to include the proximal interphalangeal joints and became more erosive and painful. On the 13th day, the patient was transferred to a second hospital for evaluation and management of the skin lesions.



At the second hospital, the patient reported weight loss of approximately 2.3 kg per week during the previous month and intermittent diarrhea. On examination, the temperature was normal. On the dorsal surfaces of the hands were well-demarcated, edematous, friable, erythematous-to-violaceous plaques, with gray discoloration at the margins. Smaller violaceous, erythematous, edematous plaques were present on the trunk and thighs. A biopsy specimen of the skin on the dorsum of the right hand was obtained; pathological examination revealed a superficial and deep, dense, diffuse neutrophilic infiltrate. Staining for microorganisms was negative, and cultures were sterile. The serum level of sodium was 130 mmol per liter (reference range, 135 to 145), and levels of the other electrolytes were normal. Tests of liver function were normal, and testing for antibodies to the platelet factor 4–heparin complex was negative; other test results are shown in . The administration of antibiotics was stopped, higher-dose systemic glucocorticoids were begun, and local wound care was performed, with partial improvement.



The patient was discharged home on the fifth day, taking prednisone (70 mg orally daily) with instructions to initiate a taper in 2 to 3 weeks. He also was prescribed an intensive wound-care regimen, with the assistance of a home nurse. Shortly after discharge, the prednisone dose was tapered from 70 mg daily to 40 mg daily, the administration of cyclosporine (300 mg daily) was begun, and intralesional triamcinolone acetonide (to a total of 60 mg daily) was injected.



Six days before admission to this hospital, a biopsy specimen of the skin on the dorsum of the right hand was obtained; pathological examination reportedly revealed neutrophilic dermatitis, with a suggestion of follicular perforation and folliculitis; no microorganisms were seen. The next day, levels of serum electrolytes, total bilirubin, total protein, globulin, and calcium were normal, as were tests of liver function; other laboratory-test results are shown in . Three days later, the lesions seemed improved; the cyclosporine dose was decreased to 100 mg orally daily, dapsone (100 mg daily) was started, and prednisone was maintained at 40 mg daily. During the next 2 days, the lesions on the patient's hands worsened. He was referred to this hospital and admitted because of increased pain and oozing of the lesions.



The patient reported feeling well except for his hands. He had a history of hidradenitis suppurativa, with recurrent abscesses, for more than 20 years, for which multiple antibiotics had been administered; diabetes mellitus, for which he had recently started taking insulin; hypertension; hyperlipidemia; cholelithiasis; a malignant melanoma of the back, which had been excised; macrocytic anemia with low folate levels; multinodular goiter; diverticulosis; microhematuria; chronic obstructive pulmonary disease; bradycardia, for which a cardiac pacemaker had been inserted; and bilateral leg edema. He had had herniorrhaphies and an appendectomy. Other medications included lisinopril and aspirin. He was allergic to penicillin. He was retired, lived with his wife, smoked cigarettes, and drank alcohol in moderation. He had no history of exposures to chemicals. There was no family history of similar skin lesions, autoimmune disorders, or inflammatory bowel disease.



On examination, the respiratory rate was 30 breaths per minute and the oxygen saturation was 97% while the patient was breathing ambient air; other vital signs were normal. There were violaceous, edematous, denuded plaques on the metacarpophalangeal joints of both hands and on both elbows, as well as bilateral leg edema. ## Differential Diagnosis

The clinical picture presented here is complex, multi-systemic, and rapidly progressive, centered on devastating necrotizing skin lesions of the hands and extremities, accompanied by systemic signs of inflammation, cytopenias, and evidence of end-organ involvement. The patient is an elderly man with a long-standing history of hidradenitis suppurativa, diabetes mellitus, and multiple comorbidities, including chronic inflammation, immunosuppression from both disease and medications, and a history of repeated antibiotic exposures. The evolution of his skin lesions—from a minor abrasion thought to be a dog bite, to hemorrhagic bullae, to diffuse purpuric and ulcerative plaques with necrosis extending to tendons—combined with histopathological findings of neutrophilic dermatitis, leukocytoclastic vasculitis, and sterile cultures, demands a broad and rigorous differential diagnosis. The absence of infectious agents despite broad-spectrum antibiotic coverage, the lack of serologic markers for classic autoimmune diseases, and the transient response to high-dose corticosteroids and immunosuppressants all point toward a non-infectious, immune-mediated, or paraneoplastic process. We must consider primary dermatologic disorders, systemic inflammatory conditions, hematologic malignancies, drug reactions, and rare paraneoplastic syndromes, while carefully weighing the significance of his comorbidities and prior treatments.

The most prominent histopathological finding across multiple biopsies is a dense, diffuse neutrophilic infiltrate in both the superficial and deep dermis, with features suggestive of follicular perforation and folliculitis in the most recent specimen. This is not typical of classic leukocytoclastic vasculitis, which usually demonstrates nuclear dust, fibrinoid necrosis of vessel walls, and perivascular neutrophils—not a diffuse, interstitial, and folliculocentric pattern. The absence of microorganisms on staining and cultures rules out common bacterial, fungal, or mycobacterial causes of necrotizing skin disease, including *Staphylococcus aureus*, *Streptococcus pyogenes*, *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*, and *Mycobacterium marinum*. The initial use of vancomycin and piperacillin-tazobactam, followed by linezolid and ciprofloxacin, would have covered the most likely bacterial pathogens associated with skin and soft tissue infections, especially in a diabetic patient. The fact that the lesions worsened despite these therapies strongly argues against a simple bacterial etiology. The development of thrombocytopenia and a purpuric rash early in the course raises concern for a hematologic or vascular disorder, but the negative test for heparin-induced thrombocytopenia (HIT) makes heparin-induced immune-mediated thrombocytopenia unlikely as the primary driver, despite the prior use of subcutaneous heparin. The thrombocytopenia may instead be secondary to a systemic inflammatory process or marrow infiltration.

The presence of neutrophilic dermatitis, particularly in the context of recurrent abscesses and hidradenitis suppurativa, immediately raises the possibility of a neutrophilic dermatosis of the skin. Among these, Sweet’s syndrome (acute febrile neutrophilic dermatosis) is a leading consideration. Sweet’s syndrome typically presents with tender, erythematous, plaque-like lesions, often on the upper extremities and face, associated with fever, leukocytosis, and a dense dermal neutrophilic infiltrate without vasculitis. However, the lesions in this patient are not only erythematous but also necrotic, ulcerative, and extending to tendons—features not typical of classic Sweet’s syndrome, which rarely causes deep tissue destruction. Furthermore, Sweet’s syndrome is usually associated with a recent upper respiratory infection, malignancy, or inflammatory bowel disease; while the patient has no history of inflammatory bowel disease, he does have significant systemic inflammation, weight loss, and cytopenias, which could represent a paraneoplastic trigger. The development of lesions on the trunk and thighs also fits the pattern of Sweet’s syndrome, but the profound tissue necrosis and tendon involvement are atypical. It is possible that this represents a variant of Sweet’s syndrome with necrotizing features, sometimes termed “necrotizing neutrophilic dermatosis,” which has been described in association with hematologic malignancies.

Another critical consideration is pyoderma gangrenosum (PG), a rare, non-infectious neutrophilic dermatosis that classically presents with painful, rapidly expanding ulcers with undermined, violaceous borders, often following minor trauma—a phenomenon known as pathergy. This patient’s history of a minor abrasion preceding the eruption, the development of necrotic, undermining, violaceous plaques with gray discoloration at the margins, and the rapid progression despite antibiotics are all highly suggestive of PG. The histopathology of PG is often non-specific, showing a dense neutrophilic infiltrate without vasculitis, which aligns with the findings here. PG is strongly associated with underlying systemic diseases, particularly inflammatory bowel disease, rheumatoid arthritis, myelodysplastic syndromes, and monoclonal gammopathies. While the patient has no history of inflammatory bowel disease, his weight loss, diarrhea, and chronic inflammation could represent an occult inflammatory process. Moreover, his history of hidradenitis suppurativa—a condition now recognized as part of the same spectrum of neutrophilic dermatoses as PG—makes the diagnosis of PG even more plausible. The fact that the lesions improved transiently with high-dose corticosteroids and cyclosporine, and then worsened again with dose reduction, is classic for PG, which is notoriously steroid-dependent and prone to rebound upon tapering. The presence of follicular perforation and folliculitis on biopsy may represent a secondary phenomenon or a variant form of PG with follicular involvement, which has been reported in the literature.

Another entity to consider is neutrophilic eccrine hidradenitis (NEH), a rare condition typically seen in patients undergoing chemotherapy, characterized by fever and painful, erythematous plaques with neutrophilic infiltration around eccrine glands. However, this patient has not received chemotherapy, and NEH typically presents with more diffuse, less ulcerative lesions, often on the trunk and extremities, with sparing of the hands. The histopathology of NEH involves necrosis of eccrine coils and surrounding neutrophils, which is not described here. Furthermore, the lesions are not confined to sweat gland-rich areas, and the degree of tissue destruction is far greater than typical for NEH.

The possibility of a paraneoplastic syndrome must be thoroughly evaluated. The patient’s weight loss of 2.3 kg per week over a month is profound and alarming. In the context of rapidly progressive, treatment-refractory skin necrosis, this raises suspicion for an underlying malignancy. The most concerning possibility is a hematologic malignancy, particularly myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS) or acute myeloid leukemia (AML), both of which can present with neutrophilic dermatoses such as Sweet’s syndrome or leukemic cutis. The patient’s history of macrocytic anemia with low folate levels could be secondary to nutritional deficiency, but in the context of unexplained weight loss and cytopenias, it may represent early marrow failure. The normal peripheral smear (though not fully detailed) and absence of blasts or dysplastic cells in the report are reassuring but do not rule out early or low-burden MDS. The elevated ferritin (540 ng/mL) in the setting of low iron and low iron-binding capacity is consistent with anemia of chronic disease, but ferritin can also be markedly elevated in hematologic malignancies and is a non-specific marker of inflammation or malignancy. The presence of microscopic hematuria with red cell casts could suggest glomerulonephritis, which in the context of neutrophilic skin lesions raises concern for vasculitis associated with a hematologic malignancy, such as myeloproliferative neoplasms or lymphoma. Although no Bence Jones proteins were detected, a monoclonal gammopathy of undetermined significance (MGUS) or smoldering myeloma could still be present and not detected by urine electrophoresis alone. A serum protein electrophoresis with immunofixation and free light chain assay should be performed, though not mentioned in the report, and their absence does not exclude a paraneoplastic process.

Cutaneous T-cell lymphoma (CTCL), particularly mycosis fungoides with a large cell transformation, must also be considered. While CTCL typically presents with patches and plaques that are slow-growing and pruritic, aggressive variants can present with rapidly expanding, necrotic, ulcerative plaques with neutrophilic infiltrates, mimicking neutrophilic dermatoses. The presence of follicular perforation on biopsy could suggest folliculotropic involvement, which is seen in folliculotropic mycosis fungoides. However, the rapid progression, systemic symptoms, and response to steroids are more consistent with an inflammatory process than a primary lymphoma. A T-cell receptor gene rearrangement study on the biopsy specimen would be necessary to exclude this, but its absence in the report does not rule it out.

Another important consideration is vasculitis, particularly polyarteritis nodosa (PAN) or microscopic polyangiitis (MPA). The patient’s history of leukocytoclastic vasculitis on an early abdominal biopsy, the presence of purpura, and the systemic symptoms of weight loss and renal involvement (hematuria, red cell casts) raise concern for a systemic small-vessel vasculitis. However, the later biopsies showed neutrophilic dermatitis without vasculitis, which is inconsistent with PAN or MPA, which typically demonstrate necrotizing vasculitis with fibrinoid necrosis and granulocytes within vessel walls. The absence of elevated ANCA (not mentioned, but assumed negative given no mention of positivity) and normal renal function (though hematuria is present) make ANCA-associated vasculitis less likely. The lack of systemic features such as mononeuritis multiplex, hypertension, or gastrointestinal ischemia further argues against PAN. The renal findings could represent a post-infectious glomerulonephritis or a manifestation of a systemic inflammatory process, but not classic vasculitis.

Drug reactions are another critical category. The patient received multiple antibiotics, including piperacillin-tazobactam, vancomycin, ciprofloxacin, and linezolid, as well as subcutaneous heparin and topical antimicrobials. While the heparin-induced thrombocytopenia test was negative, other drug reactions, such as drug reaction with eosinophilia and systemic symptoms (DRESS) or acute generalized exanthematous pustulosis (AGEP), must be considered. AGEP typically presents with sterile pustules on an erythematous base, not necrotic ulcers, and is associated with eosinophilia and fever, which are not prominent here. DRESS syndrome usually presents with a morbilliform rash, fever, lymphadenopathy, and internal organ involvement (liver, kidney), which are not fully consistent with this case. The absence of eosinophilia, hepatic injury, or lymphadenopathy makes these less likely. However, a rare variant of drug-induced neutrophilic dermatosis has been reported, particularly with antibiotics such as sulfonamides, which were initiated shortly before admission. Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole is known to rarely cause neutrophilic skin reactions, and its initiation coincided with a transient improvement, which could reflect suppression of an inflammatory trigger rather than a direct causal relationship.

Finally, we must consider the possibility of a rare paraneoplastic or autoinflammatory syndrome. Behçet’s disease, while uncommon in elderly men, can present with oral and genital ulcers, skin lesions (pseudofolliculitis, erythema nodosum), and vascular involvement. However, the patient has no history of oral or genital ulcers, and the lesions are not typical of Behçet’s. Necrotizing vasculitis associated with rheumatoid arthritis (rheumatoid vasculitis) is another consideration, but the patient is rheumatoid factor negative and has no joint deformities or nodules. The possibility of a novel or emerging autoimmune syndrome cannot be excluded, particularly given the overlap between hidradenitis suppurativa and other neutrophilic dermatoses, suggesting a shared underlying dysregulation of innate immunity.

In summary, the most likely diagnosis is pyoderma gangrenosum, triggered by the patient’s underlying inflammatory burden, including hidradenitis suppurativa and possibly occult malignancy. The clinical features—pathergy, necrotic ulcers with undermined borders, systemic inflammation, steroid responsiveness, and histopathological neutrophilic infiltrate without vasculitis—are classic. The alternative diagnosis of Sweet’s syndrome with necrotizing features remains plausible, particularly if an underlying hematologic malignancy is later identified. The profound weight loss and cytopenias necessitate urgent investigation for myelodysplastic syndrome or lymphoma, even in the absence of definitive hematologic findings. The possibility of a drug-induced neutrophilic dermatosis cannot be entirely dismissed, especially given the temporal association with trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole. The presence of renal involvement and elevated ferritin further supports a systemic inflammatory or paraneoplastic process. A multidisciplinary approach is essential, with biopsy review by a dermatopathologist, flow cytometry of peripheral blood, bone marrow biopsy, serum protein electrophoresis, and possibly a repeat skin biopsy with special stains for fungal and mycobacterial organisms. Until the underlying trigger is identified, aggressive immunosuppression with biologics such as infliximab or adalimumab may be necessary, given the patient’s refractory course.
