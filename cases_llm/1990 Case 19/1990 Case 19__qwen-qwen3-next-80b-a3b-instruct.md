# Case 19-1990 —A 12-Year-Old Boy with a Long Travel History and a Hepatic Mass

## Presentation of Case



A 12-year–old boy was admitted to the hospital because of increasing fatigue and intermittent fever of one month's duration.



The patient was a British citizen and had arrived in this country approximately two months earlier. He was in excellent health until one month before admission, while he was visiting in Yellowstone National Park, when fever and fatigue developed and continued for about three days. Increasing fatigue and fever recurred periodically thereafter while the patient was staying in Florida, and he experienced intermittent pain in the right shoulder that was not aggravated by movement. During the two weeks before entry intermittent chilliness occurred, without frank rigors. One week before admission the boy's fatigue became even more severe. Two days before entry prominent edema of the ankles was observed, and he had mild diarrhea. On the day of admission his parents found that his temperature was 39.7°C and brought him to this hospital. The boy had visited Brittany with his family annually for nine years. He had traveled to Tunisia six years earlier and to Greece and Rhodes two years before entry, and he swam in a fresh-water pool in the latter location. He visited Malta 19 months before admission and Majorca 7 months later. Four months before entry he received gamma globulin and typhoid vaccine before a two-week stay in Turkey, where he drank tap water, swam in a fresh-water estuary, and had diarrhea for one day, as did his mother. During his stay in Yellowstone Park he did not camp out or swim in or ingest stream water. He had noticed looseness of his clothing in recent weeks, with weight loss of an unknown degree and anorexia. There was a history of measles. A heart murmur had been noted in past years, and his family had observed a change in the appearance of his fingernails one or two weeks before entry.



There was no earlier history of headache, sinus tenderness, coryza, sore throat, earache, cough or sputum production, rash, abdominal pain, jaundice, dark urine, pleuritic chest pain, dysuria, or hematochezia. His immunizations were all up to date except for mumps and rubella. He used no medications, had no known allergies, and had never been admitted previously to a hospital. His father was an employee of an international corporation; both parents and two sisters were well, although his mother had had diarrhea for two weeks after a recent trip to Mexico while the patient remained in Florida; she was later discovered to have giardiasis.



The temperature was 39.7°C, the pulse was 128, and the respirations were 12. The blood pressure was 120/70 mm Hg.



On examination the patient was slender and appeared fatigued and acutely ill. No rash, cutaneous arterial spiders, lymphadenopathy, or jaundice was found. The head was normal except for a small amount of fluid behind the left tympanic membrane; the right tympanic membrane was normal. The neck was supple, and the lungs were clear. The heart was normal except for a Grade 2 systolic murmur. The abdomen was soft and flat, with normal bowel sounds; a tender liver edge descended 8 cm below the right costal margin, with a vertical span of 15 cm; no guarding or rebound tenderness was detected, and the spleen was not felt. There was + + peripheral edema, with mild digital clubbing; no sacral edema was found. The genitalia were normal. Rectal examination was negative; a stool specimen gave a negative test for occult blood. Neurologic examination was negative.



The urine was normal. The hematocrit was 32 percent; the white-cell count was 15,500, with 84 percent neutrophils, 13 percent lymphocytes, and 3 percent monocytes. The mean corpuscular volume (MCV) was 79 μm per cell, the mean corpuscular hemoglobin (MCH) 26.4 pg per red cell, and the mean corpuscular hemoglobin concentration (MCHC) 33.4 percent. The reticulocyte count was 3.3 percent, and the platelet count 498,000; the erythrocyte sedimentation rate was 81 mm per hour. The sodium was 135 mmol, the potassium 5.2 mmol, and the chloride 98 mmol per liter. The serum aspartate aminotransferase (ASAT) was 36 U, and the alkaline phosphatase 53 U per liter. An electrocardiogram showed sinus tachycardia at a rate of 118 and was within normal limits. An x-ray film of the chest revealed slight elevation of the right hemidiaphragm, without pleural effusions; the lungs, heart, mediastinum, and bony thorax appeared normal.



Microscopical examination of a stool specimen revealed ova of  *Trichuris trichiura,* without other ova or parasites. Specimens of blood, urine, and stool were obtained for culture, and acetaminophen was administered. On the second hospital day the boy complained of alternating chills and fever. The temperature was 38.9°C; physical examination was unchanged. The conjugated bilirubin was 3 μmol per liter (0.2 mg per 100 ml), and the total bilirubin 10 μmol per liter (0.6 mg per 100 ml). The cultures of blood and urine obtained on admission gave no growth of microorganisms, and the stool culture yielded only a normal flora. Microscopical examination of another stool specimen disclosed no ova or parasites.

## Differential Diagnosis

The clinical presentation of this 12-year-old boy is complex, multifaceted, and demands a systematic, layered diagnostic approach. He presents with a one-month history of intermittent fever, profound fatigue, weight loss, anorexia, hepatomegaly, peripheral edema, and a large, complex, nonhomogeneous hepatic mass with cystic and solid components. His recent travel history across multiple endemic regions, combined with laboratory findings of microcytic anemia, thrombocytosis, elevated erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR), prolonged partial thromboplastin time (PTT), and bleeding diathesis, creates a compelling constellation of signs that point toward a systemic, likely infectious or neoplastic, process with secondary hepatic involvement. The absence of classic signs of acute infection—such as cough, sore throat, rash, or gastrointestinal symptoms beyond transient diarrhea—combined with the chronicity and progression of his illness, suggests a subacute to chronic process rather than an acute bacterial or viral syndrome.

The most salient feature is the hepatic mass. The imaging findings—ultrasound, CT, and MRI—consistently describe a large (14 × 10 cm), well-circumscribed, nonenhancing, heterogeneous mass in the right hepatic lobe with multiple septated cystic components and areas of necrosis. The lack of contrast enhancement is particularly significant: it argues against highly vascular tumors such as hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) or hemangioma, which typically show characteristic enhancement patterns. HCC is exceedingly rare in children without underlying cirrhosis or chronic viral hepatitis, neither of which are present here. There is no history of hepatitis B or C, no jaundice, no elevated transaminases (AST and ALT are minimally elevated or normal), and no evidence of chronic liver disease. The normal bilirubin and albumin further argue against cirrhosis or advanced parenchymal liver disease. The mass is not invading the inferior vena cava, which argues against aggressive malignancies like hepatoblastoma with venous invasion, though hepatoblastoma remains a consideration in children.

Hepatoblastoma, the most common primary malignant liver tumor in children under age 5, is less common in older children, but not unheard of in the 10–15 age group. It typically presents with hepatomegaly, weight loss, and systemic symptoms such as fever and anorexia. However, hepatoblastoma usually demonstrates solid, enhancing components on imaging, often with calcifications, and may have elevated alpha-fetoprotein (AFP), which is not reported here. The absence of AFP data is a gap, but the imaging description of a predominantly cystic, nonenhancing mass is atypical for classic hepatoblastoma. The presence of multiple septations and cystic components is more suggestive of a necrotic or abscessed lesion than a solid neoplasm.

This leads us to the critical consideration of infectious etiologies. The travel history is a roadmap to potential pathogens. He has traveled extensively in the Mediterranean (Brittany, Greece, Rhodes, Malta, Majorca), North Africa (Tunisia), the Middle East (Turkey), and the United States (Yellowstone). He swam in freshwater pools and estuaries, drank tap water in Turkey, and had a single episode of diarrhea during that trip. His mother developed giardiasis after travel to Mexico, suggesting possible environmental exposure to waterborne pathogens. The stool specimen revealed *Trichuris trichiura* ova—a common soil-transmitted helminth in areas with poor sanitation. While *T. trichiura* infection can cause mild gastrointestinal symptoms and iron-deficiency anemia due to chronic blood loss from the cecum, it does not cause hepatic masses or systemic febrile illness of this magnitude. Its presence is likely incidental, though it does support a history of exposure to poor hygiene environments.

The most compelling infectious differential is amebic liver abscess. *Entamoeba histolytica* is endemic in many of the regions he visited, particularly Tunisia and Turkey. Amebic abscesses typically present with fever, hepatomegaly, right upper quadrant pain (often referred to the shoulder), weight loss, and systemic symptoms. The classic presentation includes a single, large, right-lobe abscess with a “flask-shaped” appearance on imaging, often with internal septations and necrotic debris. The absence of diarrhea at the time of presentation does not rule it out—up to 50% of patients with amebic liver abscess have no concurrent intestinal symptoms. The negative ELISA for anti-amebal antibodies is concerning, but false negatives can occur, especially early in infection or in immunocompromised individuals. The fact that he was started on metronidazole (the standard treatment for amebiasis) suggests clinical suspicion, though the lack of rapid response by day 4 implies either a non-amebic etiology or a more complex, mixed, or resistant process. The prolonged PTT and bleeding diathesis (prolonged bleeding time) may reflect hepatic synthetic dysfunction or vitamin K deficiency secondary to malabsorption or biliary obstruction, though bilirubin and albumin are normal. Amebic abscesses can cause local inflammation and pressure effects on adjacent structures, potentially explaining the displaced right kidney and elevated hemidiaphragm.

Another critical infectious consideration is hydatid disease (echinococcosis), particularly *Echinococcus granulosus*. This is endemic in the Mediterranean basin, including Greece, Malta, and Turkey, and is transmitted via ingestion of eggs from dog feces. Hydatid cysts are classically described as well-circumscribed, unilocular or multilocular, nonenhancing hepatic masses with internal septations, calcifications, and daughter cysts. The imaging findings here are highly suggestive of a hydatid cyst: large, well-defined, cystic with septations, no contrast enhancement, and no capsule. The absence of a clearly defined capsule on MRI is somewhat atypical, as hydatid cysts often have a thick fibrous capsule, but this may be due to rupture, infection, or degeneration. The patient’s history of swimming in freshwater and exposure to rural environments in multiple endemic countries supports this diagnosis. Hydatid disease can present with fever, hepatomegaly, and systemic symptoms due to cyst rupture, secondary bacterial infection, or hypersensitivity reactions. The prolonged PTT and bleeding time could be due to hepatic compression or impaired vitamin K-dependent clotting factor synthesis. The thrombocytosis and elevated ESR are consistent with a chronic inflammatory or parasitic process. Importantly, hydatid disease does not typically cause a positive stool exam for ova, as the eggs are passed in the feces of definitive hosts (dogs), not humans. The *Trichuris* infection is unrelated.

Other parasitic infections must be considered. *Schistosoma* species—particularly *S. mansoni* and *S. haematobium*—are endemic in North Africa and parts of the Mediterranean. Chronic schistosomiasis can cause hepatosplenomegaly, portal hypertension, and hepatic fibrosis with a characteristic “pipe-stem” fibrosis pattern. However, acute schistosomiasis (Katayama fever) typically presents with fever, urticaria, eosinophilia, and pulmonary symptoms 3–8 weeks after exposure, and often with hepatosplenomegaly and elevated transaminases. The absence of eosinophilia (not reported, but neutrophil predominance is striking), lack of pulmonary findings, and absence of hematuria or bloody diarrhea make acute schistosomiasis less likely. Chronic schistosomiasis usually presents with portal hypertension, ascites, and splenomegaly—not a discrete hepatic mass. Imaging would show fibrosis and nodularity, not a well-circumscribed cystic mass.

Fungal infections are less common in immunocompetent children but remain possible. *Histoplasma capsulatum* and *Coccidioides immitis* are endemic in certain regions of the Americas, but not in Europe or the Middle East. *Aspergillus* or *Candida* hepatic abscesses are rare and typically occur in immunocompromised hosts or after prolonged antibiotic use, neither of which applies here. The absence of pulmonary findings makes histoplasmosis unlikely.

Tuberculosis must be considered. Hepatic TB is rare but can present as multiple granulomatous nodules or abscesses, often with fever, weight loss, and hepatomegaly. It may mimic a neoplasm on imaging. The patient’s history of travel to regions with high TB burden (Turkey, Tunisia) and the presence of systemic symptoms make this plausible. However, TB liver lesions are usually multiple, small, and often associated with pulmonary or lymph node involvement, neither of which is present. The absence of lymphadenopathy on exam and normal chest X-ray make this less likely, though miliary TB can be subtle.

Malignant causes beyond hepatoblastoma must be evaluated. Metastatic disease is rare in children without a known primary, but must be considered. Neuroblastoma can metastasize to the liver and present with hepatomegaly and systemic symptoms, but it typically occurs in younger children (<5 years), is often associated with hypertension and catecholamine excess, and imaging usually shows a heterogeneous, calcified mass with vascular invasion. Wilms tumor is a renal malignancy that can metastasize to the liver, but the primary tumor would likely be detectable on abdominal imaging, and the patient has no flank mass, hematuria, or hypertension. Lymphoma, particularly Burkitt or Hodgkin lymphoma, can present with hepatosplenomegaly, fever, and weight loss. However, lymphomatous infiltration of the liver typically appears as multiple small nodules or diffuse infiltration, not a single large, well-circumscribed cystic mass. The absence of lymphadenopathy, night sweats, or B symptoms beyond fatigue makes this less likely, though not impossible.

Vascular causes are unlikely. Hepatic hemangioma is common in children but is typically small, asymptomatic, and enhances vividly on imaging. The nonenhancing nature of this mass rules it out. Hepatic adenoma is exceedingly rare in children, especially males, and is associated with anabolic steroid use or glycogen storage diseases, neither of which apply here.

Autoimmune or inflammatory conditions are also possible. IgG4-related disease can cause hepatic masses with fibrosis and cystic changes, but it is exceedingly rare in children and typically presents with multiorgan involvement (pancreas, salivary glands, retroperitoneum) and elevated IgG4 levels. Autoimmune hepatitis can cause hepatomegaly and elevated transaminases, but the transaminases are normal, and there is no hypergammaglobulinemia (globulin is low at 12 g/L). Primary sclerosing cholangitis is associated with inflammatory bowel disease and cholestatic enzymes, which are not present here.

The prolonged PTT and bleeding time are intriguing. The PTT is significantly prolonged (45–47 sec), while PT is normal. This suggests a deficiency in intrinsic pathway factors (VIII, IX, XI, XII) or the presence of an inhibitor. In the context of a large hepatic mass, this could reflect consumption of clotting factors, impaired synthesis due to hepatic dysfunction, or vitamin K deficiency. However, albumin is normal, and bilirubin is normal, making severe synthetic failure unlikely. The prolonged bleeding time suggests platelet dysfunction, which could be due to thrombocytopenia—but platelets are elevated (444,000–498,000). This paradox—thrombocytosis with bleeding diathesis—is highly suggestive of a paraneoplastic or infectious syndrome with acquired platelet dysfunction, as seen in some cases of endocarditis or disseminated infection. The Grade 2 systolic murmur, though previously noted, now raises concern for infective endocarditis. Could this be a case of right-sided endocarditis with septic emboli to the liver? This is a critical consideration. Infective endocarditis (IE) in children can present with prolonged fever, weight loss, hepatomegaly, and embolic phenomena. The hepatic mass could represent a septic embolus that has become necrotic and cystic. The absence of peripheral stigmata (Janeway lesions, Osler nodes, splinter hemorrhages) and the lack of new murmurs make this less likely, but not impossible. The prolonged PTT could reflect a lupus anticoagulant or antiphospholipid antibody syndrome triggered by infection. The thrombocytosis could be reactive to chronic inflammation.

The most compelling unifying diagnosis is **hydatid disease (cystic echinococcosis)**. The epidemiology is perfect: multiple exposures in endemic Mediterranean and Middle Eastern regions. The imaging is classic: large, well-circumscribed, multiseptated, nonenhancing hepatic cystic mass. The systemic symptoms—fever, weight loss, fatigue—are consistent with a chronic parasitic infection with possible secondary bacterial superinfection or cyst rupture. The bleeding diathesis and prolonged PTT may reflect hepatic compression or vitamin K malabsorption due to biliary compression. The *Trichuris* infection is coincidental. The negative amebic serology supports this over amebic abscess. The lack of response to metronidazole further argues against amebiasis.

Alternative considerations include **pyogenic liver abscess**, though this is typically more acute, with higher transaminases, and often presents with chills and rigors rather than low-grade intermittent fever. The lack of biliary tract disease, recent instrumentation, or immunosuppression makes this less likely. **Fungal abscess** is possible but rare. **Metastatic disease** remains a possibility if an occult primary exists, but in a 12-year-old with no prior malignancy, this is unlikely.

In conclusion, the leading diagnosis is **cystic echinococcosis of the liver**. Secondary considerations include amebic liver abscess (despite negative serology), pyogenic abscess, and, less likely, metastatic disease or a rare hepatic neoplasm such as cystic hepatoblastoma. The prolonged PTT and bleeding time require further investigation into possible acquired coagulopathy due to hepatic involvement or infection. The next diagnostic step should be serologic testing for *Echinococcus granulosus* antibodies (ELISA or Western blot), imaging for additional cysts (lung, spleen, kidney), and consideration of percutaneous aspiration under imaging guidance with albendazole therapy if confirmed. The differential must remain open, but the clinical, geographic, and radiological data converge most compellingly on hydatid disease.
