# Case 15-2011 —A 19-Year-Old South African Woman with Headache, Fatigue, and Vaginal Discharge

## Presentation of Case



   *Dr. Fundisiwe Chonco* (Department of Virology, University of KwaZulu-Natal, Durban, South Africa): A 19-year-old woman was seen in a clinic at McCord Hospital in Durban, KwaZulu-Natal Province, South Africa (which is affiliated with Massachusetts General Hospital), because of headache, fatigue, and vaginal discharge.



The patient had been well until approximately 18 days earlier, when she began to have a vaginal discharge, followed by headache, fatigue, sore throat, and anorexia. On the fifth day of symptoms, she went to an outpatient clinic in an urban area. On examination, she had a vaginal discharge; the temperature and the remainder of the examination were reportedly normal. Two different rapid screening tests for human immunodeficiency virus type 1 (HIV-1) performed on a finger-stick blood sample were negative. Doxycycline, metronidazole, and ceftriaxone were administered according to South African guidelines for sexually transmitted diseases. The patient was invited to participate in a South African clinical research study; she accepted, and additional blood specimens were obtained. She was referred to the study clinic at McCord Hospital for follow-up, where she was seen 13 days later.



The patient reported persistent headache, fatigue, sore throat, and vaginal discharge. She was nulliparous and had a history of developmental delay after an accident during childhood; she was otherwise well. She was on no medications and had no known allergies. She was born in South Africa, lived with relatives in a house in an urban area of KwaZulu-Natal Province, and attended school. She had no history of sexually transmitted diseases and reported having had sexual intercourse four times with her 25-year-old boyfriend of 6 months, who had been her only partner. He reportedly had refused her request to use condoms.



On examination, the weight was 73 kg, the temperature 36.0°C, the blood pressure 93/59 mm Hg, and the pulse 82 beats per minute. There was a white, malodorous vaginal discharge; the remainder of the examination was normal except for findings consistent with the patient's chronic developmental delay. Additional blood specimens were obtained, and both these and specimens from the first clinic were sent to a reference laboratory for testing.



During the next 14 days, headache, fatigue, anorexia, sore throat, and vaginal discharge resolved, but a painful genital ulcer developed. The patient returned to the McCord Hospital clinic. She had no history of oral or genital ulcers and had not been sexually active since her visit to the first clinic. She was aware of HIV and that it is sexually transmitted.



On examination, the vital signs were normal. There was a painful pink ulcer, 5 mm by 10 mm, on the left labium majus, with undermined borders and without surrounding induration. There were palpable lymph nodes, 1 cm or smaller in diameter, in the submental, posterior cervical, and epitrochlear regions. The remainder of the examination was normal. Penicillin G benzathine and erythromycin were administered according to South African guidelines for genital ulcers. A rapid plasma reagin test was negative.



Diagnostic test results were reviewed.

## Differential Diagnosis

The clinical course of this 19-year-old woman presents a complex, evolving constellation of symptoms that initially mimicked a common sexually transmitted infection (STI) but progressed in a manner inconsistent with typical bacterial vaginosis, trichomoniasis, or even uncomplicated gonorrhea or chlamydia. The sequence—vaginal discharge followed by systemic symptoms (headache, fatigue, sore throat, anorexia), resolution of those symptoms with development of a characteristic genital ulcer, and the presence of generalized lymphadenopathy—demands a broad differential that spans infectious, autoimmune, and neoplastic etiologies, with particular attention to pathogens known to cause mucocutaneous ulceration and systemic prodromes in young, sexually active individuals in high-HIV-prevalence settings. The negative HIV rapid tests, while reassuring, do not exclude acute HIV infection, particularly given the window period and the limitations of finger-stick assays in early seroconversion. Moreover, the failure of standard STI therapy (doxycycline, metronidazole, ceftriaxone) to resolve the constellation of symptoms, and the subsequent emergence of a painful, undermined genital ulcer, shifts the diagnostic focus toward less common but highly consequential conditions.

The most compelling diagnostic consideration is **primary HIV infection (acute retroviral syndrome)**. The timeline is highly suggestive: the onset of systemic symptoms 5–7 days after the initial vaginal discharge aligns with the typical incubation period of HIV, during which viremia peaks and the immune system mounts a vigorous, often flu-like response. Headache, fatigue, sore throat, and anorexia are classic components of acute retroviral syndrome, occurring in up to 80% of infected individuals. The vaginal discharge may represent a coincidental bacterial vaginosis or candidiasis, or, more significantly, an early mucosal manifestation of HIV-induced immune dysregulation that alters the vaginal microbiome and increases susceptibility to opportunistic or inflammatory changes. The negative rapid HIV tests are not definitive; finger-stick assays, particularly first-generation or even second-generation rapid tests, often remain negative during the first 2–4 weeks post-exposure due to the delay in antibody production. The window period for antigen/antibody combination assays is approximately 18–22 days, and this patient was tested at day 5 and then again at day 18—placing her squarely within the window for seroconversion. The subsequent development of a genital ulcer is not typical of primary HIV alone, but it is well-documented that HIV-associated mucosal ulceration can occur during acute infection, especially in the setting of concomitant herpes simplex virus (HSV) reactivation or coinfection, which is common in immunocompromised hosts. The generalized lymphadenopathy—submental, cervical, epitrochlear—is also a hallmark of acute HIV infection, reflecting widespread antigen presentation and lymphoid activation. The fact that the ulcer developed after the systemic symptoms resolved is consistent with the biphasic nature of acute HIV: initial viremia-driven systemic illness, followed by immune-mediated tissue damage as cytotoxic T cells begin to respond. The negative RPR further argues against syphilis, though it does not rule out early primary syphilis if the test was performed too early in the chancre phase.

**Primary syphilis**, however, remains a critical consideration and must be weighed carefully. The genital ulcer described—painful, 5 mm by 10 mm, with undermined borders and no surrounding induration—is atypical for a classic syphilitic chancre, which is usually painless, firm, and well-circumscribed. However, atypical presentations are common, particularly in HIV-coinfected individuals or those with altered immune responses. The presence of pain could reflect superimposed HSV, bacterial superinfection, or simply individual variation in host response. The negative RPR is concerning, but it must be interpreted in context: serologic tests for syphilis can be negative in the first 3–6 weeks after inoculation, especially if the patient was tested during the very early phase of infection. The RPR is a nontreponemal test that depends on the host’s antibody response to cardiolipin, which may take up to 4–6 weeks to become detectable. The fact that the ulcer appeared 13–14 days after the initial presentation, and the patient had not been sexually active since, makes the timeline plausible for a chancre that was developing slowly or was initially subtle. The generalized lymphadenopathy, while less specific than in HIV, can occur in early syphilis as part of the systemic immune response. The administration of penicillin G benzathine was appropriate empiric therapy, and the fact that the ulcer resolved without further intervention supports a bacterial etiology, but does not confirm syphilis. The key diagnostic challenge here is distinguishing between a primary chancre and other ulcerative conditions that mimic it. The absence of induration and the presence of pain argue against classic syphilis, but do not exclude it entirely.

**Herpes simplex virus (HSV) infection**, particularly HSV-2, must be high on the differential. Genital HSV can present with multiple painful, shallow ulcers with undermined or irregular borders, often preceded by prodromal symptoms such as malaise, fever, headache, and myalgias—exactly the constellation this patient experienced. The vaginal discharge could represent mucosal inflammation from HSV or a coincidental bacterial vaginosis. The fact that the systemic symptoms preceded the ulcer by approximately 10–12 days is consistent with HSV: the virus may have been introduced during sexual contact, established latency in sacral ganglia, and then reactivated with a systemic immune response that manifested as headache and fatigue before the cutaneous eruption. HSV-2 ulcers are typically painful, and the undermined borders described are classic. The lymphadenopathy—submental, cervical, epitrochlear—is also commonly seen in primary HSV infection, reflecting regional lymphatic drainage and immune activation. The negative serologies for syphilis and HIV (at the time) do not rule out HSV, as no specific test was mentioned. HSV diagnosis requires viral culture, PCR, or direct fluorescent antibody testing of ulcer exudate, none of which were performed. The response to penicillin and erythromycin (which have no antiviral activity) was not reported, but the resolution of the ulcer suggests either spontaneous healing (common in HSV) or perhaps an incidental improvement due to reduced trauma or secondary infection control. The fact that the patient had not been sexually active since the initial visit makes HSV reactivation from prior exposure more plausible than a new acquisition, though primary HSV can occur with minimal exposure.

**Chancroid**, caused by *Haemophilus ducreyi*, is another important consideration in this geographic and demographic context. South Africa has a high burden of STIs, and chancroid, though less common than syphilis or HSV, remains a significant cause of genital ulcers in sub-Saharan Africa. Chancroid ulcers are typically painful, have ragged or undermined borders, and are often associated with tender, suppurative inguinal lymphadenopathy. However, the lymphadenopathy described here is generalized (submental, cervical, epitrochlear) rather than localized to the inguinal region, which is a strong argument against chancroid. Furthermore, chancroid rarely presents with systemic prodromal symptoms such as headache and fatigue, and the vaginal discharge preceding the ulcer is not typical. The ulcer described is smaller and less exudative than classic chancroid lesions. While the empirical use of erythromycin (which is effective against *H. ducreyi*) may have contributed to resolution, the lack of inguinal lymphadenopathy and the presence of systemic symptoms make chancroid less likely.

**Lymphogranuloma venereum (LGV)**, caused by invasive serovars of *Chlamydia trachomatis*, should also be considered. LGV typically presents with a transient, often unnoticed genital papule or ulcer that resolves, followed weeks later by regional lymphadenopathy and systemic symptoms such as fever, malaise, and headache. The patient’s timeline—vaginal discharge followed by systemic symptoms, then ulceration—is highly suggestive. LGV can cause genital ulcers with undermined borders, and the lymphadenopathy, though typically inguinal or femoral, can occasionally be generalized if there is hematogenous spread or immune dysregulation. The vaginal discharge could represent the initial chlamydial infection. The negative rapid tests for chlamydia were not mentioned, and the doxycycline administered may have suppressed but not eradicated LGV, especially if the dose or duration was insufficient. LGV is often misdiagnosed as syphilis or HSV, and the serologic tests for syphilis are negative. The fact that the patient was treated with doxycycline and then later with erythromycin and penicillin may have partially ameliorated symptoms, delaying diagnosis. LGV is underdiagnosed in many settings, particularly in young women, and the clinical presentation described is remarkably consistent.

**Behçet’s disease**, though rare in this age group and in this population, must be considered in the differential of recurrent or persistent mucocutaneous ulcers with systemic symptoms. Behçet’s typically presents with oral and genital ulcers, uveitis, and skin lesions, and can be associated with arthralgias, headaches, and fatigue. The genital ulcer described is consistent with the painful, recurrent ulcers of Behçet’s. The systemic symptoms—headache, fatigue, sore throat—could reflect the underlying vasculitic process. However, Behçet’s usually presents with recurrent episodes over months to years, and this is the patient’s first episode. Furthermore, the absence of oral ulcers, ocular involvement, or skin lesions (papulopustular lesions, erythema nodosum) makes this diagnosis less likely. It is also exceedingly rare in sub-Saharan African populations, particularly in young women without a family history. While not impossible, it is a diagnosis of exclusion and requires chronicity and multiple organ involvement.

**Autoimmune or inflammatory conditions** such as systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) or vasculitis are possible but unlikely. SLE can present with oral or genital ulcers, fatigue, headache, and lymphadenopathy, but these are typically accompanied by other systemic features: malar rash, photosensitivity, arthritis, renal involvement, cytopenias, or positive ANA and anti-dsDNA. The absence of any other systemic signs, the acute onset, and the clear temporal association with sexual exposure make SLE improbable. Similarly, vasculitides like polyarteritis nodosa or granulomatosis with polyangiitis are associated with more severe systemic manifestations—renal failure, pulmonary nodules, neuropathy—and are exceedingly rare in young women without prior history.

**Malignancy**, particularly lymphoma or metastatic disease, is an important consideration in any patient with persistent lymphadenopathy and systemic symptoms. However, the acute onset, the presence of a genital ulcer, and the resolution of systemic symptoms prior to ulcer development argue strongly against malignancy. Lymphomas typically present with progressive, painless lymphadenopathy, weight loss, night sweats, and fever, but rarely with genital ulcers unless there is direct infiltration, which is uncommon. The patient’s age, lack of constitutional symptoms beyond the acute phase, and rapid resolution of systemic illness make malignancy highly unlikely.

**Fungal or parasitic causes** such as candidiasis or trichomoniasis were initially suspected and treated empirically. The vaginal discharge may have been due to candidiasis or bacterial vaginosis, but neither explains the systemic symptoms or the genital ulcer. Trichomoniasis can cause vaginitis and, rarely, urethral or vulvar inflammation, but not typically painful ulcers. The metronidazole administered would have treated trichomoniasis, yet the ulcer developed afterward, making this an unlikely primary cause.

In summary, the leading diagnostic possibilities are **acute HIV infection**, **primary HSV-2 infection**, and **lymphogranuloma venereum**. Each fits key elements of the clinical picture: the timing of systemic symptoms preceding the ulcer, the presence of generalized lymphadenopathy, the response to broad-spectrum antibiotics, and the geographic context. The negative HIV rapid tests are insufficient to exclude acute HIV, especially given the window period and the limitations of finger-stick assays. The genital ulcer, while painful and undermined, is not diagnostic of any single entity. The most parsimonious explanation is **acute HIV infection with superimposed HSV-2 reactivation**, as both can explain the prodrome, the ulcer, and the lymphadenopathy. The patient’s history of unprotected sex with a partner who refused condoms, the absence of prior STIs, and the high HIV prevalence in KwaZulu-Natal all support this. Secondary syphilis is less likely due to the painful nature of the ulcer and the negative RPR, but cannot be fully excluded without a treponemal test (e.g., TP-PA or FTA-ABS). LGV remains a strong contender, particularly if chlamydial testing was not performed with nucleic acid amplification tests (NAATs). The case underscores the critical importance of performing HIV RNA PCR or fourth-generation antigen/antibody tests in patients with acute febrile illness and genital ulcers in high-prevalence settings, even when rapid tests are negative. Empiric treatment, while appropriate in resource-limited settings, can mask the true etiology and delay definitive diagnosis. The resolution of symptoms does not equate to resolution of underlying infection; this patient requires urgent confirmatory HIV testing, HSV PCR from the ulcer, and NAAT for *C. trachomatis* and *N. gonorrhoeae*. The differential must be broad, but the most likely diagnosis is acute HIV infection.
