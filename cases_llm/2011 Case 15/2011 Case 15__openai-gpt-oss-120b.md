### 1. Acute Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV) Infection (Seroconversion Syndrome)

#### Epidemiologic and Risk Considerations  

- **Age & geography**: Young adults in South Africa bear the highest incidence of new HIV infections globally; women in KwaZulu‑Natal have a particularly high prevalence.  
- **Sexual exposure**: The patient reports four episodes of unprotected vaginal intercourse with a 25‑year‑old male partner who declined condom use. This pattern is consistent with a high‑risk exposure.  
- **Window period**: Rapid finger‑stick HIV‑1/2 antibody tests have sensitivities of ~99 % after 4 weeks but are less reliable within the first 2–3 weeks of infection because antibodies have not yet reached detectable levels. A negative rapid test at day 5 of symptoms does not exclude acute infection.

#### Pathophysiology and Typical Clinical Manifestations  

Acute HIV infection is characterized by a rapid rise in plasma viremia, followed by a burst of innate immune activation. The resulting cytokine storm produces a **mononucleosis‑like picture**: fever, malaise, headache, sore throat, myalgias, and **generalized lymphadenopathy** (cervical, submental, epitrochlear, and inguinal nodes). Mucocutaneous lesions—including oral ulcers, maculopapular rash, and, less commonly, genital ulcerations—may appear 1–2 weeks after the onset of systemic symptoms.  

The genital ulcer of acute HIV is usually **painful**, shallow, and may be solitary or multiple. Histologically, the ulcer is a nonspecific ulceration secondary to immune dysregulation rather than a direct viral cytopathic effect. The timing fits: systemic symptoms began ≈ 18 days before the ulcer, and the ulcer emerged after the constitutional phase had begun to resolve.

#### Laboratory Correlates  

- **Serology**: Standard rapid antibody tests are often negative early; a **fourth‑generation antigen/antibody combination assay** (detecting p24 antigen) is more sensitive in the window period.  
- **Viral load**: HIV RNA PCR is the definitive test for acute infection; viral loads are typically >100 000 copies/mL at this stage.  
- **RPR**: May be non‑reactive early in syphilis but also remains negative in HIV seroconversion, providing no discriminant value.

#### Evidence For and Against  

**For**  
- High regional prevalence and unprotected exposure.  
- Classic prodrome (headache, fatigue, sore throat, anorexia) with generalized lymphadenopathy.  
- Development of a painful genital ulcer after the prodrome, a recognized though infrequent manifestation of seroconversion.  
- Negative rapid HIV test is compatible with the early window period.

**Against**  
- Absence of fever (temperature 36 °C) at the time of the ulcer, although afebrile seroconversion is possible.  
- No documented oral ulcers or rash, which are present in up to 50 % of seroconversion cases.  
- The ulcer’s morphology (undermined edges) is more typical of chancroid than of HIV‑related ulceration, though ulcer appearance can be variable.

#### Bottom Line  

Acute HIV infection remains a **high‑probability diagnosis** given the epidemiologic context, the temporal relationship of systemic symptoms and ulcer development, and the limitations of the rapid test performed early. Confirmation would require a fourth‑generation assay and/or HIV RNA PCR.
