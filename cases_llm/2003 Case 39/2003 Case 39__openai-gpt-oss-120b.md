#### 1.1 Urothelial (Transitional Cell) Carcinoma (TCC)  

**Epidemiology & Risk Factors** – TCC accounts for > 90 % of primary bladder cancers in the United States and Europe. In Asian populations, the proportion is similar, although the overall incidence is lower. The classic risk profile includes male sex, older age (> 55 y), cigarette smoking, and occupational exposure to aromatic amines (e.g., dye, rubber, paint). However, a minority of cases arise in young, non‑smoking women, often with low‑grade, papillary disease.  

**Pathophysiology** – The urothelium is uniquely sensitive to carcinogens excreted in urine; chronic irritation can promote dysplasia and clonal expansion of mutated urothelial cells. In the absence of identifiable exposures, sporadic mutations (FGFR3, HRAS, TP53) may drive tumorigenesis.  

**Clinical Correlation** – Gross hematuria is the hallmark presentation; clots are common when the lesion is exophytic and vascular. Dysuria, urinary frequency, or urgency may be mild or absent. In this case:  

* **Hematuria with clots** – fits classic TCC presentation.  
* **Mass at bladder dome** – TCC can arise anywhere in the bladder, including the dome; however, papillary lesions are often multifocal, whereas a solitary 3–4 cm mass is more typical of a larger, possibly higher‑grade lesion.  
* **Absence of pain or irritative voiding** – aligns with TCC, which frequently presents without dysuria.  
* **Age 33** – atypical but not impossible; low‑grade papillary TCC can affect younger patients, especially women.  

**Against** – The patient lacks known carcinogenic exposures (no smoking, no industrial chemicals). Nevertheless, a sporadic mutation cannot be excluded. The CT scan showed non‑enhancing debris rather than a solid enhancing mass; however, the lack of contrast enhancement may reflect clot overlay or technical factors.  

**Conclusion** – Urothelial carcinoma remains a strong contender, especially given the presence of a visible intravesical mass and the classic hematuria picture. The size of the lesion (3–4 cm) raises concern for a higher‑grade tumor, but histology is required for definitive grading.

### 10. Therapeutic Implications of the Differential  

The therapeutic pathway diverges dramatically based on the final histologic diagnosis:

- **Urothelial carcinoma** – For a solitary 3–4 cm lesion, standard management involves complete transurethral resection with muscularis propria sampling. If the pathology reveals non‑muscle‑invasive disease (Ta/T1), intravesical therapy (e.g., Bacillus Calmette‑Guérin) may be indicated, especially for high‑grade lesions. Muscle‑invasive disease would require radical cystectomy with urinary diversion, possibly preceded or followed by neoadjuvant chemotherapy.  

- **Urachal adenocarcinoma** – Surgical excision typically entails partial cystectomy with en bloc resection of the urachal ligament and umbilicus (partial cystectomy with umbilectomy). Because these tumors often present at an advanced stage, adjuvant chemotherapy (e.g., 5‑FU/oxaliplatin) may be considered, but data are limited.  

- **Benign leiomyoma** – Simple transurethral resection or enucleation would be curative; no further oncologic therapy needed.  

- **Infectious pseudotumor** – Antibiotic therapy targeting identified organisms, together with bladder irrigation to clear clots, would resolve both the infection and the inflammatory mass.  

- **Vascular malformation** – Endoscopic coagulation or laser therapy could control bleeding; surgical excision is reserved for refractory cases.  

Thus, accurate histopathologic classification is critical not only for prognosis but also for determining the extent of surgical intervention and the need for adjunctive therapies.
