# Case 36-2001 —Acute Febrile Respiratory Illness in a 57-Year-Old Man with Recurrent Pulmonary Disorders

## Presentation of Case

Polymyositis, which was positive for anti–Jo-1 antibody, was diagnosed. This 57-year-old man presents with an acute febrile respiratory illness characterized by nonproductive cough, low-grade fever, sore throat, rhinorrhea, and dyspnea at rest, occurring against a backdrop of profound immunosuppression and a complex, multi-system history of autoimmune and infectious pulmonary complications. His immune status is uniquely compromised by a triad of factors: chronic high-dose corticosteroid therapy, chronic immunosuppression with cyclosporine and prior cyclophosphamide, and underlying diabetes mellitus with its attendant defects in neutrophil function and mucosal immunity. These factors collectively create a perfect storm for opportunistic, atypical, and reactivated infections, as well as non-infectious inflammatory flares of his underlying connective tissue disease.

The most immediate consideration is **opportunistic pulmonary infection**, particularly given his history of *Pneumocystis jirovecii* pneumonia (formerly *carinii*) on at least two prior occasions, and his current immunosuppressive regimen. Although he is on trimethoprim–sulfamethoxazole prophylaxis, its dose has been reduced to one tablet three times weekly—a suboptimal regimen for full protection, especially in the setting of ongoing corticosteroid use and cyclosporine. *P. jirovecii* pneumonia remains a leading candidate. The classic radiographic appearance of *P. jirovecii* is bilateral, symmetric, perihilar interstitial infiltrates, often with a “ground-glass” pattern, but in immunocompromised hosts with prolonged disease or prior lung injury, it may present with patchy, linear, or consolidative opacities that mimic other pneumonias or organizing pneumonia. The absence of hypoxemia (SaO₂ 98%) is atypical for severe *P. jirovecii*, but this patient has significant baseline restrictive and obstructive lung disease from prior organizing pneumonia and diffuse alveolar damage, which may blunt the expected degree of hypoxemia. Furthermore, *P. jirovecii* can recur in the setting of suboptimal prophylaxis, particularly during steroid tapering, which is precisely what occurred prior to this admission. The recent reduction in trimethoprim–sulfamethoxazole prophylaxis and the taper of prednisone from 15 mg to 5 mg likely created a window of relative immunosuppression sufficient for reactivation. A bronchoalveolar lavage (BAL) with PCR and immunofluorescence for *P. jirovecii* is imperative, even if the patient appears only mildly ill.

A second critical infectious consideration is **viral pneumonia**, particularly from respiratory syncytial virus (RSV), influenza, parainfluenza, or adenovirus. The patient received an influenza vaccine 10 days before the onset of symptoms, which reduces—but does not eliminate—the risk of influenza infection, especially in immunocompromised individuals who mount poor antibody responses. Influenza in this population can present with atypical features: absence of prominent chills, less severe hypoxemia initially, and radiographic findings that may be subtle or patchy. RSV is increasingly recognized as a cause of severe pneumonia in adults with immunosuppression, particularly those with hematologic malignancies or transplant recipients, but it also affects patients with chronic autoimmune disease on immunosuppressants. The radiographic pattern of patchy lower lobe opacities with peribronchial thickening is classic for viral bronchiolitis or viral pneumonia. The absence of wheezes or crackles does not exclude this, as the patient may have an underlying baseline airway disease from prior organizing pneumonia and bronchiolitis obliterans. A multiplex PCR panel on nasal swab and BAL fluid is essential to rule out viral pathogens. Notably, adenovirus can cause severe, necrotizing pneumonia in immunocompromised hosts and may mimic organizing pneumonia radiographically.
