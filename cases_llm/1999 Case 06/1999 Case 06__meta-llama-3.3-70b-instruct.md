# Case 6-1999 —A 17 1/2-Year-Old Girl with a Thoracoabdominal Aneurysm

## Presentation of Case



A 17 1/2-year-old girl was admitted to the hospital because of a thoracoabdominal aneurysm.



The patient had been born after a normal pregnancy and delivery, and her early growth, development, and health were normal. She was initially treated with corticosteroids administered orally, but because of abdominal discomfort, the medication was subsequently injected. The ocular symptoms improved, but her hearing did not.



Seven months before admission, a thoracoabdominal aneurysm was detected, with repeated studies showing evidence of progressive enlargement to more than 6 cm in diameter and the development of stenosis of the left renal artery. One month before admission, a “hypertensive crisis” developed, and the patient was given atenolol, which provided inadequate control of the hypertension. She was referred to this hospital.



The patient was a native of Greece; she was unmarried and was not sexually active. She had a recent history of “migraine headaches” for which she took an unidentified oral agent. A nonidentical twin sister was well. There was no history of aphthous ulcers, skin problems, or arthritis. Her medications at the time of admission consisted of prednisone, atenolol (25 mg twice daily), and ranitidine.



The temperature was 37.2°C, the pulse was 84, and the respirations were 18. The blood pressure was 120/70 mm Hg.



On physical examination, the patient was slim and had mildly cushingoid features, with increased body hair; prominent striae on the arms, legs, and trunk; and a small “buffalo hump.” No oral ulcer, rash, or lymphadenopathy was evident. The neck was supple, and the lungs were clear. A grade-2-to-3 systolic ejection murmur and a grade 2 decrescendo diastolic murmur were heard at the base of the heart. No bony deformity of the sternum or spine was found. A faint epigastric bruit was heard, but no aneurysm was palpated, and there was no organomegaly. The arms and legs were normal. Radiographs of the chest showed a double contour projected over the left aspect of the heart, a finding consistent with the presence of an aneurysm.
