## 1. Large‑Vessel Vasculitis

### 1.1 Takayasu Arteritis (TA)

**Pathophysiology & Typical Presentation**  
Takayasu arteritis is a granulomatous pan‑arteritis that preferentially involves the aorta and its major branches, most often in young women (peak incidence 10‑30 y). Classic features include pulseless disease, limb‑blood‑pressure discrepancies, bruits over affected vessels, and constitutional symptoms. Laboratory markers (ESR, CRP) are usually elevated. Renal‑artery involvement leading to hypertension is common, and aneurysmal dilatation of the thoracic or abdominal aorta can occur, though stenosis predominates.

**Arguments Supporting TA**  

* Age and sex fit the epidemiologic profile.  
* Elevated ESR and leukocytosis are compatible with an active inflammatory process.  
* Renal‑artery stenosis with refractory hypertension is a recognized manifestation.  
* Thoraco‑abdominal aneurysm, while less common than stenosis, has been described in late or “burn‑out” phases of TA.

**Arguments Against TA**  

* **Absence of classic limb‑pulse asymmetry** – pulses in the lower extremities are reported as strong, and no brachial‑pulse discrepancy is noted.  
* **Lack of upper‑extremity or carotid bruits** – only an epigastric bruit is described.  
* **Ocular‑auditory syndrome** – interstitial keratitis and sensorineural deafness are not typical features of TA; ocular involvement, when present, is usually ischemic (retinal artery occlusion) rather than corneal scarring.  
* **Time course** – TA often presents with a more insidious onset of constitutional symptoms (fever, night sweats, weight loss) before vascular complications become apparent. The patient’s only systemic symptom is a “hypertensive crisis.”  

Overall, while TA can explain the large‑vessel pathology and hypertension, it does not account for the characteristic ocular and auditory findings.  

### 1.2 Cogan’s Syndrome with Systemic Vasculitis (Large‑Vessel Variant)

**Pathophysiology & Typical Presentation**  
Cogan’s syndrome is a rare autoimmune disorder first described in 1945, classically defined by the triad of **interstitial keratitis**, **audio‑vestibular dysfunction** (typically progressive sensorineural hearing loss and/or vertigo), and a **systemic vasculitic component** that may involve the aorta, coronary arteries, renal arteries, and other medium‑ to large‑size vessels. The ocular disease is usually a non‑ulcerative, diffuse stromal keratitis; the inner‑ear disease may progress to profound deafness. Approximately 10‑20 % of patients develop a systemic vasculitis that can cause aortitis, aneurysm formation, and renovascular hypertension. Autoimmune mechanisms (antineutrophil cytoplasmic antibodies, anti‑heat‑shock protein antibodies) have been implicated, and the disease may respond to high‑dose corticosteroids and other immunosuppressants.

**Arguments Supporting Cogan’s Syndrome**  

* **Temporal correlation** – Ocular irritation and progressive hearing loss began at age 13½, a typical age for the ocular‑auditory component of Cogan’s.  
* **Ocular findings** – The slit‑lamp description of tiny ghost vessels and nummular stromal scars is consistent with **late‑stage interstitial keratitis** where neovascularization and scar formation replace the acute inflammatory infiltrate. The blue sclerae may reflect scleral thinning secondary to chronic stromal inflammation.  
* **Audiovestibular involvement** – Bilateral, rapidly progressive sensorineural deafness is a hallmark; the lack of response to steroids is also typical once the disease has entered a fibrotic phase.  
* **Systemic vasculitis** – The presence of a large thoraco‑abdominal aneurysm, left renal‑artery stenosis, and refractory hypertension aligns with the vasculitic subset of Cogan’s. The high ESR and leukocytosis further support an active inflammatory process.  
* **Age & sex** – Although Cogan’s affects both sexes, a slight female preponderance and onset in adolescence are documented.  
* **Exclusion of other large‑vessel vasculitides** – The ocular‑auditory syndrome is essentially pathognomonic for Cogan’s and is not seen in Takayasu, GCA, or PAN.  

**Arguments Against Cogan’s**  

* **Absence of documented interstitial keratitis** – The corneal lesions are described as “very small ghost vessels” and nummular scars, which is less extensive than classic diffuse stromal haze. However, early treatment with steroids may have blunted the acute inflammatory phase, leaving only residual scarring.  
* **Lack of systemic constitutional symptoms** – Fever, weight loss, or malaise are not reported, but many patients present primarily with vascular complications.  

Overall, the **convergence of interstitial‑keratitis–type corneal changes, profound sensorineural hearing loss, and large‑vessel aneurysm/renal‑artery stenosis makes Cogan’s syndrome with systemic vasculitis the leading unifying diagnosis**.

## 2. Medium‑Vessel Vasculitis

### 2.1 Polyarteritis Nodosa (PAN)

**Pathophysiology & Typical Presentation**  
PAN is a necrotizing vasculitis of medium‑sized arteries that spares the pulmonary circulation. It classically presents with livedo reticularis, peripheral neuropathy, abdominal pain, hypertension secondary to renal‑artery involvement, and aneurysms of visceral arteries (mesenteric, renal, hepatic). Laboratory findings include elevated ESR/CRP, anemia, and leukocytosis. Hepatitis B infection is a frequent association.

**Arguments Supporting PAN**  

* **Renal‑artery stenosis with hypertension** – fits PAN’s renovascular component.  
* **Aneurysm formation** – PAN can cause saccular aneurysms of medium‑sized vessels; a thoraco‑abdominal aneurysm could theoretically arise from involvement of the aortic branches.  
* **Elevated inflammatory markers** – ESR and leukocytosis are compatible.  

**Arguments Against PAN**  

* **Absence of characteristic skin findings** – No livedo, nodules, or ulcerations.  
* **No peripheral neuropathy** – Neurologic exam is limited to deafness (inner‑ear) but no mononeuritis multiplex.  
* **Lack of visceral ischemic pain** – No abdominal or mesenteric ischemic symptoms.  
* **Ocular‑auditory syndrome** – Interstitial keratitis and sensorineural deafness are not features of PAN.  
* **Age and gender** – PAN can affect any age but peaks in middle age; a 17‑year‑old is less typical.  
* **Serology** – No mention of hepatitis B markers; syphilis test negative, but hepatitis B not tested.  

Given the missing cutaneous and neurologic hallmarks, and the presence of an ocular‑auditory syndrome that is foreign to PAN, this diagnosis is less plausible.

## 12. Therapeutic Implications

The diagnosis of **vasculitic Cogan’s syndrome** carries several therapeutic imperatives:

* **Aggressive immunosuppression** – High‑dose intravenous methylprednisolone pulses followed by oral taper, combined with cyclophosphamide or mycophenolate mofetil to achieve disease remission. Biologic agents (anti‑TNFα or rituximab) have been used in refractory cases.  
* **Control of hypertension** – Dual antihypertensive regimen (beta‑blocker, ACE‑inhibitor) and correction of hypokalemia (potassium supplementation, mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists). Renal‑artery stenosis may eventually require percutaneous angioplasty or surgical revascularization.  
* **Surgical management of aneurysm** – Given the size (> 6 cm) and progression, elective repair (open graft replacement or endovascular stent graft) is indicated to prevent rupture. Peri‑operative immunosuppression reduces the risk of anastomotic dehiscence.  
* **Audiologic rehabilitation** – Early cochlear implantation is often necessary for profound deafness; immunosuppression may halt further loss.  
* **Ophthalmologic care** – Topical steroids, cycloplegics, and careful monitoring for corneal neovascularization; surgical keratoplasty may be considered for visual rehabilitation if scarring progresses.
