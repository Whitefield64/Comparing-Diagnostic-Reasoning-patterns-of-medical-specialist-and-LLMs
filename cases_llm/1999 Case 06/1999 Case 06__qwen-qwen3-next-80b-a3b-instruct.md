# Case 6-1999 —A 17 1/2-Year-Old Girl with a Thoracoabdominal Aneurysm

## Presentation of Case



A 17 1/2-year-old girl was admitted to the hospital because of a thoracoabdominal aneurysm.



The patient had been born after a normal pregnancy and delivery, and her early growth, development, and health were normal. Four years before admission, she had begun to experience ocular irritation and a decrease in hearing, which progressed over a period of one month to almost total bilateral sensorineural deafness. She was initially treated with corticosteroids administered orally, but because of abdominal discomfort, the medication was subsequently injected. The ocular symptoms improved, but her hearing did not.



Seven months before admission, a thoracoabdominal aneurysm was detected, with repeated studies showing evidence of progressive enlargement to more than 6 cm in diameter and the development of stenosis of the left renal artery. One month before admission, a “hypertensive crisis” developed, and the patient was given atenolol, which provided inadequate control of the hypertension. She was referred to this hospital.



The patient was a native of Greece; she was unmarried and was not sexually active. She had a recent history of “migraine headaches” for which she took an unidentified oral agent. A nonidentical twin sister was well. There was no history of aphthous ulcers, skin problems, or arthritis. Her medications at the time of admission consisted of prednisone, atenolol (25 mg twice daily), and ranitidine.



The temperature was 37.2°C, the pulse was 84, and the respirations were 18. The blood pressure was 120/70 mm Hg.



On physical examination, the patient was slim and had mildly cushingoid features, with increased body hair; prominent striae on the arms, legs, and trunk; and a small “buffalo hump.” No oral ulcer, rash, or lymphadenopathy was evident. The neck was supple, and the lungs were clear. A grade-2-to-3 systolic ejection murmur and a grade 2 decrescendo diastolic murmur were heard at the base of the heart. No bony deformity of the sternum or spine was found. A faint epigastric bruit was heard, but no aneurysm was palpated, and there was no organomegaly. The arms and legs were normal. Pulses in the legs were strong. Neurologic examination revealed bilateral sensorineural deafness.



The urine was normal. The hematocrit was 32.1 percent, the white-cell count was 15,200 per cubic millimeter, the platelet count was 371,000 per cubic millimeter, the mean corpuscular volume was 62 μm , and the erythrocyte sedimentation rate was 62 mm per hour; the prothrombin and partial-thromboplastin times were normal. The sodium level was 141 mmol per liter, the potassium level was 2.7 mmol per liter, the chloride level was 99 mmol per liter, and the carbon dioxide level was 31.5 mmol per liter. The cholesterol level was 171 mg per deciliter (4.4 mmol per liter), and the triglyceride level was 69 mg per deciliter (0.8 mmol per liter). The urea nitrogen, creatinine, and glucose levels were normal. A serologic test for syphilis was negative. Radiographs of the chest showed a double contour projected over the left aspect of the heart, a finding consistent with the presence of an aneurysm. A cardiac ultrasonographic examination showed dilatation of the ascending aorta, with Doppler evidence of mild aortic regurgitation; the distal thoracic aorta was also dilated.



Ophthalmologic examination revealed intermittent left exotropia of up to 30 diopters and normal visual acuity. Corneal sensation was intact. The sclerae were blue. Slit-lamp examination revealed two very small “ghost” vessels in the superotemporal area of the right cornea; they were adjacent to nummular scars, 1 mm in diameter, located in the anterior stroma. The left cornea was normal. The anterior chambers, irises, and lenses were normal except for small axial posterior opacities beneath the capsules. The vitreous was clear, and examination of the dilated fundi showed normal findings.



A diagnostic procedure was performed.

## Differential Diagnosis

The clinical picture presented by this 17½-year-old girl is extraordinarily complex and demands a systematic, multidimensional differential diagnosis that integrates vascular, immunologic, metabolic, and developmental pathophysiology. At its core is a thoracoabdominal aneurysm of considerable size (>6 cm), progressive in nature, associated with renal artery stenosis, severe hypokalemia, systemic inflammation (elevated ESR, leukocytosis), sensorineural deafness, ocular abnormalities including blue sclerae and corneal scarring, cushingoid features, and a history of steroid-responsive ocular inflammation. The constellation of findings is not typical of any single common disease but rather suggests a rare, systemic disorder with multiorgan involvement, likely of genetic or autoimmune etiology. The absence of sexual activity, negative syphilis serology, and lack of classic signs of connective tissue disease such as arthritis or aphthous ulcers eliminate several common mimics, forcing us to consider more obscure, often underrecognized syndromes.

The most salient diagnostic puzzle lies in the convergence of three major features: (1) a large, rapidly expanding thoracoabdominal aortic aneurysm in a young female with no atherosclerotic risk factors; (2) bilateral sensorineural deafness; and (3) blue sclerae, corneal scarring, and cushingoid phenotype. These are not random associations. The presence of blue sclerae is classically associated with osteogenesis imperfecta (OI), but OI does not typically cause aortic aneurysms of this magnitude, nor does it cause sensorineural hearing loss or renal artery stenosis. Moreover, the corneal findings—nummular scars with ghost vessels—are highly suggestive of prior inflammation, possibly interstitial keratitis, which is not a feature of OI. The combination of deafness and ocular inflammation, particularly when steroid-responsive, raises the specter of autoimmune or autoinflammatory disorders. The hypokalemia, metabolic alkalosis (elevated CO2), and renal artery stenosis point to a renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) activation, most likely secondary to fibromuscular dysplasia (FMD) of the renal arteries. FMD is a well-documented cause of renovascular hypertension in young women, often presenting with focal stenoses and “beading” on angiography. However, FMD alone does not explain the systemic inflammation, deafness, or aortic root dilation.

The aortic aneurysm itself must be categorized by location and mechanism. The dilation extends from the ascending aorta through the thoracic aorta into the abdominal aorta, suggesting a continuous, diffuse process rather than a focal dissection or mycotic aneurysm. The presence of aortic regurgitation on echo supports root dilation, which in young patients is most commonly due to connective tissue disorders such as Marfan syndrome, Loeys-Dietz syndrome, or Ehlers-Danlos syndrome vascular type. Marfan syndrome is the most familiar candidate: tall stature, arachnodactyly, lens dislocation, and aortic root dilation are classic. But this patient lacks tall stature, arachnodactyly, or ectopia lentis. She has blue sclerae, which can occur in Marfan but are more typical of OI. Moreover, Marfan does not typically cause sensorineural deafness or corneal scarring. Loeys-Dietz syndrome, caused by mutations in TGF-β signaling genes (e.g., TGFBR1/2, SMAD3), presents with aggressive aortic aneurysms, hypertelorism, bifid uvula, and arterial tortuosity, but again, sensorineural deafness and corneal pathology are not features. The absence of skeletal deformities (e.g., pectus excavatum, scoliosis) further argues against classical Marfan.

The hypokalemia (2.7 mmol/L) and metabolic alkalosis (CO2 31.5) with renal artery stenosis strongly suggest a renovascular etiology of secondary hyperaldosteronism. This is not due to primary adrenal pathology, as cortisol levels are not reported, but the cushingoid features—striae, buffalo hump, hirsutism—are highly suggestive of exogenous glucocorticoid exposure. The patient was treated with oral corticosteroids for ocular inflammation and then switched to intramuscular injections due to abdominal discomfort, suggesting prolonged, high-dose steroid use. This explains the cushingoid phenotype and possibly the leukocytosis and elevated ESR (steroids can elevate ESR in some inflammatory states, though typically they suppress it). However, steroid use alone does not explain the aortic aneurysm or deafness. It is more likely that the steroids were administered to treat an underlying inflammatory process that was causing both the ocular symptoms and the vascular inflammation. This leads us to consider vasculitides.

The most compelling diagnosis in this context is **Takayasu arteritis (TA)**, a large-vessel vasculitis that predominantly affects young women, particularly of Asian or Mediterranean descent. The patient is Greek, and while TA is more common in Asia, it is well-documented in Mediterranean populations. TA typically presents with systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss, fatigue), hypertension due to renal artery stenosis, diminished or absent pulses, bruits over major vessels, and aortic regurgitation from root dilation. The thoracoabdominal aneurysm and renal artery stenosis are classic. The elevated ESR and leukocytosis are consistent with active inflammation. The sensorineural deafness is not a classic feature of TA, but there are case reports of cochlear involvement in large-vessel vasculitis, likely due to microvascular ischemia or immune-mediated inner ear disease. The blue sclerae and corneal findings are problematic: TA does not typically involve the cornea or sclera. However, the corneal “ghost vessels” and nummular scars are highly suggestive of prior interstitial keratitis, which is classically associated with syphilis—but syphilis serology is negative. Alternatively, these findings could represent a manifestation of **sarcoidosis** or **relapsing polychondritis**, both of which can involve the eye and the vasculature.

Sarcoidosis is an important consideration. It is a systemic granulomatous disease that can cause uveitis, interstitial keratitis, and even aortic root inflammation leading to aneurysm formation. The corneal scarring and ghost vessels are consistent with chronic interstitial keratitis. Sarcoidosis can also cause sensorineural deafness via cochlear granulomas or cranial nerve involvement. The elevated ESR and leukocytosis are consistent. The cushingoid features may be coincidental, but sarcoidosis can cause hypercalcemia and adrenal suppression with chronic steroid use, which may have been the reason for steroid administration. However, sarcoidosis does not typically cause hypokalemia or renal artery stenosis as a primary feature, and the aortic aneurysm, while reported in sarcoid, is exceedingly rare. Moreover, sarcoidosis usually presents with bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy, which is not mentioned here.

**Relapsing polychondritis (RP)** is another strong candidate. RP is a rare autoimmune disorder characterized by recurrent inflammation of cartilaginous structures: ears, nose, trachea, larynx, and occasionally the aortic root. Aortic root dilation and aneurysm formation are well-documented complications of RP, often leading to aortic regurgitation. The patient has aortic root dilation and regurgitation, which fits. RP can also cause sensorineural hearing loss due to inflammation of the cochlear cartilage or inner ear structures. The blue sclerae are not typical, but the corneal findings are intriguing: RP can cause keratitis, including peripheral ulcerative keratitis, and the “ghost vessels” may represent vascularization secondary to chronic inflammation. The patient’s ocular symptoms improved with steroids, which is classic for RP. The hypokalemia could be secondary to renal artery stenosis, which has been reported in RP due to vasculitis of the renal arteries. The cushingoid features again point to steroid therapy, which is often required to control RP. The absence of ear or nasal cartilage deformity (e.g., saddle nose, floppy ears) is notable, but these may be early or subtle in a teenager. The diagnosis of RP is often missed because it is rare and requires a high index of suspicion. The diagnostic criteria (McAdam’s criteria) include bilateral auricular chondritis, nasal chondritis, ocular inflammation, respiratory tract chondritis, cochlear or vestibular dysfunction, and histopathologic confirmation. While the patient lacks overt auricular or nasal involvement, the combination of aortic aneurysm, deafness, ocular inflammation, steroid responsiveness, and young female sex makes RP a leading contender.

Another rare but critical consideration is **Behçet’s disease**. Although the patient lacks oral or genital ulcers, which are hallmark features, Behçet’s can present with atypical forms, especially in young women. It can cause large-vessel vasculitis, including thoracic and abdominal aortic aneurysms, and has been associated with sensorineural hearing loss, uveitis, and retinal vasculitis. The corneal scarring could represent a healed keratitis. However, the absence of aphthous ulcers, skin lesions (erythema nodosum, pathergy), and genital ulcers makes Behçet’s less likely, though not impossible.

The hypokalemia is a critical clue. With a potassium of 2.7 mmol/L, we must consider not only secondary hyperaldosteronism from renal artery stenosis but also primary mineralocorticoid excess. Primary hyperaldosteronism (Conn’s syndrome) typically presents with hypertension and hypokalemia but not with aortic aneurysm or deafness. However, **apparent mineralocorticoid excess (AME)** due to 11β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase type 2 deficiency is a rare autosomal recessive disorder that causes severe hypertension, hypokalemia, and metabolic alkalosis in childhood. It is caused by mutations in the HSD11B2 gene, leading to cortisol-mediated activation of the mineralocorticoid receptor. AME is associated with low renin and aldosterone, which is not reported here. Moreover, AME does not cause aortic aneurysms, deafness, or ocular pathology. Another possibility is **Liddle syndrome**, a rare genetic disorder causing hypertension and hypokalemia due to gain-of-function mutations in the epithelial sodium channel (ENaC). Again, no extrarenal manifestations. The renal artery stenosis is more likely fibromuscular dysplasia, which is common in young women and can be associated with systemic vasculitis.

The corneal findings are perhaps the most enigmatic. The “ghost vessels” adjacent to nummular scars suggest healed interstitial keratitis. This is pathognomonic for syphilis, but the serology is negative. Could this be a false negative? Possibly, but unlikely given the patient’s lack of risk factors and the fact that syphilis rarely presents with isolated ocular disease without systemic signs. Alternatively, the keratitis could be due to **tuberculosis** or **leprosy**, but these are exceedingly rare in this demographic and would not explain the aortic aneurysm. Another possibility is **Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada (VKH) syndrome**, a multisystem autoimmune disorder affecting melanin-containing tissues: uveitis, meningoencephalitis, vitiligo, poliosis, and sensorineural deafness. VKH can cause bilateral uveitis and hearing loss, and in rare cases, vasculitis has been reported. However, VKH typically presents with bilateral panuveitis, not corneal scarring, and does not cause aortic aneurysms. The blue sclerae are not a feature of VKH.

The most parsimonious diagnosis, integrating all elements, is **relapsing polychondritis with superimposed fibromuscular dysplasia and steroid-induced cushingoid features**. The aortic aneurysm and regurgitation are due to inflammation of the aortic root and ascending aorta, a recognized complication of RP. The sensorineural deafness results from cochlear cartilage inflammation. The corneal scarring and ghost vessels reflect prior interstitial keratitis, which is a known ocular manifestation of RP, though less common than uveitis. The renal artery stenosis is likely due to vasculitis of the renal arteries, a documented but rare feature of RP. The hypokalemia and hypertension are secondary to renal artery stenosis. The cushingoid features are iatrogenic from prolonged steroid therapy, which was initiated for the ocular inflammation (likely episcleritis or keratitis). The elevated ESR and leukocytosis reflect ongoing systemic inflammation. The blue sclerae remain unexplained but may represent a coincidental connective tissue abnormality or a mild variant of a collagenopathy.

However, a final, critical consideration is **Marfan syndrome with an unusual phenotype** or a **novel genetic syndrome**. The patient’s blue sclerae, aortic root dilation, and lack of tall stature could represent a variant of Marfan or a related disorder such as **Shprintzen-Goldberg syndrome**, which combines Marfanoid features with craniosynostosis, intellectual disability, and aortic aneurysm—but again, deafness and corneal scarring are not features. The absence of skeletal findings makes this less likely.

In conclusion, the leading diagnosis is **relapsing polychondritis**, supported by the triad of aortic root aneurysm, sensorineural deafness, and steroid-responsive ocular inflammation with corneal scarring, in a young woman with systemic inflammation and renal artery stenosis. The fibromuscular dysplasia of the renal artery is likely part of the same vasculopathic process. The cushingoid features are secondary to therapy. The diagnosis requires confirmation with biopsy of cartilaginous tissue (e.g., nasal, auricular) or aortic wall, though this may be high-risk given the aneurysm. Alternative diagnoses include Takayasu arteritis with atypical ocular involvement or a rare collagenopathy with systemic vasculitis. Given the clinical trajectory, immunosuppressive therapy beyond steroids—such as methotrexate, azathioprine, or biologics like TNF-alpha inhibitors—should be considered to prevent further vascular deterioration. The patient’s prognosis depends on early recognition and aggressive immunomodulation.
