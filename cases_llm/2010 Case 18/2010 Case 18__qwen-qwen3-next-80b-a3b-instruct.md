# Case 18-2010 —A 7-Year-Old Boy with Elevated HIV RNA Levels despite Antiretroviral Medications

## Presentation of Case

## Differential Diagnosis

The clinical scenario presented involves a 7-year-old boy with documented HIV-1 infection who, despite being on antiretroviral therapy (ART) for over two years, continues to exhibit persistently elevated HIV RNA levels. This is a complex case that demands a rigorous, multi-layered differential diagnosis, integrating virologic, pharmacologic, immunologic, infectious, and psychosocial considerations. The central question is not merely why the viral load remains detectable, but why, despite apparent adherence, clinical improvement, and regimen changes, viral suppression has not been achieved. The differential must account for both common and uncommon causes of ART failure in pediatric HIV, particularly in a resource-limited setting with a history of prior tuberculosis (TB) and BCG vaccination, which introduces additional diagnostic and therapeutic complexities.

The most immediate consideration is **nonadherence to antiretroviral therapy**. Although the mother reports good adherence, and the patient is described as well-appearing and performing well in school, adherence in pediatric HIV is notoriously difficult to assess by self-report alone. Children depend entirely on caregivers for medication administration, and even subtle lapses—such as missed doses during travel, illness, or disruptions in routine—can lead to subtherapeutic drug levels. The fact that the mother had not previously received formal ART training suggests potential gaps in understanding of the necessity of strict daily dosing, especially with complex regimens. The initial regimen included ritonavir alone, later replaced with lopinavir–ritonavir, and zidovudine and lamivudine doses were increased—this indicates a prior regimen that may have been suboptimal in potency or pharmacokinetic profile for a child of this age and weight. The transition to lopinavir–ritonavir, a boosted protease inhibitor, is appropriate for salvage therapy, but if the prior regimen contained a nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitor (NRTI) backbone with low genetic barrier to resistance (e.g., lamivudine monotherapy or suboptimal dosing), resistance mutations may have already been selected before the switch. The persistence of detectable viremia despite this switch suggests either pre-existing resistance or ongoing suboptimal exposure.

This leads to the second major category: **drug resistance**. HIV-1, particularly in untreated or poorly suppressed populations, rapidly develops resistance mutations. In settings where antiretroviral access is limited, patients may have received suboptimal or incomplete regimens prior to formal HIV diagnosis, as appears to be the case here. The patient was diagnosed at age 5, after a severe pneumonia and presumed TB episode, suggesting prolonged undiagnosed infection. During that time, he may have been exposed to subtherapeutic ART or even monotherapy (e.g., single-dose nevirapine for prevention of mother-to-child transmission, if administered at birth, though not explicitly stated). The most common resistance mutations in children exposed to NRTIs are M184V (conferring high-level resistance to lamivudine and emtricitabine) and K70R or thymidine analog mutations (TAMs) conferring resistance to zidovudine. Given that lamivudine and zidovudine were part of his initial regimen and were later increased in dose, it is plausible that these mutations were already present and rendered the backbone ineffective. The fact that his viral load remained elevated after switching to a boosted protease inhibitor suggests that the protease inhibitor component may also be compromised—either due to pre-existing protease mutations (e.g., M46I, I54V, V82A) or, more likely, due to the presence of multiple resistance mutations across drug classes that reduce the overall efficacy of even potent regimens. Resistance testing, though not mentioned, would be critical here, and its absence in the case report is a significant diagnostic gap. Without it, we are left to infer resistance from clinical response.

A third, and often underappreciated, category is **pharmacokinetic issues**. Children, especially those with chronic illness and wasting, have altered drug metabolism and distribution. The patient’s weight and height are below the fifth percentile, indicating chronic malnutrition or HIV-associated wasting syndrome. This can profoundly affect the volume of distribution and clearance of antiretroviral drugs. Lopinavir–ritonavir, for instance, is highly protein-bound and has variable absorption in the setting of malabsorption or gastrointestinal inflammation. Chronic HIV infection can cause enteropathy, reducing intestinal absorption of oral medications. Additionally, the patient was previously treated with rifampin for presumed TB, which is a potent cytochrome P450 3A4 inducer. Although the antituberculosis therapy was stopped 12 months prior to this evaluation, the long-term effects of rifampin on hepatic enzyme induction may have persisted, particularly if the patient’s liver function was not fully restored. Even after discontinuation, the recovery of CYP3A4 activity can take weeks to months, and in a child with compromised nutritional status, this recovery may be delayed. This could have led to subtherapeutic levels of lopinavir–ritonavir during the early phase of the new regimen, allowing continued viral replication and selection of resistance. Furthermore, the transition from ritonavir alone to lopinavir–ritonavir may have been poorly timed or inadequately dosed for his body weight, especially if weight-based dosing was not precisely calculated. Pediatric dosing of antiretrovirals is weight-dependent and requires frequent recalibration as the child grows; if dosing was not adjusted appropriately after the initial regimen change, therapeutic failure could result.

Another critical consideration is **coinfection or immune activation driving persistent viremia**. The patient had a history of a supraclavicular abscess with histopathological findings of granulomas with caseating necrosis, interpreted as consistent with active tuberculosis, despite negative cultures and no organisms on microscopy. This is a classic diagnostic dilemma in HIV-infected children in high-TB-burden regions. The BCG vaccine, administered in infancy, can cause localized lymphadenitis and granulomatous inflammation that mimics TB, particularly in immunocompromised children. The granulomas described could represent BCG-lymphadenitis with secondary necrosis, rather than Mycobacterium tuberculosis. However, the fact that the abscess resolved after drainage and that the patient was treated with full-course antituberculosis therapy complicates this interpretation. If the granulomas were truly due to TB, then the patient may have had concurrent active TB disease during the period of ART initiation. TB is a potent immune activator and can increase HIV replication through cytokine-mediated pathways (e.g., TNF-alpha, IL-6). Even if TB was treated, the residual immune activation and lymphoid tissue inflammation may have sustained a pro-inflammatory milieu that supports low-level HIV replication. This phenomenon is well documented in HIV-TB coinfection, where viral load often remains elevated during and after TB treatment, even with effective ART, due to persistent antigenic stimulation. In this case, the absence of microbiological confirmation of TB raises the possibility that the patient was treated unnecessarily for TB, exposing him to rifampin and its enzyme-inducing effects without true benefit. Alternatively, if TB was truly present and inadequately treated, ongoing mycobacterial antigen presence could be fueling HIV replication.

The possibility of **another chronic infection** must also be considered. The patient had recurrent otitis media and pneumonia. While these are common in HIV-infected children due to impaired humoral and cellular immunity, they could also reflect coinfection with other pathogens that contribute to immune dysregulation. For example, chronic cytomegalovirus (CMV) infection, common in HIV-infected children with low CD4 counts, can cause systemic inflammation and is associated with increased HIV replication. Epstein-Barr virus (EBV), human herpesvirus 6 (HHV-6), or even chronic hepatitis B or C could be contributing to a state of chronic immune activation. Although the case does not report serologies or PCR for these agents, their presence would be plausible in a child with prolonged HIV infection and immune compromise. Chronic viral coinfections can act as reservoirs of immune stimulation, preventing full immune reconstitution and sustaining low-level HIV viremia despite ART.

Another less common but important consideration is **HIV reservoir dynamics and latency reversal failure**. In children, especially those infected perinatally, the viral reservoir is established very early and is highly stable. Even with effective ART, HIV persists in long-lived memory CD4+ T cells and other sanctuaries (e.g., central nervous system, lymphoid tissues). In this case, the patient was diagnosed at age 5, meaning he had been infected since birth and had over five years of uncontrolled replication before ART initiation. The size and diversity of the reservoir are likely substantial. While ART suppresses active replication, it does not eliminate the reservoir. However, this does not explain why the viral load remains detectable on ART—it should be undetectable if the regimen is fully suppressive. Therefore, persistent viremia here is more likely due to ongoing replication rather than intermittent release from latency. This distinction is crucial: if the virus is actively replicating, the problem is therapeutic failure; if it is only from reservoirs, then the viral load should be below detection limits on effective ART. The fact that viral load remains elevated suggests ongoing replication, not just blips from latency.

We must also consider **diagnostic misclassification**. Could this child have a different immunodeficiency? The presentation of recurrent infections, wasting, and failure to suppress HIV despite ART raises the possibility of a primary immunodeficiency, such as severe combined immunodeficiency (SCID) or a disorder of T-cell signaling (e.g., ZAP-70 deficiency, CD3 deficiency). However, the presence of positive HIV antibody tests, detectable HIV RNA, and a clinical course consistent with HIV infection strongly argues against this. Moreover, the patient responded to ART with clinical improvement (weight gain, resolution of lymphadenopathy), which is not typically seen in primary immunodeficiencies without specific therapy. The fact that his mother is also HIV-positive confirms vertical transmission, making a primary immunodeficiency highly unlikely.

Finally, **psychosocial and structural factors** cannot be ignored. The patient lives with extended family, and while the mother and grandmother are involved in care, the absence of the father and potential economic hardship in a resource-limited setting may lead to inconsistent access to food, transportation to clinic, or even medication storage (e.g., lack of refrigeration for some formulations, though not applicable to the drugs listed). The mother’s lack of prior ART training, though later addressed, may have led to initial misunderstandings about the importance of perfect adherence. The child’s unawareness of his HIV diagnosis may also be a barrier to future adherence as he matures. These factors, while not direct biological causes of virologic failure, are critical contributors to the persistence of suboptimal therapy.

In summary, the most likely explanation for persistent HIV viremia in this child is a combination of **pre-existing drug resistance** (likely to NRTIs, possibly to protease inhibitors) compounded by **suboptimal pharmacokinetics due to malnutrition and prior rifampin exposure**, with possible **persistent immune activation from unresolved or misdiagnosed mycobacterial infection** (BCG vs. TB). The regimen change to lopinavir–ritonavir was appropriate, but without resistance testing, it may have been a gamble. The case underscores the critical need for routine genotypic resistance testing in pediatric HIV patients with virologic failure, especially in high-burden settings. The next diagnostic step should be genotypic resistance testing of the HIV strain, measurement of drug levels (e.g., lopinavir trough concentration), and evaluation for other chronic infections (CMV, EBV) and nutritional status. If resistance is confirmed, a regimen based on integrase inhibitors (e.g., dolutegravir) or newer agents with high genetic barriers and activity against resistant strains would be indicated. If resistance is absent, optimizing adherence support, nutritional rehabilitation, and monitoring for drug interactions or absorption issues would be paramount. The granulomatous lesion remains enigmatic but likely represents BCG lymphadenitis rather than TB, given the negative cultures and the high prevalence of BCG complications in this setting. This case is a stark reminder that in pediatric HIV, virologic failure is rarely due to a single cause—it is a multifactorial failure of biology, pharmacology, and system.
