### 1. Amniotic Fluid Embolism (AFE)

#### Pathophysiology  
Amniotic fluid embolism, historically called “anaphylactoid syndrome of pregnancy,” is a rare but fulminant disorder in which **amniotic‑derived debris (fetal squamous cells, mucin, lanugo hair, vernix, meconium, and debris) enters the maternal pulmonary circulation**. The result is an abrupt rise in pulmonary vascular resistance, acute right‑ventricular (RV) overload, severe hypoxemia, and **cardiovascular collapse**. The patient’s **absence of prior cardiac disease** and the **lack of typical ECG changes** (if they had been present, they would likely have been noted) argue against a primary coronary event.

### Anaphylaxis  
Anaphylactic reactions produce **rapid hypotension** and **bronchospasm**, but are **usually accompanied by cutaneous signs** (urticaria, angioedema) and **tachycardia** due to catecholamine surge.  The **absence of any such clues** and the **delayed timing** make anaphylaxis **unlikely**.

### Mechanical Causes (Air Embolism, Tension Pneumothorax)  
These are **highly unlikely** given the operative details (no central line placement, no use of insufflation, no ventilation prior to arrest).  The **clinical signs** (e.g., unilateral breath sounds) were not reported, further reducing their plausibility.

### Endocrine/Metabolic Crises  
Severe hypoglycemia, hyperkalemia, or adrenal crisis would not produce the **combined respiratory and circulatory collapse** observed, nor would they manifest **bradycardia** to this degree.  Moreover, no predisposing history exists.

## Integrated Diagnostic Algorithm for the Present Situation  

1. **Immediate Recognition of PEA Arrest** → Initiate **high‑quality CPR** and **advanced cardiac life support (ACLS)**.  
2. **Rapid Assessment of Potential Etiologies**:  
   - **Obstetric** (hemorrhage, AFE)  
   - **Cardiovascular** (PE, MI, SCAD)  
   - **Anesthetic** (high spinal)  
   - **Allergic** (anaphylaxis)  
   - **Mechanical** (air embolism, tension pneumothorax)  
3. **Prioritize AFE** based on **temporal association with placental removal**, **bradycardic PEA**, **hypoxemia**, and **lack of overt bleeding**.  
4. **Concurrent Management**: while CPR continues, **activate massive transfusion protocol**, **administer vasopressors**, **consider fibrinolysis** only if PE is strongly suspected and AFE less likely (but risk of worsening DIC).  
5. **Diagnostic Confirmation (post‑resuscitation)**:  
   - **TEE** for RV dysfunction.  
   - **Coagulation panel** (fibrinogen, PT/PTT, D‑dimer).  
   - **Serum tryptase** (may be elevated in AFE).  
   - **Pulmonary CT angiography** (if patient stabilizes) to rule out PE.  
   - **Pathology**: examination of pulmonary microvasculature
