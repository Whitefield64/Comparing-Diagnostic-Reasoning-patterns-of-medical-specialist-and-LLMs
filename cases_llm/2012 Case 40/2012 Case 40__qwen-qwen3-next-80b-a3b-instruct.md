# Case 40-2012 —A 43-Year-Old Woman with Cardiorespiratory Arrest after a Cesarean Section

## Presentation of Case



   *Dr. Britta Panda* (Obstetrics and Gynecology): A 43-year-old woman (a multigravida) was admitted to the labor and delivery service of this hospital at 36.4 weeks of gestation because of vaginal bleeding.



The patient had had regular prenatal care at this hospital; routine prenatal screening tests were negative. She had had four previous uncomplicated pregnancies, with spontaneous vaginal deliveries, the most recent 14 years earlier. She had a history of obesity (prenatal body-mass index [the weight in kilograms divided by the square of the height in meters], 32.1) and migraine headaches; she had no known allergies. She did not smoke, drink alcohol, or use illicit drugs. Marginal placenta previa was seen on obstetrical ultrasonography, and two episodes of bleeding occurred, at 27.7 weeks and 32.7 weeks of gestation, which resolved after the patient was admitted for bed rest, hydration, and the administration of betamethasone. She was advised to maintain bed rest at home and continue prenatal visits; the medications on discharge were iron sulfate and prenatal vitamins. At a routine prenatal visit 8 days before this presentation (at 35.3 weeks of gestation), obstetrical ultrasonography revealed an anterior placenta with the edge covering the internal os, a finding consistent with placenta previa. Heavy vaginal bleeding developed on the day of admission; the patient came to this hospital and was admitted to labor and delivery.



On examination in the labor and delivery unit, the vital signs and oxygen saturation were normal; there was active vaginal bleeding. Plans were made for emergency cesarean delivery. The hematocrit was 30.9% (reference range in women who are not pregnant, 36.0 to 46.0), the hemoglobin level was 10.7 g per deciliter (reference range, 12.0 to 16.0), and the ABO blood type was O, Rh-positive, with negative antibody screening; the white-cell and platelet counts were normal. A spinal anesthetic was administered, and a cesarean section was performed, with delivery of a healthy boy; the 1-minute and 5-minute Apgar scores were 8 and 9, respectively. The placenta previa was removed. Twenty minutes after delivery, as the abdominal fascia was being closed, the patient's systolic blood pressure fell to 70 to 80 mm Hg, the pulse to 30 to 39 beats per minute, and oxygen saturation to 70 to 80%. The patient reported chest pain, and her lips became white; apnea developed rapidly thereafter, and she became unresponsive. Electrical activity was present on electrocardiography (ECG), but radial and carotid pulses were not palpable. The trachea was intubated, and cardiopulmonary resuscitation was begun, with closed chest compressions and the administration of pressors.



Diagnostic procedures were performed, and additional management decisions were made.

## Differential Diagnosis

The clinical scenario presented is one of acute, catastrophic cardiorespiratory arrest occurring in the immediate postpartum period following an uncomplicated cesarean delivery in a 43-year-old multigravida with marginal placenta previa. The abrupt onset of hypotension, bradycardia, hypoxemia, apnea, and pulseless electrical activity (PEA) within 20 minutes of delivery—amidst an otherwise uneventful surgical course and a healthy neonate—demands a highly focused and exhaustive differential diagnosis. This is not a case of delayed postpartum hemorrhage or anesthetic complication alone; rather, it represents a sudden, systemic collapse consistent with a physiologic catastrophe occurring at the intersection of obstetric physiology, coagulation, cardiovascular dynamics, and possibly embolic or metabolic derangements. The differential must encompass both common and rare but life-threatening entities that can manifest with PEA in the peripartum period, with particular attention to those with a temporal relationship to delivery, placental pathology, and the physiological stress of pregnancy.

The leading diagnostic considerations fall into four major categories: obstetric emergencies (particularly amniotic fluid embolism and postpartum hemorrhage with coagulopathy), cardiovascular catastrophes (including peripartum cardiomyopathy, pulmonary embolism, and myocardial infarction), systemic metabolic or toxic insults (such as eclampsia, pheochromocytoma, or anesthetic toxicity), and rare but devastating conditions like maternal sepsis or air embolism. Each must be weighed against the clinical timeline, the presence of placenta previa, and the absence of prior warning signs.

Amniotic fluid embolism (AFE) remains the most compelling and urgent consideration. AFE is a rare but often fatal syndrome characterized by the sudden onset of hypotension, hypoxemia, coagulopathy, and cardiovascular collapse during labor, delivery, or immediately postpartum. The classic triad—acute hypoxia, hypotension, and coagulopathy—is not always fully manifest, but the temporal proximity to delivery, the rapid progression to PEA, and the absence of prior cardiac disease make AFE highly plausible. The presence of placenta previa, with its associated abnormal placental architecture and potential for placental sinus rupture, creates a direct anatomical pathway for amniotic fluid, fetal squames, lanugo, or other debris to enter the maternal circulation via open venous sinuses in the placental bed. This is not merely theoretical: placenta previa and placental abruption are among the most frequently associated obstetric conditions with AFE. The abrupt onset of chest pain, cyanosis, and respiratory arrest followed by cardiovascular collapse aligns with the proposed pathophysiology: amniotic fluid components trigger a profound inflammatory cascade, pulmonary vasoconstriction, right heart failure, and systemic release of vasoactive mediators, leading to acute pulmonary hypertension, left ventricular dysfunction, and disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC). The absence of overt bleeding at the time of collapse does not exclude AFE; coagulopathy may be evolving but not yet clinically apparent on initial labs. The fact that the hematocrit and platelet count were normal at admission does not rule out rapid-onset DIC, which can develop within minutes. Furthermore, the ECG showing electrical activity without pulses is consistent with PEA, a hallmark of AFE. While AFE is a diagnosis of exclusion and lacks a definitive confirmatory test, its clinical presentation is so characteristic and time-sensitive that it must be treated immediately upon suspicion, as survival depends on rapid recognition and aggressive supportive care.

Postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) must also be considered, though the mechanism here is more nuanced. While the patient had significant antepartum bleeding due to placenta previa, the cesarean delivery was performed, the placenta was removed, and there was no immediate postoperative hemorrhage noted before the collapse. However, delayed or concealed hemorrhage remains possible. Placenta previa is strongly associated with placenta accreta spectrum disorders (PAS), particularly in women with prior cesarean deliveries and advanced maternal age. Though this patient had four prior vaginal deliveries, the risk of accreta increases with each prior cesarean, but even without prior uterine surgery, the presence of placenta previa in a 43-year-old gravid woman confers a nontrivial risk of abnormal placental invasion. If the placenta was abnormally adherent, incomplete removal or uterine atony due to overdistension or prolonged labor could lead to concealed retroplacental hemorrhage or uterine rupture with intraperitoneal bleeding. Such bleeding may be slow, not immediately apparent at the surgical field, and masked by the effects of spinal anesthesia and volume resuscitation. The hypotension and bradycardia could reflect profound hypovolemia with compensatory vagal activation, leading to reflex bradycardia—a phenomenon well-documented in hypovolemic shock, particularly in pregnant women due to heightened vagal tone. The sudden drop in oxygen saturation could then reflect poor perfusion and tissue hypoxia rather than primary respiratory failure. The absence of visible bleeding does not exclude this; the bleeding may have been retroperitoneal or into the broad ligament. A ruptured uterine artery aneurysm or other vascular injury during placental removal is also possible, though exceedingly rare. The key differentiator from AFE is that PPH typically presents with more gradual hemodynamic deterioration, and the bradycardia is less common unless vagally mediated. However, in the context of massive hemorrhage, profound acidosis, and hypothermia, PEA with bradycardia can occur. The fact that the patient had a hematocrit of 30.9% preoperatively suggests significant preexisting anemia, and a further acute drop could have precipitated cardiovascular decompensation.

Pulmonary embolism (PE) is another critical consideration. Pregnancy is a hypercoagulable state, and the risk of venous thromboembolism (VTE) is highest in the peripartum period. The patient’s obesity, advanced maternal age, and recent surgery all increase her risk. The sudden onset of hypoxemia, hypotension, chest pain, and PEA is classic for massive PE. However, the absence of prior dyspnea, leg swelling, or risk factors such as prolonged immobility makes this less likely, though not impossible. The placenta previa itself does not predispose to PE, but the cesarean delivery and postoperative immobilization do. The key distinguishing feature from AFE is the more common presence of tachycardia in PE, whereas this patient developed bradycardia—a feature more typical of advanced shock, right heart failure, or vagal overstimulation. In massive PE, bradycardia can occur as a terminal event due to profound hypoxia and acidosis, but it is not the typical initial rhythm. ECG findings in PE often show S1Q3T3, right axis deviation, or atrial arrhythmias, but these were not described. The rapid progression to apnea and arrest without prior respiratory distress is more suggestive of AFE than PE. Nevertheless, PE cannot be excluded without imaging or autopsy, and it remains a leading contender.

Peripartum cardiomyopathy (PPCM) is another possibility, though less likely given the abruptness of the event. PPCM typically presents with progressive dyspnea, fatigue, and signs of heart failure in the last month of pregnancy or within months postpartum. The patient had no prior symptoms of heart failure, no history of hypertension or preeclampsia, and no evidence of cardiac enlargement or edema. Her collapse was immediate and catastrophic, not progressive. While PPCM can rarely present with sudden arrhythmia or cardiac arrest, the absence of any prodromal symptoms and the timing immediately post-delivery make this less likely. However, the possibility of undiagnosed occult cardiomyopathy cannot be entirely dismissed, particularly in a 43-year-old woman with multiple pregnancies and obesity.

Anesthetic complications must be considered, though the use of spinal anesthesia makes many of the classic risks less likely. Spinal anesthesia can cause hypotension due to sympathetic blockade, but this is typically gradual, responsive to fluids and vasopressors, and not associated with apnea or bradycardia of this magnitude unless there is total spinal blockade—which would present with high sensory level, respiratory arrest, and profound hypotension from the outset. The patient was stable during surgery and only deteriorated during fascial closure, suggesting the anesthetic effect had waned. Local anesthetic toxicity is unlikely, as the dose would have been calculated for spinal anesthesia, and symptoms would have occurred earlier. Epinephrine toxicity from inadvertent intravascular injection is also improbable, as this would cause tachycardia and hypertension, not bradycardia and hypotension. The administration of pressors was therapeutic, not diagnostic, and does not point to a specific etiology.

Eclampsia or preeclampsia is unlikely given the absence of hypertension, proteinuria, or neurological symptoms prior to collapse. The patient had no signs of severe preeclampsia, and her blood pressure was normal on admission. The sudden chest pain and arrest are not typical of eclampsia, which usually presents with seizures, headache, or visual disturbances. However, atypical presentations of preeclampsia can occur, including pulmonary edema or HELLP syndrome, but again, the timeline and lack of prior symptoms make this improbable.

Maternal sepsis is another consideration, though the absence of fever, chills, or leukocytosis (the white count was normal) makes this unlikely as the primary cause. Sepsis-induced cardiac arrest typically has a more insidious onset, with fever, tachycardia, and hypotension developing over hours to days. The rapidity of collapse here argues against sepsis as the initial trigger, though secondary sepsis from a hemorrhagic or infectious source cannot be ruled out.

Other rare but critical diagnoses include air embolism, which can occur during cesarean delivery, particularly in the setting of placental disruption and venous sinus exposure. Air can enter the maternal circulation through open uterine veins, especially in the Trendelenburg position or with positive pressure ventilation. Air embolism typically presents with sudden hypotension, hypoxemia, and a “mill-wheel” murmur, and can cause PEA. The timing—during fascial closure, when the uterus was manipulated and the surgical field exposed—makes this plausible. However, air embolism is usually associated with more dramatic, immediate desaturation and is often witnessed by the surgical team as a sudden drop in end-tidal CO2. The absence of such details makes this less certain but still possible.

Metabolic causes such as hypoglycemia or severe electrolyte imbalance are unlikely given the patient’s normal preoperative labs and lack of prolonged fasting or insulin use. Similarly, anaphylaxis to medications (e.g., antibiotics, neuromuscular blockers) would typically present with urticaria, bronchospasm, or angioedema, none of which were described. The patient had no known allergies.

Finally, a rare but devastating possibility is a ruptured abdominal aortic aneurysm (AAA) or uterine artery rupture. Although AAA in pregnancy is exceedingly rare, the hyperdynamic circulation, increased cardiac output, and hormonal weakening of connective tissue can predispose to rupture, particularly in women with preexisting vascular disease. Advanced maternal age and obesity are risk factors. The sudden collapse with hypotension and PEA could be explained by massive intraperitoneal hemorrhage from a ruptured vessel. However, the absence of abdominal tenderness, back pain, or prior history of vascular disease makes this less likely, though not impossible.

In summary, the differential diagnosis centers on three primary entities: amniotic fluid embolism, massive postpartum hemorrhage (particularly from placenta accreta or uterine atony), and massive pulmonary embolism. Among these, amniotic fluid embolism is the most compelling given the classic triad of sudden hypoxia, hypotension, and coagulopathy occurring immediately postpartum in a patient with placenta previa—a known risk factor. The bradycardia is a late, terminal finding consistent with profound hypoxia and acidosis, not an initial feature, and does not rule out AFE. The rapid progression to PEA without prior warning is hallmark. Postpartum hemorrhage remains a strong alternative, particularly if there was occult bleeding from an adherent placenta or uterine rupture. Pulmonary embolism, while common in pregnancy, is less likely due to the absence of typical risk factors and the presence of bradycardia rather than tachycardia.

Given the clinical context, the leading diagnosis is amniotic fluid embolism, with placenta previa as the probable inciting factor. Postpartum hemorrhage due to placenta accreta spectrum disorder is a close second, particularly in a woman of advanced maternal age with prior pregnancies and placenta previa. The diagnosis of AFE carries profound implications for counseling, future pregnancies, and potential systemic inflammatory sequelae. Immediate management must be aggressive, multidisciplinary, and supportive, as survival depends on rapid recognition and intervention. The absence of confirmatory biomarkers does not diminish the clinical likelihood; in obstetric emergencies, time is tissue, and treatment must be initiated on clinical suspicion alone. This case underscores the critical importance of maintaining a high index of suspicion for AFE in any peripartum woman who suffers sudden cardiovascular collapse, regardless of the apparent stability of the surgical procedure or the health of the neonate.
