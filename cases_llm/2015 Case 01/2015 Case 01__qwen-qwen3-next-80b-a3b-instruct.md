# Case 1-2015 —A 66-Year-Old Woman with Metastatic Breast Cancer after Endocrine Therapy

## Presentation of Case



   *Dr. Michelle C. Specht:* A 66-year-old woman with a history of breast cancer was seen in the outpatient cancer center of this hospital 7 months after completion of adjuvant endocrine therapy because of changes seen on routine breast imaging.



The patient had been well until 6 years before the current evaluation, when she noted a mass in the superolateral quadrant of the left breast.



   *Dr. Elizabeth A. Rafferty:* Mammography that was performed at the time of the initial presentation, 6 years previously, revealed extremely dense breast tissue but was otherwise normal. Targeted ultrasound evaluation of the palpable mass in the left breast revealed an irregular, hypoechoic mass, measuring 2.0 cm by 1.8 cm by 0.9 cm, which was consistent with cancer. Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) revealed a lobulated, enhancing mass in the central superior left breast that measured 2.5 cm by 2.3 cm and had irregular margins, which corresponded to the ultrasound finding and was consistent with cancer. No additional suspicious findings were seen in the ipsilateral or contralateral breast. An ultrasound-guided core biopsy of the mass was performed.



   *Dr. Melinda F. Lerwill*  *:* Pathological examination of the core-biopsy specimen revealed invasive carcinoma. A lumpectomy of the left breast, with sentinel-lymph-node biopsy and subsequent axillary-lymph-node dissection, was performed. Examination of the lumpectomy specimen revealed a tannish-pink irregular mass, measuring 2.5 cm by 2.5 cm by 2 cm; on microscopic examination, the mass was composed of irregular nests of malignant epithelial cells infiltrating the stroma. The tumor cells contained enlarged, atypical nuclei with distinct nucleoli and showed a moderate degree of mitotic activity. The histologic features were those of a moderately differentiated, invasive ductal carcinoma of the breast (grade 2 of 3), with associated ductal carcinoma in situ (grade 2 to 3 of 3). There was extensive lymphatic invasion in the surrounding tissue. One axillary sentinel lymph node had evidence of macrometastatic disease with extracapsular tumor extension, but an additional 10 axillary lymph nodes did not have evidence of cancer. The breast tumor was positive for estrogen receptor (ER), less than 1% positive for progesterone receptor (PR), and negative for human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2) overexpression. The hormone-receptor (HR) status was consistent with a luminal B genetic subtype of breast carcinoma. Invasive carcinoma or ductal carcinoma in situ was located less than 0.2 cm from several margins. Reexcision was performed, and examination of the specimen revealed multiple foci of lymphatic invasion, with possible foci of stromal invasion located less than 0.05 cm from the surgical margin in the left lateral breast.



A diagnosis of infiltrating ductal carcinoma of the left breast was made. The disease was ER-positive, minimally PR-positive, and HER2-negative and had a clinical stage of T2–N1–M0 (according to the tumor–node–metastasis staging system of the American Joint Committee on Cancer).



   *Dr. Specht:* A combination of doxorubicin and cyclophosphamide (AC chemotherapy) was administered for four cycles, followed by paclitaxel for four cycles, and the patient received radiation therapy (a total of 6000 cGy over a period of 5 weeks) to the left breast and supraclavicular region. Eight months after the diagnosis was made, anastrozole therapy was begun, along with routine serial surveillance with digital mammography (later in combination with tomosynthesis, or three-dimensional mammography), alternating every 6 months with MRI of the breasts. After 5 years, anastrozole therapy was stopped.

   *Dr. Specht:* A complete blood count was normal, as were blood levels of electrolytes, calcium, glucose, total protein, albumin, globulin, and cancer antigen 15-3 and results of renal- and liver-function tests. The patient was referred to the cancer clinic of this hospital.

## Differential Diagnosis

The clinical scenario presented involves a 66-year-old woman with a prior history of ER-positive, PR-low, HER2-negative, moderately differentiated invasive ductal carcinoma of the left breast, treated with lumpectomy, sentinel lymph node biopsy with axillary dissection, adjuvant chemotherapy (AC followed by paclitaxel), radiation therapy, and five years of adjuvant anastrozole. She is now presenting with newly identified internal mammary lymph nodes (IMLNs) on MRI, along with multiple small pulmonary nodules on CT, in the absence of skeletal metastases or other visceral involvement. The cytology from a CT-guided fine-needle aspiration of one of the IMLNs shows malignant epithelioid cells with nuclear atypia consistent with her prior breast cancer, but the possibility of metastatic melanoma cannot be excluded due to insufficient tissue for ancillary studies. The key diagnostic challenge lies in determining the origin of this new malignant process: is it a recurrence of her breast carcinoma, a second primary malignancy (most plausibly melanoma), or a rare alternative?

The most compelling and statistically likely diagnosis is **metastatic recurrence of luminal B-type breast carcinoma**. This conclusion is supported by multiple converging lines of evidence. First, the histologic and cytologic features of the IMLN aspirate are described as “similar to those of the patient’s prior breast carcinoma,” which is a highly specific observation. Invasive ductal carcinoma of the breast, particularly of the luminal B subtype, has a well-documented propensity for late recurrence—often beyond five years after initial diagnosis, even in the setting of endocrine therapy. The median time to recurrence for ER-positive breast cancer is approximately 5–7 years, with a persistent risk extending beyond 15 years. This patient’s recurrence occurred precisely at the 5-year mark after cessation of anastrozole, a pattern consistent with the known biology of hormone receptor–positive disease, which often remains dormant in micrometastatic niches and is reactivated upon withdrawal of endocrine suppression. The internal mammary chain is a recognized, though less commonly emphasized, site of nodal spread in breast cancer, particularly in centrally located or large tumors with lymphatic invasion, as was present in this patient’s original pathology. The fact that the internal mammary nodes were not detected on initial staging (T2N1M0) but have now appeared 5 years after treatment is not unexpected; the internal mammary chain is not routinely assessed by standard staging modalities such as CT or bone scan, and its involvement may represent progression of subclinical disease or late lymphatic seeding.

The presence of multiple small pulmonary nodules (4–5 mm) further supports this diagnosis. While these are indeterminate and may represent benign granulomas, hamartomas, or post-radiation changes, their appearance in a patient with prior breast cancer raises strong suspicion for metastatic disease. Breast cancer, particularly ER-positive subtypes, commonly metastasizes to the lungs, often as small, multiple, well-circumscribed nodules, sometimes with a predilection for the lower lobes—though this distribution is not pathognomonic. The absence of pleural effusion, lymphangitic spread, or large masses makes a more aggressive, rapidly proliferating process less likely, which aligns with the indolent biology of luminal B cancer. The fact that the bone scan is negative further supports a pattern of metastatic spread consistent with breast cancer, which typically involves bone, liver, lung, and lymph nodes in descending order of frequency, but in this case, bone involvement has been excluded. The liver findings—benign cysts—are not suspicious for metastasis, which would more likely present as hypervascular or hypodense lesions with irregular borders, not simple cysts. The normal tumor markers (CA 15-3) are not reassuring in isolation; they are frequently normal in early or low-volume metastatic disease, especially in ER-positive cancers, which may not secrete large amounts of tumor-associated antigens.

The second major diagnostic consideration is **metastatic melanoma**. This is a critical alternative because of the patient’s significant prior history of cutaneous melanoma: a stage IA melanoma (0.46 mm) resected three years ago, followed by melanoma in situ on the calf one year ago, and multiple non-melanoma skin cancers. Melanoma is notorious for its unpredictable, often late, and bizarre patterns of metastasis. It can recur years after seemingly complete resection, even in the absence of high-risk features such as ulceration or high Breslow thickness. The internal mammary lymph nodes, while unusual for melanoma, are not impossible; melanoma can metastasize to any lymphatic basin, including the internal mammary chain, particularly if the primary lesion was on the ipsilateral upper extremity or chest wall. The cytologic description of “clusters of malignant cells with enlarged, hyperchromatic nuclei” is non-specific and could easily represent either melanoma or carcinoma. Melanoma cells, especially in poorly differentiated or spindle-cell variants, can mimic epithelial carcinomas on cytology, lacking obvious melanin pigment and exhibiting epithelioid morphology. The fact that the biopsy sample was insufficient for immunohistochemical staining (e.g., S-100, HMB-45, Melan-A, MITF) leaves this diagnostic possibility open and clinically significant. The absence of cutaneous lesions on examination does not rule out melanoma recurrence—metastatic melanoma may present with nodal or visceral disease without visible skin lesions, particularly if the primary was completely excised and the patient is under regular dermatologic surveillance.

However, several factors argue against melanoma as the primary diagnosis. First, the original melanoma was very thin (0.46 mm) with no ulceration, placing it in a very low-risk category for metastasis (<1% risk at 10 years). The subsequent melanoma in situ carries no metastatic potential. While multiple non-melanoma skin cancers suggest chronic UV exposure and possibly a genetic predisposition to skin neoplasia (e.g., xeroderma pigmentosum, though not suggested here), they do not imply a high risk for melanoma metastasis. Second, the cytologic resemblance to the prior breast carcinoma is a powerful clue. Melanoma and breast carcinoma, while both capable of epithelioid morphology, have distinct nuclear features. Breast carcinoma typically shows more uniform, round-to-oval nuclei with distinct nucleoli and less pleomorphism than the often bizarre, multinucleated, or pleomorphic nuclei seen in melanoma. The description of “enlarged, hyperchromatic nuclei” without mention of nuclear pleomorphism, multinucleation, or cytoplasmic pigmentation is more typical of carcinoma. Third, the clinical course—five years after cessation of endocrine therapy—is far more typical of breast cancer recurrence than melanoma. Melanoma recurrences, when they occur, are usually within the first 2–3 years after resection, with a steep decline in risk thereafter. A recurrence at the 5-year mark is unusual and would raise suspicion for a different primary unless the original lesion had been misstaged or had occult high-risk features not documented here. The absence of any new skin lesions, ocular or mucosal symptoms, or neurological findings further reduces the likelihood of melanoma.

A third, less likely but still important, consideration is **a second primary carcinoma**. This could include a new primary breast cancer in the contralateral breast or a carcinoma arising in another organ with metastatic spread to the IMLNs and lungs. However, bilateral breast MRI showed no abnormality in the contralateral breast, and the IMLNs are on the same side as the original tumor, making a new ipsilateral primary breast cancer highly unlikely without a new parenchymal mass. Moreover, the cytologic similarity to the prior breast cancer strongly argues against a new primary, as different histologic subtypes (e.g., invasive lobular, metaplastic, or mucinous carcinoma) would have different cytologic appearances. A new lung primary is also improbable: the pulmonary nodules are too small and too numerous to represent a single primary lung cancer, and the histology from the IMLN is not consistent with lung adenocarcinoma or squamous cell carcinoma, which would typically show different nuclear features, keratinization, or mucin production. A gastrointestinal or ovarian primary is implausible given the lack of symptoms, normal abdominal imaging (benign cysts only), and the anatomical proximity of the IMLNs to the original breast tumor. The internal mammary nodes drain the central and medial breast, the upper abdominal wall, and the anterior mediastinum—but they are not a common site for metastasis from gastrointestinal or gynecologic malignancies, which typically spread to peritoneal, pelvic, or para-aortic nodes.

Another rare but important consideration is **lymphoma**, particularly indolent B-cell lymphomas such as follicular lymphoma or marginal zone lymphoma, which can present with isolated lymphadenopathy and mimic carcinoma on cytology. Lymphoma can involve the internal mammary chain, and cytologically, lymphoma cells may appear as monotonous populations with hyperchromatic nuclei, potentially mimicking carcinoma. However, lymphoma typically presents with multiple lymph node groups (e.g., cervical, axillary, mediastinal), and the absence of B symptoms (fever, night sweats, weight loss), normal CBC, and lack of splenomegaly or hepatomegaly make this less likely. Moreover, lymphoma would not typically present with pulmonary nodules unless in advanced stage, and the cytologic description of “clusters” rather than “sheets” or “diffuse infiltration” is more suggestive of epithelial carcinoma than lymphoid neoplasm. Immunohistochemistry would be diagnostic, but the sample was insufficient.

**Sarcoma** is an exceedingly unlikely possibility. While sarcomas can metastasize to lung and lymph nodes, they rarely present with epithelioid cytology. The original tumor was a carcinoma, and sarcomas arising de novo in the breast or IMLNs are vanishingly rare. The cytologic features described are not consistent with spindle-cell or pleomorphic sarcoma.

Inflammatory or infectious causes of lymphadenopathy, such as tuberculosis, fungal infections, or sarcoidosis, must be considered but are improbable. The patient has no fever, weight loss, cough, or systemic symptoms. The pulmonary nodules are too small and too numerous to suggest granulomatous disease, which typically presents with bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy and upper lobe predominance. Sarcoidosis rarely involves the internal mammary chain, and tuberculosis would be unlikely in a patient with no risk factors (no travel, no exposure, no immunosuppression). The absence of elevated ACE, normal calcium, and lack of hilar lymphadenopathy on CT further argue against sarcoidosis.

Finally, **benign causes** of internal mammary lymphadenopathy, such as reactive lymphoid hyperplasia or post-radiation fibrosis with lymphoid aggregates, must be considered. However, the cytology revealed malignant cells, not benign lymphocytes or histiocytes. The description of “clusters of malignant cells with enlarged, hyperchromatic nuclei” is unequivocally neoplastic and not consistent with benign reactive changes.

In summary, the leading diagnosis is **metastatic recurrence of luminal B breast carcinoma**, supported by the timing of recurrence after endocrine therapy withdrawal, the cytologic similarity to the original tumor, the anatomical location of the IMLNs as a known drainage site, and the presence of indeterminate pulmonary nodules consistent with low-volume metastatic spread. The possibility of **metastatic melanoma** remains a critical differential due to the patient’s history, but is less likely given the low-risk profile of her prior melanomas, the unusual timing of recurrence, and the cytologic resemblance to her prior breast cancer. The insufficient tissue for immunohistochemistry is the critical diagnostic gap. The next step must be to obtain additional tissue—either from another IMLN or a pulmonary nodule via biopsy or resection—to perform a panel of immunohistochemical stains: ER, PR, GATA3, mammaglobin, GCDFP-15, and TTF-1 for breast origin; and S-100, HMB-45, Melan-A, and MITF for melanoma. If breast markers are positive and melanoma markers negative, the diagnosis of breast cancer recurrence is confirmed. If melanoma markers are positive and breast markers negative, the diagnosis shifts to metastatic melanoma, which would require a complete dermatologic and ophthalmologic evaluation, and potentially molecular testing for BRAF, NRAS, or KIT mutations. The therapeutic implications are profound: endocrine therapy may still be effective in recurrent ER-positive breast cancer, whereas melanoma would require immunotherapy or targeted therapy. The presence of multiple pulmonary nodules suggests systemic disease, and the absence of bone metastases suggests a relatively indolent course, making systemic therapy the mainstay of management regardless of origin. This case underscores the necessity of adequate tissue for ancillary studies in patients with prior malignancies, particularly when the clinical picture is ambiguous.
