# Case 16-1992 —A 27-Year-Old Woman with Pain in the Neck and Shoulder and Clumsiness of the Hand

## Presentation of Case



A 27-year-old woman was admitted to the hospital because of cervical pain that radiated to the left shoulder.



The patient was well until six weeks earlier, when she fell while skiing and landed on her back; she experienced no symptoms thereafter except for stiffness of her shoulders on the following day. Five days before entry she was riding in a truck that stopped suddenly, and her occipital region struck the rear window. On the next day she awoke with pain in the neck that radiated to the left shoulder, accompanied by paresthesias in the left hand. Two days before admission she came to the Emergency Ward, where only local tenderness and muscle spasm were found, without motor or sensory abnormalities. X-ray films of the cervical spine, performed in flexion and extension, showed very limited flexion and no extension; no subluxation was found. A rigid cervical collar was prescribed, and bed rest was advised. One day before entry a magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) scan of the cervical spine revealed an abnormal signal in the superoposterior portion of the fifth cervical vertebra, with involvement of the adjacent portion of the C4–5 intervertebral disk space; the anterior half of the disk space had a normal signal. On the following day the scan became available to the physician who had examined the patient. When contacted by telephone, the patient reported that the pain in her neck and shoulder was more severe and that her left hand was clumsy.



The patient was a surgical assistant. There was a history of a paronychia of the right first toe and infected cuticles on several fingers of several months' duration. There was no history of use of tobacco, excess alcohol ingestion, or illicit drugs; the patient denied bladder or bowel dysfunction, muscle weakness or gait disorder, fever, chills, night sweats, or fatigue.



The temperature was 37°C, the pulse was 90, and the respirations were 18. The blood pressure was 100/70 mm Hg.



On examination the patient was in pain while wearing a cervical collar. The head and neck were normal except that paraspinal muscle spasm was present and cervical flexion provoked pain that radiated to the left shoulder and upper arm; no notable tenderness was found. The lungs, heart, breasts, and abdomen were normal. There was a healing paronychia of the right first toe; no peripheral edema, clubbing, or cyanosis was detected. On neurologic examination the patient's speech was fluent, and her cranial-nerve functions were intact. Strength was graded 5/5 except that the intrinsic muscles of the left hand were 5—/5. Her sensation of pinprick, touch, position, and vibration was preserved. The deep tendon reflexes were ++ and symmetric; tapping the left biceps tendon provoked flexion of the left fingers; no clonus was present; the plantar responses were flexor; no crossed adductor response was elicited.



The urine was normal. The hematocrit was 36.9 percent; the white-cell count was 3900 (3.9×109 per liter), with 55 percent neutrophils, 34 percent lymphocytes, 8 percent monocytes, 2 percent eosinophils, and 1 percent basophils. The platelet count was normal, and the erythrocyte sedimentation rate 50 mm per hour. X-ray films of the chest were normal. A CT scan of the cervical spine, performed before and after the intravenous injection of contrast material, revealed an osteolytic lesion at the superior end plate and upper posterior body of the fifth cervical vertebra, with an associated soft-tissue mass that protruded posteriorly into the neural canal; no fracture or malalignment was detected.



Codeine, dexamethasone, and cimetidine were given, and use of the cervical collar was continued. The patient remained afebrile.



A diagnostic procedure was performed.

The constellation of findings includes both mechanical and neurologic components, layered over a background of recent trauma and a chronic, low-grade infectious process (recurrent paronychia and cuticle infections). The absence of fever, systemic symptoms, or overt signs of infection, combined with an elevated erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) of 50 mm/h, points toward an inflammatory or neoplastic process rather than acute bacterial infection. The MRI and CT findings are critical: an osteolytic lesion involving the superior endplate and posterior body of C5, with a soft-tissue mass encroaching on the spinal canal, raises immediate concern for a space-occupying lesion with destructive bone involvement. The fact that the anterior half of the C4–5 disk space has normal signal on MRI suggests the pathology is primarily osseous and posteriorly directed, not discogenic. The absence of subluxation on flexion-extension radiographs and no fracture on CT rules out acute traumatic instability, but does not exclude underlying bone pathology that may have been exacerbated by trauma.

The leading diagnostic considerations must be grouped into four major categories: infectious, neoplastic, inflammatory/autoimmune, and rare degenerative or vascular etiologies. Each must be weighed against the clinical, laboratory, and imaging data.

**Infectious Causes**

Infectious etiologies are compelling given the patient’s history of chronic, recurrent soft-tissue infections—paronychia of the right first toe and infected cuticles on multiple fingers. While these may seem like trivial, localized events, they suggest a possible underlying predisposition to infection, such as impaired local immunity, repeated microtrauma, or even a subtle immunodeficiency. More importantly, chronic or subacute osteomyelitis must be considered. The vertebral body is a known site for hematogenous spread of infection, particularly in young adults with a history of skin or nail infections. *Staphylococcus aureus* is the most common pathogen in vertebral osteomyelitis, and it is frequently associated with skin and soft-tissue infections such as paronychia. The organism can seed the vertebrae via transient bacteremia, particularly in individuals with repeated microtrauma to the digits. The insidious onset over weeks, absence of fever, and elevated ESR are classic for subacute vertebral osteomyelitis. The osteolytic appearance on CT, with adjacent soft-tissue extension, is consistent with the destructive nature of staphylococcal infection. The preservation of the anterior disk space signal on MRI is also typical—osteomyelitis usually spares the disk initially, as it is avascular, and the infection spreads along the vascular endplates. The posterior soft-tissue mass may represent a paravertebral abscess or granulation tissue. The fact that the patient had no systemic symptoms does not exclude infection; in fact, many cases of vertebral osteomyelitis present with pain as the sole symptom for weeks to months before systemic signs emerge. The neutrophil count is low-normal, and lymphocytes are elevated, which may reflect a chronic rather than acute infectious process. The ESR is markedly elevated, which is more consistent with chronic inflammation than with a viral or non-inflammatory etiology. The possibility of *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* must also be considered, particularly in regions where TB is endemic, though the patient’s lack of systemic symptoms, normal chest X-ray, and absence of weight loss or night sweats make this less likely. However, spinal TB (Pott’s disease) can present with isolated vertebral destruction and paraspinal abscess formation without systemic signs. The osteolytic lesion with relative sparing of the disk space and posterior extension would be consistent with TB. Fungal infections (e.g., *Candida*, *Aspergillus*) are exceedingly rare in immunocompetent hosts and would typically occur in the setting of prolonged antibiotic use, IV drug use, or immunosuppression—none of which are present here. The chronicity of the paronychia raises the question of whether the patient may have an underlying immunodeficiency, such as chronic granulomatous disease (CGD), which predisposes to recurrent staphylococcal skin and soft-tissue infections and can lead to osteomyelitis. CGD typically presents in childhood, but milder variants may remain undiagnosed until adulthood. A history of recurrent skin infections with staphylococci, especially if associated with granuloma formation, should prompt consideration of CGD. However, the absence of lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly, or pulmonary involvement makes this less likely, though not impossible. The diagnostic procedure performed (likely a biopsy) would be critical to differentiate between bacterial, fungal, or tuberculous etiologies via culture and histopathology.

**Neoplastic Causes**

Neoplastic etiologies must be considered with high priority given the osteolytic lesion with soft-tissue extension. The patient is young, which makes primary bone malignancies less common but not impossible. The most likely primary bone tumor in this age group is Ewing sarcoma, which typically affects the spine, pelvis, and long bones in adolescents and young adults. It presents with localized pain, often radiating, and may be associated with systemic symptoms such as fever, weight loss, or fatigue—none of which are present here. However, Ewing sarcoma can present insidiously with pain alone, and the absence of systemic symptoms does not exclude it. The CT findings of an osteolytic lesion with a soft-tissue mass are classic for Ewing sarcoma. The MRI signal characteristics (abnormal signal in the vertebral body with disk preservation) are also consistent. The elevated ESR is a common finding in Ewing sarcoma due to the inflammatory response to tumor necrosis. The fact that the lesion is centered in the posterior body and extends into the neural canal may explain the progressive neurologic deficit—clumsiness of the left hand suggests involvement of the lateral corticospinal tracts or anterior horn cells at the C5–C6 level, possibly due to direct compression or infiltration. The patient’s occupation as a surgical assistant, involving repetitive manual tasks and potential microtrauma, raises the question of whether this could be a benign tumor such as a giant cell tumor, which can occur in the spine and present with osteolytic lesions. However, giant cell tumors are typically located in the epiphyses of long bones and are rare in the vertebral body. They are usually expansile rather than destructive and rarely extend into the spinal canal. Another possibility is metastatic disease. While uncommon in a 27-year-old woman without a known primary cancer, it cannot be ruled out. Primary cancers that commonly metastasize to bone include breast, lung, renal, and thyroid carcinomas, and less commonly lymphoma or multiple myeloma. Multiple myeloma is exceedingly rare at this age and typically presents with multiple lytic lesions, anemia, renal insufficiency, and hypercalcemia—none of which are present here. Breast cancer can metastasize to the spine and is more common in young women, especially if there is a family history or BRCA mutation. The patient has no history of hematuria, flank pain, or hypertension, which would raise suspicion. Lymphoma, particularly non-Hodgkin lymphoma, can involve the spine and present with osteolytic lesions and epidural extension. Primary spinal lymphoma is rare, but systemic lymphoma can involve the spine. The absence of lymphadenopathy, splenomegaly, or B symptoms (fever, night sweats, weight loss) makes this less likely, though not impossible—some patients with lymphoma can have localized presentations without systemic signs. The key to distinguishing lymphoma from other causes is the histopathology: lymphoma typically shows a monotonous population of lymphoid cells, whereas Ewing sarcoma shows small round blue cells with positive staining for CD99. **Inflammatory and Autoimmune Causes**

Inflammatory causes are less common but must be considered. The elevated ESR and the absence of infection or malignancy raise the possibility of a rheumatologic condition. Sarcoidosis can involve the spine and cause osteolytic lesions, though this is rare. Vertebral sarcoidosis typically presents with granulomatous inflammation and may be associated with bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy, which is absent here. CRMO presents with recurrent, painful, osteolytic lesions in multiple bones, often without systemic symptoms, and with elevated inflammatory markers. It is non-infectious and non-neoplastic. While CRMO typically affects the metaphyses of long bones and the clavicle, it can involve the spine. The lesion in C5, the absence of fever, and the elevated ESR are consistent. However, CRMO is usually multifocal, and the patient has only one lesion described. The unilateral neurologic deficit and posterior mass extending into the spinal canal are atypical for CRMO, which usually does not cause significant neurologic compromise unless there is massive bone destruction. The fact that the lesion is solitary and associated with a soft-tissue mass makes CRMO less likely, though not impossible. LCH can also involve the skin, and the patient’s history of chronic paronychia could be misinterpreted as cutaneous involvement, but paronychia is not a typical manifestation of LCH.
