# Case 16-1992 —A 27-Year-Old Woman with Pain in the Neck and Shoulder and Clumsiness of the Hand

## Presentation of Case



A 27-year-old woman was admitted to the hospital because of cervical pain that radiated to the left shoulder.

Five days before entry she was riding in a truck that stopped suddenly, and her occipital region struck the rear window. On the next day she awoke with pain in the neck that radiated to the left shoulder, accompanied by paresthesias in the left hand. Two days before admission she came to the Emergency Ward, where only local tenderness and muscle spasm were found, without motor or sensory abnormalities. X-ray films of the cervical spine, performed in flexion and extension, showed very limited flexion and no extension; no subluxation was found. A rigid cervical collar was prescribed, and bed rest was advised. One day before entry a magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) scan of the cervical spine revealed an abnormal signal in the superoposterior portion of the fifth cervical vertebra, with involvement of the adjacent portion of the C4–5 intervertebral disk space; the anterior half of the disk space had a normal signal. On the following day the scan became available to the physician who had examined the patient. When contacted by telephone, the patient reported that the pain in her neck and shoulder was more severe and that her left hand was clumsy.



The patient was a surgical assistant. There was a history of a paronychia of the right first toe and infected cuticles on several fingers of several months' duration. There was no history of use of tobacco, excess alcohol ingestion, or illicit drugs; the patient denied bladder or bowel dysfunction, muscle weakness or gait disorder, fever, chills, night sweats, or fatigue.



The temperature was 37°C, the pulse was 90, and the respirations were 18. The blood pressure was 100/70 mm Hg.



On examination the patient was in pain while wearing a cervical collar. The head and neck were normal except that paraspinal muscle spasm was present and cervical flexion provoked pain that radiated to the left shoulder and upper arm; no notable tenderness was found. The lungs, heart, breasts, and abdomen were normal. There was a healing paronychia of the right first toe; no peripheral edema, clubbing, or cyanosis was detected. On neurologic examination the patient's speech was fluent, and her cranial-nerve functions were intact. Strength was graded 5/5 except that the intrinsic muscles of the left hand were 5—/5. Her sensation of pinprick, touch, position, and vibration was preserved. The deep tendon reflexes were ++ and symmetric; tapping the left biceps tendon provoked flexion of the left fingers; no clonus was present; the plantar responses were flexor; no crossed adductor response was elicited.



The urine was normal. The hematocrit was 36.9 percent; the white-cell count was 3900 (3.9×109 per liter), with 55 percent neutrophils, 34 percent lymphocytes, 8 percent monocytes, 2 percent eosinophils, and 1 percent basophils. The platelet count was normal, and the erythrocyte sedimentation rate 50 mm per hour. X-ray films of the chest were normal. A CT scan of the cervical spine, performed before and after the intravenous injection of contrast material, revealed an osteolytic lesion at the superior end plate and upper posterior body of the fifth cervical vertebra, with an associated soft-tissue mass that protruded posteriorly into the neural canal; no fracture or malalignment was detected.
