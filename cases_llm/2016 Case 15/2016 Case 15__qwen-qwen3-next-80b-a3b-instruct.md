# Case 15-2016 —A 32-Year-Old Man with Olfactory Hallucinations and Paresthesias

## Presentation of Case



   *Dr. Ivana Vodopivec* (Neurology): A 32-year-old man was seen in an outpatient clinic of this hospital because of olfactory hallucinations, intermittent numbness and paresthesias on the left side, and a mass in the right temporal lobe.



The patient had been well until approximately 1 year before admission, when olfactory hallucinations (including sulfur and burning odors) developed, along with associated nausea. Episodes occurred every few days and lasted for approximately 2 minutes. Approximately 6 weeks before this evaluation, transient daily episodes of numbness and paresthesias on the left side began. Results of a neurologic examination performed at another hospital were reportedly normal. Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) of the head reportedly revealed a partially enhancing mass in the medial aspect of the right temporal lobe; fluid-attenuated inversion recovery (FLAIR) images showed extension of the abnormality into the insula and basal ganglia. Levetiracetam was administered for management of suspected focal seizures, and the patient was referred to this hospital for evaluation.



The patient was right-handed. He had pain associated with left molar caries (for which a root canal was planned) and a history of attention deficit–hyperactivity disorder. He reported no changes in his skin or vision, joint pain, dry mouth, decreased lacrimation, or respiratory symptoms. During childhood, he had had ophthalmologic surgery on his left lens after an injury. Medications included levetiracetam and dextroamphetamine. He had no known allergies. He lived alone and worked in an office. He drank alcohol in moderation, had smoked cigarettes briefly more than 10 years earlier, and did not use illicit drugs. He was of both African-American and Caribbean ancestry, was born in the United States, and had not traveled to the Caribbean. There was no family history of autoimmune diseases, malignant tumors, or migraine headache.



On examination, there were postsurgical changes in the patient’s left pupil; the right pupil was reactive to light. The remainder of the examination was normal, including examination of the visual fields, strength, coordination, and balance. A complete blood count and urinalysis were normal, as were blood levels of electrolytes, calcium, and glucose and results of coagulation and renal-function tests. An electrocardiogram showed a normal sinus rhythm and met the voltage criteria for left-ventricular hypertrophy in the precordial leads. A chest radiograph was normal.

Sagittal and coronal views also showed leptomeningeal nodules of enhancement along the inferior margin of the right basis pontis and, more inferiorly, along the right side of the medulla oblongata.



In addition, perfusion imaging and multivoxel magnetic resonance spectroscopy were performed. The results of the spectroscopy were essentially normal, and the perfusion imaging was unhelpful.

## Differential Diagnosis

The clinical presentation of this 32-year-old man—olfactory hallucinations, left-sided paresthesias, and a complex, enhancing, infiltrative mass in the right temporal lobe extending into the insula, basal ganglia, cerebral peduncle, and leptomeninges of the pons and medulla—demands a highly focused yet expansive differential diagnosis. The constellation of symptoms and imaging findings is not typical of common benign or idiopathic conditions; rather, it points toward a process with both local parenchymal invasion and leptomeningeal dissemination. The olfactory hallucinations, in particular, are a classic ictal or interictal phenomenon arising from the uncus or amygdala, regions of the medial temporal lobe that are part of the primary olfactory cortex. Their persistence over a year, coupled with the development of sensory symptoms and progressive neuroimaging abnormalities, strongly suggests an underlying structural lesion with epileptogenic and infiltrative properties, rather than a functional or psychiatric disorder. The absence of systemic symptoms—no fever, weight loss, rash, joint pain, or respiratory complaints—makes systemic inflammatory or infectious processes less likely, though not impossible. The lack of family history of malignancy or autoimmunity further reduces the probability of inherited syndromes, though sporadic mutations remain plausible. The patient’s history of childhood ocular trauma and current dental pathology are likely red herrings, given the absence of any plausible neuroanatomic or immunologic link to the current neurologic picture. The normal bloodwork, including inflammatory markers (inferred from normal CBC, renal function, and electrolytes), further argues against acute systemic inflammation or metabolic encephalopathy.

The most immediate diagnostic consideration is a primary central nervous system (CNS) neoplasm. Gliomas, particularly those of lower grade but with infiltrative potential, must be at the top of the differential. The location—medial right temporal lobe extending into the insula and basal ganglia—is classic for a diffuse astrocytoma, particularly an IDH-mutant type. These tumors often present with focal seizures, including olfactory auras, and can grow slowly over years, infiltrating adjacent structures without causing dramatic mass effect early on. The fact that the lesion enhances irregularly and nodularly on MRI suggests a higher grade of malignancy, possibly an anaplastic astrocytoma (WHO grade III) or glioblastoma (WHO grade IV). Glioblastomas frequently exhibit heterogeneous enhancement due to areas of necrosis and neovascularization, and their tendency to spread along white matter tracts, including the internal capsule and cerebral peduncle, aligns with the imaging findings here. The extension into the leptomeninges is less common in primary gliomas but not unheard of, particularly in the setting of high-grade transformation or aggressive molecular subtypes such as those with EGFR amplification or CDKN2A/B deletion. The absence of significant FLAIR hyperintensity beyond the enhancing regions is somewhat atypical for glioblastoma, which usually shows extensive peritumoral edema; however, in some cases, particularly those with more infiltrative than vasogenic edema, the FLAIR signal may be relatively modest. The normal magnetic resonance spectroscopy is an important negative finding: elevated choline, reduced N-acetylaspartate, and the presence of lactate or lipid peaks are common in high-grade gliomas. Their absence here argues against a highly metabolically active tumor, but spectroscopy can be normal in early or low-grade infiltrative tumors, especially if the voxel placement avoids the most aggressive areas. Thus, while spectroscopy does not support a typical glioblastoma, it does not rule out a lower-grade glioma with recent malignant transformation or a variant subtype with atypical metabolic profile.

A second major category of neoplastic etiology is a primary CNS lymphoma. Though more common in older patients and immunocompromised individuals, primary CNS lymphoma can occur in immunocompetent adults in their 30s. It typically presents as a periventricular or deep gray matter lesion, often with homogeneous enhancement, but can occasionally arise in the temporal lobe. The leptomeningeal involvement here is highly suggestive of lymphoma, as CNS lymphoma frequently spreads along the subarachnoid space. However, the nodular, irregular enhancement pattern and the parenchymal extension into the basal ganglia and cerebral peduncle are less typical than the smooth, diffuse enhancement seen in lymphoma. Moreover, lymphomas usually demonstrate marked restriction on diffusion-weighted imaging (DWI) due to high cellularity, a feature not mentioned in the case, which raises doubt. Additionally, lymphomas rarely cause prolonged olfactory hallucinations; their presentation is more often with cognitive decline, personality changes, or focal deficits. The normal spectroscopy also argues against lymphoma, as these tumors typically show a dominant choline peak with absent or markedly reduced NAA and creatine, and sometimes a lipid/lactate peak. The absence of systemic lymphadenopathy or B symptoms further reduces the likelihood of systemic lymphoma with CNS involvement, though primary CNS lymphoma can occur in isolation.

Another neoplastic consideration is a metastatic tumor. Although metastases are more common in older patients and typically multiple, solitary metastases can occur, particularly from primary malignancies such as melanoma, lung, or breast cancer. The patient’s age and lack of known primary malignancy make this less likely, but not impossible. Melanoma, in particular, can present with isolated CNS metastases and can have a prolonged, insidious course. The leptomeningeal involvement is a red flag for metastatic spread, as this is a common route for melanoma and some carcinomas to disseminate. The enhancing nodules along the basis pontis and medulla are highly suggestive of leptomeningeal carcinomatosis. However, the primary lesion in the temporal lobe is large and parenchymal, which is unusual for metastatic disease, which typically presents as multiple, well-circumscribed, peripherally enhancing lesions with surrounding edema. The absence of any systemic symptoms, skin changes, or respiratory complaints makes a primary extracranial malignancy unlikely, though occult melanoma or a slow-growing bronchogenic carcinoma cannot be entirely excluded. The lack of significant FLAIR signal abnormality also argues against metastasis, which usually causes more pronounced vasogenic edema.

A third category to consider is infectious or inflammatory etiologies. Tuberculosis, particularly tuberculous meningitis with parenchymal granulomas, can present with leptomeningeal enhancement and temporal lobe involvement. The patient’s African-American and Caribbean ancestry raises the possibility of exposure to Mycobacterium tuberculosis, though he has not traveled to endemic regions and has no history of immunosuppression or prior TB exposure. TB granulomas can be nodular and enhancing, and the insula and basal ganglia are known sites of tuberculoma formation. However, TB typically presents with systemic symptoms—fever, night sweats, weight loss—which are absent here. The chronicity of symptoms over a year is also unusual for active TB, which tends to progress more rapidly. Fungal infections such as cryptococcosis or aspergillosis are also possibilities, especially in immunocompromised individuals, but the patient has no history of immunosuppression, diabetes, or recent antibiotic use. The normal serum markers and lack of systemic signs make these less likely. Neurocysticercosis, a common cause of seizures in endemic areas, is improbable given the patient’s birthplace and lack of travel. However, the temporal lobe is a classic site for cysticercal granulomas, which can enhance and cause seizures. The chronicity and leptomeningeal spread are atypical, as neurocysticercosis usually presents with multiple lesions and calcifications, neither of which are mentioned here.

Inflammatory and autoimmune disorders must also be considered. Autoimmune encephalitis, particularly anti-LGI1 or anti-GABAB receptor encephalitis, can cause temporal lobe seizures, olfactory hallucinations, and limbic system involvement. However, these typically present with subacute cognitive decline, memory loss, hyponatremia, and sometimes movement disorders, none of which are present here. The imaging findings in autoimmune encephalitis usually show bilateral, symmetric FLAIR hyperintensity in the medial temporal lobes, not a unilateral, enhancing, infiltrative mass with leptomeningeal spread. The absence of CSF pleocytosis (inferred from no mention of lumbar puncture results) and lack of response to steroids (implied by no prior trial) further reduce this likelihood. Sarcoidosis is another important consideration. Neurosarcoidosis can present with cranial nerve palsies, leptomeningeal enhancement, and parenchymal granulomas, particularly in the basal ganglia and brainstem. The patient’s age and ethnicity make sarcoidosis more plausible, as it is more common in African Americans and typically presents between ages 20 and 40. The leptomeningeal nodules along the pons and medulla are classic for neurosarcoidosis, as are the basal ganglia lesions. The temporal lobe mass could represent a granulomatous infiltrate. The lack of systemic symptoms such as dry eyes, dry mouth, lung nodules, or skin lesions (e.g., lupus pernio) is concerning, but neurosarcoidosis can occur in isolation. The normal chest radiograph does not rule out sarcoidosis, as pulmonary involvement can be subtle or absent in up to 15% of cases. The enhancement pattern described—irregular and nodular—is consistent with granulomatous inflammation. The normal spectroscopy and absence of significant edema are also compatible with granulomatous disease, which tends to be less destructive than neoplasms. The fact that the patient is on dextroamphetamine, which can cause CNS hyperexcitability, does not explain the structural lesion or the leptomeningeal spread, but it may have masked or exacerbated subtle symptoms.

Another rare but critical consideration is a vascular malformation with secondary inflammatory or neoplastic transformation. A cavernous malformation in the temporal lobe can cause seizures and focal deficits, but they typically do not enhance significantly, and they rarely cause leptomeningeal spread. The nodular enhancement described is inconsistent with the typical “popcorn” appearance of cavernomas. Arteriovenous malformations (AVMs) are usually associated with hemorrhage, not chronic hallucinations, and do not cause leptomeningeal nodules. A more exotic possibility is a primary CNS vascular tumor such as a hemangioblastoma, but these are typically located in the cerebellum or spinal cord and are highly vascular, with prominent flow voids—features not mentioned here.

The leptomeningeal involvement is perhaps the most puzzling and critical feature. In the context of a solitary parenchymal mass, this suggests either a highly aggressive neoplasm with leptomeningeal seeding (e.g., glioblastoma or lymphoma) or a primary inflammatory process with leptomeningeal tropism (e.g., sarcoidosis, tuberculosis). The fact that the lesion is unilateral and asymmetric, with no contralateral involvement, argues against a systemic inflammatory process such as multiple sclerosis, which typically presents with multiple, periventricular, ovoid lesions. The patient’s young age also makes demyelinating disease unlikely to present with such a focal, enhancing, infiltrative mass.

Given all of this, the leading diagnostic possibilities are: (1) a high-grade glioma, particularly an IDH-wildtype glioblastoma with unusual imaging features; (2) neurosarcoidosis; and (3) primary CNS lymphoma. Of these, neurosarcoidosis stands out as the most compelling, given the patient’s ethnicity, the leptomeningeal nodularity, the basal ganglia and brainstem involvement, the chronicity, and the absence of systemic symptoms. The temporal lobe mass could represent a granulomatous infiltrate with secondary gliosis and enhancement. The normal spectroscopy and modest FLAIR signal are consistent with granulomas, which are less metabolically active than neoplasms. The olfactory hallucinations and paresthesias can be explained by the involvement of the uncus and insula, regions densely populated with sensory and limbic structures. The absence of fever or weight loss does not exclude sarcoidosis, as neurosarcoidosis can present in isolation. The diagnosis would be supported by elevated ACE levels, lymphopenia, or a biopsy showing non-caseating granulomas. The fact that the patient was started on levetiracetam suggests a clinical suspicion of seizure disorder, which is common in both sarcoidosis and glioma, but does not differentiate them.

In conclusion, while glioblastoma remains a serious consideration, the pattern of leptomeningeal nodularity, the location in the basal ganglia and brainstem, the patient’s demographic profile, and the absence of typical neoplastic imaging features (e.g., extensive edema, necrosis, pronounced spectroscopic abnormalities) make neurosarcoidosis the most plausible and diagnostically urgent consideration. A biopsy of the temporal lesion and leptomeningeal nodules is essential, but a trial of corticosteroids may be diagnostically and therapeutically informative. If the lesion and symptoms improve with steroids, neurosarcoidosis is strongly confirmed. If not, then aggressive glioma or lymphoma must be pursued with surgical resection and molecular profiling. The differential diagnosis must remain broad until histopathology is obtained, but the clinical and radiologic picture, when synthesized, points most compellingly toward a granulomatous process, with neurosarcoidosis as the leading hypothesis.
