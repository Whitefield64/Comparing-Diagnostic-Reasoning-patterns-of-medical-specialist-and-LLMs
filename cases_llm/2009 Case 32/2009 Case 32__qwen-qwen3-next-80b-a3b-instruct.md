## Differential Diagnosis

The clinical presentation of this 27-year-old man is complex and multisystemic, involving progressive respiratory failure, diffuse parenchymal lung disease with bronchiectasis and honeycombing, erythrocytosis, platelet dysfunction, congenital abnormalities including albinism and nystagmus, and evidence of pulmonary hypertension with right ventricular dysfunction. The constellation of findings—particularly the combination of albinism, pulmonary fibrosis, bronchiectasis, platelet dysfunction, and a family history of similar respiratory disease in siblings with albinism—strongly suggests a rare, inherited disorder rather than a common acquired pulmonary condition. While infectious, inflammatory, and environmental etiologies must be considered, the chronicity, early onset, multisystem involvement, and familial pattern point decisively toward a genetic syndrome.

The most compelling diagnostic hypothesis is **Hermansky-Pudlak syndrome (HPS)**, a rare autosomal recessive disorder of lysosomal trafficking that belongs to the group of oculocutaneous albinism syndromes. HPS is characterized by oculocutaneous albinism (hypopigmentation of skin, hair, and irises—consistent with this patient’s pale skin and pigmented irises, a key distinguishing feature from complete albinism), platelet storage pool deficiency (leading to impaired secondary aggregation, as seen here), and, in certain subtypes, progressive pulmonary fibrosis and granulomatous colitis. The pulmonary manifestation typically presents in the second to fourth decade with dyspnea, cough, and progressive restrictive lung disease, often accompanied by bronchiectasis and honeycombing on high-resolution CT—exactly as described in this case. The presence of clubbing, decreased lung volumes, ground-glass opacities, and architectural distortion are classic radiographic hallmarks of HPS-related pulmonary fibrosis. The development of pulmonary hypertension, right ventricular dilation, and elevated right ventricular systolic pressure (56 mm Hg) further supports the progression of this fibrotic interstitial lung disease to cor pulmonale, a known terminal complication of HPS. The erythrocytosis is likely a compensatory response to chronic hypoxemia, exacerbated by the patient’s reduced exercise tolerance and impaired gas exchange. The fact that his siblings also have albinism and milder respiratory problems strongly supports an autosomal recessive inheritance pattern, which is typical of HPS.

The platelet dysfunction is a critical diagnostic clue. The finding of absent secondary aggregation despite intact primary aggregation is pathognomonic for a dense granule storage pool deficiency, which occurs in HPS due to defective biogenesis of lysosome-related organelles, including platelet dense granules. This defect leads to a bleeding diathesis, consistent with the patient’s history of easy bruisability and avoidance of aspirin due to bleeding problems. This finding is not seen in common causes of thrombocytopenia or platelet dysfunction such as immune thrombocytopenia, myelodysplastic syndromes, or drug-induced platelet defects. The normal platelet count and absence of coagulation factor deficiencies (normal PT, aPTT, protein C and S, factor V Leiden, lupus anticoagulant) rule out other causes of bleeding, further supporting the diagnosis of a storage pool disorder.

The presence of albinism and nystagmus is consistent with ocular involvement in HPS. Although the patient has pigmented irises—a feature more typical of partial albinism than complete albinism—this does not exclude HPS, as pigmentary variation exists across subtypes. The horizontal nystagmus is a direct consequence of abnormal foveal development due to defective melanin synthesis in the retinal pigment epithelium during development, a hallmark of all forms of oculocutaneous albinism. The nearsightedness is also commonly associated with these disorders due to foveal hypoplasia and misrouting of optic nerve fibers.

The patient’s history of chronic productive cough, wheezing, and bronchiectasis, initially misattributed to asthma or infection, is also consistent with HPS. While bronchiectasis is often considered a consequence of recurrent infection, in HPS it may arise from intrinsic defects in airway epithelial cell function and mucociliary clearance, secondary to abnormal lysosomal trafficking in ciliated cells. The absence of positive microbiologic cultures or immunoglobulin deficiencies makes primary immunodeficiency an unlikely primary driver. The negative sweat chloride test effectively excludes cystic fibrosis, despite the mother’s historical suspicion. The normal alpha-1 antitrypsin level also rules out alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency, another common cause of early-onset emphysema and bronchiectasis, though typically without fibrosis or platelet defects.

The finding of fatty liver on ultrasound is an interesting but not uncommon incidental finding in HPS, possibly reflecting metabolic dysregulation or secondary to chronic hypoxemia and systemic inflammation. It is not diagnostic but may represent a manifestation of systemic lysosomal dysfunction. The elevated hematocrit and erythrocyte count are secondary to chronic hypoxia and are not due to polycythemia vera or other myeloproliferative disorders, as there are no signs of JAK2 mutation, splenomegaly, or leukocytosis.

Other inherited disorders of albinism must be considered. Chediak-Higashi syndrome, another autosomal recessive disorder, also presents with oculocutaneous albinism, platelet dysfunction, and recurrent infections due to defective lysosomal trafficking. However, Chediak-Higashi is typically associated with giant granules in leukocytes, severe immunodeficiency with recurrent pyogenic infections, and a high risk of developing an accelerated phase (lymphohistiocytosis) with fever, hepatosplenomegaly, and pancytopenia—none of which are present here. The absence of recurrent infections, normal immunoglobulin levels, and lack of abnormal granules on peripheral smear (implied by normal CBC without giant granulocytes) make Chediak-Higashi unlikely.

Griscelli syndrome, another rare albinism disorder, can present with partial albinism, immune dysfunction, and neurological abnormalities, but it is typically associated with hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis (HLH), which would present with fever, cytopenias, hepatosplenomegaly, and elevated ferritin—none of which are present. The patient has no signs of HLH, and his immune testing is normal.

Pulmonary fibrosis with bronchiectasis and albinism could theoretically arise from a rare mutation in genes encoding surfactant proteins (e.g., SFTPC, SFTPA2), but these typically present in infancy or childhood with respiratory distress and are not associated with platelet dysfunction or albinism. Similarly, idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF) is exceedingly rare in this age group and is not associated with systemic features such as albinism, nystagmus, or platelet defects. The familial clustering of respiratory disease in siblings with albinism is not typical of IPF.

The possibility of an undiagnosed connective tissue disease must be considered, particularly since interstitial lung disease can occur in scleroderma, rheumatoid arthritis, or dermatomyositis. However, the absence of Raynaud’s phenomenon, skin thickening, arthralgias, myositis, or autoantibodies (normal ANA, ENA, anti-CCP, etc.—implied by negative lupus anticoagulant and no mention of other serologies) makes this unlikely. Furthermore, connective tissue disease–associated ILD typically presents with a different radiographic pattern, often basal predominance with traction bronchiectasis rather than the upper lobe predominance and honeycombing seen here.

Infectious causes such as tuberculosis or fungal infections (e.g., histoplasmosis) can cause upper lobe fibrosis and bronchiectasis, especially in immunocompromised individuals. However, the patient’s negative tuberculin skin test, absence of fever, weight loss, or night sweats, and lack of cavitary lesions on imaging make this improbable. The serologic evidence of past exposure to CMV, EBV, and toxoplasma does not suggest active infection, and there is no evidence of immunodeficiency. The negative HIV test also rules out HIV-associated pulmonary disease.

Environmental exposures such as hypersensitivity pneumonitis or occupational lung disease (e.g., asbestos, silica) are possible causes of interstitial fibrosis, but the chronicity, early onset, familial pattern, and presence of albinism and platelet defects make this an unlikely primary cause. The patient’s history of smoking (10 pack-years) may have contributed to airway damage and accelerated progression, but it cannot account for the systemic features.

The finding of poor R-wave progression on ECG and nonspecific ST-T changes is consistent with chronic right heart strain and pulmonary hypertension, not primary myocardial disease. The preserved left ventricular ejection fraction and normal left heart dimensions confirm that the pathology is primarily pulmonary, not cardiac.

The exercise test revealing a breathing reserve index of 0.77 indicates a mechanical limitation to ventilation, likely due to reduced lung compliance from fibrosis and decreased lung volumes, rather than a primary cardiovascular limitation. The drop in oxygen saturation from 94% at rest to 77% with exercise, despite high supplemental oxygen, demonstrates severe diffusion impairment and ventilation-perfusion mismatch—hallmarks of advanced interstitial lung disease.

The fact that the patient’s oxygen saturation improved dramatically with supplemental oxygen (from 76% to 98%) confirms that hypoxemia is primarily due to impaired gas exchange rather than hypoventilation or shunt. The arterial blood gas on 6 L O2 showing a PaO2 of 205 mm Hg is expected and confirms the adequacy of oxygen delivery when supplemented, but the persistent hypoxemia at rest on room air (91%) and marked desaturation with exertion reflect the severity of the underlying parenchymal disease.

The combination of albinism, platelet dysfunction, pulmonary fibrosis, and familial occurrence is virtually diagnostic of Hermansky-Pudlak syndrome, particularly subtype HPS-1 or HPS-4, which are most commonly associated with pulmonary fibrosis. HPS-1 is the most common subtype in Puerto Ricans, and the patient’s Puerto Rican ancestry further strengthens this diagnosis. The genetic basis lies in mutations in genes encoding proteins involved in biogenesis of lysosome-related organelles (LROs), including melanosomes, platelet dense granules, and possibly surfactant-containing lamellar bodies in type II pneumocytes. The pulmonary fibrosis is thought to result from defective surfactant processing, chronic epithelial injury, and aberrant repair mechanisms.

In conclusion, while numerous differential diagnoses exist for progressive dyspnea, bronchiectasis, and interstitial fibrosis, the unique constellation of albinism, platelet dysfunction, nystagmus, familial occurrence, and the specific pattern of pulmonary disease—particularly the upper lobe predominance with honeycombing—is overwhelmingly consistent with Hermansky-Pudlak syndrome. The absence of other systemic features (e.g., colitis, immunodeficiency, neurological decline) does not exclude HPS, as phenotypic expression can vary even within the same genetic subtype. The diagnosis should be confirmed by genetic testing for mutations in HPS1, HPS4, or other HPS-associated genes, and by electron microscopy of platelets demonstrating absence of dense granules. The prognosis is guarded, with pulmonary fibrosis being the leading cause of mortality, often in the fourth or fifth decade. Management is supportive: oxygen therapy, pulmonary rehabilitation, avoidance of smoking, and consideration of lung transplantation in advanced disease. Early recognition is critical for genetic counseling of family members and anticipatory guidance regarding risk of bleeding and progression to respiratory failure.
