### 1.  Hereditary Disorders Linking Albinism, Platelet Dysfunction, and Pulmonary Fibrosis  

#### 1.1 Hermansky‑Pudlak Syndrome (HPS)  

**Pathophysiology.**  HPS is an autosomal‑recessive lysosomal‑related disorder caused by mutations in at least ten known genes (HPS1‑HPS10).  The defect lies in biogenesis of melanosomes, platelet dense granules, and lysosome‑related organelles of alveolar type II cells.  In the lung, absent or dysfunctional lamellar bodies impair surfactant turnover and predispose to chronic alveolar injury, inflammation, and progressive fibrosis.  The pulmonary phenotype is most severe in HPS‑1 and HPS‑2, both of which are prevalent in individuals of Puerto Rican descent (founder mutation in HPS‑1).  

**Why the case fits HPS.**  

| Feature | Consistent with HPS |
|---------|----------------------|
| **Oculocutaneous albinism** – absent melanin in skin, hair, and iris. | Classic hallmark. |
| **Platelet dense‑granule deficiency** – easy bruisability, impaired secondary aggregation on platelet‑function testing. | Direct laboratory correlate. |
| **Progressive interstitial fibrosis** – CT with subpleural honeycombing, ground‑glass, and architectural distortion; histology (when obtained) typically shows usual interstitial pneumonia (UIP) pattern. | HPS lung disease mimics UIP; appears in the third decade. |
| **Upper‑lobe predominant bronchiectasis** – chronic airway obstruction secondary to surfactant dysfunction and recurrent infections. | Frequently reported. |
| **Digital clubbing and secondary polycythemia** – chronic hypoxemia. | Expected in advanced ILD. |
| **Pulmonary hypertension** – secondary to extensive fibrosis and loss of vascular bed. | Documented in HPS patients; right‑ventricular systolic pressure ~56 mm Hg matches. |
| **Hepatic steatosis** – fatty infiltration seen in many HPS patients, possibly related to metabolic derangements. | Present. |
| **Puerto Rican ancestry** – high carrier frequency for HPS‑1 founder mutation. | Matches. |
| **Absence of cystic fibrosis, normal sweat test, normal α‑1 antitrypsin, normal immunoglobulins** – helps exclude other common genetic ILDs. | Supports a distinct disorder. |

The constellation of pigmentation abnormality, platelet storage‑pool deficiency, and early‑onset fibrotic lung disease is essentially pathognomonic for HPS.  The only other hereditary albinism that can be associated with lung disease is Chediak‑Higashi syndrome, but it is characterized by giant lysosomal granules, severe immunodeficiency, and neurologic decline—features not evident here.  Therefore, the leading diagnosis is **Hermansky‑Pudlak syndrome (most likely HPS‑1)**.

#### 1.2 Chediak‑Higashi Syndrome (CHS)  

CHS also presents with oculocutaneous albinism and platelet abnormalities, but the platelet defect is usually a quantitative reduction of dense granules rather than the functional secondary aggregation defect seen in this patient.  Moreover, CHS patients develop recurrent severe bacterial infections, neurologic degeneration, and a characteristic “giant lysosomal granule” on electron microscopy.  The absence of immunodeficiency, neurologic symptoms, and the presence of an ILD pattern typical for HPS make CHS far less likely.

#### 1.3 Other Albinism‑Related Syndromes  

- **Oculocutaneous albinism (OCA) without systemic disease** – does not explain platelet dysfunction or pulmonary fibrosis.  
- **Griscelli syndrome** – presents with hypopigmentation and immune dysfunction, but not pulmonary fibrosis.  

Thus, among albinism‑associated disorders, HPS best accounts for every major finding.

### 17.  Clinical Management Implications  

Recognition of HPS has several immediate ramifications:

- **Bleeding Risk** – The platelet storage‑pool defect mandates careful peri‑procedural planning (e.g., desmopressin, platelet transfusion, or recombinant factor VIIa) for any invasive diagnostic or therapeutic interventions, including bronchoscopy or lung transplantation.  
- **Transplant Candidacy** – HPS patients can undergo lung transplantation, but the platelet defect increases peri‑operative hemorrhagic risk; thus, a multidisciplinary transplant evaluation is essential.  
- **Surveillance for Extra‑Pulmonary Manifestations** – HPS can involve colitis, renal disease, and ocular abnormalities; periodic gastrointestinal and ophthalmologic assessments are advisable.  
- **Genetic Counseling** – Autosomal‑recessive inheritance implies a 25 % recurrence risk for siblings; carrier testing for family members, especially the partner, is indicated.
