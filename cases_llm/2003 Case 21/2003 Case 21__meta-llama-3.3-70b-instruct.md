# Case 21-2003 —A 72-Year-Old Man with Repetitive Strokes in the Posterior Circulation

## Presentation of Case



A 72-year-old, right-handed man was admitted to the hospital because of recurrent neurologic symptoms.



The patient had been well until 10 days earlier, when he briefly had numbness of the right side of the mouth and tongue. That evening, he had slurred speech and a hyponasal quality in his speech for 30 minutes, and vertical diplopia later developed but soon cleared. The next morning, there was a brief recurrence of the vertical diplopia, followed by vertigo. Later that morning, transient diplopia again recurred, and the patient came to this hospital and was admitted.



The patient had undergone an apical lung resection 50 years earlier because of tuberculosis. He had a history of labile hypertension that did not require medication, and he had mild hyperlipidemia that had improved since he began to follow a diet. He had stopped smoking 20 years earlier, and he did not drink alcohol. He had had migraine headaches, but none for the previous eight years. Nine months before admission, the patient had had bilateral sinus pain with clear nasal discharge, which lasted four months and resolved after antibiotic treatment. There was no focal weakness, hemisensory change, difficulty with language, or palpitations, and at the time of admission the patient had no weakness, nausea, vomiting, or imbalance. There was no family history of stroke or aneurysm.



The temperature was 36.2°C, and the blood pressure 140/110 mm Hg. The patient appeared well. No bruits were heard around the neck, and examination of the heart revealed no abnormalities. A neurologic examination revealed only slight tottering to the right on tandem-gait testing and a questionable right Babinski reflex.



The urine was normal. The plasma homocysteine level was 6.0 μmol per liter (normal range, 0 to 12). The levels of urea nitrogen, creatinine, glucose, bilirubin, total protein, albumin, globulin, aspartate aminotransferase, alanine aminotransferase, alkaline phosphatase, creatine kinase, creatine kinase isoenzymes, and troponin T were normal. An electrocardiogram showed a normal rhythm at a rate of 70 beats per minute, with a possible septal infarct. Computed tomographic (CT) scanning of the head, performed without the use of contrast material, revealed a small focus of hypoattenuation in the right side of the cerebellum, a finding consistent with the presence of an old infarct, and slight hypoattenuation in the periventricular white matter, a finding consistent with the presence of microangiopathic disease. A CT angiographic study disclosed that both vertebral arteries were small and contained irregularities that might be due to moderate, diffuse atherosclerotic changes. In small portions of these arteries, there was evidence of more severe stenosis, and there was possibly severe circumferential, noncalcified atheromatous disease at the origin of the left vertebral artery.



Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) of the head and a magnetic resonance angiographic study with diffusion-weighted imaging showed a small right cerebellar lesion suggestive of subacute infarction. Just above that area were two small acute lesions, a left cerebellar lesion with infarction, and bilateral punctate subcortical lesions suggestive of acute infarction. There was slight-to-moderate narrowing at the midportion of the dominant right vertebral artery, and the left vertebral artery was diffusely narrowed. There was slight narrowing near the origin of the right external carotid artery, slight narrowing of the cavernous portion of the left internal carotid artery, and slight-to-moderate narrowing near the origin of the left external carotid artery. Chest radiographs showed extensive pleural calcifications in the diaphragmatic and posterior lateral regions of the left hemithorax, with elevation of the left hemidiaphragm; the remaining lung zones were normal. The heart was normal, and the aorta was slightly ectatic.



Transcranial Doppler studies showed higher than normal blood-flow velocity in the right and left intracranial vertebral arteries, a change suggestive of severe stenosis; blood flow in the basilar artery was antegrade, and the right proximal segment of the middle cerebral artery and the right supraclinoid segment of the carotid artery also had high blood-flow velocities suggestive of severe focal stenosis. The ophthalmic arteries and carotid siphons were normal, but the left middle cerebral and anterior cerebral arteries and both posterior cerebral arteries could not be evaluated because of limited bone windows. Ultrasonographic examination of the neck vessels showed moderate stenosis of both external carotid arteries, but there was no stenosis of the internal carotid arteries that was of hemodynamic importance. There were no abnormalities in the cervical vertebral arteries.



A cardiac ultrasonographic examination revealed that the proximal ascending aorta was dilated, at 42 mm. Both atria and the left ventricle were normal in size, and there was no evidence of a patent foramen ovale. The left ventricular ejection fraction was 57 percent, without segmental wall-motion abnormalities. No valvular vegetations were seen in this transthoracic study.



Treatment with heparin and warfarin was begun. A few transient episodes of diplopia occurred, each lasting 30 to 60 seconds, and there was one bout of vertigo, with buzzing in the ears. The patient's condition improved, and he was discharged on the fourth hospital day with instructions to take dalteparin sodium and warfarin without aspirin.



Three days after discharge, diffuse weakness developed, along with dizziness and transient vertical diplopia. (The international normalized ratio, obtained at another hospital, was 1.7). Treatment with aspirin was resumed. MRI performed the next day showed new acute infarctions that involved the cerebellum bilaterally, as well as the midbrain and the left parieto-occipital lobe. Subacute infarctions were again seen in the cerebellar hemispheres and in the left occipital lobe. The next morning when the patient awoke, he had slurred and slowed speech and was making guttural sounds but was able to repeat spoken phrases. He was readmitted to this hospital. He had no history of sensory loss below the neck, seizures, or language difficulties.



The temperature was 36.2°C, the pulse was 60 beats per minute, and the respiratory rate was 20 breaths per minute. The blood pressure was 180/80 mm Hg.



On physical examination, the patient appeared generally well. The temporal arteries were prominent, with strong pulses and no nodularity or tenderness. The neck was supple and without bruits. The lungs were clear, and the heart sounds were normal. The left radial pulse was diminished, and the blood pressure could not be measured in the left arm.



On neurologic examination, the patient was alert and oriented; his memory was intact, as was his naming ability. His speech was dysarthric. Treatment with heparin and clopidogrel without aspirin was given, and on the second hospital day the first of five daily plasmapheresis procedures was performed.
