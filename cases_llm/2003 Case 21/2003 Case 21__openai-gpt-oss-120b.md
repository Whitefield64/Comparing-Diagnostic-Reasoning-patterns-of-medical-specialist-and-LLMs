### 1. Atherosclerotic Vertebro‑Basilar Disease (Large‑Vessel Atherothrombosis)

### 2. Antiphospholipid Antibody Syndrome (APS) – Primary or Secondary

#### Pathophysiologic Basis

Cardio‑embolic sources are **unlikely** based on the echocardiographic assessment and lack of arrhythmia.  They do not account for the vertebral artery stenoses or the inflammatory markers.

### 8. Hypercoagulable States Other Than APS (Inherited Thrombophilias, Malignancy‑Associated Coagulopathy)

#### Pathophysiologic Basis  

Inherited thrombophilias (e.g., factor V Leiden, prothrombin G20210A mutation, protein C/S deficiency) predispose to arterial and venous thrombosis.  Malignancy, particularly adenocarcinomas, can trigger a paraneoplastic hypercoagulable state (Trousseau syndrome) leading to arterial emboli.  Elevated D‑dimer levels and abnormal coagulation panels may be clues.

#### Evidence Supporting Additional Hypercoagulable States  

1. **Negative Prothrombin‑Gene Test** – The case explicitly states that PCR did not detect the G20210A mutation.  

2. **Normal Protein C/S, Antithrombin** – Not directly reported, but the extensive laboratory panel (Table not shown) likely included these; no abnormality is mentioned.  

3. **Normal D‑Dimer** – The D‑dimer test was negative, arguing against an active disseminated intravascular coagulation or high‑grade thrombotic activity at the time of testing.  

4. **Tumor Markers** – AFP, PSA, CEA are normal; CT of the chest shows only calcified pleural plaques and tiny indeterminate pulmonary nodules, without a mass lesion.  

#### Evidence Against Additional Hypercoagulable States  

- The negative screening for common inherited thrombophilias and absent evidence of occult malignancy make **additional hypercoagulable conditions unlikely**.  

#### Synthesis  

APS remains the dominant pro‑thrombotic factor.  Other inherited or malignancy‑related hypercoagulable states are **not supported** by the available data.

### 9. Infectious Vasculitis (Syphilis, Tuberculosis, Others)

#### Pathophysiologic Basis  

Chronic infections can produce vasculitis via direct invasion of vessel walls or immune‑mediated mechanisms.  Neurosyphilis can cause endarteritis obliterans of small‑medium cerebral vessels, leading to stroke.  Tuberculous meningitis can involve the basal cisterns and produce vasculitic infarcts in the posterior circulation.  

#### Evidence Supporting Infectious Vasculitis  

1. **Remote Pulmonary Tuberculosis** – The patient had surgical resection for TB 50 years ago; pleural plaques suggest prior infection.  

2. **Prior Sinusitis** – Nine months before admission, the patient experienced bilateral sinus pain and clear discharge, which could be a manifestation of chronic sinus infection, though it resolved with antibiotics.  

#### Evidence Against Infectious Vasculitis  

1. **Negative Serologies** – Syphilis serology is negative.  

2. **CSF Normal** – No pleocytosis, normal glucose, normal protein (44 mg/dL) – inconsistent with active meningitis or neurosyphilis, which typically yields elevated protein and lymphocytic pleocytosis.  

3. **Lack of Systemic Infectious Symptoms** – No fever, weight loss, night sweats, or constitutional signs.  

4. **Imaging** – No meningeal enhancement or basal exudates on MRI, which would be expected in tuberculous meningitis.  

#### Synthesis  

Infectious vasculitis is **unlikely** given the normal CSF and negative serologies.  Prior TB is probably an incidental historical finding.

### 10. Other Rare or Overlapping Entities  

#### 10.1. Moyamoya Disease / Moyamoya‑like Vasculopathy  

Moyamoya is a progressive stenosis of the distal internal carotid arteries with collateral network formation, typically affecting the anterior circulation.  Posterior‑circulation involvement can occur, but the pattern is usually “puff of smoke” collaterals on angiography.  The patient’s imaging does not describe such collaterals, and the stenoses are proximal (vertebral origins), making moyamoya **unlikely**.

#### 10.2. Reversible Cerebral Vasoconstriction Syndrome (RCVS)  

RCVS presents with thunderclap headaches, reversible segmental arterial narrowing, and sometimes ischemic strokes.  The absence of severe headaches, the presence of inflammatory markers, and the persistence of stenoses beyond the acute phase argue against RCVS.

#### 10.3. Cerebral Autosomal Dominant Arteriopathy with Subcortical Infarcts and Leukoencephalopathy (CADASIL)  

CADASIL produces recurrent subcortical infarcts, white‑matter hyperintensities, and migraine with aura.  The patient’s prior migraine history (now resolved) and white‑matter changes could be compatible, but the age of onset (70s) is late, and there is no family history.  Moreover, CADASIL does **not** cause large‑vessel vertebral stenoses or elevated CRP/ANCA.  

#### 10.4. Radiation‑Induced Vasculopathy  

There is no history of cranial irradiation, making this diagnosis irrelevant.

#### 10.5. Fibromuscular Dysplasia (FMD)  

FMD can affect the vertebral and renal arteries, producing a “string‑of‑beads” appearance.  It is more common in women < 50 years old, and typically involves the distal vertebral artery, not the proximal origin.  The patient’s age and gender make FMD **unlikely**.

#### 10.6. Aortitis (Takayasu, Large‑Vessel Vasculitis)  

Takayasu arteritis involves the aortic arch and its branches, but it predominantly affects women < 40 years.  The patient’s aorta is only mildly ectatic, without thickening or aneurysm; no systemic inflammatory signs besides CRP.  Thus, Takayasu is **unlikely**.

#### 10.7. Paraneoplastic Hypercoagulability  

Paraneoplastic syndromes can cause hypercoagulability, but tumor markers are negative and imaging does not reveal an overt malignancy.  Small, indeterminate pulmonary nodules could represent early neoplasia, yet there is no corroborating evidence; therefore, this remains **possible but low probability**.
