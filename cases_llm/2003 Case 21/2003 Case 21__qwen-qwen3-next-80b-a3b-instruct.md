# Case 21-2003 —A 72-Year-Old Man with Repetitive Strokes in the Posterior Circulation

## Presentation of Case



A 72-year-old, right-handed man was admitted to the hospital because of recurrent neurologic symptoms.



The patient had been well until 10 days earlier, when he briefly had numbness of the right side of the mouth and tongue. That evening, he had slurred speech and a hyponasal quality in his speech for 30 minutes, and vertical diplopia later developed but soon cleared. The next morning, there was a brief recurrence of the vertical diplopia, followed by vertigo. Later that morning, transient diplopia again recurred, and the patient came to this hospital and was admitted.



The patient had undergone an apical lung resection 50 years earlier because of tuberculosis. He had a history of labile hypertension that did not require medication, and he had mild hyperlipidemia that had improved since he began to follow a diet. He had stopped smoking 20 years earlier, and he did not drink alcohol. He had had migraine headaches, but none for the previous eight years. Nine months before admission, the patient had had bilateral sinus pain with clear nasal discharge, which lasted four months and resolved after antibiotic treatment. There was no focal weakness, hemisensory change, difficulty with language, or palpitations, and at the time of admission the patient had no weakness, nausea, vomiting, or imbalance. There was no family history of stroke or aneurysm.



The temperature was 36.2°C, and the blood pressure 140/110 mm Hg. The patient appeared well. No bruits were heard around the neck, and examination of the heart revealed no abnormalities. A neurologic examination revealed only slight tottering to the right on tandem-gait testing and a questionable right Babinski reflex.



The urine was normal. The plasma homocysteine level was 6.0 μmol per liter (normal range, 0 to 12). The levels of urea nitrogen, creatinine, glucose, bilirubin, total protein, albumin, globulin, aspartate aminotransferase, alanine aminotransferase, alkaline phosphatase, creatine kinase, creatine kinase isoenzymes, and troponin T were normal. The patient's condition improved, and he was discharged on the fourth hospital day with instructions to take dalteparin sodium and warfarin without aspirin.

## Differential Diagnosis

The clinical course of this 72-year-old man is characterized by recurrent, multifocal ischemic events confined predominantly to the posterior circulation—cerebellum, brainstem, and occipital lobes—with superimposed systemic findings suggesting an underlying vasculopathic or autoimmune process. The temporal pattern is one of relentless progression: initial transient ischemic attacks (TIAs) involving vertebrobasilar territory, followed by multiple acute infarcts on serial MRI, culminating in worsening neurological deficits including dysarthria, nystagmus, ataxia, and cranial nerve dysfunction. The absence of cardiac embolic sources, absence of large-vessel occlusion on angiography, and the presence of multifocal, irregular stenoses of bilateral vertebral arteries raise the question of a non-atherosclerotic, inflammatory, or autoimmune vasculopathy. However, the patient’s age, long-standing hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and evidence of microangiopathic white matter disease make atherosclerosis a dominant consideration. The challenge lies in distinguishing between atherosclerotic progression, a primary vasculitis, a prothrombotic state, and a systemic connective tissue disorder with vascular involvement.

The most compelling evidence pointing away from pure atherosclerosis is the multifocal, irregular nature of the vertebral artery stenoses, particularly their asymmetry and distribution along the dural ring and mid-vertebral segments—regions less commonly affected by typical atherosclerotic plaques, which tend to localize at ostial or bifurcation sites. The presence of non-calcified, circumferential stenoses at the origin of the left vertebral artery, coupled with elevated intracranial flow velocities on transcranial Doppler and the absence of significant internal carotid disease, suggests a process that is not simply age-related plaque accumulation. Furthermore, the patient’s persistent, progressive neurological deterioration despite anticoagulation and antiplatelet therapy indicates that thrombosis alone cannot account for the injury; the underlying vessel wall pathology must be actively evolving. The dramatic rise in C-reactive protein (29.8 mg/L) strongly implies systemic inflammation, and the positive ANCA serology—though preliminary—is perhaps the most significant clue. ANCA-associated vasculitis (AAV), particularly microscopic polyangiitis (MPA), must be considered as a leading diagnosis. MPA commonly affects small- to medium-sized vessels, including the vertebral and basilar arteries, and can present with multifocal posterior circulation strokes without renal or pulmonary involvement in early stages. The absence of alveolar hemorrhage, glomerulonephritis, or pulmonary nodules does not exclude MPA; up to 30% of patients present with isolated neurological manifestations. The borderline lupus anticoagulant and elevated anticardiolipin antibodies (IgG 56.8 GPL, IgM 28.5 MPL) suggest a secondary antiphospholipid syndrome (APS), which could coexist with vasculitis. However, APS typically causes thrombosis in large vessels and is less commonly associated with the diffuse, irregular stenoses seen here. The absence of thrombocytopenia, livedo reticularis, or recurrent pregnancy loss further weakens a primary APS diagnosis, though secondary APS due to an underlying autoimmune process remains plausible.

Another critical consideration is Takayasu arteritis (TA), a large-vessel vasculitis typically affecting the aorta and its major branches, including the subclavian and vertebral arteries. Although TA is more common in young women, late-onset cases do occur, particularly in older men of Asian descent, and the presentation can be atypical. The patient’s history of pleural calcifications from old tuberculosis raises the possibility of post-tuberculous vascular changes, but the bilateral, multifocal stenoses extending into the intracranial vertebral segments, the elevated inflammatory markers, and the ANCA positivity are not typical of fibrotic sequelae. TA usually presents with pulselessness, asymmetric blood pressures, and bruits—findings partially present here, with diminished left radial pulse and inability to measure BP in the left arm, suggesting subclavian artery stenosis. However, TA typically spares the intracranial vessels and is more often associated with aortic arch syndrome rather than basilar territory ischemia. The angiographic findings here—diffuse irregular stenoses of the vertebral arteries, particularly at the dural ring—do not align with the concentric, long-segment stenoses typical of TA. Furthermore, TA is usually ANCA-negative, making it less likely than MPA.

Giant cell arteritis (GCA) is another large-vessel vasculitis, but it predominantly affects the extracranial branches of the carotid artery, especially the temporal artery, and rarely involves the vertebral or basilar arteries. The patient’s prominent temporal arteries with strong pulses and no tenderness or nodularity make GCA unlikely. Moreover, GCA typically presents with headache, jaw claudication, scalp tenderness, and elevated ESR/CRP in elderly patients—here, CRP is elevated, but the classic symptoms are absent, and the stroke pattern is posterior, not anterior. The lack of visual loss or amaurosis fugax further argues against GCA.

Atherosclerosis remains a critical component of the differential. The patient has multiple risk factors: age, hypertension, hyperlipidemia, prior smoking, and evidence of microangiopathy on MRI. The CT angiography and MRA show diffuse atherosclerotic changes, and the transcranial Doppler findings of increased flow velocities are consistent with severe stenosis. However, the rapid progression of strokes over days to weeks, the multifocal nature of new infarcts in the brainstem and cerebellum, and the lack of progression in the anterior circulation (despite mild carotid stenoses) argue against a purely atherosclerotic etiology. Atherosclerosis tends to progress slowly; the development of multiple acute infarcts in different vascular territories within a week, despite anticoagulation, suggests a hypercoagulable or inflammatory trigger accelerating the process. The patient’s elevated homocysteine (6.0 μmol/L) is within normal limits and thus cannot account for the thrombotic burden. The negative prothrombin G20210A mutation and negative D-dimer further argue against inherited or acute disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)-like states.

The presence of bilateral, small pulmonary nodules on CT—too small to characterize—raises the possibility of systemic vasculitis with pulmonary involvement, such as granulomatosis with polyangiitis (GPA), formerly Wegener’s. However, GPA typically presents with upper and lower respiratory tract involvement (sinusitis, nasal ulcers, pulmonary nodules with cavitation), renal disease, and c-ANCA (PR3-ANCA) positivity. The patient had a history of prolonged sinusitis nine months prior, which resolved with antibiotics. While this could represent an early manifestation of GPA, the absence of current sinonasal symptoms, hemoptysis, or renal dysfunction, along with the ANCA pattern (likely p-ANCA given the association with MPA rather than c-ANCA), makes GPA less likely. The negative serology for syphilis rules out neurosyphilis, which can cause arteritis of the vasa vasorum leading to stroke, particularly in the posterior circulation, but typically presents with tabes dorsalis, Argyll Robertson pupils, and positive serologic tests.

Another rare but critical consideration is primary angiitis of the central nervous system (PACNS), a vasculitis confined to the brain and spinal cord, without systemic involvement. PACNS can present with recurrent strokes, cognitive decline, and headaches, and often shows multifocal stenoses on angiography. However, PACNS typically affects small and medium-sized intracranial arteries, not the extracranial vertebral arteries, and is usually ANCA-negative. The angiographic findings here include extracranial vertebral stenoses, which are not typical of PACNS, and the systemic inflammation (CRP elevation) and positive ANCA point to a systemic vasculitis rather than a CNS-limited process. Additionally, the CSF findings—clear fluid with 14 RBCs, no WBCs, normal glucose, and mildly elevated protein—are inconsistent with active CNS vasculitis, which often shows lymphocytic pleocytosis and elevated protein.

The possibility of a paraneoplastic vasculopathy must be considered, particularly given the small pulmonary nodules and the patient’s age. However, tumor markers (AFP, PSA, CEA) are normal, and there is no lymphadenopathy, weight loss, or other systemic signs of malignancy. Paraneoplastic syndromes rarely cause the pattern of multifocal vasculopathy seen here, and the temporal association with ANCA positivity makes an autoimmune mechanism more plausible.

The patient’s history of apical lung resection for tuberculosis 50 years ago is intriguing. While tuberculosis can cause vascular complications via granulomatous inflammation of the vasa vasorum, this typically occurs during active infection and is rarely seen decades later. The pleural calcifications are classic for healed TB, but the current vascular findings are not consistent with post-tuberculous fibrosis. Moreover, the presence of ANCA and CRP elevation is not explained by old TB.

The antiphospholipid antibody profile is significant. The IgG anticardiolipin level is markedly elevated (>3x upper limit), and the borderline lupus anticoagulant suggests a prothrombotic state. According to the Sydney criteria, this meets the serologic threshold for antiphospholipid syndrome (APS). However, APS typically causes thrombosis in large vessels (deep vein thrombosis, pulmonary embolism, stroke in anterior circulation) and is less commonly associated with multiple small-vessel infarcts in the posterior circulation. Moreover, APS-related strokes are usually single, large-vessel occlusions, not the multifocal, small, punctate lesions seen here. The coexistence of APS with an underlying autoimmune vasculitis (secondary APS) is well documented, particularly in systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) or MPA. The absence of other SLE criteria (malar rash, arthritis, renal disease, positive dsDNA) makes primary SLE unlikely, but the possibility of an overlap syndrome with MPA and secondary APS remains compelling.

The patient’s response to therapy also informs the differential. The initial use of heparin and warfarin did not halt progression, suggesting that the pathology is not purely thrombotic. The addition of aspirin was ineffective. The initiation of clopidogrel and plasmapheresis, along with the clinical worsening despite these measures, suggests an active inflammatory vasculitis rather than a simple hypercoagulable state. Plasmapheresis is rarely used in atherosclerosis or primary APS, but is a cornerstone of treatment in severe AAV, especially with neurological or renal involvement. The fact that it was initiated strongly implies clinical suspicion of vasculitis.

The lumbar puncture findings are unremarkable—no pleocytosis, normal glucose, normal protein—arguing against infectious or inflammatory CNS processes such as meningitis, encephalitis, or even active PACNS. The absence of oligoclonal bands or elevated IgG index further reduces suspicion for multiple sclerosis or neurosarcoidosis.

In summary, the most likely diagnosis is microscopic polyangiitis (MPA), an ANCA-associated vasculitis affecting small- to medium-sized vessels, including the vertebral arteries. The multifocal, irregular stenoses on angiography, the systemic inflammation (elevated CRP), the positive ANCA serology, the recurrent posterior circulation strokes despite anticoagulation, and the partial response to plasmapheresis all align with MPA. The antiphospholipid antibodies are likely secondary to the underlying autoimmune process. The atherosclerosis is a contributing factor, particularly given the patient’s age and vascular risk profile, but cannot explain the rapid progression and multifocal nature of the strokes. Other vasculitides (Takayasu, GCA, GPA) are less likely due to anatomical and serological mismatches. Primary APS and PACNS are also less consistent with the full clinical and radiological picture. The case represents a classic, albeit atypical, presentation of MPA with predominant neurological involvement—a diagnosis that requires prompt immunosuppression with cyclophosphamide or rituximab and glucocorticoids, which should have been initiated alongside plasmapheresis. The absence of renal or pulmonary symptoms does not rule out MPA; neurological involvement can be the dominant or even sole manifestation, particularly in elderly patients. This case underscores the importance of considering vasculitis in any elderly patient with recurrent, multifocal strokes in the posterior circulation, especially when conventional risk factors and therapies fail to explain the clinical trajectory.
