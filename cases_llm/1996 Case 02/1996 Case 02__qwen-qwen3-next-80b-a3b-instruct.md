# Case 2-1996 —A 32-Year-Old Woman with Subcostal Pain and a Left Hepatic Mass

## Presentation of Case



A 32-year-old woman was admitted to the hospital because of left subcostal pain, bouts of fever, and a mass in the left hepatic lobe.



The patient had been well until four years earlier, when she began to have progressive, constant left subcostal pain, with an intermittent increase in the temperature to 39.4°C, anorexia, and nausea. The episodes occurred approximately every six months and lasted for a week or two; they had recently begun to occur every four months.



Three months before admission an evaluation elsewhere included an ultrasonographic examination, a computed tomographic (CT) scan of the abdomen, and a magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) scan, which showed a lesion in the left hepatic lobe that was interpreted as a hemangioma. Because of worsening pain she came to this hospital.



The patient was an unemployed child-care worker. She had a history of eczema and of asthma after exposure to multiple environmental antigens. She had lost 18 kg in weight during the preceding 18 months. Her only medications were an albuterol inhaler, which was used as needed, and an oral contraceptive, which she had taken during the month before admission. There was no history of jaundice, dark urine, light stools, intravenous drug abuse, hypertension, diabetes mellitus, tuberculosis, risk factors for infection with the human immunodeficiency virus, or a change in bowel habits. She did not smoke and drank little alcohol.



The temperature was 36.5°C, the pulse was 68, and the respirations were 16. The blood pressure was 110/50 mm Hg.



On examination the patient was slim and appeared well. Scaling eczematous plaques were present on the hands and elbows. The head and neck were normal; no tenderness was detected in the spine or the costovertebral angles. The lungs, heart, and breasts were normal. An abdominal examination revealed a soft systolic bruit in the midepigastrium that overlay a firm, nontender mass, 8 cm in diameter; the mass had a smooth surface and moved with respirations. A rectal examination was normal; a stool specimen was negative for occult blood. The arms and legs were normal, and a neurologic examination was normal.



The urine was normal. The hematocrit was 32.8 percent, the white-cell count was 7400 per cubic millimeter, and the platelet count was 365,000 per cubic millimeter; the mean corpuscular volume was 85 μm . The prothrombin and partial-thromboplastin times were normal. The serum aspartate aminotransferase level was 27 U per liter; the values for urea nitrogen, creatinine, bilirubin, calcium, phosphorus, total protein, albumin, globulin, electrolytes, and alkaline phosphatase were normal. An electrocardiogram and radiographs of the chest were normal. The imaging studies performed elsewhere were reviewed. The abdominal ultrasonographic examination showed a heterogeneous solid mass, 8 cm by 7 cm, in the left hepatic lobe; the remainder of the liver was normal. The abdominal CT scan revealed focal areas of increased enhancement in the mass, which suggested a slightly hypervascular component, and nonenhancing central areas, which suggested a central scar or necrosis; no calcifications were seen in the mass. The MRI scan showed that the mass had lower signal intensity than the normal right lobe of the liver on T    -weighted and proton-density images and had signal intensity similar to that of normal liver on T    -weighted images. A mesenteric angiographic examination, performed at this hospital, revealed a hypervascular mass in the lateral segment of the left hepatic lobe, with tumor vascularity on the arterial phase and a central area of decreased enhancement on delayed images, suggesting a scar or necrosis. The portal vein was patent, and no lymphadenopathy or other abnormality was seen.



A diagnostic procedure was performed.

## Differential Diagnosis

The clinical presentation of this 32-year-old woman—chronic left subcostal pain, intermittent high fevers, significant unintentional weight loss, and a well-defined, hypervascular hepatic mass with central scar-like necrosis—demands a broad yet methodical differential diagnosis. The absence of jaundice, normal liver enzymes, normal bilirubin, and absence of risk factors for viral hepatitis or alcohol-related liver disease effectively exclude common causes of hepatic dysfunction such as cirrhosis, steatohepatitis, or viral hepatitis. The normal renal, hematologic, and metabolic parameters further narrow the focus to a primary or metastatic process localized to the liver, with systemic inflammatory or neoplastic features. The key diagnostic clues are the chronicity of symptoms (four years), the cyclical nature of fevers (every four to six months), the 18 kg weight loss over 18 months, the presence of a large (8 cm), hypervascular, solid hepatic mass with central necrosis on imaging, and the associated systolic bruit—a rare but highly significant physical finding.

The most compelling imaging findings are the hypervascularity on angiography and CT, the central nonenhancing area suggestive of a scar or necrosis, and the MRI signal characteristics: low signal on T1 and proton-density sequences, and isointense signal on T2, which is atypical for a simple hemangioma. Although the prior interpretation of the lesion as a hemangioma was reasonable, it is now highly questionable. Classic hepatic hemangiomas are typically well-circumscribed, hypervascular on arterial phase imaging, but they are usually homogeneous, lack central necrosis, and demonstrate progressive peripheral nodular enhancement on delayed phases, not a persistent central scar. The presence of a central scar is more characteristic of focal nodular hyperplasia (FNH), but FNH is typically asymptomatic, does not cause fever or weight loss, and is not associated with systemic symptoms. Moreover, FNH is usually isointense on T1 and hyperintense on T2, whereas this lesion is isointense on T2—more suggestive of a solid tumor with fibrotic or necrotic components. The systolic bruit over the mass is perhaps the most telling physical sign. Vascular bruits over hepatic masses are exceedingly rare and are most classically associated with hypervascular tumors such as hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC), hepatic adenoma, or, less commonly, metastatic disease with rich neovascularity. The presence of a bruit implies high-flow arterial supply, often due to tumor angiogenesis or arteriovenous shunting, and is virtually never seen in benign lesions such as hemangiomas or simple cysts.

Given the patient’s age, absence of cirrhosis, and lack of known etiologies for HCC (hepatitis B or C, alcohol, NAFLD, aflatoxin exposure), hepatocellular carcinoma remains an unlikely but not impossible diagnosis. HCC can arise in non-cirrhotic livers, particularly in the setting of metabolic syndrome, hereditary hemochromatosis, or alpha-1-antitrypsin deficiency—none of which are present here. However, the chronic inflammatory state suggested by recurrent fevers and weight loss could theoretically create a procarcinogenic microenvironment. The central necrosis could represent tumor outgrowth beyond its blood supply, and the hypervascularity is consistent with HCC. Yet, the absence of elevated alpha-fetoprotein (AFP)—though not routinely measured here—is a major argument against HCC. AFP is elevated in approximately 70% of HCC cases, and its absence in a patient with a large hypervascular mass should raise suspicion for alternative diagnoses. Furthermore, HCC rarely presents with systemic symptoms such as intermittent fevers and weight loss unless it is advanced or necrotizing; the cyclical pattern of fever every four months is not typical of HCC, which usually presents with progressive symptoms.

The intermittent fevers, occurring every four months and resolving spontaneously after a week or two, suggest an infectious or inflammatory etiology. The absence of night sweats, chills, or rigors, and the lack of lymphadenopathy or pulmonary findings, make tuberculosis less likely, though hepatic TB can present as a solitary mass with necrosis and fever. Miliary TB typically presents with diffuse nodules, but localized hepatic tuberculomas can occur, especially in immunocompromised individuals. However, this patient has no known immunodeficiency, no history of TB exposure, and no radiographic lung findings. The lack of eosinophilia, normal ESR (not reported but implied by normal inflammatory markers), and absence of granulomas on biopsy (though not yet performed) further argue against TB. Fungal infections such as histoplasmosis or candidiasis are also unlikely without risk factors such as HIV, prolonged antibiotics, or immunosuppression. Brucellosis, a zoonotic infection, can cause hepatic granulomas, intermittent fevers, and weight loss, and is associated with hepatomegaly and hepatic abscesses. However, brucellar liver lesions are typically multiple, hypoechoic, and lack the hypervascularity and central scar seen here. Moreover, brucellosis usually presents with arthralgias, splenomegaly, and a history of exposure to unpasteurized dairy or livestock—none of which are present.

Another infectious consideration is pyogenic liver abscess. These are typically tender, feverish, and associated with leukocytosis, elevated alkaline phosphatase, and often a history of biliary disease, diverticulitis, or appendicitis. This patient has no abdominal pain between episodes, no jaundice, normal liver enzymes, and no evidence of biliary obstruction. The mass is described as firm and nontender, and the absence of elevated inflammatory markers (WBC normal, normal CRP not reported but implied by lack of mention) makes acute infection less likely. However, chronic or partially treated abscesses can present as complex hepatic masses with central necrosis and low-grade systemic symptoms. The intermittent fevers could represent recurrent microabscess formation or incomplete drainage. The hypervascularity on imaging could reflect granulation tissue or vascular proliferation around the abscess cavity. The central scar could be a fibrotic wall or organizing necrosis. Yet, the lack of prior antibiotic use, absence of biliary pathology, and normal bilirubin and alkaline phosphatase make this less compelling.

The presence of eczema and asthma, both atopic conditions, raises the possibility of an underlying immune dysregulation or eosinophilic disorder. Hypereosinophilic syndrome (HES) can cause hepatic infiltrates, eosinophilic hepatitis, and hepatic masses due to eosinophilic infiltration and tissue necrosis. Eosinophilic abscesses in the liver can mimic tumors and may present with fever, weight loss, and elevated IgE (not measured here). However, the white blood cell count is normal, and no eosinophilia is reported—this is a critical missing piece. If the eosinophil count were elevated, HES would be a top consideration. But in the absence of eosinophilia, this diagnosis is unlikely, though not entirely ruled out, as some variants of HES have normal eosinophil counts but tissue infiltration. A liver biopsy would be required to identify eosinophilic infiltration.

The most compelling alternative is a benign but locally aggressive tumor: hepatic adenoma. Hepatic adenomas are estrogen-dependent tumors, commonly seen in young women on oral contraceptives—this patient is taking an oral contraceptive, which is a major risk factor. Adenomas can grow to large sizes (>5 cm), become symptomatic due to hemorrhage or rupture, and may present with pain and fever if there is intratumoral necrosis or microhemorrhage. They are typically hypervascular on imaging and may have central scar-like areas due to fibrosis or hemorrhagic necrosis. The T1 and T2 signal characteristics described are consistent with adenoma: hypointense on T1 (due to fat or hemorrhage), isointense on T2. The systolic bruit is highly suggestive of adenoma, as these tumors are often supplied by multiple arterial branches and can develop arteriovenous shunts. The intermittent fevers could be due to small, self-resolving hemorrhagic episodes or inflammatory reactions to necrotic tissue. The weight loss is unusual for adenoma, but could reflect chronic pain, anorexia, or a systemic inflammatory response. Importantly, adenomas carry a risk of malignant transformation to hepatocellular carcinoma, particularly those larger than 5 cm, which this one is. The fact that the patient has had symptoms for four years and the lesion has grown (implied by worsening pain) supports a slowly progressive tumor rather than a static lesion like a hemangioma.

Another rare but critical consideration is a hepatic metastasis from a primary malignancy. The weight loss, fever, and mass are classic red flags. The most common primaries that metastasize to the liver are lung, breast, melanoma, and gastrointestinal cancers. However, the imaging characteristics are unusual for metastases: most are multiple, hypovascular, and lack central scars. A solitary metastasis is rare but possible, especially from renal cell carcinoma, thyroid cancer, or sarcoma. Renal cell carcinoma (RCC) is particularly relevant here. RCC can present with a solitary hepatic metastasis, often hypervascular, with central necrosis and a fibrotic scar. It can also cause paraneoplastic syndromes, including fever of unknown origin and weight loss. The patient is a young woman, and RCC is more common in men, but it does occur in women. There is no history of hematuria, flank pain, or hypertension—classic RCC symptoms—but RCC can be asymptomatic until metastatic. The systemic symptoms fit perfectly: intermittent fevers, weight loss, and a single large liver lesion. The systolic bruit could represent tumor neovascularity or arteriovenous shunting.

Another possibility is a primary hepatic sarcoma—such as angiosarcoma or epithelioid hemangioendothelioma. Angiosarcoma is rare, aggressive, and often associated with exposure to vinyl chloride or arsenic, neither of which are present. It typically presents with rapid growth, hemorrhage, and liver failure, not the slow progression seen here. Epithelioid hemangioendothelioma is a low-grade vascular tumor that can present as multiple or solitary hepatic masses, often with central fibrosis and necrosis. It is more common in women and can be associated with chronic inflammation or estrogen exposure. The MRI signal characteristics and hypervascularity are consistent. Weight loss and fever can occur. However, it is usually multifocal, and the solitary nature of this lesion makes it less likely.

The lesion is large, hypervascular, and has a central scar—consistent with adenoma. The intermittent fevers likely reflect small, self-limiting hemorrhagic or necrotic events within the tumor. The systolic bruit is a classic, albeit rare, finding in large hepatic adenomas due to high arterial flow and shunting. The prior misdiagnosis as hemangioma is common, as both can be hypervascular on arterial phase imaging. However, the central scar and lack of classic peripheral nodular enhancement on MRI argue against hemangioma. While FNH is typically asymptomatic, a subset with prominent inflammatory infiltrates and central fibrotic scars can present with pain and low-grade fever. The hormonal influence of oral contraceptives can stimulate FNH growth. However, FNH does not typically cause weight loss or a bruit, and its imaging characteristics are usually more homogeneous than this lesion.
