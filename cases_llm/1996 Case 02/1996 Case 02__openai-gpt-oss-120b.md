### 1. Benign Vascular and Hypervascular Hepatic Lesions  

#### 1.1. Cavernous Hemangioma (including “giant” hemangioma)  

**Epidemiology & Pathogenesis** – Cavernous hemangiomas are the most common benign liver tumor, occurring in up to 5 % of the adult population and with a slight female predominance. They are composed of large, blood‑filled vascular channels lined by a single layer of endothelium, separated by fibrous septa. Giant hemangiomas (>5 cm) can become symptomatic because of mass effect, capsular stretch, or intralesional thrombosis/hemorrhage.

**Typical Imaging** – On contrast‑enhanced CT or MRI, classic hemangiomas demonstrate peripheral, discontinuous nodular enhancement in the arterial phase with progressive centripetal fill‑in on delayed phases, eventually becoming isodense/isointense with the surrounding liver. Central necrosis or fibrosis may be seen in very large lesions, producing a low‑attenuation core that can mimic a scar. Calcifications are uncommon. Doppler ultrasound may reveal a slow‑flow vascular pattern.

**Laboratory & Clinical Correlates** – Most patients are asymptomatic; when symptoms occur they are usually vague right‑upper‑quadrant discomfort, early satiety, or a palpable mass. Fever, weight loss, or a systemic inflammatory response are atypical, though rare reports describe low‑grade fever associated with tumor necrosis or secondary infection.

**Fit to the Present Case** – The lesion’s size (≈8 cm) and hypervascular arterial phase are compatible with a giant hemangioma. The central non‑enhancing area could represent necrosis or fibrosis. However, the recurrent high‑grade fevers (up to 39.4 °C), significant weight loss (18 kg in 18 months), and the presence of a systolic bruit over the mass are not characteristic of a benign hemangioma. Moreover, the imaging description lacks the classic peripheral nodular enhancement and centripetal fill‑in; instead, the CT report emphasizes “focal areas of increased enhancement” and a central scar‑like region, which is more suggestive of a lesion with a true fibrous scar rather than simple necrosis. Consequently, while a hemangioma remains a possibility, the systemic manifestations argue against a purely benign vascular tumor.

#### 1.2. Focal Nodular Hyperplasia (FNH)  

**Epidemiology & Pathogenesis** – FNH is the second most common benign hepatic lesion, predominantly affecting women of reproductive age (median age 30–40). It is thought to arise from a localized hyperplastic response to a congenital arteriovenous malformation, leading to a central stellate scar composed of fibrous tissue and malformed vessels.

**Typical Imaging** – On MRI, FNH classically appears isointense to liver on T1‑weighted images and iso‑ to mildly hyperintense on T2‑weighted images. The hallmark is a central scar that is hypointense on T1, hyperintense on T2, and shows delayed enhancement after gadolinium due to its fibrous nature. In the arterial phase, the lesion is intensely hypervascular, often becoming isointense in the portal venous phase. The lesion usually lacks a true capsule and is well‑circumscribed.

**Laboratory & Clinical Correlates** – FNH is almost always asymptomatic and discovered incidentally. Serum liver enzymes, AFP, and tumor markers are normal. Fever, weight loss, or systemic inflammatory signs are exceedingly rare.

**Fit to the Present Case** – The patient’s age and sex fit the epidemiology of FNH, and the imaging description of a hypervascular mass with a central scar is reminiscent of FNH. However, the presence of recurrent high‑grade fevers, substantial weight loss, and a palpable, firm mass that is tender to deep palpation (despite being described as “nontender,” the chronic pain suggests capsular stretch) are discordant. Moreover, FNH typically does not generate a bruit, whereas the auscultated systolic bruit suggests a high‑flow arterial shunt, more characteristic of a true neoplasm or a large arteriovenous malformation. Therefore, while FNH must be considered, the systemic symptomatology makes it unlikely to be the primary driver of the patient’s illness.

#### 1.3. Hepatocellular Adenoma (HCA)  

**Epidemiology & Pathogenesis** – HCAs are benign epithelial tumors that arise almost exclusively in women of childbearing age and are strongly associated with oral‑contraceptive (OC) use, anabolic steroid exposure, and metabolic disorders (e.g., glycogen storage disease). The lesion is composed of sheets of hepatocytes lacking normal portal triads, and may harbor beta‑catenin or inflammatory molecular subtypes.

**Typical Imaging** – On arterial‑phase CT or MRI, HCAs appear markedly hyperenhancing, often with a homogeneous appearance. Some subtypes (e.g., inflammatory adenoma) may demonstrate a peripheral rim of enhancement and central low‑attenuation areas due to hemorrhage or necrosis. Unlike FNH, HCAs typically lack a central scar. Small lesions may be iso‑ or hypointense on T1 and hyperintense on T2. The presence of fat or hemorrhage can alter signal characteristics.

**Clinical Features** – Most HCAs are asymptomatic; larger lesions (>5 cm) can cause right‑upper‑quadrant discomfort, a palpable mass, or spontaneous hemorrhage. Fever and systemic inflammatory signs are uncommon, although the inflammatory subtype can be associated with mild elevations in C‑reactive protein. Elevated serum AFP is rare.

**Fit to the Present Case** – The patient’s recent OC use (taken during the month before admission) is a relevant exposure, and the lesion’s size (>5 cm) is within the range where adenomas become symptomatic. The imaging description of hypervascularity and central non‑enhancing region could correspond to hemorrhagic necrosis within a large adenoma. However, the presence of a well‑defined central scar is not typical for HCA. Moreover, the episodic fevers, marked weight loss, and a bruit are not characteristic of an adenoma. While HCA cannot be excluded, especially given the OC exposure, the systemic manifestations remain difficult to reconcile.

#### 1.4. Hepatic Arteriovenous Malformation (AVM) / Focal Hypervascular Lesion  

**Epidemiology & Pathogenesis** – Congenital hepatic AVMs are rare, often presenting in children, but acquired high‑flow lesions can develop secondary to trauma, iatrogenic injury, or vascular tumors. A large AVM can produce a palpable mass, a continuous bruit, and high‑output cardiac findings.

**Imaging & Clinical Correlates** – Doppler ultrasound demonstrates turbulent arterial flow with arterial‑phase early venous enhancement (“early venous return”). CT may show a tangle of vessels with rapid enhancement and washout. Patients may develop portal hypertension or heart failure but rarely have fever or weight loss.

**Fit to the Present Case** – The presence of a systolic bruit over the mass is a clue toward a high‑flow vascular lesion. Nonetheless, the mass appears solid rather than a tangle of vessels, and the central scar/necrosis is not typical for an AVM. The lack of cardiac findings (normal pulse, blood pressure, no signs of high‑output state) further reduces the likelihood of a dominant AVM.

**Summary of Benign Hypervascular Lesions** – The most plausible benign entities—giant hemangioma, FNH, and HCA—each explain some imaging features but fail to account for the systemic inflammatory picture (recurrent fevers, weight loss) and the auscultatory bruit. Consequently, while they remain in the differential, the balance of evidence tilts toward a neoplastic process with a more aggressive biology.

### 2. Primary Malignant Hepatic Neoplasms  

#### 2.1. Fibrolamellar Hepatocellular Carcinoma (FL‑HCC)  

**Epidemiology & Pathogenesis** – FL‑HCC is a distinct variant of HCC that occurs almost exclusively in adolescents and young adults (median age 25–35) without underlying cirrhosis or chronic liver disease. It is not associated with viral hepatitis, alcohol, or aflatoxin exposure. Molecularly, a characteristic DNAJB1‑PRKACA fusion protein drives oncogenesis. The tumor is composed of large polygonal hepatocytes with abundant eosinophilic cytoplasm, laminated fibrous stroma, and a central scar.

**Imaging Characteristics** – On contrast‑enhanced CT or MRI, FL‑HCC typically appears as a well‑circumscribed, hypervascular mass in the arterial phase, often with a central scar that is hypodense on CT and hypointense on T1‑weighted MRI, but which shows delayed enhancement (reflecting fibrous tissue). The scar is a key differentiator from conventional HCC, which usually lacks a central scar. On angiography, the lesion demonstrates a prominent arterial supply with a “central scar” that remains hypoenhancing in delayed phases—a pattern described in the present case.

**Laboratory Profile** – Serum alpha‑fetoprotein (AFP) is usually normal or only mildly elevated, contrasting with conventional HCC where AFP is frequently markedly raised. Liver enzymes and bilirubin are often normal because the tumor grows in a non‑cirrhotic liver. Mild anemia may be present due to chronic disease or occult hemorrhage.

**Clinical Presentation** – Patients typically present with vague abdominal discomfort, a palpable mass, and systemic symptoms such as low‑grade fever, night sweats, and unintentional weight loss. The constitutional symptoms are thought to be mediated by cytokine release from the tumor. Fever spikes up to 39 °C, as reported in this case, are not unusual. Because the tumor can be large at presentation, it may cause capsular stretch and referred subcostal pain, exactly as observed.

**Fit to the Present Case** – The patient’s age (32 y), female sex, lack of cirrhosis or viral hepatitis, normal liver function tests, and normal AFP (not reported but likely normal given the full panel) align perfectly with FL‑HCC. Imaging shows a hypervascular left‑lobe mass with a central scar or necrosis, a classic radiologic signature of fibrolamellar carcinoma. The presence of a systolic bruit could be explained by the tumor’s arterial neovascularity. The recurrent fevers, progressive weight loss, and chronic left‑subcostal pain constitute a textbook systemic presentation. Importantly, the lesion’s size (≈8 cm) is within the typical range at diagnosis. Consequently, FL‑HCC emerges as the leading diagnostic consideration.

#### 2.2. Conventional Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC) in a Non‑Cirrhotic Liver  

**Epidemiology & Pathogenesis** – While most HCCs arise in cirrhotic livers, a minority (≈10–20 %) develop in non‑cirrhotic livers, often linked to non‑alcoholic fatty liver disease, hereditary hemochromatosis, or exposure to aflatoxin. HCC in a young woman without risk factors is rare but not impossible.

**Imaging** – Typical HCC displays intense arterial phase enhancement with rapid washout in the portal venous phase. A central scar is unusual, being more characteristic of the fibrolamellar variant. Calcifications may be present in some subtypes (e.g., cholangiocellular). **Laboratory** – AFP is often markedly elevated (>400 ng/mL) in conventional HCC, although some tumors are AFP‑negative. Liver enzymes may be mildly elevated; bilirubin can be normal unless biliary obstruction occurs.

**Clinical Features** – Patients often have a background of chronic liver disease, may develop jaundice, ascites, or hepatic decompensation. Systemic symptoms such as fever and weight loss can occur but are less common at presentation.

**Fit to the Present Case** – The absence of any known liver disease, normal AFP (presumed), and the imaging pattern with a central scar argue against conventional HCC. While it cannot be excluded entirely, the probability is markedly lower than that for FL‑HCC.

#### 2.3. Intrahepatic Cholangiocarcinoma (ICC)  

**Epidemiology & Pathogenesis** – ICC arises from the biliary epithelium and typically presents in patients over 50 y, often with underlying cholestatic disease (primary sclerosing cholangitis, Caroli disease). It is a hypovascular tumor that frequently shows peripheral rim enhancement and delayed central enhancement due to desmoplastic stroma.

**Imaging** – On CT/MRI, ICC often appears as a heterogeneous mass with peripheral enhancement and a delayed central enhancement (the “target” sign). A central scar is uncommon; instead, a central area of low attenuation reflects necrosis or fibrosis. The tumor is usually hypovascular relative to the surrounding liver in the arterial phase.

**Laboratory & Clinical** – Elevated alkaline phosphatase and gamma‑glutamyl transpeptidase are frequent, reflecting cholestasis. CA 19‑9 may be modestly elevated. Fever and weight loss can occur but are not typical presenting features.

**Fit to the Present Case** – The patient’s normal alkaline phosphatase, normal bilirubin, and hypervascular arterial phase are discordant with ICC. The central scar is more characteristic of FL‑HCC. Thus, ICC is an unlikely diagnosis.

#### 2.4. Hepatic Angiosarcoma  

**Epidemiology & Pathogenesis** – Hepatic angiosarcoma is a rare, aggressive malignant vascular tumor, constituting <2 % of primary liver cancers. It is classically associated with exposure to vinyl chloride, arsenic, thorium dioxide, or anabolic steroids. Median age is 60–70 y; however, sporadic cases in younger adults have been reported.

**Imaging** – These tumors are typically heterogeneous, with areas of hemorrhage, necrosis, and calcifications. On contrast‑enhanced studies, they demonstrate peripheral nodular enhancement with rapid washout, sometimes mimicking a hemangioma. Central necrosis is common. The lesions are often multifocal.

**Clinical & Laboratory** – Patients present with abdominal pain, weight loss, and occasionally constitutional symptoms (fever). Laboratory tests are nonspecific; liver enzymes may be mildly elevated. Coagulopathy can develop due to tumor‑related consumption of clotting factors.

**Fit to the Present Case** – The patient’s lack of known exposure to known carcinogens, her relatively young age, and the solitary nature of the lesion make angiosarcoma less probable. #### 2.5. Hepatoblastoma (Adult Variant)  

**Epidemiology & Pathogenesis** – Hepatoblastoma is a pediatric tumor; adult cases are exceedingly rare (≈100 reported). When they occur, they are usually associated with underlying liver disease or germline mutations (e.g., β‑catenin).

**Imaging & Clinical** – Adult hepatoblastoma may appear as a large, heterogeneous mass with mixed solid and cystic components, often with calcifications. AFP is markedly elevated (often >1000 ng/mL). Systemic symptoms are variable.

**Fit to the Present Case** – The patient’s age and normal AFP (presumed) make hepatoblastoma highly unlikely.

**Summary of Primary Malignant Tumors** – The imaging hallmark of a hypervascular lesion with a central scar, coupled with normal liver function and systemic inflammatory symptoms, points most strongly toward fibrolamellar HCC. ### 3. Metastatic Hepatic Lesions

By systematically juxtaposing the patient’s demographic profile, clinical course (recurrent high‑grade fevers, substantial weight loss, chronic left‑subcostal pain), laboratory data (normal liver function, normal coagulation, mild anemia), and detailed imaging findings (arterial‑phase hypervascularity, central non‑enhancing scar, absence of calcifications, solitary left‑lobe location), the most compelling diagnosis is **fibrolamellar hepatocellular carcinoma**.

Fibrolamellar HCC uniquely integrates all observed elements: it occurs in young adults without cirrhosis, presents as a hypervascular mass with a central fibrous scar, often elicits systemic inflammatory symptoms (fever, weight loss), and typically shows normal AFP and liver chemistries. The presence of an audible bruit over the lesion further supports a highly arterial tumor.
