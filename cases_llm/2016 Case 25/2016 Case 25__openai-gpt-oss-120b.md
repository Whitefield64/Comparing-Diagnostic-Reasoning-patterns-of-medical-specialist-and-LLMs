The patient reported that he had had a sexual encounter with a man approximately 6 weeks earlier. On examination, a tender external hemorrhoid was noted; the vital signs and the remainder of the examination were normal. Approximately 10 weeks later, the patient returned to the primary care clinic and reported that rectal pain and bleeding with defecation had persisted. On examination, the patient appeared well. Psyllium, naproxen, and topical hydrocortisone cream were continued, and the patient was referred to the colorectal surgery clinic at this hospital.



At the surgery clinic, the patient reported that the rectal pain and bleeding persisted and that he had not noted any protrusion of tissue through the anus. His medications included naproxen, as needed, and hydrocortisone cream applied topically to the anus, which had provided only minimal pain relief. He had no known allergies. He was bisexual; he reported that he had never had a sexually transmitted infection. His mother had hypertension. There was no family history of colorectal cancer.

### 1. Anal Fissure – Primary Consideration  

**Typical clinical picture**  
Anal fissures are longitudinal mucosal tears most often located in the posterior (≈ 70 %) or anterior (≈ 20 %) midline.  They classically cause sharp, stabbing pain during and shortly after defecation, frequently accompanied by a small amount of bright‑red blood that is noted on the toilet paper.  The pain is usually out of proportion to the amount of bleeding, and chronic fissures may develop a hypertrophic edge, a sentinel skin tag, and a hypertrophied anal papilla.  The hallmark is severe, episodic pain that can last minutes to hours after a bowel movement, often leading to avoidance of defecation and a secondary component of constipation.  

**Correlation with the case**  
- **Pain pattern** – The patient’s pain is described as “painful bowel movements” that have persisted for months; this aligns with the typical fissure‑related pain that is triggered by stool passage.  
- **Bleeding** – Bright red blood on toilet paper, without blood mixed in the stool, is classic for fissure‑related bleeding.  
- **Physical findings** – The presence of **large, superficial fissures**, **sentinel skin tags**, and **moderate tenderness on digital rectal examination** are textbook for chronic fissures.  
- **Absence of constipation** – Although many fissures are associated with hard stools, they can occur in patients with normal stool consistency, especially when there is a predisposing factor such as local trauma, an underlying inflammatory process, or a history of anal sexual activity that can cause micro‑tears.  

**Pathophysiology**  
The anal canal is supplied by the superior, middle, and inferior rectal arteries, creating a watershed area at the distal anal canal.  Ischemia and sphincter spasm at the site of a mucosal tear impair healing, establishing a vicious cycle of pain → sphincter spasm → reduced perfusion → delayed healing.  Chronic fissures develop a fibrotic edge and a hypertrophied papilla (sentinel tag) that may be mistaken for a small polyp or a neoplastic lesion.  

**Arguments against**  
- **Mild distal proctitis** – The sigmoidoscopic finding of edema and patchy erythema extending 10 cm proximally is not typical for an isolated fissure, which is confined to the anal verge.  However, chronic fissuring can induce a low‑grade inflammatory response in the adjacent distal rectal mucosa.  
- **External hemorrhoids** – While hemorrhoids co‑exist with fissures, they could be an independent source of bleeding; still, the fissure explains the pain more convincingly.  

**Conclusion**  
Anal fissure, especially a chronic fissure with sentinel tags, is the most straightforward explanation for the patient’s pain and bleeding.  The presence of modest distal proctitis suggests either a concomitant process (infectious or inflammatory) or a secondary inflammatory reaction to the fissure.  The fissure remains the cornerstone of the differential and must be addressed therapeutically (e.g., stool softeners, topical nifedipine or nitroglycerin, sitz baths) regardless of other diagnoses.

2. **Endoscopic Biopsies**  
   - Obtain **targeted biopsies** of the distal rectal mucosa (the erythematous area) and of the fissure edges if feasible.  Histology will clarify whether there is **crypt architectural distortion** (suggestive of IBD), **granulomas** (Crohn’s), **viral cytopathic changes**, or **dysplasia/neoplasia**.  

3. **High‑Resolution Anoscopy**  
   - Perform **anoscopy with a colposcope** to examine the sentinel tags and fissure margins more closely.  Any area that appears indurated or atypical should be biopsied to exclude early anal carcinoma.  

4. **Medical Management**

5. **Follow‑up**  
   - Re‑evaluate in **4–6 weeks** after initiating fissure‑focused therapy.  Persistent pain or bleeding warrants repeat sigmoidoscopy with biopsies.
