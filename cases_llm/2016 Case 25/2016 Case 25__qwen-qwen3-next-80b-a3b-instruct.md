# Case 25-2016 —A 33-Year-Old Man with Rectal Pain and Bleeding

## Presentation of Case



   *Dr. Robert H. Goldstein* (Medicine): A 33-year-old man was seen in the surgery clinic of this hospital because of painful bowel movements and rectal bleeding.



The patient had been well until approximately 4 months before this presentation, when pain with defecation developed. He typically had one bowel movement each day and noted bright red blood on the toilet paper after wiping, although blood did not appear to be mixed in with the stool itself. The patient had not had abdominal pain or weight loss. On the sixth day of illness, he was seen by his primary care physician. The patient reported that he had had a sexual encounter with a man approximately 6 weeks earlier. On examination, a tender external hemorrhoid was noted; the vital signs and the remainder of the examination were normal. Tests were negative for hepatitis B surface antigen and antibodies to hepatitis B virus, hepatitis C virus, and the human immunodeficiency virus (HIV). Psyllium, naproxen, and topical hydrocortisone cream were prescribed.



Approximately 10 weeks later, the patient returned to the primary care clinic and reported that rectal pain and bleeding with defecation had persisted. On examination, the patient appeared well. The temperature was 36.4°C, the pulse 82 beats per minute, and the blood pressure 126/76 mm Hg. Bleeding hemorrhoids were noted around the anal opening; the remainder of the examination was normal. Psyllium, naproxen, and topical hydrocortisone cream were continued, and the patient was referred to the colorectal surgery clinic at this hospital.



At the surgery clinic, the patient reported that the rectal pain and bleeding persisted and that he had not noted any protrusion of tissue through the anus. His medications included naproxen, as needed, and hydrocortisone cream applied topically to the anus, which had provided only minimal pain relief. He had no known allergies. He had emigrated from Brazil 16 years earlier and now lived with his wife in an urban area of New England. He worked in construction and did not smoke tobacco. He was bisexual; he reported that he had never had a sexually transmitted infection. His mother had hypertension. There was no family history of colorectal cancer.



The patient appeared well. Examination of the neck, heart, lungs, and abdomen was normal. At the anterior and posterior midline anal verge, there were large, superficial anal fissures and small-to-moderate-sized distal sentinel skin tags. Results of a digital rectal examination were normal except for the presence of moderate tenderness. Anoscopy revealed the fissures and small internal hemorrhoids. Flexible sigmoidoscopy was performed, with insertion of the sigmoidoscope to a depth of 30 cm; mild-to-moderate mucosal inflammation of the most distal 10 cm of the rectum was present and was characterized by edema and patchy erythema.



A diagnosis was made.

## Differential Diagnosis

The clinical presentation of this 33-year-old man—persistent rectal pain with bright red perianal bleeding, anal fissures, sentinel tags, and distal rectal mucosal inflammation—is highly suggestive of a localized anorectal pathology, yet the persistence of symptoms despite standard medical therapy for hemorrhoids and fissures raises important diagnostic considerations beyond the most common benign etiologies. The absence of systemic symptoms such as fever, weight loss, or abdominal pain, along with negative serologies for HIV, hepatitis B, and hepatitis C, initially supports a localized, non-systemic process. However, the failure to respond to conservative measures—including fiber supplementation, NSAIDs, and topical corticosteroids—demands a more nuanced differential that encompasses infectious, inflammatory, neoplastic, and iatrogenic causes, some of which may be easily overlooked in the absence of classic risk factors or dramatic findings.

The most straightforward and statistically most likely diagnosis is chronic anal fissure with associated hemorrhoidal disease. Anal fissures—typically located in the posterior midline—are common in young adults and are frequently associated with trauma from hard stool, though they can also arise from increased anal sphincter tone or local ischemia. The presence of sentinel tags and mild internal hemorrhoids supports this diagnosis, as these are classic sequelae of chronic fissures. Bright red bleeding from fissures is typically superficial, occurring on wiping rather than mixed within stool, which aligns with this patient’s description. The tenderness on digital rectal examination and anoscopy further supports this. However, the persistence of symptoms for over 10 weeks despite appropriate conservative therapy is unusual. While up to 80% of acute fissures heal within six weeks with conservative measures, chronic fissures—defined as those persisting beyond six to eight weeks—are less likely to resolve spontaneously and may require more aggressive intervention. Yet even chronic fissures rarely persist unresponsive to topical nitroglycerin, calcium channel blockers, or botulinum toxin, which were not attempted here. The fact that topical hydrocortisone provided only minimal relief raises the possibility that the underlying pathology is not purely mechanical or inflammatory in the typical sense, but may be driven by an alternative etiology that mimics fissure disease.

One critical consideration is infectious etiologies, particularly sexually transmitted infections (STIs) involving the anorectal mucosa. Although the patient’s HIV serology was negative, this does not exclude other pathogens that can cause anorectal inflammation, pain, and ulceration. The patient’s recent history of receptive anal intercourse with a man six weeks prior is a significant risk factor for anogenital infections, and the timing aligns with the onset of symptoms. While HIV was ruled out, other pathogens such as *Chlamydia trachomatis*, *Neisseria gonorrhoeae*, and *Treponema pallidum* must be considered. Lymphogranuloma venereum (LGV), a highly invasive serovar of *C. trachomatis*, can present with proctitis characterized by rectal pain, bleeding, tenesmus, and mucosal inflammation—often with a delayed onset of several weeks after exposure. LGV proctitis can mimic inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) or nonspecific proctitis and may be associated with sentinel tags and fissure-like lesions due to chronic inflammation and fibrosis. Importantly, LGV is frequently misdiagnosed as hemorrhoids or fissures in its early stages, especially in patients without obvious ulcers or lymphadenopathy. The patchy erythema and edema seen on sigmoidoscopy are consistent with LGV-induced mucosal inflammation, and the absence of systemic symptoms does not rule it out. Given the patient’s sexual history and the refractory nature of his symptoms, nucleic acid amplification testing (NAAT) for *C. trachomatis* and *N. gonorrhoeae* from a rectal swab is essential and should have been performed at the initial presentation.

Another critical infectious consideration is syphilis. Primary syphilis typically presents as a painless chancre, but secondary syphilis can manifest with mucocutaneous lesions, including painless or painful anorectal ulcers, condylomata lata, or even fissure-like lesions. Tertiary syphilis, though rare, can cause gummatous lesions that ulcerate and mimic malignancy or chronic inflammation. The patient’s negative serology for HIV does not preclude syphilis, and if only a non-treponemal test (e.g., RPR) was performed without a confirmatory treponemal test (e.g., FTA-ABS or TP-PA), false negatives can occur in the early phase. The timing of his sexual encounter six weeks prior places him squarely in the window for secondary syphilis, which can present with systemic symptoms such as rash, fever, or lymphadenopathy—but may also present with isolated anorectal symptoms. Painful fissures with mucosal inflammation are not classic for syphilis, but atypical presentations are well documented, particularly in immunocompromised or HIV-negative individuals with high-risk sexual behavior. The absence of rash or lymphadenopathy does not exclude syphilis, as localized anorectal manifestations can occur without systemic signs.

Herpes simplex virus (HSV) infection, particularly HSV-2, is another important consideration. Anorectal HSV can present with multiple painful ulcers, dyschezia, bleeding, and tenesmus. While HSV lesions are typically multiple, shallow, and surrounded by erythema, they can occasionally present as a single, deep, painful fissure-like lesion, especially in recurrent or atypical cases. The patient’s pain is consistent with HSV, and the fact that he has not had prior sexually transmitted infections does not preclude a first-episode infection. HSV can be diagnosed via PCR of lesion swabs or viral culture, but the diagnosis is often missed if testing is not specifically requested. The lack of vesicular lesions on examination does not rule out HSV, as vesicles may rupture early and leave only ulcerated or fissured surfaces.

Less common but still relevant infectious causes include *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* (anorectal tuberculosis), which can present with chronic pain, bleeding, and ulceration, particularly in immigrants from endemic regions such as Brazil. Although rare in immunocompetent individuals in the U.S., it can occur as a primary infection from ingestion of unpasteurized dairy or via hematogenous spread. Anorectal TB may mimic Crohn’s disease, with deep, irregular ulcers, fistulas, and fibrosis. The patient’s history of emigration from Brazil, while not definitive, warrants consideration, especially in the context of persistent inflammation unresponsive to standard therapy. Similarly, amoebiasis or schistosomiasis, though uncommon in the U.S., could be considered in immigrants from endemic areas, particularly if there is a history of exposure to contaminated water. However, these typically present with diarrhea, systemic symptoms, and more diffuse colitis rather than isolated distal rectal inflammation and fissures.

Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), particularly ulcerative colitis (UC) or Crohn’s disease, must be carefully evaluated. While UC typically presents with bloody diarrhea, urgency, and diffuse distal inflammation extending proximally, proctitis-only UC can present with isolated rectal bleeding, tenesmus, and pain—sometimes mimicking fissures or hemorrhoids. The patchy erythema and edema seen on sigmoidoscopy to 30 cm could represent early or limited UC. However, the presence of sentinel tags and external hemorrhoids is not typical of IBD, which usually lacks these features unless there is chronic inflammation with secondary changes. Crohn’s disease, on the other hand, can present with perianal disease including fissures, fistulas, and skin tags, often preceding intestinal symptoms. The patient’s age, lack of family history of IBD, and absence of systemic symptoms or abdominal pain make Crohn’s less likely, but not impossible. The key distinguishing feature is that Crohn’s-related fissures are typically deep, longitudinal, and associated with fistulas or abscesses, whereas the patient’s fissures are described as superficial and midline. Biopsy of the inflamed mucosa during sigmoidoscopy would be critical here: if granulomas are present, Crohn’s is strongly supported; if crypt architectural distortion and basal plasmacytosis are seen, UC is more likely. Without biopsy, this remains a diagnostic uncertainty.

Neoplastic causes must also be considered, though less commonly in a 33-year-old without family history of colorectal cancer. Rectal adenocarcinoma is rare at this age, but not unheard of, particularly in the setting of chronic inflammation or genetic syndromes (e.g., Lynch syndrome). However, the patient’s symptoms—painful defecation, bright red blood on wiping, and absence of weight loss or change in bowel habits—make malignancy unlikely. Rectal cancer typically presents with change in bowel habits, narrower stools, and bleeding mixed with stool rather than on wiping. Moreover, the mucosal inflammation described is patchy and superficial, not the irregular, nodular, or infiltrative mass typically seen with cancer. Squamous cell carcinoma of the anal canal is another consideration, particularly in patients with a history of receptive anal intercourse. Anal SCC can present with bleeding, pain, and perianal lesions, sometimes mimicking fissures or hemorrhoids. The presence of sentinel tags could represent a reaction to a subclinical tumor. Biopsy of the fissure margins or surrounding mucosa would be essential to rule this out. Although rare, the patient’s sexual history elevates this possibility beyond mere statistical likelihood.

Other rare but important considerations include anorectal granulomatous disease from non-tuberculous mycobacteria, sarcoidosis (which can rarely involve the anorectum with granulomatous inflammation), and even Behçet’s disease, which can present with oral and genital ulcers and occasionally anorectal ulcers or fissures. The absence of oral ulcers, uveitis, or cutaneous lesions makes these less likely, but they should remain in the differential if other causes are excluded.

The most compelling diagnostic hypothesis is lymphogranuloma venereum (LGV) proctitis. The patient’s sexual history, the timing of symptom onset, the refractory nature of the fissures and inflammation, and the lack of response to standard hemorrhoid/fissure therapy all point strongly toward an infectious etiology. LGV proctitis often presents with rectal pain, bleeding, mucopurulent discharge, and tenesmus, and can cause chronic inflammation leading to fibrosis, stricture, and fissure formation. The patchy erythema and edema on sigmoidoscopy are classic. Moreover, LGV is frequently underdiagnosed in men who have sex with men, even in the absence of HIV co-infection, and rectal NAAT testing is not routinely performed unless specifically indicated. The patient’s negative HIV test may have led to a false sense of security regarding STIs, but LGV is a distinct entity requiring specific testing.

In conclusion, while chronic anal fissure with hemorrhoids remains the most common diagnosis, the persistence of symptoms, the sexual history, and the mucosal inflammation seen on sigmoidoscopy strongly suggest an infectious or inflammatory etiology beyond simple mechanical trauma. LGV proctitis is the leading diagnostic consideration, followed closely by syphilis and HSV infection. IBD and malignancy are less likely but must be excluded with appropriate biopsies. The failure to perform rectal swabs for NAAT for *C. trachomatis* and *N. gonorrhoeae*, as well as serologic testing for syphilis (including treponemal assays), represents a critical diagnostic gap. The patient’s clinical course underscores the importance of considering STIs in all patients with persistent anorectal symptoms, regardless of HIV status or the apparent benign appearance of lesions. A targeted workup with rectal PCR, syphilis serology, and mucosal biopsy is urgently indicated to confirm the diagnosis and guide appropriate therapy.
