### 1. Primary Epidermal Necrolysis Syndromes  

#### 1.1 Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis (TEN) – Drug‑Induced

**Pathophysiology**  
TEN represents the most severe end of the spectrum of severe cutaneous adverse reactions (SCARs), sharing a common immunopathogenic basis with Stevens‑Johnson syndrome (SJS). Cytotoxic T‑cell–mediated keratinocyte apoptosis, driven by granulysin, perforin‑granzyme pathways, and Fas‑FasL interactions, culminates in full‑thickness epidermal necrosis. The reaction is typically precipitated by an offending drug that acts as a hapten or triggers a drug‑specific T‑cell response. The latency period between drug exposure and onset of skin lesions is usually 1–3 weeks for most agents, but can be shorter with re‑exposure or for drugs with rapid metabolism.

**Evidence Supporting TEN in This Patient**  

- **Temporal relationship** – The patient received several high‑risk agents in the weeks preceding the eruption: vancomycin (started 3 weeks prior), clindamycin, aztreonam, fluconazole, gentamicin, and methotrexate (initiated 6 weeks prior, discontinued after 2 weeks). The morbilliform eruption appeared 2 weeks after methotrexate cessation, evolving within 48 h to bullae and widespread epidermal detachment, fitting the classic evolution from macular erythema → targetoid lesions → bullae → sheet‑like denudation. Vancomycin, in particular, is a well‑documented trigger of TEN, with reported latency of 5–14 days, matching the timeline.  

- **Clinical morphology** – The rash covered ≈85 % BSA, with confluent epidermal sloughing, positive Nikolsky sign, and mucosal involvement (oral, pharyngeal, conjunctival). Histology from a skin biopsy confirmed TEN (full‑thickness epidermal necrosis with subepidermal detachment, minimal dermal inflammation).  

- **Systemic features** – Fever, tachycardia, hemodynamic instability, and a rapid decline in skin barrier function are characteristic of TEN.  

- **Risk factors** – The patient’s age (>65 y), chronic renal failure, and ongoing immunosuppression (prednisone, recent methotrexate) increase susceptibility to drug‑induced SCARs.

**Evidence Against Alternative Mechanisms**  

- **Absence of target lesions** – Classical SJS may display targetoid lesions; however, TEN often presents with diffuse erythema and bullae without classic targets, consistent with the described presentation.  

- **Lack of staphylococcal exotoxin evidence** – No mention of a prodrome of “strawberry tongue” or a known staphylococcal infection that could cause Staphylococcal Scalded Skin Syndrome (SSSS).  

Overall, drug‑induced TEN remains the most parsimonious explanation for the primary dermatologic event.

#### 1.2 Stevens‑Johnson Syndrome (SJS) and Overlap Syndrome  

SJS is distinguished from TEN by involvement of <10 % BSA; the overlap syndrome involves 10–30 % BSA. In this case, the skin involvement far exceeds the threshold for TEN, making SJS or overlap unlikely as primary diagnoses. Nonetheless, the early morbilliform eruption could have represented an SJS‑like prodrome before progression to TEN, a recognized pattern in severe drug reactions.

#### 1.3 Staphylococcal Scalded Skin Syndrome (SSSS)  

SSSS is mediated by exfoliative toxins (ETA/ETB) from Staphylococcus aureus, causing superficial epidermolysis limited to the stratum granulosum. It predominantly affects neonates and immunocompromised adults. Key distinguishing features include a lack of mucosal involvement and a positive Nikolsky sign with a superficial split. In this patient, mucosal ulceration, conjunctival injection, and histology showing full‑thickness necrosis argue strongly against SSSS. Moreover, cultures grew gram‑negative organisms (Klebsiella, E. coli) rather than S. aureus, and there was no mention of staphylococcal toxin assays.

#### 1.4 Acute Generalized Exanthematous Pustulosis (AGEP)  

AGEP manifests as an acute eruption of sterile pustules, fever, and neutrophilia, usually drug‑induced, with rapid resolution upon drug withdrawal. The absence of pustular lesions, the presence of large bullae, and biopsy findings are incompatible with AGEP.

#### 1.5 Autoimmune Bullous Dermatoses (Pemphigus, Bullous Pemphigoid)  

Pemphigus vulgaris can cause widespread erosions and mucosal lesions, but the histopathology shows suprabasal acantholysis, not full‑thickness necrosis. Bullous pemphigoid typically spares mucosa and produces tense bullae with a subepidermal split, again not aligning with the biopsy. The rapid progression and systemic toxicity are atypical for these chronic autoimmune disorders.

#### 1.6 Cutaneous Vasculitis with Necrosis  

Severe leukocytoclastic vasculitis can produce ulcerative necrotic lesions, but it rarely results in confluent epidermal sloughing over >80 % BSA. The presence of a positive Nikolsky sign and subepidermal detachment on histology is not characteristic of vasculitis.

### 9. Management Implications and Lessons Learned  

While the case discussion focuses on differential diagnosis, it is valuable to extrapolate therapeutic considerations that could inform future similar presentations:

- **Early drug discontinuation** – Prompt identification and cessation of the offending agent (vancomycin) is paramount. In practice, a systematic drug review and substitution with a non‑cross‑reactive agent (e.g., linezolid) may reduce ongoing antigenic stimulation.  

- **Supportive care akin to burn management** – Fluid resuscitation guided by the Parkland formula adapted for TEN, meticulous wound care, and early ophthalmology input are essential.  

- **Aggressive infection control** – Given the high risk of bacteremia, early removal of all indwelling catheters, use of antibiotic lock therapy, and targeted antimicrobial stewardship based on culture data can mitigate sepsis.  

- **Adjunctive immunomodulation** – The role of systemic steroids remains controversial; alternative agents such as intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) or cyclosporine have shown benefit in some series and may be considered, especially when steroids are contraindicated or ineffective.  

- **Fungal prophylaxis** – In patients receiving prolonged broad‑spectrum antibiotics and corticosteroids, prophylactic antifungal therapy (e.g., fluconazole or echinocandin) may preempt invasive candidiasis or aspergillosis.  

- **Renal dosing adjustments** – For patients on dialysis, drug dosing must be meticulously calculated to avoid accumulation of potentially toxic agents that could act as additional triggers.  

- **SCORTEN assessment** – Early calculation of the SCORTEN score can guide prognostication and resource allocation (e.g., ICU admission, burn‑unit transfer).

The most defensible primary diagnosis in this patient is **drug‑induced toxic epidermal necrolysis**, most likely triggered by vancomycin, with a possible contributory role of methotrexate. The extensive epidermal loss set the stage for catastrophic **Gram‑negative sepsis**, predominantly caused by Klebsiella pneumoniae, which persisted despite broad‑spectrum antimicrobial therapy. Secondary complications—including respiratory tract involvement of TEN, possible invasive pulmonary aspergillosis, biliary colonization, and multi‑organ failure—intertwined to produce a fatal outcome. The patient’s underlying chronic kidney disease, diabetes, and chronic corticosteroid exposure magnified both the risk of TEN and the severity of subsequent infections.  

In summary, the differential diagnosis converges on a cascade beginning with a drug‑induced severe cutaneous adverse reaction, progressing to overwhelming infection and systemic collapse. Recognizing the interplay between drug hypersensitivity, barrier failure, and opportunistic infection is essential for early intervention and may improve survival in future cases of TEN.
