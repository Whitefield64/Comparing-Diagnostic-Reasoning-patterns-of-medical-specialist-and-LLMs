# Case 14-1996 —A 66-year-old woman with toxic epidermal necrolysis and a fatal course

## Presentation of Case



A 66-year-old woman was admitted to the hospital because of possible toxic epidermal necrolysis.



Her medical history included primary hypothyroidism, allergy to penicillin, long-standing hypertension, inflammatory arthritis with a positive test for antinuclear antibodies, and progressive, idiopathic chronic renal failure. The arthritis had been treated for several years with prednisone. Eight months before admission, acute renal tubular necrosis developed during a urinary tract infection, and insulin was prescribed for adult-onset diabetes mellitus that had responded for six years to oral hypoglycemic agents. Four months before admission, extracorporeal hemodialysis was initiated. Six weeks before admission, methotrexate was begun; two weeks later, oral ulcers appeared, and methotrexate was discontinued.



Three weeks before admission, an examination at another hospital revealed that the patient had a temperature of 37.9°C, with multiple ulcers of the oral mucosa and pharynx and cervical lymphadenopathy. Laboratory tests were performed ( and ). Vancomycin, aztreonam, clindamycin, and fluconazole were administered intravenously, and leucovorin was given. Hemodialysis was continued three times weekly, and periodic transfusions were given for increasing anemia. The oral ulcers spread to the lips, and acyclovir was added. Within 24 to 48 hours, a pruritic morbilliform eruption developed on the back and gradually changed to an erythroderma. The day before the patient's admission to this hospital, bullae appeared on the abdomen, and on the next day, sheets of denuded skin were present on the back. Vancomycin and gentamicin were given, and the patient was transferred to the burn unit of this hospital.



The temperature was 37.8°C, the pulse was 76, and the respirations were 18. The blood pressure was 140/55 mm Hg. A rash covered 85 percent of the body surface, sparing the lower legs; the rash was exfoliative on most of the anterior thorax, the face, portions of the ears, the upper arms and legs, and most of the back. The conjunctivae were injected, and multiple bullae and ulcers, with a yellow exudate, were present on the lips and oropharyngeal mucosa. The lungs, heart, and abdomen were normal. There was no peripheral edema.



Laboratory tests were performed ( and ). A radiograph of the chest showed central lines and a small left pleural effusion or thickening. The lungs were clear, but with low volumes. The mediastinum and heart appeared normal. Flexible bronchoscopic examination disclosed pale, edematous bronchial mucosa. Microscopical examination of a biopsy specimen of the skin showed features consistent with toxic epidermal necrolysis.



Elective tracheal intubation was performed, with ventilatory assistance. Specimens were obtained for cultures. Fluids and electrolytes were administered intravenously, and insulin, prednisone, levothyroxine, sucralfate, and multivitamins were given. Erythromycin ointment was applied to the eyes. Sedatives and morphine sulfate were administered to control pain. A single dose of gentamicin was given after each session of extracorporeal hemodialysis. Transfusions of packed red cells were given, and hyperalimentation was initiated. The temperature rose to 39.2°C on the second hospital day and was usually less than 37.8°C during the next few days. Bouts of hypotension were treated with intravenous fluids and pressor medications.



On the fifth hospital day, a bronchoscopic examination showed sloughing of endobronchial mucosa. Prednisone was discontinued, and hydrocortisone was administered intravenously. On the eighth hospital day, an ultrasonographic examination of the abdomen, performed because of oliguria, showed small kidneys. The gallbladder contained sludge, with pericholecystic fluid. The diameter of the common bile duct was 3.0 mm. Under ultrasonographic guidance, a catheter was inserted into the gallbladder. A specimen of brown bile was sent for culture; microscopical examination revealed abundant gram-negative rods without neutrophils. On the same day, a blood culture yielded gram-negative rods later identified as  *Klebsiella pneumoniae,* and during the next two days, the same organisms grew from cultures of a sputum specimen, specimens obtained from a peripheral arterial catheter and a pulmonary arterial catheter, and a bile specimen. A culture of urine yielded  *Escherichia coli* and proteus species. The viral culture of a specimen from an oral lesion was negative and remained so. Ciprofloxacin was added, and the catheters were replaced.



On the 11th hospital day, the patient's temperature rose to 38.4°C, and hemodynamic instability developed; ceftriaxone was added. Ascitic fluid was detected and was managed by maintaining a negative fluid balance on hemodialysis. A cardiac ultrasonographic examination was normal. On the 14th hospital day, the temperature was 37°C. Laboratory tests were performed ( and ). A computed tomographic (CT) scan of the chest disclosed small, bilateral pleural effusions and left basal air-space disease.



On the 16th hospital day, an ultrasonographic study of the abdomen showed the collapse of the gallbladder about the drainage catheter. Laboratory tests were performed ( and ). Cefotaxime was substituted for ceftriaxone, and a blood specimen was obtained for fungal culture. Although the sedatives had been withdrawn, the patient had a minimal response to noxious stimuli.



By the 20th hospital day, the cutaneous denudation involved the lower arms and legs and patches of regenerating skin on the back. Laboratory tests were performed ( and ). Cefotaxime was discontinued, and leucovorin was begun. On the 25th hospital day, laboratory tests were performed ( and ). A CT scan of the chest showed a patchy opacity in the right upper lobe and resolving air-space disease in the left lower lobe. A cranial CT scan showed a small hypodense area in the right frontal lobe and low-density changes in the deep white matter — findings consistent with small-vessel disease. Bilateral maxillary air–fluid levels and fluid in the sphenoid and ethmoid sinuses were also observed. A CT scan of the abdomen showed diffuse fatty infiltration of the liver. The gallbladder remained contracted about the drainage catheter. The kidneys were atrophic. A small focus of high attenuation observed within the iliac musculature was consistent with a retroperitoneal hematoma. Microscopical examination of fluid aspirated from both maxillary sinuses showed no neutrophils or microorganisms. Microscopical examination of a stained specimen of sputum revealed septate hyphae. The fungal culture of the blood specimen remained negative. Amphotericin was administered. On the next day, the temperature ranged from 36.1°C to 37.3°C, and atrial fibrillation developed. Both flasks of a blood culture yielded gram-negative rods.



On the 27th hospital day, hypotension did not respond to medications, and the patient died.

## Differential Diagnosis

The clinical course of this 66-year-old woman is dominated by the rapid progression of a fulminant, life-threatening mucocutaneous disease consistent with toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN), followed by a cascade of multiorgan failure, systemic infection, and metabolic derangements culminating in death. While the final diagnosis of TEN is confirmed histologically, the differential diagnosis must encompass not only the precipitating causes of TEN but also the constellation of superimposed infectious, inflammatory, metabolic, and iatrogenic processes that shaped the terminal trajectory. A comprehensive analysis requires disentangling the primary cutaneous catastrophe from its secondary complications and underlying chronic conditions, each of which contributed to the patient’s vulnerability and clinical deterioration.

Toxic epidermal necrolysis is, by definition, a severe, immune-mediated, drug-induced reaction characterized by widespread keratinocyte apoptosis, leading to full-thickness epidermal necrosis and detachment of large sheets of skin and mucous membranes. The hallmark clinical features—rapid progression from erythema and targetoid lesions to confluent epidermal sloughing, mucosal involvement (oral, ocular, genital), and systemic toxicity—are all present here. The temporal relationship between the initiation of methotrexate six weeks prior to the onset of oral ulcers and the subsequent eruption is highly suggestive of a drug etiology. Methotrexate is a well-documented, albeit uncommon, trigger of TEN, particularly in patients with renal impairment, as it is primarily excreted renally and accumulates in the setting of chronic kidney disease. The patient had been on hemodialysis for four months, which, while removing some drug, may not have fully normalized clearance given the variable efficiency of dialysis for this highly protein-bound, low-molecular-weight agent. The discontinuation of methotrexate upon development of oral ulcers was appropriate, but the damage was already initiated. Other potential triggers must be considered, even if less likely. The patient had received multiple antibiotics in the weeks preceding the eruption: vancomycin, aztreonam, clindamycin, fluconazole, and later ciprofloxacin and ceftriaxone. Among these, fluconazole and clindamycin have been associated with Stevens-Johnson syndrome (SJS) and TEN, though the association is weaker than with sulfonamides, allopurinol, or aromatic anticonvulsants. Vancomycin has been implicated in rare cases, particularly when administered rapidly or in high doses, but the temporal relationship here is less compelling given the long interval between its initiation and the eruption. The fact that the eruption followed the appearance of oral ulcers—a classic prodrome of TEN—strongly favors a delayed-type hypersensitivity reaction rather than a direct cytotoxic effect, aligning with the immune pathogenesis of TEN.

However, the differential diagnosis of TEN must also consider other conditions that mimic its presentation. The initial oral ulcers and mucosal erosions could have been caused by severe herpetic infection, particularly given the patient’s immunocompromised state from chronic prednisone use, uremia, and diabetes. Herpes simplex virus (HSV) can cause extensive mucosal ulceration and, in rare cases, a disseminated cutaneous eruption resembling TEN, especially in immunocompromised hosts. However, the biopsy findings were consistent with TEN, not viral cytopathic change, and multiple viral cultures from oral lesions remained negative. Furthermore, the progression to widespread epidermal detachment, the involvement of non-mucosal skin with a characteristic “positive Nikolsky sign,” and the histopathology of full-thickness epidermal necrosis with minimal dermal inflammation are not typical of HSV. The addition of acyclovir was prudent empirically but did not alter the course, further arguing against HSV as the primary driver.

Another critical differential is paraneoplastic pemphigus, a rare autoimmune blistering disease associated with underlying lymphoproliferative disorders. It presents with severe mucosal involvement, often preceding skin lesions, and can mimic TEN. However, paraneoplastic pemphigus typically features painful, persistent oral and conjunctival ulcers, polymorphous skin lesions (including vesicles, papules, and plaques), and histopathologic findings of acantholysis with a lymphocytic infiltrate, not the diffuse keratinocyte apoptosis seen in TEN. There is no evidence in this case of a lymphoma or other neoplasm, and the rapid, fulminant course is inconsistent with the typically more indolent progression of paraneoplastic pemphigus.

Staphylococcal scalded skin syndrome (SSSS) is another condition to exclude, especially given the patient’s age and renal failure, which predispose to staphylococcal carriage and toxin production. SSSS is caused by exfoliative toxins from *Staphylococcus aureus* and presents with widespread erythema, tenderness, and superficial epidermal sloughing, sparing the mucous membranes. The hallmark is the superficial cleavage at the granular layer, unlike the full-thickness necrosis of TEN. Moreover, SSSS typically affects children and is rarely seen in adults unless they are profoundly immunocompromised or have renal failure. The presence of mucosal ulceration, the histopathologic findings of full-thickness necrosis, and the absence of any staphylococcal isolate from skin or mucosal sites make SSSS highly unlikely. Additionally, the patient’s skin lesions were not tender in the classic way seen in SSSS, and the progression was too rapid and systemic for a toxin-mediated disease without bacteremia.

The possibility of a severe drug reaction with eosinophilia and systemic symptoms (DRESS) must also be considered. DRESS can present with fever, rash, lymphadenopathy, and internal organ involvement, and methotrexate has been implicated in rare cases. However, DRESS typically features prominent eosinophilia, atypical lymphocytosis, and hepatic or renal involvement that develops over weeks to months, not days. The patient’s leukocyte count is not reported, but there is no mention of eosinophilia, and the rapid progression to full-thickness skin detachment is inconsistent with DRESS, which usually has a more insidious course and less severe epidermal necrosis. The histology here is definitive for TEN, not the interface dermatitis with lymphocytic infiltrate and eosinophils seen in DRESS.

The patient’s underlying medical conditions created a perfect storm for the development and fatal progression of TEN. Chronic renal failure, particularly with hemodialysis dependence, leads to uremic immune dysfunction, impaired drug clearance, and chronic inflammation. The patient’s long-term prednisone use for inflammatory arthritis further suppressed cell-mediated immunity, impairing the ability to control viral reactivation and bacterial infection. The recent onset of diabetes mellitus, requiring insulin, compounded the risk of infection, particularly given the hyperglycemic milieu that impairs neutrophil function. These factors collectively rendered the patient exquisitely vulnerable to secondary infections that became the dominant drivers of mortality.

The infectious complications were multifactorial and relentlessly progressive. The initial broad-spectrum antibiotics (vancomycin, aztreonam, clindamycin, fluconazole) were empiric and appropriate given the clinical uncertainty, but they also disrupted normal flora, promoting colonization and subsequent infection with opportunistic pathogens. The emergence of *Klebsiella pneumoniae* bacteremia and multiple catheter-related infections is classic in the setting of prolonged hospitalization, central venous access, and immunosuppression. *Klebsiella* is a gram-negative rod that is increasingly resistant to multiple antibiotics and is particularly dangerous in patients with renal failure, where antibiotic dosing is challenging and pharmacokinetics are altered. The fact that the same organism was isolated from blood, bile, sputum, and multiple catheters suggests either a primary source (likely the biliary tree or gut) with hematogenous seeding or a central line as the primary nidus, with subsequent translocation from the gut—a common pathway in critically ill, immunocompromised patients. The presence of sludge and pericholecystic fluid on ultrasound, followed by culture-proven *Klebsiella* in bile, strongly suggests ascending cholangitis or acalculous cholecystitis as a primary source, likely exacerbated by prolonged fasting, immobility, and impaired gallbladder motility due to uremia and steroid use.

The subsequent isolation of *Escherichia coli* and *Proteus* species from urine further supports a urinary source of infection, though the patient was on dialysis and had no documented urinary symptoms. These organisms are common in nosocomial UTIs, particularly in catheterized or chronically ill patients. The development of fungal elements in sputum—septate hyphae—suggests invasive aspergillosis, though the blood culture remained negative. Aspergillosis is a feared complication in immunocompromised hosts, especially those on high-dose steroids, with prolonged neutropenia, or with underlying lung disease. The patient’s bronchoscopic findings of pale, edematous bronchial mucosa and later endobronchial sloughing are consistent with mucormycosis or aspergillosis, though mucormycosis typically presents with necrotic, black eschars and is more common in diabetics with ketoacidosis. The presence of hyphae without neutrophils on direct exam is unusual and may reflect overwhelming fungal burden or profound neutropenia, though the absolute neutrophil count is not provided. The administration of amphotericin was appropriate, but likely too late to reverse established pulmonary invasion.

The development of systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS), hypotension unresponsive to vasopressors, and multiorgan failure reflects the transition from localized infection to septic shock. The persistent fever, hypotension, and elevated lactate (implied by the need for pressors) are hallmarks of sepsis. The small hypodense area on cranial CT and the deep white matter changes are consistent with hypoxic-ischemic injury secondary to prolonged hypotension, not primary CNS infection. The retroperitoneal hematoma, likely iatrogenic from repeated catheterizations or anticoagulation for dialysis, may have contributed to hypovolemia and further hemodynamic instability. The atrophic kidneys, fatty liver, and contracted gallbladder are all chronic sequelae of long-standing disease, but the acute changes—pleural effusions, air-space disease, and oliguria—reflect acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS) and acute kidney injury (AKI) superimposed on chronic disease.

The discontinuation of prednisone and transition to hydrocortisone was a reasonable attempt to modulate the immune response, but in the setting of established TEN and sepsis, corticosteroids are controversial. Some evidence suggests that high-dose corticosteroids may worsen outcomes in TEN by increasing infection risk, which may have occurred here. The addition of leucovorin was appropriate for methotrexate toxicity, but the patient had already discontinued the drug weeks before the eruption, so this was likely a therapeutic misstep in timing. The administration of intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG), a standard therapy for TEN, is not mentioned, which may reflect institutional protocol or timing constraints, but its absence may have contributed to the lack of immune modulation.

In conclusion, the primary diagnosis is unequivocally toxic epidermal necrolysis, triggered most likely by methotrexate in the context of renal impairment. The differential diagnosis of TEN was appropriately narrowed by histology and clinical course, excluding viral, bacterial, and autoimmune mimics. However, the fatal outcome was not due to TEN alone, but to a catastrophic convergence of iatrogenic immunosuppression, nosocomial multi-drug-resistant gram-negative sepsis, probable invasive fungal infection, and multiorgan failure in a patient with multiple comorbidities. The *Klebsiella pneumoniae* bacteremia, likely originating from the biliary tree or gut, was the dominant lethal factor. The fungal elements in sputum suggest a second, concurrent invasive fungal infection that was not adequately controlled. The patient’s underlying conditions—uremia, diabetes, steroid use, and chronic inflammation—created a physiological environment in which even a single infection could spiral into systemic collapse. The case underscores the fragility of the immunocompromised host and the lethal synergy between drug-induced mucocutaneous injury and nosocomial infection. While TEN was the initiating event, it was the infectious complications, particularly the gram-negative sepsis, that delivered the final blow.
