## Differential Diagnosis

The clinical presentation of this 59-year-old woman with type 1 diabetes mellitus is profoundly complex, involving acute neurologic deterioration with obtundation, symmetric basal ganglia and thalamic abnormalities on neuroimaging, cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) findings suggestive of a non-infectious process, and a prodromal phase of progressive cognitive and behavioral decline over two years. The differential diagnosis must be approached systematically, considering malignant, metabolic, autoimmune, neurodegenerative, and rare toxic causes, while rigorously excluding common mimics such as infectious encephalitis, vascular events, or structural lesions.

The most compelling and specific imaging finding is the symmetric, bilateral hypointensity on T2-weighted and susceptibility-weighted MRI in the basal ganglia, thalami, dentate nuclei, and leptomeningeal surfaces. This pattern is classically associated with manganese toxicity, but it is also seen in hepatic encephalopathy, mitochondrial disorders, and certain inherited metabolic diseases. However, the patient has no known liver disease—her liver function tests are normal, and there is no history of cirrhosis, portal hypertension, or portosystemic shunting. Furthermore, the acute presentation with hyperglycemia and ketonuria, rather than hyperammonemia or lactic acidosis, makes hepatic encephalopathy unlikely. Manganese toxicity, while a strong candidate, must be weighed against the patient’s lack of occupational exposure (e.g., welding, mining) or parenteral nutrition, which are the usual contexts for this condition. However, in patients with chronic liver disease or biliary obstruction, manganese accumulates due to impaired excretion; in the absence of liver dysfunction, one must consider whether there is an alternative mechanism of manganese retention—such as altered blood-brain barrier permeability, chronic inflammation, or an underlying hematologic disorder. Chronic lymphocytic leukemia (CLL), which she has been treated with rituximab, is not typically associated with manganese accumulation, but CLL can be associated with immune dysregulation and paraneoplastic phenomena. More importantly, the patient’s chronic anemia raises the possibility of iron metabolism disorders, which may have downstream effects on manganese homeostasis, as both metals share transport pathways via divalent metal transporter 1 (DMT1) and ferroportin.

Another critical consideration is the hyperglycemia and ketonuria at presentation. The blood glucose level of 321 mg/dL is markedly elevated, yet the patient was not in diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) by standard criteria—there is no significant acidosis, no elevated anion gap reported, and no ketonemia quantified. However, the presence of ketones in urine and a high specific gravity suggests a state of relative insulin deficiency with ongoing lipolysis. In type 1 diabetes, this could reflect pump malfunction, nonadherence, or increased insulin requirements due to an intercurrent illness. Yet the insulin pump interrogation showed no hypoglycemic episodes in the preceding week, and the patient had been managing her diabetes with “high sophistication” until the cognitive decline began two years prior. This raises the possibility that the hyperglycemia was not the primary driver of the encephalopathy but rather a consequence of her deteriorating ability to self-manage her disease—a result of underlying neurodegeneration. The ketonuria, in this context, may reflect chronic, low-grade insulin deficiency rather than acute DKA. Importantly, hyperglycemia alone does not explain the symmetric basal ganglia lesions or the profound neurocognitive decline.

The CSF findings are equally revealing. The opening pressure is normal (14 cm H2O), ruling out idiopathic intracranial hypertension. The cell count is minimally elevated with rare neutrophils and rare atypical cells—this is inconsistent with bacterial meningitis, viral encephalitis, or lymphoma. The absence of organisms on Gram stain and negative cultures for bacteria, viruses (including HSV, VZV, enteroviruses, West Nile), and acid-fast bacilli effectively excludes the most common infectious causes. The presence of atypical cells is concerning but nonspecific; it could represent reactive lymphocytes, malignant cells from underlying CLL with CNS infiltration, or even degenerative cellular debris from a metabolic encephalopathy. The cytology was not diagnostic of lymphoma or leukemia, and the patient’s CLL has been managed with rituximab, which reduces circulating B-cell counts but does not eliminate the risk of transformation or CNS involvement. The symmetric, non-enhancing, susceptibility-based signal changes are atypical for lymphoma. The MMSE score of 24, with deficits in recall and calculation, suggests a non-amnestic, fronto-subcortical dementia. The patient has diabetic retinopathy, which could be confused with pigmentary retinopathy, but PKAN typically presents in childhood or adolescence. At age 59, this is highly unlikely.

The symmetric basal ganglia hypointensity on susceptibility MRI, the progressive cognitive and behavioral decline over two years, the absence of infectious or neoplastic etiologies, the lack of response to antibiotics, and the normal liver function tests (which can be misleading in early or compensated Wilson disease) all point toward this diagnosis. However, the two-year prodrome is unusually long. A third possibility is **frontotemporal dementia with corticobasal syndrome features**, with the MRI changes representing gliosis and iron deposition secondary to neurodegeneration.
