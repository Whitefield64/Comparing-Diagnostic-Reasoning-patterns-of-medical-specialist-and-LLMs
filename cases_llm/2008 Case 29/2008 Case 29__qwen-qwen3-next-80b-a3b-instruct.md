## Differential Diagnosis

The clinical presentation of this 19-year-old man is complex, multidimensional, and deeply intertwined with chronic systemic dysfunction stemming from a long-standing, under-recognized eating disorder—specifically, restrictive anorexia nervosa. The acute admission was precipitated by profound abdominal distention, diffuse tenderness, and ileus-like findings on imaging, ultimately diagnosed as fecal impaction with massive small bowel and gastric dilatation. However, to understand the pathophysiology of this acute event and to anticipate future risks, one must construct a differential diagnosis that spans gastrointestinal, endocrine, metabolic, cardiovascular, and psychiatric domains. The goal is not merely to explain the acute obstruction but to contextualize it within the chronic, multisystem derangements of severe, prolonged malnutrition.

The most immediate and clinically salient problem was the mechanical ileus. The CT scan revealed massive dilatation of the stomach and distal small bowel with abundant stool in the colon and no transition point—a classic radiographic constellation of functional ileus secondary to chronic fecal impaction, not a mechanical obstruction from tumor, adhesion, or volvulus. The absence of air-fluid levels, free air, or vascular compromise on imaging argues strongly against acute mechanical obstruction, ischemia, or perforation. The resolution of symptoms with bowel evacuation and nasogastric decompression further supports a functional etiology. In this context, the leading diagnosis for the acute presentation is **functional ileus due to chronic fecal impaction secondary to severe, prolonged anorexia nervosa**. However, this is not an isolated phenomenon—it is the endpoint of a cascade of physiological adaptations and pathologies that must be systematically considered.

First, we must consider whether there are other gastrointestinal disorders capable of producing similar findings. Chronic constipation and megacolon can occur in Hirschsprung disease, but this is typically diagnosed in infancy or early childhood with failure to pass meconium, and the patient’s developmental history—including normal bowel movements until recently and normal Tanner staging—is inconsistent with this diagnosis. Neurogenic bowel from spinal cord pathology is another consideration, but there is no history of trauma, neurological deficits, or sensory changes, and the MRI of the brain showed no structural lesions affecting autonomic pathways. The patient’s normal bone age (15.5 years) and absence of neurologic signs on exam further argue against a central or peripheral neuropathy. Chronic intestinal pseudo-obstruction (CIPO) is a rare but important consideration. CIPO can manifest with recurrent episodes of ileus, abdominal distention, and failure to thrive, often with normal structural imaging. However, CIPO typically presents earlier in life, often with failure to thrive from infancy, and is associated with abnormal motility studies (e.g., manometry showing absent or dyscoordinated contractions), which were not performed here. Furthermore, the patient’s history of weight stabilization followed by gradual decline, coupled with behavioral restriction of food intake, strongly points to a behavioral-psychiatric etiology rather than a primary motility disorder. The fact that his ileus resolved completely with bowel evacuation and refeeding argues against a primary myopathic or neuropathic gut disorder; in true CIPO, refeeding alone rarely resolves the underlying motility defect.

Infectious causes of ileus, such as cytomegalovirus (CMV) or Clostridioides difficile colitis, are unlikely. There was no fever, diarrhea, leukocytosis, or recent antibiotic exposure. The stool studies were not reported, but the clinical context of chronic malnutrition and absence of acute infectious symptoms makes these improbable. Parasitic infections such as strongyloidiasis can cause ileus and malabsorption, particularly in immunocompromised states, but there is no history of travel to endemic areas, no eosinophilia, and no evidence of chronic diarrhea or skin manifestations (e.g., larva currens). Tuberculosis of the gastrointestinal tract is exceedingly rare in this demographic without known exposure or immunosuppression and would typically present with chronic weight loss, night sweats, and lymphadenopathy—not acute ileus with fecal impaction.

Autoimmune or inflammatory conditions must also be considered. Celiac disease can cause chronic malabsorption, weight loss, and constipation (not just diarrhea), and is associated with hypothyroidism and other autoimmune phenomena. However, the patient’s normal serum electrolytes, liver enzymes, and absence of anemia or low albumin (at least at the time of initial labs) make classic celiac disease unlikely. Furthermore, if celiac were the primary driver, one would expect more consistent malabsorption, steatorrhea, and failure to gain weight since childhood, not a period of weight stability followed by decline. The patient’s normal bone density at age 16 (though later shown to be severely low) is inconsistent with long-standing, untreated celiac disease, which typically causes profound osteopenia by adolescence. Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD)—Crohn’s disease in particular—can cause strictures, ileus, and weight loss. However, Crohn’s typically presents with diarrhea, bloody stools, perianal disease, or fistulas, none of which are reported. The absence of fever, elevated inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR not reported, but normal liver enzymes and albumin suggest no active inflammation), and the lack of transition point on CT argue against luminal narrowing or fibrostenotic disease. The patient’s acute presentation was one of dilatation, not obstruction, and the resolution with evacuation further argues against structural pathology.

Endocrine causes of constipation and ileus must be weighed. Hypothyroidism is a well-known cause of decreased gut motility, constipation, bradycardia, and cold intolerance. The patient’s bradycardia (pulse 38 bpm), hypothermia (temperature 35.0°C), and constipation are consistent with hypothyroidism. However, the patient’s thyroid function tests were reported as normal during prior hospitalizations, and there is no history of thyroid disease, goiter, or autoimmune conditions. Furthermore, in true hypothyroidism, one typically observes more pronounced myxedema, dry skin, periorbital edema, and delayed deep tendon reflexes—all absent here. The patient’s muscle mass, while wasted, retained a muscular appearance, and there was no puffiness or edema. The normalization of heart rate and temperature with refeeding and weight gain further supports that these were secondary to malnutrition rather than primary endocrine dysfunction. Hypopituitarism is another consideration, particularly given the prominence of ventricles and sulci on MRI—sometimes interpreted as cerebral atrophy, which can occur in severe malnutrition, but also can be seen in hypopituitarism with long-standing hormone deficiency. However, the patient had normal pubertal progression to Tanner stage 4, with descended testes and sparse facial hair, suggesting intact gonadotropin axis function. The normal pituitary on MRI and absence of other pituitary hormone deficiencies (e.g., cortisol, growth hormone) make hypopituitarism unlikely. Growth hormone deficiency could theoretically contribute to low weight and delayed maturation, but the patient’s height (157.5 cm) is within the range expected for parental height (father 175.3 cm, mother 149.9 cm), and his bone age (15.5 years) is only mildly delayed relative to chronological age (19 years), consistent with delayed puberty from malnutrition rather than a primary growth hormone disorder. His brothers’ heights also suggest familial short stature, not a pathological growth disorder.

Metabolic and electrolyte disturbances are central to this case. The patient developed profound hypophosphatemia during refeeding, requiring phosphate replacement. This is a hallmark of refeeding syndrome, a life-threatening condition occurring when nutrition is reintroduced to a severely malnourished individual. Refeeding syndrome is driven by insulin-driven intracellular shifts of phosphate, potassium, and magnesium, leading to hypophosphatemia, hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, and subsequent cardiac, neurologic, and respiratory complications. The patient’s bradycardia, arrhythmias, and hypothermia are consistent with electrolyte derangements and metabolic depression from prolonged starvation. The fact that his heart rate improved slowly over weeks with nutritional rehabilitation confirms that his bradycardia was not due to primary cardiac disease but to autonomic dysregulation secondary to chronic energy deficit. The absence of prior arrhythmias or structural heart disease on echocardiography (normal EF, only mild regurgitation) further supports this. The mild mitral and aortic regurgitation likely represent volume-depleted, hyperdynamic states common in anorexia nervosa, where the heart atrophies and regurgitation is functional rather than valvular.

Cardiovascular causes of bradycardia and hypotension must be considered. Sinus bradycardia in athletes is common, but this patient’s pulse of 29–38 bpm at rest, coupled with orthostatic hypotension and hypothermia, is far beyond physiologic adaptation. In elite endurance athletes, heart rates rarely fall below 40 bpm at rest, and they do not exhibit hypothermia or orthostatic instability. The patient’s exercise regimen—running 8 km and lifting weights on alternate days—was not consistent with elite athletic training but rather with compulsive, driven behavior typical of anorexia nervosa. His “exercise” was not recreational but pathological, used to offset caloric intake and maintain low weight. The bradycardia here is a consequence of autonomic nervous system remodeling: increased vagal tone and decreased sympathetic outflow due to chronic energy deprivation. This is a well-documented phenomenon in anorexia nervosa and is reversible with weight restoration. Primary conduction system disease (e.g., sick sinus syndrome, heart block) is extremely rare in a 19-year-old without structural heart disease or family history of sudden cardiac death, and the absence of syncope, presyncope, or documented AV block on ECG argues against this.

Psychiatric and behavioral factors are not merely comorbid but central. The diagnosis of restrictive anorexia nervosa was made four years prior, and the patient’s denial of illness, poor insight, and continued caloric restriction despite medical deterioration are diagnostic hallmarks. The family history of maternal dieting and paternal panic attacks suggests a possible genetic or environmental predisposition to anxiety and disordered eating. The patient’s academic excellence and solitary living situation may have facilitated isolation and concealment of his behaviors. The fact that he performed sit-ups in bed while denying exercise is a classic example of denial and cognitive distortion. His refusal to accept the severity of his illness, despite multiple hospitalizations and clear clinical deterioration, is not simply noncompliance—it is a core feature of the illness itself. The MRI findings of ventricular and sulcal prominence are consistent with cerebral atrophy from chronic malnutrition, a known neuroanatomical consequence of prolonged anorexia nervosa, and correlate with impaired insight and executive dysfunction.

Finally, we must consider whether other eating disorders could mimic this presentation. Bulimia nervosa is excluded by the absence of binge-purge cycles, vomiting, or laxative abuse. Avoidant/restrictive food intake disorder (ARFID) is a consideration, but ARFID typically lacks body image distortion and fear of weight gain, which are central here. The patient’s history of weight loss after cessation of growth at age 11, his intense focus on caloric restriction despite normal appetite (he reported early satiety, which is likely a consequence of gastric atrophy and delayed emptying), and his denial of illness are inconsistent with ARFID and strongly support anorexia nervosa.

In summary, the acute presentation of fecal impaction and ileus is best understood as the gastrointestinal manifestation of severe, chronic anorexia nervosa. The constellation of bradycardia, hypothermia, orthostatic hypotension, constipation, muscle wasting, and poor insight forms a diagnostic triad specific to this disorder. While other gastrointestinal, endocrine, metabolic, and infectious causes were considered, none fit the clinical narrative as completely. The resolution of symptoms with refeeding and the gradual normalization of vital signs confirm that this was a functional, reversible consequence of starvation—not a primary structural or infectious disease. The leading diagnosis is **restrictive anorexia nervosa with complications of refeeding syndrome, functional ileus from fecal impaction, and autonomic dysregulation**. The differential diagnosis, while broad in theory, collapses into this single, unifying diagnosis when viewed through the lens of chronic malnutrition, behavioral pathology, and physiological adaptation. All other possibilities are either excluded by clinical data or rendered highly improbable by the temporal and behavioral context. This case is a stark reminder that in eating disorders, the body’s most vital systems—gut, heart, brain—are not merely affected; they are remade by starvation.
