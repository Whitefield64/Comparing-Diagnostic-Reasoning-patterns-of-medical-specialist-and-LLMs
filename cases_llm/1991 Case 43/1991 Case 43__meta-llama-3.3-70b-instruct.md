# Case 43-1991 —A 27-Year-Old Man with AIDS, a Cough, Fever, and Pulmonary Infiltrates

## Presentation of Case



A 27-year-old man with the acquired immunodeficiency syndrome (AIDS) was admitted to the hospital because of a cough, fever, and an abnormal radiograph of the chest.



The patient was well until 8 1/2 months earlier, when he was first admitted to this hospital because of a headache, fever, cough, and dyspnea for two days. He had emigrated from Puerto Rico two months earlier, after his wife and child died of AIDS; his wife had received transfusions in the past after a gynecologic operation. The patient had lost 9 kg in weight during the previous two or three months. He smoked one pack of cigarettes daily for five years and drank as many as 12 beers weekly. He was unemployed. There was a history of recurrent genital herpes and of multiple sexual partners. One week before admission he had first come to this hospital, where a test for human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) antibodies was positive and the CD4+ cell count was 45×10 . There was a history of intravenous drug abuse. The patient denied homosexual activity or exposure to prostitutes.



Physical examination on admission showed that the temperature was 39.2°C. Scattered crackles were heard in both lungs. The hematocrit was 40.1 percent; the white-cell count was 8700, with 76 percent neutrophils. The urea nitrogen was 3.2 mmol per liter (9 mg per 100 ml), and the glucose 4.3 mmol per liter (78 mg per 100 ml). An electrocardiogram revealed a mean QRS axis of approximately 120 degrees and was otherwise normal. An x-ray film of the chest disclosed low lung volumes bilaterally; an ill-defined patchy density was observed at the left-lung base, with a subtle, diffuse increase in interstitial markings; the pleural spaces, cardiomediastinal silhouette, and bony thorax appeared normal. A specimen of arterial blood, drawn while the patient was breathing room air, showed that the partial pressure of oxygen (PaO    ) was 74 mm Hg, the partial pressure of carbon dioxide (PaCO    ) 45 mm Hg, and the pH 7.41. A lumbar puncture revealed that the cerebrospinal fluid was normal. A CT scan of the cranium, performed without the intravenous injection of contrast material, showed a normal appearance of the intracranial structures; slight mucosal thickening was seen in the ethmoid and sphenoid sinuses. Microscopical examination of an induced specimen of sputum disclosed no  *Pneumocystis carinii* . A test for toxoplasma IgG antibodies and a serologic test for syphilis were negative. A culture of sputum yielded a normal respiratory tract flora. A tuberculin skin test (PPD, 5 TU) and a skin test with mumps antigen were negative. Trimethoprim–sulfamethoxazole was administered by vein, with prompt defervescence. One day before discharge, examination with a fiberoptic bronchoscope revealed no abnormality. Microscopical examination of bronchial washings from the right middle lobe disclosed no neutrophils; abundant gram-positive and gram-negative microorganisms of mixed morphologic pattern were observed, without a predominant type; no acid-fast bacilli were seen; a fungal wet preparation revealed budding yeasts; a culture yielded  *Candida albicans* , and a mycobacterial culture was later reported to be negative. The patient was discharged on the sixth hospital day on trimethoprim–sulfamethoxazole by mouth for 10 days.



At follow-up examination four days after discharge a rash was present. Treatment with zidovudine (100 mg five times daily) and pentamidine (monthly doses by aerosol) was begun. Information was received that the patient's compliance with the zidovudine regimen was poor and that he continued to use illicit drugs, although he no longer injected them.



The patient felt well until two months before entry, when there was the onset of gradually increasing exertional dyspnea and fatigue. Two to four weeks before entry a mild cough and fever recurred, without sputum production.



Nineteen days before admission the patient returned to his physician. On physical examination he did not appear acutely ill and was afebrile; wheezes were heard at both lung bases. An x-ray film of the chest disclosed a diffuse but indistinct increase in interstitial densities throughout both lungs, without frank consolidation; no mediastinal or hilar lymphadenopathy was observed, and the pleural spaces, cardiomediastinal silhouette, and bony thorax remained normal. Eight days before entry the patient was again seen by his physician, who found no change except that the temperature was 37.8°C. Four days before admission, during a routine treatment with aerosol pentamidine, bronchospasm developed. An x-ray film of the chest disclosed diffuse, fine nodular opacities throughout both lungs, with questionable bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy; the pleural spaces and cardiac silhouette remained normal. The patient was urged to return to the hospital and was admitted after a delay on his part; he reportedly coughed up or vomited bright-red blood on the day of entry.
