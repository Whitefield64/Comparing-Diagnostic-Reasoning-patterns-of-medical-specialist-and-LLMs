# Case 43-1991 —A 27-Year-Old Man with AIDS, a Cough, Fever, and Pulmonary Infiltrates

## Presentation of Case



A 27-year-old man with the acquired immunodeficiency syndrome (AIDS) was admitted to the hospital because of a cough, fever, and an abnormal radiograph of the chest.



The patient was well until 8 1/2 months earlier, when he was first admitted to this hospital because of a headache, fever, cough, and dyspnea for two days. He had emigrated from Puerto Rico two months earlier, after his wife and child died of AIDS; his wife had received transfusions in the past after a gynecologic operation. The patient had lost 9 kg in weight during the previous two or three months. He smoked one pack of cigarettes daily for five years and drank as many as 12 beers weekly. He was unemployed. There was a history of recurrent genital herpes and of multiple sexual partners. One week before admission he had first come to this hospital, where a test for human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) antibodies was positive and the CD4+ cell count was 45×10 . There was a history of intravenous drug abuse. The patient denied homosexual activity or exposure to prostitutes.



Physical examination on admission showed that the temperature was 39.2°C. Scattered crackles were heard in both lungs. The hematocrit was 40.1 percent; the white-cell count was 8700, with 76 percent neutrophils. The urea nitrogen was 3.2 mmol per liter (9 mg per 100 ml), and the glucose 4.3 mmol per liter (78 mg per 100 ml). An electrocardiogram revealed a mean QRS axis of approximately 120 degrees and was otherwise normal. An x-ray film of the chest disclosed low lung volumes bilaterally; an ill-defined patchy density was observed at the left-lung base, with a subtle, diffuse increase in interstitial markings; the pleural spaces, cardiomediastinal silhouette, and bony thorax appeared normal. A specimen of arterial blood, drawn while the patient was breathing room air, showed that the partial pressure of oxygen (PaO    ) was 74 mm Hg, the partial pressure of carbon dioxide (PaCO    ) 45 mm Hg, and the pH 7.41. A lumbar puncture revealed that the cerebrospinal fluid was normal. A CT scan of the cranium, performed without the intravenous injection of contrast material, showed a normal appearance of the intracranial structures; slight mucosal thickening was seen in the ethmoid and sphenoid sinuses. Microscopical examination of an induced specimen of sputum disclosed no  *Pneumocystis carinii* . A test for toxoplasma IgG antibodies and a serologic test for syphilis were negative. A culture of sputum yielded a normal respiratory tract flora. A tuberculin skin test (PPD, 5 TU) and a skin test with mumps antigen were negative. Trimethoprim–sulfamethoxazole was administered by vein, with prompt defervescence. One day before discharge, examination with a fiberoptic bronchoscope revealed no abnormality. Microscopical examination of bronchial washings from the right middle lobe disclosed no neutrophils; abundant gram-positive and gram-negative microorganisms of mixed morphologic pattern were observed, without a predominant type; no acid-fast bacilli were seen; a fungal wet preparation revealed budding yeasts; a culture yielded  *Candida albicans* , and a mycobacterial culture was later reported to be negative. The patient was discharged on the sixth hospital day on trimethoprim–sulfamethoxazole by mouth for 10 days.



At follow-up examination four days after discharge a rash was present. Treatment with zidovudine (100 mg five times daily) and pentamidine (monthly doses by aerosol) was begun. Information was received that the patient's compliance with the zidovudine regimen was poor and that he continued to use illicit drugs, although he no longer injected them.



The patient felt well until two months before entry, when there was the onset of gradually increasing exertional dyspnea and fatigue. Two to four weeks before entry a mild cough and fever recurred, without sputum production.



Nineteen days before admission the patient returned to his physician. On physical examination he did not appear acutely ill and was afebrile; wheezes were heard at both lung bases. An x-ray film of the chest disclosed a diffuse but indistinct increase in interstitial densities throughout both lungs, without frank consolidation; no mediastinal or hilar lymphadenopathy was observed, and the pleural spaces, cardiomediastinal silhouette, and bony thorax remained normal. Eight days before entry the patient was again seen by his physician, who found no change except that the temperature was 37.8°C. Microscopical examination of an induced specimen of sputum showed no evidence of  *P. carinii* . Four days before admission, during a routine treatment with aerosol pentamidine, bronchospasm developed. An x-ray film of the chest disclosed diffuse, fine nodular opacities throughout both lungs, with questionable bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy; the pleural spaces and cardiac silhouette remained normal. The patient was urged to return to the hospital and was admitted after a delay on his part; he reportedly coughed up or vomited bright-red blood on the day of entry.



There was no history of recent headache, diarrhea, or night sweats or of recent travel outside the Boston area.

## Differential Diagnosis

This 27-year-old man with advanced AIDS (CD4+ count of 45 cells/μL) presents with a prolonged, relapsing, and progressively deteriorating pulmonary illness marked by recurrent fever, exertional dyspnea, nonproductive cough, diffuse bilateral interstitial and nodular infiltrates on chest imaging, episodic hemoptysis, and persistent systemic inflammation despite multiple courses of antimicrobial therapy. The clinical trajectory spans nearly a year, beginning with an initial episode of fever, cough, and infiltrates—initially presumed to be *Pneumocystis jirovecii* pneumonia (PJP)—followed by a second, more severe presentation characterized by worsening dyspnea, new nodular opacities, hemoptysis, and persistent high-grade fevers unresponsive to trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole, pentamidine, and erythromycin. The absence of definitive microbiologic identification despite multiple bronchoalveolar lavages (BAL), transbronchial biopsies, blood cultures, and serologic testing underscores the diagnostic complexity inherent in advanced immunosuppression. The differential diagnosis must be approached systematically, considering not only the most common opportunistic infections but also rare pathogens, malignancies, and non-infectious inflammatory syndromes that can mimic or coexist in the setting of profound T-cell deficiency. The patient’s history of intravenous drug use, recent immigration from Puerto Rico, poor medication adherence, cigarette smoking, and multiple sexual partners further complicates risk stratification and broadens the scope of potential etiologies.

The initial episode eight months prior, treated successfully with trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole, was likely misattributed to PJP. While the clinical presentation—fever, dyspnea, bilateral interstitial infiltrates, and hypoxemia (PaO₂ 74 mm Hg)—is classic for PJP, the absence of *P. jirovecii* on sputum induction and BAL, combined with the lack of eosinophilia and the fact that trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole has broad antimicrobial activity against gram-positive and gram-negative organisms, suggests that the initial response may have been due to a concomitant bacterial pneumonia, such as *Streptococcus pneumoniae* or *Haemophilus influenzae*, or even a mild, self-limiting fungal or viral process. The recurrence two months later with more severe symptoms, new nodular opacities, hemoptysis, and persistent fevers despite prophylactic pentamidine strongly implies either a failure of PJP prophylaxis due to nonadherence, a new infectious process, or a neoplastic etiology. The development of hemoptysis is particularly significant: while it can occur in PJP, it is exceedingly rare and usually mild. In this context, hemoptysis raises the strong possibility of vascular erosion due to malignancy, granulomatous necrosis from fungal or mycobacterial infection, or pulmonary infarction secondary to vasculitis or thrombosis.

The most compelling diagnostic consideration remains **disseminated histoplasmosis**. *Histoplasma capsulatum*, a dimorphic fungus endemic to the Ohio and Mississippi River valleys, is increasingly recognized in urban centers and among immigrant populations, including those from Latin America, where environmental exposure may occur via contaminated soil, bird or bat droppings, or even through airborne transmission in crowded or poorly ventilated settings. In patients with CD4+ counts below 150 cells/μL, histoplasmosis frequently manifests as a disseminated disease with fever, weight loss, hepatosplenomegaly, pancytopenia, and diffuse pulmonary infiltrates that appear as fine nodular or reticulonodular opacities—precisely the pattern observed here. The radiographic findings of bilateral, diffuse interstitial and nodular densities with questionable hilar lymphadenopathy are classic for hematogenous dissemination. The patient’s spiking fevers, with afternoon and evening peaks, are highly characteristic of systemic fungal infections, reflecting circadian fluctuations in cytokine release and macrophage activation. The markedly elevated ferritin (725 μg/L) and LDH (470 U/L) are not merely nonspecific markers of inflammation but are strongly suggestive of macrophage activation syndrome (MAS), a known complication of disseminated histoplasmosis, wherein activated macrophages release excessive interleukin-1, interleukin-6, and tumor necrosis factor-alpha, leading to hyperferritinemia, cytopenias, and hepatic dysfunction. The bone marrow biopsy, though described as inadequate, showed an increased myeloid-to-erythroid ratio and scattered plasma cells—findings consistent with chronic inflammatory or infectious marrow infiltration, as seen in histoplasmosis, tuberculosis, or lymphoma. The negative BAL and transbronchial biopsy for fungi may reflect sampling error: *Histoplasma* organisms are often sparse, intracellular within macrophages, and require special stains (GMS, PAS) for visualization, which may not have been performed or adequately evaluated. Moreover, the organism is notoriously difficult to culture from respiratory specimens unless large volumes are processed. The Isolator blood cultures drawn for mycobacteria and yeasts are critical: in disseminated histoplasmosis, blood cultures are positive in up to 80% of cases, and the organism can take up to two to four weeks to grow. The patient’s prior exposure in Puerto Rico does not preclude infection; *H. capsulatum* is present in soil throughout the Caribbean, particularly in areas with high humidity and bird or bat roosting sites. The failure of pentamidine (which has no antifungal activity against *Histoplasma*) and erythromycin (which has limited activity) to improve his condition further supports a fungal etiology, as these agents are not effective against *Histoplasma*. The presence of *Candida albicans* in BAL is likely incidental colonization, as the absence of inflammatory cells and the lack of response to antifungal therapy argue against *Candida* as the primary pathogen.

A second major diagnostic possibility is **disseminated tuberculosis**, particularly in light of the patient’s recent immigration from Puerto Rico, a region with a higher incidence of tuberculosis than the U.S. average. In AIDS, TB often presents atypically: paucibacillary disease, negative sputum smears, extrapulmonary involvement, and diffuse miliary or interstitial infiltrates rather than classic upper lobe cavitary lesions. The radiographic findings of bilateral nodular opacities and questionable hilar lymphadenopathy are classic for miliary TB, which results from hematogenous dissemination of *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*. The persistent fevers, weight loss, and elevated LDH are hallmark features. The negative tuberculin skin test (PPD) is expected in advanced AIDS due to anergy, and the negative AFB stains on multiple samples do not exclude TB, as the bacillary load is often low in immunocompromised hosts. Mycobacterial cultures, though reported negative, require prolonged incubation (up to six to eight weeks) for *M. tuberculosis* to grow, especially in patients with advanced immunosuppression, where organism replication is slower. The presence of a small left apical pneumothorax is highly suggestive of underlying cavitary disease or lymph node rupture into the pleural space, a known complication of TB in immunocompromised individuals. The elevated globulin (53 g/L) and albumin/globulin inversion (27/53) reflect chronic antigenic stimulation, consistent with persistent mycobacterial infection. The transient improvement with trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole may have been due to a coincidental bacterial superinfection or an immunomodulatory effect of the drug, which has been shown to reduce TNF-alpha production and may transiently dampen inflammatory responses in TB. The patient’s continued illicit drug use and poor adherence to zidovudine further increase the risk of TB reactivation or superinfection. A repeat bronchoscopy with extensive lavage for mycobacterial culture, PCR for *M. tuberculosis*, and a lymph node biopsy (if accessible) are essential. The absence of lymphadenopathy on imaging does not exclude TB, as extranodal or parenchymal disease can occur without nodal enlargement.

A third critical consideration is **Kaposi sarcoma (KS)**, an endothelial cell neoplasm caused by human herpesvirus 8 (HHV-8), which is strongly associated with AIDS, particularly in men with multiple sexual partners and intravenous drug use. Pulmonary KS can present with dyspnea, cough, fever, and hemoptysis, often mimicking infection. Radiographically, it manifests as bilateral, diffuse, nodular or reticulonodular infiltrates, sometimes with pleural effusions or lymphadenopathy—findings nearly identical to those described here. Hemoptysis is a particularly characteristic feature, resulting from the fragile, dilated vascular channels within the tumor that erode into bronchial vessels. The absence of cutaneous lesions does not rule out pulmonary KS; up to 20% of patients with pulmonary involvement have no visible skin lesions. The transbronchial biopsy, which showed only “scanty alveoli and histiocytes,” may have missed the diagnosis if the biopsy samples were too small, superficial, or taken from non-involved areas. KS lesions are often submucosal, perivascular, or parenchymal and may not be visible on bronchoscopy. The elevated LDH, anemia (hematocrit 28.4%), and chronic fever are consistent with a malignant process. The failure of antimicrobial therapy to improve symptoms, the progressive nature of the disease, and the persistence of nodular opacities despite broad-spectrum antibiotics strongly support a neoplastic etiology. A definitive diagnosis requires histopathologic confirmation of spindle-shaped endothelial cells forming slit-like vascular spaces, ideally with immunohistochemical staining for HHV-8 LANA-1 protein. The presence of plasma cells in the bone marrow may reflect a paraneoplastic response to HHV-8 or a coincident B-cell lymphoproliferative disorder, which can coexist with KS.

A fourth major consideration is **lymphoma**, particularly AIDS-related non-Hodgkin lymphoma (NHL). Primary pulmonary lymphoma is rare, but systemic NHL with pulmonary involvement is more common in AIDS, especially diffuse large B-cell or Burkitt lymphoma. These malignancies can present with fever, weight loss, elevated LDH, and pulmonary nodules or infiltrates. The “scanty histiocytes” on biopsy may represent infiltrating lymphoma cells misidentified as benign macrophages, especially if the tissue was insufficiently sampled or poorly processed. The patient’s elevated globulin, anemia, and persistent fevers are consistent with a lymphoproliferative disorder. The absence of lymphadenopathy on imaging does not exclude lymphoma, as pulmonary lymphoma can present with parenchymal nodules alone. The presence of a small pneumothorax may reflect tumor necrosis or rupture of a lymphomatous nodule into the pleural space. A repeat biopsy with flow cytometry, immunohistochemistry, and molecular studies for clonality is essential. The patient’s history of intravenous drug use and multiple sexual partners also raises the possibility of Epstein-Barr virus (EBV)-associated lymphoproliferative disorders, though EBV is more commonly linked to central nervous system lymphoma in AIDS.

Other infectious causes must be considered, including **invasive aspergillosis**. Although typically seen in neutropenic patients, *Aspergillus* can cause disease in severely immunocompromised hosts with T-cell deficiency, especially those with prior lung damage from smoking or recurrent infections. The radiographic findings of nodular opacities, sometimes with a halo sign (not described but possible), and hemoptysis are classic. The negative fungal stains and cultures may reflect early disease or inadequate sampling. Serum galactomannan or PCR for *Aspergillus* on BAL fluid would be diagnostic. The failure of pentamidine and erythromycin to improve symptoms is expected, as neither agent has activity against *Aspergillus*. **Mycobacterium avium complex (MAC)** is another important consideration. In AIDS patients with CD4+ counts below 50, MAC commonly causes disseminated disease with fever, weight loss, anemia, and elevated alkaline phosphatase. Pulmonary involvement may be nodular or cavitary. The patient’s negative mycobacterial cultures may reflect slow growth or inadequate sampling. MAC is often resistant to standard antibiotics and requires macrolide-based regimens; erythromycin was administered, but likely at suboptimal dose or duration. **Cryptococcosis** is less likely given the negative cryptococcal antigen and normal CSF (low protein, no organisms), but atypical pulmonary presentations with nodules can occur, especially if dissemination is early.

Non-infectious causes such as **drug-induced lung injury** from zidovudine or pentamidine must be considered. Pentamidine can cause interstitial pneumonitis, but typically presents with dry cough and hypoxemia without fever or hemoptysis. Zidovudine can cause eosinophilic pneumonia, but the patient’s eosinophil count is not elevated. The temporal association is weak, and the persistence of symptoms after discontinuation of these agents argues against a primary drug etiology. **Hypersensitivity pneumonitis** is unlikely given the absence of known antigen exposure and the chronic, progressive nature of the disease.

In summary, the leading diagnostic possibilities are **disseminated histoplasmosis**, **disseminated tuberculosis**, and **Kaposi sarcoma**, each supported by clinical, radiographic, and laboratory findings. Histoplasmosis is favored by the fever pattern, elevated ferritin and LDH, geographic exposure, and failure of antimicrobials. Tuberculosis is favored by the immigration history and chronicity. Kaposi sarcoma is favored by the hemoptysis and lack of response to therapy. The negative cultures and biopsies likely reflect sampling limitations rather than true absence of disease. The next diagnostic steps must include repeat transbronchial biopsy with multiple samples, GMS and AFB stains, culture for mycobacteria and fungi on enriched media, PCR for *M. tuberculosis* and HHV-8, serum histoplasma antigen testing, and extended blood culture incubation. Without definitive histopathology, the patient remains at high risk of fatal progression. The clinical trajectory—worsening despite therapy, persistent fever, and hemoptysis—demands a diagnosis beyond common opportunistic infections. The most likely unifying diagnosis is disseminated histoplasmosis, with tuberculosis and Kaposi sarcoma as critical alternatives requiring immediate exclusion.
