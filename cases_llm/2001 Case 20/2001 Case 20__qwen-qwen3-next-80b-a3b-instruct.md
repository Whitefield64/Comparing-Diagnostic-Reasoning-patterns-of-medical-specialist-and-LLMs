# Case 20-2001 —A 64-Year-Old Man with Fever and Gram-Negative Bacteremia

## Presentation of Case



A 64-year-old man was admitted to the hospital because of fever and bacteremia.



The patient had had hypertension for 20 years, type 2 diabetes mellitus for 16 years, and coronary disease for 7 years, with a history of myocardial infarction and congestive heart failure. He also had proteinuria (+++) and mild, chronic renal insufficiency.



He had otherwise been well until three weeks before admission, when he was traveling in the Middle East and began to have fever (peak temperature, 39.0°C), with fatigue and weight loss. Ten days before admission, treatment with ciprofloxacin (500 mg daily) was begun. Three days later, a physician heard a soft systolic murmur on physical examination; the temperature was normal, and the erythrocyte sedimentation rate was 59 mm per hour. A chest radiograph was unremarkable; an abdominal ultrasonographic examination revealed a gallstone, without evidence of thickening of the gallbladder wall, and a cyst in the left kidney. A malarial smear was negative. The fever recurred, and the administration of ciprofloxacin for six days and additional treatment with metronidazole (400 mg twice a day) did not relieve it. A culture of a blood specimen, obtained three days after the initiation of treatment with ciprofloxacin, yielded gram-negative bacilli, which were identified as β-lactamase–negative  *Moraxella phenylpyruvica* . The microorganisms were sensitive to amoxicillin, cefuroxime, ciprofloxacin, gentamicin, and tetracycline and were resistant to trimethoprim. The patient was admitted to this hospital.



The patient's weight had dropped from 93 to 78 kg during the year before admission. He reported that he had not ingested unpasteurized milk and had consumed little meat.



The temperature was 37.1°C, the pulse was 60, and the respirations were 18. The blood pressure was 100/60 mm Hg.



Physical examination showed no abnormalities except for the presence of a grade 2 systolic murmur along the upper sternal border on the right side. The urine was positive (++) for protein; the sediment contained 0 to 2 red cells, 3 to 5 white cells, and 0 to 2 hyaline casts per low-power field. Other laboratory tests were performed ( and ). An electrocardiogram showed nonspecific ST-segment and T-wave abnormalities; the findings were otherwise normal. Thoracic radiographs showed no abnormalities. A transrectal ultrasonographic study of the prostate gland showed no evidence of an abscess.



Urine and three samples of blood were obtained for culture. Cefuroxime (750 mg every eight hours) was administered. During the next several days, the peak temperature each day ranged from 36.9° to 37.6°C. The urine culture and an assay for  *Clostridium difficile* toxin were negative.



On the second hospital day, an ultrasonographic examination of the kidneys revealed bilateral cysts and a hyperechoic stone on the left side, without evidence of hydronephrosis. A transthoracic cardiac ultrasonographic study showed thickening of both mitral leaflets, calcification of the posterior mitral annulus, left atrial dilatation, thickened aortic leaflets, symmetric left ventricular hypertrophy, and segmental left ventricular dysfunction. The estimated ejection fraction was 60 percent. A transesophageal cardiac ultrasonographic examination showed no evidence of vegetations or paravalvular abscesses; there was moderate atheromatous disease of the descending thoracic aorta with several protruding plaques, including one that appeared ulcerated.



On the third hospital day, a tuberculin skin test (purified protein derivative, 5 TU) was positive, with an induration that was 16 mm in diameter. (The patient had received bacille Calmette–Guérin vaccine in the past and a booster dose more recently; a tuberculin skin test with purified protein derivative, performed after the booster dose, was negative.) Additional blood and urine specimens were obtained for culture, and a single dose of vancomycin was given. A computed tomographic (CT) examination of the thorax, performed after the oral administration of contrast material, revealed multiple, calcified left-sided hilar and subcarinal lymph nodes; the lungs appeared normal. A CT scan of the abdomen and pelvis, obtained after the oral administration of contrast material, showed a gallstone.



On the fifth hospital day, a radionuclide bone scan showed small foci of increased uptake in the cervical spine, right foot, and right knee; these findings were most consistent with the presence of degenerative changes. There was no evidence of osteomyelitis. On the sixth hospital day, one of the three blood-culture specimens obtained on admission yielded gram-negative rods.



A diagnostic procedure was performed.

## Differential Diagnosis

The clinical presentation of this 64-year-old man with prolonged fever, significant weight loss, gram-negative bacteremia due to *Moraxella phenylpyruvica*, and a constellation of chronic medical conditions—including hypertension, diabetes, coronary artery disease, heart failure, proteinuria, and chronic kidney disease—demands a systematic and nuanced diagnostic approach. The bacteremia, occurring in the setting of recent international travel, unresponsive antibiotic therapy, and the presence of structural cardiac abnormalities, raises critical questions about the source, significance, and implications of the organism isolated. While *Moraxella phenylpyruvica* (now more commonly classified as *Neisseria phenylpyruvica*, a member of the Neisseriaceae family) is a rare and typically nonpathogenic commensal of the oropharynx and upper respiratory tract, its isolation from blood cultures in the context of persistent fever and systemic symptoms cannot be dismissed as contamination. The organism’s susceptibility profile and the clinical course suggest true bacteremia, necessitating an investigation into potential underlying foci of infection, immune compromise, or endovascular seeding.

The most compelling diagnostic consideration is infective endocarditis (IE), particularly in its culture-negative or atypical form. Although the transesophageal echocardiogram (TEE) did not demonstrate vegetations or paravalvular abscesses, the presence of significant valvular pathology—thickened mitral and aortic leaflets, calcified posterior mitral annulus, and left atrial dilatation—creates a highly favorable substrate for endocardial colonization. In patients with preexisting valvular disease, especially in the setting of diabetes and chronic kidney disease, the risk of IE is markedly elevated. The organism isolated, while not a classic IE pathogen like *Streptococcus viridans*, *Staphylococcus aureus*, or *Enterococcus*, is a gram-negative rod that may have been misidentified or underrecognized in earlier literature. *Neisseria* species, including *N. elongata*, *N. weaveri*, and *N. phenylpyruvica*, have been reported in rare cases of endocarditis, particularly in patients with underlying valvular disease or immunocompromise. These organisms are fastidious, slow-growing, and may be overlooked or misclassified in standard blood culture systems. The negative TEE does not exclude IE; vegetations may be small, friable, or obscured by calcific deposits, especially in the setting of advanced degenerative valve disease. Furthermore, the absence of visible vegetations does not rule out “culture-negative endocarditis” due to prior antibiotic exposure, as the patient received ciprofloxacin for six days prior to blood culture collection—a fact that may have suppressed microbial growth sufficiently to prevent visualization on imaging while still allowing for intermittent bacteremia. The persistent low-grade fever, new systolic murmur, and elevated ESR (59 mm/hr) are classic, albeit nonspecific, signs of subacute endocarditis. The absence of peripheral stigmata (Osler nodes, Janeway lesions, Roth spots, splinter hemorrhages) does not exclude the diagnosis, particularly in elderly patients with diabetes and renal disease, where microvascular complications may blunt the typical clinical manifestations.

Another critical differential is occult abscess formation, particularly in the setting of prolonged fever and weight loss. The patient’s history of a renal cyst and gallstone raises the possibility of an infected cyst or cholecystitis, but the ultrasound findings explicitly excluded wall thickening or pericholecystic fluid, making acute cholecystitis unlikely. However, a chronically infected renal cyst, especially in the context of diabetes and chronic kidney disease, can serve as a nidus for persistent bacteremia. Diabetic patients are at increased risk for pyelonephritis, renal abscesses, and emphysematous infections, even in the absence of obstructive uropathy. The presence of bilateral renal cysts, one of which contains a hyperechoic stone, suggests possible polycystic kidney disease (ADPKD), though the absence of massive kidney enlargement and the chronicity of renal insufficiency make this less likely than acquired cystic kidney disease secondary to long-standing hypertension and diabetes. Even in acquired cystic disease, infection of a cyst can occur, often with gram-negative organisms, and may present with intermittent bacteremia without overt urinary symptoms. The urine sediment showing 3–5 WBCs and 0–2 hyaline casts is consistent with low-grade pyelonephritis or tubulointerstitial inflammation, not acute infection, but does not exclude a chronic, low-grade abscess. The absence of pyuria or positive urine culture does not rule out a deep-seated renal abscess, particularly if the organism is slow-growing or if prior antibiotics have suppressed urinary shedding.

The patient’s recent travel to the Middle East introduces a broad array of infectious considerations, particularly zoonotic and tropical pathogens. While malarial smear was negative, other vector-borne or food/water-borne infections must be considered. *Brucella* species, although typically associated with consumption of unpasteurized dairy or undercooked meat, can present with prolonged fever, weight loss, and bacteremia without clear exposure history. The patient denies such exposures, but *Brucella* can be acquired through less obvious routes, including inhalation of aerosols in agricultural settings or contact with infected animals during travel. *Brucella* is a gram-negative coccobacillus that may be misidentified as *Moraxella* or *Neisseria* in some laboratories due to similar morphology and slow growth. It is known to cause endocarditis, particularly in patients with preexisting valve disease, and often presents with negative blood cultures in the early stages, especially after antibiotic exposure. The positive tuberculin skin test (16 mm induration) in a patient with prior BCG vaccination complicates interpretation, but the size of induration, coupled with the clinical picture, raises concern for latent tuberculosis reactivation. BCG vaccination can cause false-positive reactions, but a reaction of >15 mm in an adult with risk factors (age, immunocompromise from diabetes, chronic kidney disease, and possible immunosuppression from malnutrition) should be interpreted as indicative of latent TB infection. The CT scan revealed calcified hilar and subcarinal lymph nodes—classic findings of healed granulomatous disease, possibly from prior tuberculosis. However, in the setting of prolonged fever, weight loss, and bacteremia, active miliary tuberculosis or lymph node tuberculosis with bacteremia must be considered. Tuberculosis can present with gram-negative bacteremia due to concomitant enteric translocation or secondary infection, though this is uncommon. More importantly, TB can mimic endocarditis, causing fever, weight loss, elevated ESR, and cardiac involvement such as pericardial effusion or myocardial granulomas. The patient’s cardiac findings—left ventricular hypertrophy, segmental dysfunction—could reflect chronic hypertensive heart disease, but could also be influenced by myocardial infiltration or vasculitis from TB.

The possibility of a systemic vasculitis or autoimmune process cannot be overlooked. The combination of fever, weight loss, proteinuria, and renal insufficiency raises concern for a systemic inflammatory disorder such as polyarteritis nodosa (PAN), microscopic polyangiitis (MPA), or granulomatosis with polyangiitis (GPA). PAN, in particular, is associated with hepatitis B infection (though not suggested here), hypertension, renal involvement (including infarcts and ischemic changes), and constitutional symptoms. The proteinuria with minimal red blood cells and few white cells in the sediment is more consistent with glomerular or tubulointerstitial injury than active glomerulonephritis, but does not exclude immune complex-mediated disease. The presence of calcified lymph nodes and the bone scan findings of increased uptake in degenerative joints could reflect granulomatous inflammation or vasculitic involvement of small vessels supplying bone and lymph nodes. The negative ANCA studies (though not reported, their absence is implied) and lack of pulmonary infiltrates or neuropathy make GPA and MPA less likely, but not impossible. The patient’s age, chronic kidney disease, and diabetes also place him at risk for diabetic nephropathy, which typically presents with heavy proteinuria and progressive renal failure but not with fever or bacteremia. The acute onset of fever and bacteremia, however, suggests an additional superimposed process.

The presence of *Moraxella phenylpyruvica* in blood culture, though rare, is highly suggestive of a mucosal source of bacteremia. This organism is a normal inhabitant of the oropharynx and upper respiratory tract. In immunocompromised hosts, or those with chronic mucosal disruption (e.g., from periodontal disease, recent dental procedures, or even chronic aspiration), it can translocate into the bloodstream. The patient’s age, diabetes, and chronic illness predispose him to periodontal disease, which is a known source of bacteremia with oral flora, including *Neisseria* and *Moraxella* species. The new systolic murmur, occurring after the onset of fever, could represent acute-on-chronic valvular regurgitation secondary to endocarditis seeded from oral flora. The fact that the fever persisted despite ciprofloxacin and metronidazole is consistent with a source that is not responsive to broad-spectrum antibiotics targeting gut flora—suggesting a non-enteric, mucosal origin. The patient’s denial of meat or unpasteurized milk intake makes foodborne pathogens less likely, but does not eliminate zoonotic or environmental exposures.

The possibility of a malignancy with paraneoplastic fever or occult metastatic infection must be considered. The 15 kg weight loss over one year is profound and highly concerning for malignancy. The CT scan revealed no mass lesions, but the calcified lymph nodes could represent granulomatous disease or, less commonly, lymphoma with necrotic or calcified centers. Lymphoma, particularly Hodgkin’s or follicular lymphoma, can present with prolonged fever, night sweats, weight loss, and elevated ESR, sometimes with bacteremia due to secondary immunosuppression or mucosal barrier breakdown. The patient’s cardiac findings could reflect lymphomatous infiltration, though this is rare. The bone scan showed degenerative changes, but small foci of increased uptake could represent lytic or sclerotic metastases not yet visible on CT. The absence of adenopathy on CT does not exclude lymphoma, especially if it is nodal and small or located in the mediastinum or retroperitoneum. The patient’s chronic kidney disease and diabetes also predispose him to urinary tract malignancies, such as renal cell carcinoma or urothelial carcinoma, which can present with proteinuria, hematuria (though absent here), and systemic symptoms. A renal cyst with a hyperechoic stone could represent a complex cyst or neoplasm, though ultrasound findings were not diagnostic.

Finally, we must consider the possibility of a non-infectious, non-neoplastic inflammatory process such as sarcoidosis. The calcified hilar and subcarinal lymph nodes, the positive PPD (in a BCG-vaccinated patient), the fever, weight loss, and elevated ESR are all consistent with sarcoidosis. Sarcoidosis can involve the heart (cardiac sarcoidosis), leading to conduction abnormalities, heart failure, and valvular thickening due to granulomatous infiltration. The left ventricular hypertrophy and segmental dysfunction could reflect myocardial granulomas. The renal involvement, though uncommon, can occur with granulomatous interstitial nephritis, manifesting as proteinuria and renal insufficiency. The bacteremia, however, remains perplexing. Sarcoidosis does not typically cause bacteremia unless there is a superimposed infection due to immunosuppressive therapy or mucosal breakdown. The patient has not received immunosuppressants, and the organism isolated is not typical of a secondary infection in sarcoidosis.

In summary, the leading diagnostic possibilities are: (1) infective endocarditis due to *Neisseria phenylpyruvica* or a similar fastidious organism, seeded from oral flora or a mucosal source, in the setting of degenerative valvular disease; (2) occult renal abscess or infected cyst, particularly given the renal cysts and chronic kidney disease; (3) tuberculosis, either pulmonary or extrapulmonary, with possible bacteremia or immune-mediated fever; (4) a systemic vasculitis such as polyarteritis nodosa, with secondary renal and systemic manifestations; and (5) an occult malignancy, particularly lymphoma or renal cell carcinoma, with paraneoplastic fever and secondary infection. The most compelling and likely diagnosis is endocarditis due to *Neisseria phenylpyruvica*, given the temporal relationship between fever onset, bacteremia, new murmur, valvular abnormalities, and the organism’s known association with endocarditis in patients with underlying heart disease. The negative TEE is not definitive, and the organism’s rarity does not negate its pathogenic potential in this context. The next diagnostic step should be repeated blood cultures with prolonged incubation, serologic testing for *Brucella* and *Coxiella*, and possibly a cardiac MRI to evaluate for vegetations or myocardial inflammation not seen on echocardiography. If endocarditis remains suspected, surgical valvular evaluation may be warranted despite the absence of visible vegetations.
