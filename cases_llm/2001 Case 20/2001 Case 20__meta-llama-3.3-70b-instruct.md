A culture of a blood specimen, obtained three days after the initiation of treatment with ciprofloxacin, yielded gram-negative bacilli, which were identified as β-lactamase–negative  *Moraxella phenylpyruvica* . The microorganisms were sensitive to amoxicillin, cefuroxime, ciprofloxacin, gentamicin, and tetracycline and were resistant to trimethoprim. The patient was admitted to this hospital.



The patient's weight had dropped from 93 to 78 kg during the year before admission. He reported that he had not ingested unpasteurized milk and had consumed little meat.



The temperature was 37.1°C, the pulse was 60, and the respirations were 18. The blood pressure was 100/60 mm Hg.



Physical examination showed no abnormalities except for the presence of a grade 2 systolic murmur along the upper sternal border on the right side. The urine was positive (++) for protein; the sediment contained 0 to 2 red cells, 3 to 5 white cells, and 0 to 2 hyaline casts per low-power field. Other laboratory tests were performed ( and ). A transrectal ultrasonographic study of the prostate gland showed no evidence of an abscess.



Urine and three samples of blood were obtained for culture. Cefuroxime (750 mg every eight hours) was administered. During the next several days, the peak temperature each day ranged from 36.9° to 37.6°C. The urine culture and an assay for  *Clostridium difficile* toxin were negative.

A transesophageal cardiac ultrasonographic examination showed no evidence of vegetations or paravalvular abscesses; there was moderate atheromatous disease of the descending thoracic aorta with several protruding plaques, including one that appeared ulcerated.

(The patient had received bacille Calmette–Guérin vaccine in the past and a booster dose more recently; a tuberculin skin test with purified protein derivative, performed after the booster dose, was negative.) Additional blood and urine specimens were obtained for culture, and a single dose of vancomycin was given. A computed tomographic (CT) examination of the thorax, performed after the oral administration of contrast material, revealed multiple, calcified left-sided hilar and subcarinal lymph nodes; the lungs appeared normal. A CT scan of the abdomen and pelvis, obtained after the oral administration of contrast material, showed a gallstone.



On the fifth hospital day, a radionuclide bone scan showed small foci of increased uptake in the cervical spine, right foot, and right knee; these findings were most consistent with the presence of degenerative changes. There was no evidence of osteomyelitis. On the sixth hospital day, one of the three blood-culture specimens obtained on admission yielded gram-negative rods.
