# Case 40-2015 —A 40-Year-Old Homeless Woman with Headache, Hypertension, and Psychosis

## Presentation of Case



   *Dr. Alex S. Keuroghlian* (Psychiatry): A 40-year-old woman with a history of psychosis was admitted to this hospital from a homeless shelter because of headache and hypertension.



One week before admission, the patient had accepted placement in a homeless shelter, after spending the previous 4 years sleeping in a public building. During that time, she had repeatedly declined assistance from outreach workers in finding shelter; she maintained that it was her mission from God to stay outside and watch over people. On evaluation at the shelter, she was disheveled and had disorganized speech and thought content that was focused on spiritual themes. She attributed her impairments to a traumatic brain injury that she had incurred in a motor vehicle accident more than 12 years earlier. She did not think she had a psychiatric illness and refused psychiatric medications but agreed to see an on-site primary care internist at the shelter. A limited physical examination revealed extensive tinea pedis and venous stasis dermatitis of the legs.



On the morning of admission, the patient reported a severe headache that had begun the night before. She permitted a nurse to measure her vital signs; the blood pressure was 180/110 mm Hg. Emergency medical services were called; on evaluation, the systolic blood pressure measured 212 mm Hg. She was brought by ambulance to the emergency department of this hospital.



The patient reported feeling “off” and intermittently said she had a frontal headache; she did not report visual symptoms, chest or abdominal pain, nausea, or vomiting. She had a history of hypertension and a psychotic illness of more than 12 years’ duration that was characterized by somatic, paranoid, grandiose delusions, a disorganized thought process, and poor self-care. Five years before this admission, she was admitted to a psychiatric hospital; as an inpatient, she had brief symptomatic improvement while she was receiving olanzapine. After discharge, she did not pursue follow-up psychiatric care or continue to take olanzapine. She had taken hydrochlorothiazide 4 years before this admission but currently took no medications; she reported an allergy to nifedipine, which had caused palpitations. She was born in a Caribbean country and had immigrated to the northeastern United States many years earlier. She smoked intermittently and did not use illicit drugs but reported being injected in the thigh with an unknown substance in the past. The family history was unknown.



On examination, the patient was obese, malodorous, disheveled, and wearing multiple layers of clothing. The blood pressure was 208/118 mm Hg; a repeat measurement was 240/130 mm Hg. ## Differential Diagnosis

The clinical presentation of this 40-year-old homeless woman is complex, integrating severe, acute-onset hypertension with a headache and a long-standing psychotic disorder. The constellation of symptoms—acute hypertensive crisis, headache, psychosis, chronic poor self-care, and multiple somatic comorbidities—demands a broad and nuanced differential diagnosis that considers both primary neurological, cardiovascular, and systemic etiologies, as well as the interplay between chronic psychiatric illness, socioeconomic deprivation, and potential underlying organic pathology. The absence of focal neurologic deficits, normal head CT without contrast, normal cardiac biomarkers, and lack of evidence for acute end-organ damage on initial labs complicates the picture but does not eliminate serious diagnoses. The resolution of headache and blood pressure with antihypertensive therapy is reassuring but does not elucidate the root cause of the hypertensive emergency.

The most immediate concern is the hypertensive emergency, defined by severe elevation in blood pressure (systolic >180 mm Hg and/or diastolic >120 mm Hg) with evidence of acute end-organ damage. In this case, the headache was the predominant symptom, and while no other overt signs of end-organ injury (retinopathy, encephalopathy, renal failure, myocardial ischemia, or aortic dissection) were documented, the absence of evidence is not evidence of absence. Head CT was performed without contrast, which is suboptimal for evaluating posterior circulation pathology, small vessel ischemia, or subtle parenchymal changes such as posterior reversible encephalopathy syndrome (PRES). PRES is a critical diagnosis to consider in patients with acute severe hypertension and headache, particularly in the setting of psychiatric illness, chronic stress, and possible electrolyte or metabolic derangements. Although the patient’s neurologic examination was non-focal and the CT was normal, PRES often presents with headache, altered mental status, visual disturbances, and seizures—symptoms that may be masked or misattributed to underlying psychosis. The absence of visual symptoms in this case reduces, but does not eliminate, the possibility. PRES is frequently associated with conditions that impair endothelial autoregulation, including chronic hypertension, preeclampsia, immunosuppressive therapy, and renal disease. While the patient has no known renal disease, her chronic poor nutrition, possible volume overload from sodium retention, and long-standing uncontrolled hypertension create a perfect storm for endothelial dysfunction. The fact that her blood pressure responded dramatically to labetalol and captopril supports a diagnosis of hypertensive crisis, and PRES remains a plausible, though unconfirmed, contributor to her headache.

Another critical consideration is pheochromocytoma, a catecholamine-secreting tumor of the adrenal medulla or extra-adrenal paraganglia. Pheochromocytoma classically presents with paroxysmal hypertension, headache, palpitations, and diaphoresis, but in many cases, particularly in atypical or chronic presentations, symptoms can be insidious and mistaken for anxiety, psychosis, or essential hypertension. This patient’s history of episodic hypertension (from 180/110 to 240/130), headache, and a long-standing psychiatric illness raises concern. The absence of classic triad symptoms—palpitations, sweating, pallor—is not reassuring, as up to 30% of patients with pheochromocytoma present without the full triad. The fact that she reported a history of being injected with an unknown substance in the past is highly relevant. While the urine toxicology screen was negative, it does not rule out endogenous catecholamine excess. Pheochromocytoma can be associated with hereditary syndromes such as multiple endocrine neoplasia type 2 (MEN2), von Hippel-Lindau disease, or neurofibromatosis type 1, but the patient’s unknown family history and lack of other stigmata (e.g., cutaneous neurofibromas, retinal hemangioblastomas, medullary thyroid cancer) make these less likely. However, sporadic pheochromocytomas are common and can present in young adults without any predisposing features. The dramatic response to labetalol—a non-selective beta-blocker with alpha-blocking properties—is consistent with catecholamine excess, as beta-blockade alone can be dangerous in pheochromocytoma without prior alpha-blockade, but labetalol’s combined action may have been protective. The fact that her blood pressure normalized with antihypertensives and that she had no recurrence of symptoms after discharge does not exclude the diagnosis; pheochromocytomas can have intermittent secretion, and plasma or urinary metanephrines were not obtained. This remains a high-priority exclusion, particularly given the severity of her hypertension and the lack of another clear trigger.

Renovascular hypertension must also be considered. The patient’s obesity, chronic hypertension, and age place her at risk for atherosclerotic renal artery stenosis. Although she has no history of renal insufficiency, her chronic venous stasis dermatitis and pitting edema suggest possible volume overload, which could be secondary to renal hypoperfusion and activation of the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS). The dramatic response to ACE inhibition with captopril and subsequent lisinopril is consistent with RAAS-mediated hypertension. However, the absence of a bruit over the renal arteries, normal serum creatinine, and lack of hypokalemia or metabolic alkalosis make this less likely as the primary cause. Still, in the setting of uncontrolled hypertension in a young woman with poor access to care, atherosclerotic disease may be accelerated. Inflammatory causes of renal artery stenosis, such as fibromuscular dysplasia, are more common in younger women and can present with severe, refractory hypertension. Fibromuscular dysplasia typically affects the mid to distal renal arteries and may be associated with headaches due to cerebral hypoperfusion or secondary hypertension. While the patient is 40, fibromuscular dysplasia can present in this age group, particularly in women. The absence of other signs such as carotid or mesenteric artery involvement (e.g., abdominal bruits, claudication) makes this less likely, but not impossible. A renal artery Doppler or CT angiography would be needed to exclude this, and the fact that it was not pursued is a missed opportunity.

Another important category is systemic autoimmune or vasculitic disorders. Although the patient has no fever, weight loss, arthralgias, or rash, the combination of severe hypertension, headache, and psychosis can be the presenting manifestation of central nervous system vasculitis or systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE). Primary angiitis of the central nervous system (PACNS) is a rare, underdiagnosed condition that typically presents with headache, cognitive dysfunction, focal deficits, and seizures, but can manifest with psychiatric symptoms such as psychosis or delusions, particularly when the frontal or temporal lobes are involved. The normal head CT does not rule out PACNS, as the condition often involves small vessels and may only be visible on MRI with contrast or biopsy. The patient’s history of chronic psychosis could represent a late manifestation of an underlying autoimmune process that has been slowly progressing for years. SLE can also present with neuropsychiatric lupus (NPSLE), which includes psychosis, seizures, and cerebrovascular events. While the patient has no history of malar rash, photosensitivity, or arthralgias, and her ANA and other autoantibodies were not tested, the absence of these does not exclude SLE. Many patients, especially those with limited access to care, present with neuropsychiatric manifestations as the initial or only sign. The chronic nature of her psychosis, combined with the acute hypertensive episode, could reflect a flare of underlying autoimmune disease affecting the kidneys (lupus nephritis) and the brain. The normal urinalysis is reassuring but not definitive—microscopic hematuria or proteinuria may be intermittent, and dipstick urinalysis can miss early disease.

Infectious causes of hypertension and psychosis are less common but must be considered, especially in a patient with a history of injection drug use (even if denied) and poor hygiene. Neurosyphilis remains a critical consideration in any patient with unexplained psychiatric symptoms, headache, and hypertension, particularly in those with limited access to care. Tertiary syphilis can present with general paresis (dementia paralytica), tabes dorsalis, or meningovascular syphilis, which can cause stroke-like syndromes, headaches, and psychosis. The vascular involvement in meningovascular syphilis can lead to stenosis of cerebral arteries, resulting in ischemic events and secondary hypertension. The patient’s Caribbean origin, long-term homelessness, and lack of prior screening make her at higher risk. Although she has no history of prior syphilis, a negative RPR or FTA-ABS was not performed. The headache could reflect meningitis or vasculitis, and the psychosis could be a manifestation of neurosyphilis. This diagnosis is easily missed and treatable with penicillin, making its exclusion essential.

Another infectious consideration is endocarditis, particularly in the context of her history of intravenous drug use. Although she denies current illicit drug use, she reported being injected with an unknown substance in the past. Right-sided endocarditis can cause septic emboli to the lungs, leading to pulmonary infiltrates or infarcts, but left-sided endocarditis can cause cerebral emboli, leading to stroke, meningitis, or mycotic aneurysms. The patient’s headache and psychosis could represent a septic embolus to the brain. The absence of fever, murmur progression, or positive blood cultures makes this less likely, but not impossible—culture-negative endocarditis, particularly due to fastidious organisms like *Bartonella* or *HACEK* organisms, can present with neurologic and psychiatric symptoms. The fact that her troponin was normal and her ECG was unremarkable reduces the likelihood of myocardial involvement, but does not exclude endocarditis. The presence of venous stasis dermatitis and tinea pedis suggests chronic skin breakdown and possible bacteremia, raising the possibility of occult infection seeding the valves.

Hypertensive encephalopathy, while often used synonymously with PRES, deserves specific mention as a distinct clinical entity. It is characterized by acute hypertension leading to breakdown of cerebral autoregulation, resulting in vasogenic edema, headache, confusion, visual disturbances, and seizures. The patient’s headache resolved with blood pressure control, which is classic. The absence of visual symptoms or seizures does not exclude it, as symptoms can be subtle or masked by psychosis. The fact that her mental status remained “cooperative” and “euthymic” despite disorganized thought process may reflect the masking effect of her chronic psychosis rather than true absence of encephalopathy.

A less common but important consideration is cerebral venous sinus thrombosis (CVST). Although classically associated with headache, seizures, papilledema, and focal deficits, CVST can present with isolated headache and psychiatric symptoms, especially if the frontal or parietal sinuses are involved. Risk factors include dehydration, oral contraceptive use, pregnancy, and hypercoagulable states. The patient is not pregnant, has no history of contraception, but her chronic malnutrition, possible dehydration, and venous stasis from immobility could predispose her. The normal head CT without contrast is insufficient to rule out CVST, which requires MRI with venography or CT venography. The absence of papilledema or focal signs makes this less likely, but it remains a diagnostic possibility, particularly given the severity and acute onset of her headache.

Lastly, we must consider the possibility that her psychosis and hypertension are both manifestations of an underlying metabolic or toxic encephalopathy. Chronic thiamine deficiency, common in homeless populations with poor nutrition, can cause Wernicke’s encephalopathy, which presents with confusion, ataxia, and ophthalmoplegia—but can also manifest with psychosis and autonomic instability. While she received thiamine empirically, the resolution of symptoms may have been due to antihypertensives rather than thiamine repletion. However, the fact that she had no ataxia or ophthalmoplegia makes Wernicke’s less likely. Other metabolic derangements—such as uremia, hepatic encephalopathy, or electrolyte abnormalities—were excluded by normal labs. Lead poisoning, though rare in adults in the U.S., can cause hypertension, abdominal pain, encephalopathy, and psychosis, particularly in those with occupational exposure or ingestion of contaminated substances. The patient’s history of injection with an unknown substance raises the possibility of exposure to heavy metals, though no specific evidence supports this.
