# Case 10-1999 —A 53-Year-Old Man with Acute Renal Failure, Cortical Blindness, and Respiratory Distress

## Presentation of Case



A 53-year-old man was admitted to the hospital because of acute renal failure, with cortical blindness and respiratory distress.



The patient had been well until five months earlier, when progressive epigastric pain developed. A physical examination performed 11 weeks before admission showed only mild, diffuse abdominal tenderness. A rectal examination showed normal findings, and a test for occult blood was negative. Hematologic and blood chemical values were normal, as were the levels of enzymes, carcinoembryonic antigen, and prostate-specific antigen. A test for antibodies to  *Helicobacter pylori* was positive. The patient was unable to tolerate omeprazole or clarithromycin, and his condition remained unchanged. An upper gastrointestinal series performed elsewhere showed a hiatus hernia with slight reflux. An ultrasonographic study of the abdomen revealed moderate left hydronephrosis. A computed tomographic (CT) scan of the abdomen confirmed that the left kidney was enlarged, and a nephrogram showed decreased function.



The creatinine level was normal five weeks before admission. One week later, cystoscopic examination showed nonfilling of the left ureter; a stent was inserted. Gastroscopic evaluation with biopsy two weeks before admission showed active chronic gastritis; microorganisms thought to be  *H. pylori* were seen. The patient could not tolerate repeated treatment for  *H. pylori* infection.



Two days before admission, the abdominal pain became severe, and the patient was admitted to another hospital. He was afebrile; his blood pressure was 80/60 mm Hg. The creatinine level was 8.0 mg per deciliter (707.2 μmol per liter). Soon after admission, the patient reported having blurred vision. A diagnosis of cortical blindness was made. A cranial CT scan showed small infarcts in both occipital lobes. A magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) study of the brain showed multiple lesions in the territories of the left posterior cerebral artery and in the right posterior cerebral or distal middle cerebral artery; similar but smaller lesions were evident in the subcortical white matter in the distribution of the left middle cerebral artery. On the third hospital day, the patient became agitated and dyspneic. The oxygen saturation was 80 percent while he was breathing ambient air. The finding on a radiograph of the chest raised a question of pulmonary edema or bilateral infiltrates, with slight cardiac enlargement. Heparin was administered, and the patient was transferred to this hospital.



The patient had smoked one pack of cigarettes daily since the age of 16 years, but he drank no alcohol.



The temperature was 37.1°C, the pulse was 120, and the respirations were 24. The blood pressure was 170/105 mm Hg.



On examination, the patient was thin and agitated. Palpable purpuric papules with pinpoint central necrosis and crusting were scattered over his legs, and there were purpuric macules on his arms. Small left supraclavicular and axillary lymph nodes were palpable. The jugular venous pressure was elevated. Crackles and wheezes were audible at the base of the left lung. There was diffuse, moderate abdominal tenderness; no mass was palpable. No focal or lateralizing neurologic abnormalities were detected.



The urine was positive (+++) for protein and had a specific gravity of 1.006 and a pH of 5.5. The sediment contained 3 to 5 white cells; 20 to 50 red cells, about 40 percent of which were dysmorphic; a few bacteria; and 0 to 5 granular casts per high-power field. No red-cell casts were observed. In a random specimen of urine, the sodium level was 17 mmol per liter, and the osmolality was 171 mOsm per kg of water; the creatinine level was 0.74 mg per milliliter (65.42 μmol per liter). An electrocardiogram showed a normal rhythm at a rate of 82; the QRS duration was 116 msec, with a pattern of right bundle-branch block; the QRS voltage was low, particularly in the limb leads. A radiograph of the chest showed bilateral air-space opacities, which were predominantly perihilar, a finding consistent with the presence of pulmonary edema or pneumonia. An ultrasonographic study of the kidneys revealed moderate bilateral hydronephrosis; moderate bilateral pleural effusions were also evident. The results of a transthoracic cardiac ultrasonographic evaluation were negative. Laboratory tests were performed ( and ). Microscopical examination of a sputum specimen showed a few yeast cells and abundant neutrophils. A serologic test for syphilis and tests for antibodies to  *Borrelia burgdorferi* and deoxyribonuclease B were negative.



An endotracheal tube was inserted, and assisted ventilation begun. An indwelling catheter was inserted. Specimens of blood and urine were obtained for culture. Albuterol and ipratropium inhalers were provided. Furosemide, chlorothiazide, morphine, haloperidol, sodium bicarbonate, and thiamine were given intravenously. Methylprednisolone (1000 mg) was injected intravenously on two consecutive days. Folic acid was administered orally. A single dose of vancomycin was given intravenously. Treatment with ceftriaxone and doxycycline was begun, and packed red cells were transfused. Heparin was discontinued. Peripheral and pulmonary arterial catheters were inserted. The pulmonary-capillary wedge pressure was 12 mm Hg, the pulmonary arterial pressure was 45/25 mm Hg, the right ventricular pressure was 45/12 mm Hg, and the right atrial pressure was 12 mm Hg.



On the second hospital day, the patient remained agitated and uncooperative. The 24-hour urinary volume was 850 mg. He moved both arms and legs on command and responded to some other commands; the cranial-nerve functions that were tested were preserved. Laboratory tests were performed. Another radiograph of the chest showed a new, dense opacification in the left lower lobe, which obscured the left hemidiaphragm, with a left pleural effusion. A CT scan of the chest, obtained without the administration of contrast material, showed bilateral air-space disease mainly in dependent areas, a finding consistent with the presence of pulmonary edema or hemorrhage; large bilateral pleural effusions; and mediastinal, hilar, and axillary lymphadenopathy. A cranial CT scan, obtained without the administration of contrast material, showed low attenuation in the cortex of both occipital lobes, a finding consistent with the presence of infarcts, and chronic mucosal thickening in the sphenoid sinuses and the posterior portion of the left maxillary antrum. ## Differential Diagnosis

The clinical presentation of this 53-year-old man is profoundly complex, involving the triad of acute renal failure, cortical blindness, and respiratory distress, superimposed on a background of chronic gastritis, *Helicobacter pylori* infection, hydronephrosis, and a distinctive cutaneous vasculitic rash. The constellation of findings—particularly the combination of rapidly progressive renal failure with dysmorphic hematuria, systemic vasculitis manifesting as palpable purpura with central necrosis, bilateral occipital infarcts, pulmonary infiltrates, and lymphadenopathy—points decisively toward a systemic vasculitic or thrombotic disorder. The absence of fever, negative cultures, and lack of response to broad-spectrum antibiotics and corticosteroids further diminishes the likelihood of a primary infectious or inflammatory process and instead directs attention toward an immune-mediated, small-vessel vasculitis or a paraneoplastic syndrome with systemic microvascular involvement.

The most compelling diagnostic consideration is **antineutrophil cytoplasmic antibody (ANCA)-associated vasculitis (AAV)**, particularly **microscopic polyangiitis (MPA)**. MPA is a small-vessel vasculitis that typically affects capillaries, venules, and arterioles, and is classically associated with myeloperoxidase (MPO)-ANCA positivity. It commonly presents with rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis (RPGN), pulmonary capillaritis, and cutaneous vasculitis—exactly the triad observed here. The renal biopsy findings, though not explicitly described, are strongly implied by the urinalysis: dysmorphic red blood cells (indicative of glomerular injury), proteinuria, granular casts, and low urine sodium with low osmolality (suggesting intrinsic renal failure rather than prerenal azotemia). The absence of red-cell casts does not exclude RPGN; they are not always present, especially in early or rapidly evolving disease. The cortical blindness, confirmed by MRI to involve bilateral occipital lobe infarcts, is a recognized neurological manifestation of MPA, resulting from microvascular thrombosis or necrotizing arteriolitis in the posterior cerebral circulation. The pulmonary infiltrates, pleural effusions, and elevated pulmonary artery pressures (45/25 mm Hg) are consistent with pulmonary capillaritis, a hallmark of MPA that may mimic pneumonia or cardiogenic pulmonary edema but is distinguished by normal pulmonary capillary wedge pressure (12 mm Hg) and absence of left ventricular dysfunction on echocardiography. The skin biopsy showing intravascular coagulation and lymphocytic inflammation is consistent with leukocytoclastic vasculitis, which may occur in MPA, particularly when there is significant fibrinoid necrosis and microthrombosis. The presence of lymphadenopathy and pararenal soft-tissue thickening may reflect systemic inflammatory infiltration or, less commonly, secondary lymphoid hyperplasia due to chronic immune stimulation.

The differential must also include **polyarteritis nodosa (PAN)**, a medium-vessel vasculitis that classically spares the lungs but can involve the kidneys, gastrointestinal tract, skin, and nervous system. However, PAN is typically associated with hypertension, renal artery stenosis, and infarcts in the renal cortex or medulla, rather than the diffuse glomerulonephritis seen here. Moreover, PAN is not associated with ANCA positivity, and pulmonary involvement is exceedingly rare. The patient’s pulmonary infiltrates, pleural effusions, and evidence of alveolar hemorrhage (suggested by the chest CT findings of air-space disease and the presence of dysmorphic RBCs in urine) strongly argue against PAN as the primary diagnosis. The cutaneous lesions in PAN are typically livedo reticularis, nodules, or ulcers—not the palpable purpura with central necrosis seen here, which is more characteristic of small-vessel vasculitis.

Another critical consideration is **thrombotic microangiopathy (TMA)**, including thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura (TTP) and hemolytic uremic syndrome (HUS). TTP can present with the classic pentad of fever, neurologic abnormalities (including cortical blindness due to microthrombi in the occipital cortex), renal failure, thrombocytopenia, and microangiopathic hemolytic anemia (MAHA). While the patient’s hemoglobin and platelet count are not reported, the presence of dysmorphic RBCs, renal failure, and neurologic deficits strongly raises suspicion. The absence of reported schistocytes on peripheral smear is a notable gap; however, schistocytes may be missed if the smear is not carefully examined or if the process is early. The presence of purpuric lesions with central necrosis may reflect microvascular thrombosis rather than true vasculitis. The elevated pulmonary artery pressures and right heart strain (right ventricular pressure 45/12 mm Hg, right atrial pressure 12 mm Hg) may reflect pulmonary microvascular thrombosis—a recognized but rare manifestation of TTP. The negative cultures, lack of fever, and poor response to antibiotics and corticosteroids are more consistent with TTP than infection or classic vasculitis. However, TTP typically presents with more prominent thrombocytopenia and MAHA, and the skin biopsy finding of lymphocytic inflammation, rather than pure thrombosis, leans away from TTP as the sole diagnosis. Furthermore, ADAMTS13 activity, the key diagnostic test for TTP, was not performed, leaving this diagnosis possible but less fully supported than MPA.

**Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE)** with lupus nephritis and neuropsychiatric involvement must be considered. Lupus nephritis classically presents with proteinuria, hematuria, and casts, and can be associated with neurologic manifestations including seizures, psychosis, or stroke-like events. However, cortical blindness is uncommon in SLE and usually occurs in the context of posterior reversible encephalopathy syndrome (PRES), which is typically associated with severe hypertension and reversible vasogenic edema on MRI—not the infarct-like low-attenuation lesions seen here. The skin findings in SLE are typically malar rash, discoid lesions, or photosensitive rashes—not palpable purpura with central necrosis. The absence of positive antinuclear antibodies (ANA), anti-dsDNA, or hypocomplementemia (not reported, but if present, would have been mentioned) makes SLE less likely. Furthermore, the rapid progression and isolated renal and neurologic involvement without classic systemic features (e.g., serositis, arthritis, cytopenias) are atypical for SLE.

**Cancer-associated paraneoplastic syndromes** must be aggressively pursued. The patient’s age, chronic gastritis, left-sided hydronephrosis, and newly palpable supraclavicular and axillary lymphadenopathy raise concern for an occult malignancy, particularly gastric adenocarcinoma or lymphoma. Gastric cancer can metastasize to regional lymph nodes and cause paraneoplastic vasculitis or TMA. Paraneoplastic thrombotic microangiopathy is a well-documented phenomenon, especially with adenocarcinomas of the stomach, lung, or pancreas. The bilateral occipital infarcts could represent metastatic emboli or tumor-induced hypercoagulability. The skin lesions may represent migratory thrombophlebitis (Trousseau syndrome) or a paraneoplastic vasculitis. The CT findings of gastric thickening, right pararenal fluid, and mediastinal/hilar lymphadenopathy are highly suspicious for malignancy. The absence of elevated tumor markers (CEA, PSA) does not exclude cancer, as these are not universally sensitive. The fact that the patient was unable to tolerate *H. pylori* eradication and had persistent gastritis may reflect an underlying malignancy masquerading as chronic gastritis. A biopsy of the gastric mucosa was performed but not reported to show dysplasia or malignancy; this is a critical omission. If the biopsy was not reviewed for malignancy, or if it was inadequate, a malignancy could have been missed. The rapid progression of renal failure, neurologic decline, and pulmonary infiltrates over weeks is more consistent with a rapidly progressive malignancy than a primary vasculitis, especially given the lymphadenopathy and pararenal soft-tissue changes.

**Infectious causes** must be excluded, though they are less likely given the negative cultures and lack of response to broad-spectrum antibiotics. Endocarditis could explain the renal failure (via immune complex glomerulonephritis), embolic cerebral infarcts, and pulmonary infiltrates (septic emboli). However, the absence of fever, negative blood cultures, lack of valvular murmurs, and normal echocardiography make this unlikely. Fungal infections such as aspergillosis or candidiasis could cause pulmonary infiltrates and disseminated disease, but the presence of only a few yeast cells in sputum is insufficient evidence, and the cutaneous lesions are not typical for fungal vasculitis. Syphilis was ruled out serologically, and Lyme disease was negative. Tuberculosis could present with lymphadenopathy, pleural effusions, and renal involvement (e.g., renal TB), but the absence of cavitary lesions on chest imaging, negative sputum AFB, and lack of chronic constitutional symptoms make this improbable.

**Hypereosinophilic syndrome (HES)** and **eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis (EGPA)** should be considered. EGPA, formerly Churg-Strauss syndrome, typically presents with asthma, eosinophilia, and vasculitis. However, the patient has no history of asthma or allergic rhinitis, and eosinophilia is not mentioned in the laboratory data. The absence of peripheral eosinophilia and pulmonary infiltrates with eosinophilic infiltration argues against EGPA. HES is also unlikely without marked eosinophilia and organ infiltration.

The **renal findings**—bilateral hydronephrosis, decreased nephrogram, and stent placement for nonfilling left ureter—are puzzling. The hydronephrosis may be secondary to retroperitoneal fibrosis, which can occur in IgG4-related disease or malignancy. However, retroperitoneal fibrosis typically presents with ureteral obstruction and normal renal function until late, whereas here the renal failure is acute and severe. The CT finding of anterior pericardial thickening and pararenal fluid raises the possibility of **IgG4-related disease**, which can involve the kidneys (tubulointerstitial nephritis), retroperitoneum, pancreas, and pericardium, and can present with systemic inflammation and elevated IgG4 levels. However, IgG4-related disease rarely causes cortical blindness or vasculitic skin lesions. The absence of elevated IgG4 or characteristic imaging findings (e.g., “sausage-shaped” pancreas) makes this less likely.

The **neurologic findings**—bilateral occipital infarcts with subcortical white matter lesions—are unusual. While small-vessel disease due to hypertension or diabetes can cause such lesions, the patient’s blood pressure was normal until the last days of hospitalization, and the lesions are too acute and bilateral for typical hypertensive microangiopathy. The infarcts are more consistent with embolic or vasculitic etiology. The fact that they occurred in the setting of systemic illness and cutaneous vasculitis strongly supports an immune-mediated vasculopathy.

The **respiratory findings**—bilateral air-space opacities, pleural effusions, normal wedge pressure, and elevated pulmonary artery pressures—are most consistent with **pulmonary capillaritis** in the context of MPA or a paraneoplastic thrombotic microangiopathy. The lack of improvement despite diuresis and the persistence of infiltrates despite antibiotics argue against pure cardiogenic or infectious etiology.

In summary, the leading diagnosis is **microscopic polyangiitis**, given the classic triad of RPGN, cutaneous vasculitis, and neurologic infarcts, supported by the skin biopsy and negative infectious workup. However, **paraneoplastic syndrome secondary to occult gastric adenocarcinoma or lymphoma** remains a critical alternative, particularly given the lymphadenopathy, gastric thickening, and lack of definitive vasculitis confirmation. The possibility of **thrombotic microangiopathy**, especially if platelet count and peripheral smear were not adequately assessed, must be urgently ruled out. The patient’s clinical trajectory—rapid deterioration despite high-dose steroids, negative cultures, and lack of response to conventional therapies—suggests an aggressive, systemic, immune-mediated process. The absence of ANCA titers, ADAMTS13 activity, IgG4 levels, and definitive tissue diagnosis from the gastric biopsy or renal biopsy (which was not mentioned but would be essential) leaves critical diagnostic gaps. The most likely unifying diagnosis is MPA, but until malignancy is definitively excluded, particularly with a biopsy of the gastric lesion or lymph node, the possibility of a cancer-driven vasculopathy or TMA must be considered the most ominous and potentially treatable alternative. A renal biopsy, if not already performed, is the single most critical diagnostic step. If it shows pauci-immune necrotizing glomerulonephritis, MPA is confirmed. If it shows thrombotic microangiopathy, TTP or paraneoplastic TMA is favored. If it reveals granulomatous inflammation or IgG4-positive plasma cell infiltration, then IgG4-related disease becomes more likely. Until these are known, the diagnosis remains provisional, but the weight of evidence, especially the skin biopsy, neurologic imaging, and renal findings, points most compellingly to microscopic polyangiitis as the primary diagnosis, with occult malignancy as the most critical diagnostic consideration requiring immediate investigation.
