# Case 10-1999 —A 53-Year-Old Man with Acute Renal Failure, Cortical Blindness, and Respiratory Distress

## Presentation of Case



A 53-year-old man was admitted to the hospital because of acute renal failure, with cortical blindness and respiratory distress.



The patient had been well until five months earlier, when progressive epigastric pain developed. A physical examination performed 11 weeks before admission showed only mild, diffuse abdominal tenderness. A rectal examination showed normal findings, and a test for occult blood was negative. Hematologic and blood chemical values were normal, as were the levels of enzymes, carcinoembryonic antigen, and prostate-specific antigen. A test for antibodies to  *Helicobacter pylori* was positive. The patient was unable to tolerate omeprazole or clarithromycin, and his condition remained unchanged. An upper gastrointestinal series performed elsewhere showed a hiatus hernia with slight reflux. An ultrasonographic study of the abdomen revealed moderate left hydronephrosis. A computed tomographic (CT) scan of the abdomen confirmed that the left kidney was enlarged, and a nephrogram showed decreased function.



The creatinine level was normal five weeks before admission. One week later, cystoscopic examination showed nonfilling of the left ureter; a stent was inserted. Gastroscopic evaluation with biopsy two weeks before admission showed active chronic gastritis; microorganisms thought to be  *H. pylori* were seen. The patient could not tolerate repeated treatment for  *H. pylori* infection.



Two days before admission, the abdominal pain became severe, and the patient was admitted to another hospital. He was afebrile; his blood pressure was 80/60 mm Hg. The creatinine level was 8.0 mg per deciliter (707.2 μmol per liter). Soon after admission, the patient reported having blurred vision. A diagnosis of cortical blindness was made. A cranial CT scan showed small infarcts in both occipital lobes. A magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) study of the brain showed multiple lesions in the territories of the left posterior cerebral artery and in the right posterior cerebral or distal middle cerebral artery; similar but smaller lesions were evident in the subcortical white matter in the distribution of the left middle cerebral artery. On the third hospital day, the patient became agitated and dyspneic. The oxygen saturation was 80 percent while he was breathing ambient air. The finding on a radiograph of the chest raised a question of pulmonary edema or bilateral infiltrates, with slight cardiac enlargement. Heparin was administered, and the patient was transferred to this hospital.



The patient had smoked one pack of cigarettes daily since the age of 16 years, but he drank no alcohol.



The temperature was 37.1°C, the pulse was 120, and the respirations were 24. The blood pressure was 170/105 mm Hg.



On examination, the patient was thin and agitated. Palpable purpuric papules with pinpoint central necrosis and crusting were scattered over his legs, and there were purpuric macules on his arms. Small left supraclavicular and axillary lymph nodes were palpable. The jugular venous pressure was elevated. Crackles and wheezes were audible at the base of the left lung. There was diffuse, moderate abdominal tenderness; no mass was palpable. No focal or lateralizing neurologic abnormalities were detected.



The urine was positive (+++) for protein and had a specific gravity of 1.006 and a pH of 5.5. The sediment contained 3 to 5 white cells; 20 to 50 red cells, about 40 percent of which were dysmorphic; a few bacteria; and 0 to 5 granular casts per high-power field. No red-cell casts were observed. In a random specimen of urine, the sodium level was 17 mmol per liter, and the osmolality was 171 mOsm per kg of water; the creatinine level was 0.74 mg per milliliter (65.42 μmol per liter). An electrocardiogram showed a normal rhythm at a rate of 82; the QRS duration was 116 msec, with a pattern of right bundle-branch block; the QRS voltage was low, particularly in the limb leads. A radiograph of the chest showed bilateral air-space opacities, which were predominantly perihilar, a finding consistent with the presence of pulmonary edema or pneumonia. An ultrasonographic study of the kidneys revealed moderate bilateral hydronephrosis; moderate bilateral pleural effusions were also evident. The results of a transthoracic cardiac ultrasonographic evaluation were negative. Laboratory tests were performed ( and ). Microscopical examination of a sputum specimen showed a few yeast cells and abundant neutrophils. A serologic test for syphilis and tests for antibodies to  *Borrelia burgdorferi* and deoxyribonuclease B were negative.



An endotracheal tube was inserted, and assisted ventilation begun. An indwelling catheter was inserted. Specimens of blood and urine were obtained for culture. Albuterol and ipratropium inhalers were provided. Furosemide, chlorothiazide, morphine, haloperidol, sodium bicarbonate, and thiamine were given intravenously. Methylprednisolone (1000 mg) was injected intravenously on two consecutive days. Folic acid was administered orally. A single dose of vancomycin was given intravenously. Treatment with ceftriaxone and doxycycline was begun, and packed red cells were transfused. Heparin was discontinued. Peripheral and pulmonary arterial catheters were inserted. The pulmonary-capillary wedge pressure was 12 mm Hg, the pulmonary arterial pressure was 45/25 mm Hg, the right ventricular pressure was 45/12 mm Hg, and the right atrial pressure was 12 mm Hg.



On the second hospital day, the patient remained agitated and uncooperative. The 24-hour urinary volume was 850 mg. He moved both arms and legs on command and responded to some other commands; the cranial-nerve functions that were tested were preserved. Laboratory tests were performed. Another radiograph of the chest showed a new, dense opacification in the left lower lobe, which obscured the left hemidiaphragm, with a left pleural effusion. A CT scan of the chest, obtained without the administration of contrast material, showed bilateral air-space disease mainly in dependent areas, a finding consistent with the presence of pulmonary edema or hemorrhage; large bilateral pleural effusions; and mediastinal, hilar, and axillary lymphadenopathy. A cranial CT scan, obtained without the administration of contrast material, showed low attenuation in the cortex of both occipital lobes, a finding consistent with the presence of infarcts, and chronic mucosal thickening in the sphenoid sinuses and the posterior portion of the left maxillary antrum. A CT scan of the abdomen ( and ), obtained after the oral administration of contrast material, showed slight gastric thickening and distention. The left kidney was slightly hydronephrotic; the right kidney was slightly enlarged, with some fullness of the upper collecting system. There was a small amount of fluid or soft-tissue thickening in the anterior right pararenal space and the right paracolic gutter. The anterior pericardium was focally thickened.



On the third hospital day, the rash was more prominent. The 24-hour urinary output was 2110 ml. All cultures remained negative. Laboratory tests were performed ( and ). Treatment with methylprednisolone was continued empirically on a daily basis; the dose was reduced to 80 mg on the fifth hospital day. Examination of a skin-biopsy specimen obtained on the second hospital day showed diffuse intravascular coagulation with lymphocytic inflammation.



On the fourth hospital day, laboratory tests were performed ( and ). A radiograph of the chest showed improvement in the bilateral air-space opacities and a right pleural effusion. A right-sided thoracentesis was performed under ultrasonographic guidance.



During the next few days, the rectal temperature did not exceed 38.1°C. The blood pressure remained normal. Serial electrocardiograms showed little change. Repeated chest radiographs revealed minimal improvement in the air-space disease. Another cardiac ultrasonographic study showed no change. No shunt flow was seen across the interatrial septum after the injection of agitated saline. All culture specimens remained negative.
