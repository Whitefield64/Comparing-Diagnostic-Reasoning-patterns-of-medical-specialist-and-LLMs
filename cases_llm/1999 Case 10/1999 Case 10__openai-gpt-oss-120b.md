### 1. ANCA‑Associated Small‑Vessel Vasculitis  

#### a. Microscopic Polyangiitis (MPA)  

**Pathophysiology** – MPA is a pauci‑immune necrotizing vasculitis that predominantly involves capillaries, venules, arterioles, and small arteries. ## Synthesis of the Evidence

2. **Cerebral Infarcts in Posterior Circulation** – Bilateral occipital infarcts and additional cortical lesions point to a **vasculitic or thrombotic process affecting small cerebral vessels**, a known manifestation of MPA and also reported in catastrophic APS.

3. **Cutaneous Small‑Vessel Vasculitis** – The skin lesions, together with the biopsy showing **intravascular coagulation with lymphocytic inflammation**, strongly suggest a **leukocytoclastic vasculitis** that is often seen in MPA and other immune‑complex vasculitides.

4. **Systemic Inflammation & Lymphadenopathy** – The modest lymphadenopathy and pericardial thickening fit the systemic inflammatory milieu of systemic vasculitis.

5. **Absence of Classic Features for Other Entities** – Lack of granulomatous nodules (GPA), lack of eosinophilia (EGPA), absence of hemoptysis with anti‑GBM antibodies, missing thrombocytopenia/hemolysis (TTP/HUS), and presence of pulmonary disease (which rules out PAN) all narrow the field.

7. **Temporal Evolution** – A **subacute prodrome of epigastric pain** evolving over months, followed by a **rapid multiorgan crisis**, mirrors the typical course of MPA, where constitutional symptoms precede organ‑specific manifestations.

Given these observations, **Microscopic Polyangiitis (MPA)** emerges as the **leading diagnosis**.  The disease’s hallmark is a **pauci‑immune necrotizing vasculitis** affecting capillaries, venules, and arterioles, producing pulmonary capillaritis (alveolar hemorrhage), necrotizing glomerulonephritis, and cutaneous leukocytoclastic vasculitis.  Cerebral small‑vessel involvement accounts for the occipital infarcts, and systemic inflammation can cause pericardial and pleural effusions.  The presence of **moderate hydronephrosis** can be secondary to ureteral wall edema or perirenal inflammatory mass, a recognized but uncommon manifestation.

## Management Implications (Brief Overview)  

While the primary objective of this discussion is diagnostic, it is worth noting that **MPA requires urgent immunosuppression**: high‑dose glucocorticoids (e.g., methylprednisolone 1 g IV daily for 3 days) followed by oral prednisone, combined with **cyclophosphamide** or **rituximab** for induction.  Plasma exchange is considered when there is severe pulmonary hemorrhage or rapidly progressive renal failure.  Early identification and treatment dramatically improve renal and neurologic outcomes.  

If anti‑GBM disease were identified, **plasma exchange** would be essential to remove pathogenic antibodies, together with steroids and cyclophosphamide.  

In the case of catastrophic APS, **anticoagulation** (heparin) and high‑dose steroids, with possible plasma exchange and rituximab, would be indicated.  

Thus, **rapid, targeted serologic and histologic confirmation** is critical to guide therapy.
