# Case 17-2012 —A 54-Year-Old Man with Visual-Field Loss and a Mass in the Brain

## Presentation of Case



A 54-year-old man was admitted to this hospital because of visual-field loss and a mass in the brain.



The patient had been well until 3 weeks before admission, when loss of vision in the right eye, associated with diplopia, developed while he was jogging; it resolved spontaneously after several minutes. Four days before admission, the symptoms recurred transiently, and he bumped into a tree while running. On the morning of admission, dizziness and loss of vision in the right lower visual field in both eyes developed, which did not resolve and resulted in difficulty driving. He went to the emergency department at another hospital. On examination, nystagmus was present in both eyes on left and right gaze. The vital signs and the remainder of the examination were normal, as were the results of laboratory tests, including a complete blood count; blood levels of electrolytes, calcium, and glucose; and tests of coagulation and renal and hepatic function. Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) of the brain, after the administration of gadolinium, revealed two adjacent masses (2 cm by 2 cm and 1 cm by 1.5 cm) in the left occipital and posterior parietal regions. Mass effect on the left occipital horn was associated with abnormal T    -weighted and fluid-attenuated inversion recovery (FLAIR) signal hyperintensity extending through the splenium of the corpus callosum. The patient was admitted to the hospital, and acetylsalicylic acid, dexamethasone, and phenytoin were administered. Later that day, he was transferred to this hospital.



The patient reported difficulty seeing objects in the right lower visual field and dizziness. He reported no headache, nausea, vomiting, numbness, weakness, bowel or bladder dysfunction, or seizures. He had a history of gastroesophageal reflux disease and  *Helicobacter pylori* infection and had recently had hematuria. A computed tomographic (CT) scan of the abdomen obtained 3 months before admission showed prostatic enlargement and was otherwise normal. He had had inguinal herniorrhaphies in the past. He took esomeprazole and had no known allergies. He was divorced, had children, and was physically active, running daily and competing in multiple marathons. He drank alcohol in moderation, had never smoked, and had no recent exposure to ill persons, tuberculosis, or asbestos. An uncle had had an inoperable primary brain tumor; the patient's siblings and children were healthy.



On examination, there was bilateral right inferior quadrantanopia; the vital signs, oxygen saturation, and remainder of the general and neurologic examination were normal. The administration of dexamethasone was continued, and omeprazole was added. The blood glucose level was 199 mg per deciliter (11.0 mmol per liter) (reference range, 70 to 110 mg per deciliter [3.9 to 6.1 mmol per liter]), and the lactate dehydrogenase level 217 U per liter (reference range, 110 to 210). The complete blood count and blood levels of electrolytes, protein, albumin, globulin, calcium, phosphorus, magnesium, carcinoembryonic antigen, prostate-specific antigen, CA 19-9, and nonmaternal alpha-fetoprotein were normal, as were tests of coagulation and renal function and a urinalysis. A chest radiograph was normal. On the second day, an MRI scan of the brain, obtained after the administration of gadolinium, showed two heterogeneously enhancing, well-circumscribed lesions in the left occipital lobe (2.2 cm by 1.8 cm and 1.1 cm by 1.4 cm). Within the enhancing portions of the lesions, there was restricted diffusion. Extensive signal abnormality on T    -weighted and FLAIR images was seen in the surrounding white matter of the left occipital lobe, extending into the posterior left temporal lobe and the splenium of the corpus callosum. Mass effect was present on the surrounding sulci and occipital horn and trigone of the left lateral ventricle, with no evidence of midline shift or hydrocephalus.



The next day, an MRI scan of the abdomen and pelvis after the administration of gadolinium, a CT scan of the chest, and a bone scan showed no evidence of cancer. The patient was discharged on the fourth hospital day, taking omeprazole and dexamethasone.



Three days later, the patient was readmitted, and a diagnostic procedure was performed.

## Differential Diagnosis

The clinical presentation of this 54-year-old man—with acute, transient, then persistent visual-field loss, bilateral inferior quadrantanopia, dizziness, and two adjacent, heterogeneously enhancing, well-circumscribed intracranial masses with restricted diffusion and extensive perilesional white matter edema extending into the splenium of the corpus callosum—demands a meticulously structured differential diagnosis. The absence of systemic symptoms such as headache, nausea, vomiting, seizures, weakness, or bowel/bladder dysfunction, coupled with normal systemic laboratory values and negative imaging of the chest, abdomen, and pelvis, strongly suggests a primary or isolated central nervous system (CNS) process. However, the presence of restricted diffusion, heterogeneous enhancement, and splenial involvement raises critical concerns for neoplastic, infectious, inflammatory, or vascular etiologies. Each must be weighed against the imaging characteristics, clinical course, and epidemiologic context.

The most immediately concerning diagnostic category is primary CNS malignancy. Glioblastoma multiforme (GBM) is the most common malignant primary brain tumor in adults and remains a leading consideration. GBMs typically arise in the cerebral hemispheres, particularly in the frontal and temporal lobes, but occipital lobe involvement is well documented. The presence of two adjacent masses raises the possibility of multifocal GBM, which occurs in approximately 5–10% of cases and is associated with a more aggressive course. Heterogeneous enhancement is classic for GBM, reflecting areas of necrosis, viable tumor, and disrupted blood-brain barrier. Restricted diffusion within enhancing components may reflect high cellularity, necrosis with viscous debris, or microabscess formation—features consistent with the tumor’s invasive and necrotic biology. The extension of T2/FLAIR hyperintensity into the splenium of the corpus callosum is particularly suggestive of transcallosal spread, a known pattern of GBM dissemination along white matter tracts. The absence of systemic metastases on imaging does not rule out GBM, as it is rarely metastatic outside the CNS. The patient’s age, lack of smoking or significant environmental exposures, and absence of prior malignancy are not inconsistent with GBM, which arises sporadically in older adults. The transient visual symptoms preceding the persistent deficit may reflect transient ischemic events due to tumor-induced vascular compression or microhemorrhages, or alternatively, epileptiform activity from cortical irritation, though no seizures were reported. The elevated blood glucose (199 mg/dL) and mildly elevated lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) may reflect stress hyperglycemia or tumor lysis, though neither is diagnostic.

Another glioma subtype to consider is anaplastic astrocytoma (WHO grade III). These tumors often present with progressive neurological deficits and may exhibit heterogeneous enhancement, though less pronounced than in GBM. The restricted diffusion and splenial involvement are less typical for grade III tumors, which tend to have more infiltrative but less necrotic growth patterns. However, the transition from grade II to III can be rapid, and the presence of two distinct masses may suggest a more aggressive phenotype. Oligodendrogliomas, particularly those with 1p/19q co-deletion, typically occur in younger patients, are more frontal or temporal, and often have calcifications and a more homogeneous appearance on imaging—features not seen here. The absence of calcifications and the presence of restricted diffusion argue against a classic oligodendroglioma.

Primary CNS lymphoma (PCNSL) is a critical consideration, especially in immunocompetent older adults. PCNSL classically presents as a solitary, strongly enhancing, periventricular mass, but multifocal disease occurs in up to 30% of cases. The splenium of the corpus callosum is a known site of predilection for lymphoma, often showing symmetric involvement and restricted diffusion due to high cellular density. The lesions are typically homogeneous in enhancement, but heterogeneous enhancement can occur with necrosis or hemorrhage, especially in immunocompromised patients or those with prolonged disease. The patient’s age, lack of immunosuppression, and absence of systemic lymphadenopathy or B symptoms make PCNSL less likely, but not impossible. The bilateral inferior quadrantanopia is consistent with occipital lobe involvement, and the rapid progression over days aligns with the aggressive nature of lymphoma. The elevated LDH, while only mildly so, is a known biomarker for lymphoma burden. The lack of systemic disease on imaging is not surprising, as PCNSL is by definition confined to the CNS in immunocompetent individuals. However, the presence of two distinct masses rather than a single confluent lesion, and the extensive surrounding edema, are more typical of glioblastoma than lymphoma, which tends to be more circumscribed with less vasogenic edema. The transient visual symptoms are less commonly reported in lymphoma, which typically presents with more insidious, progressive deficits.

Metastatic disease remains a leading consideration, despite negative systemic imaging. The patient’s recent hematuria and prostatic enlargement raise the possibility of prostate cancer, which can metastasize to the brain, though typically in advanced stages and with multiple lesions. Prostate cancer brain metastases are rare and usually occur with widespread systemic disease, which is absent here. The absence of elevated PSA, normal abdominal imaging, and lack of bone metastases on bone scan make this unlikely. Other primary malignancies—lung, breast, melanoma, renal cell carcinoma—must be considered. Renal cell carcinoma can metastasize to the brain with strikingly hemorrhagic, cystic, and enhancing lesions, and can present with visual field defects if occipital. However, the absence of renal masses on abdominal MRI and normal renal function make this improbable. Melanoma is notorious for brain metastases and can show restricted diffusion due to melanin content, but again, no cutaneous lesions or history were reported. The negative chest CT and abdominal MRI make metastatic disease from common solid tumors less likely, though rare primaries (e.g., sarcomas, germ cell tumors) cannot be entirely excluded. The fact that two distinct, adjacent lesions appeared simultaneously over days is more suggestive of a primary CNS process than metastatic seeding, which usually presents with multiple, randomly distributed lesions.

Infectious etiologies must be carefully evaluated. Primary brain abscesses, particularly from hematogenous spread, can present as ring-enhancing lesions with surrounding edema and restricted diffusion—features that overlap significantly with glioblastoma. The patient’s history of *H. pylori* infection and recent hematuria raises the possibility of a distant infectious focus, though no fever, leukocytosis, or systemic signs of infection are reported. Abscesses are typically single, though multiple abscesses can occur in immunocompromised hosts or in the setting of endocarditis or intravenous drug use, neither of which is present. The bilateral nature of the visual deficit and the involvement of the splenium are atypical for a bacterial abscess, which usually remains focal. Toxoplasmosis is exceedingly rare in immunocompetent adults and typically presents with multiple ring-enhancing lesions in the basal ganglia and corticomedullary junction, not the occipital lobe. Neurocysticercosis is unlikely in a patient with no travel history to endemic areas or exposure to undercooked pork. Tuberculosis is also improbable without systemic symptoms, positive PPD or IGRA, or other CNS findings like basal meningeal enhancement. Fungal infections (e.g., aspergillosis, mucormycosis) are rare in immunocompetent individuals and typically occur in diabetics or those with neutropenia. The patient’s elevated glucose may raise concern, but it is stress-induced, not chronic diabetes, and there is no evidence of immunosuppression. The rapid progression over days and the absence of fever or inflammatory markers make acute infection less likely than neoplastic or inflammatory causes.

Inflammatory and autoimmune conditions must be considered. Primary angiitis of the CNS (PACNS) can present with multifocal brain lesions, edema, and restricted diffusion, mimicking neoplasm. It typically affects middle-aged men and can cause progressive neurological deficits, including visual field loss. The splenium of the corpus callosum is a common site of involvement in PACNS, and the lesions may enhance heterogeneously. However, PACNS usually presents with headache, cognitive changes, or seizures, which are absent here. The absence of systemic vasculitic markers (e.g., elevated ESR, CRP) and normal renal and hepatic function make PACNS less likely, though not impossible. The transient episodes preceding the permanent deficit may represent ischemic events from vasculitic occlusion. However, the presence of two distinct, well-circumscribed masses with restricted diffusion is not classic for PACNS, which tends to cause more diffuse, patchy, or linear enhancement along vessels.

Autoimmune encephalitis, particularly anti-NMDA receptor or LGI1 encephalitis, typically presents with psychiatric symptoms, seizures, or memory deficits—not isolated visual field loss. The absence of CSF pleocytosis or oligoclonal bands (though not reported) and the lack of limbic system involvement make this unlikely. Multiple sclerosis (MS) can involve the splenium and cause visual field defects, but lesions are typically ovoid, periventricular, and Dawson’s fingers are classic. MS lesions do not typically enhance heterogeneously or demonstrate restricted diffusion unless acute and edematous. The bilateral, symmetric, and well-circumscribed nature of the lesions here is inconsistent with MS. Neuromyelitis optica spectrum disorder (NMOSD) can involve the optic nerves and spinal cord, but rarely presents with occipital lobe masses. The absence of optic neuritis or longitudinally extensive transverse myelitis makes this improbable.

Vascular causes must be considered. Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis can cause multifocal edema and hemorrhage, but typically presents with headache, papilledema, and seizures, and imaging shows thrombosed sinuses, not discrete parenchymal masses. The patient’s lack of hypercoagulable risk factors, normal coagulation studies, and absence of venous congestion on imaging argue against this. Hypertensive hemorrhage or microbleeds are unlikely given the absence of hypertension and the enhancing nature of the lesions. The restricted diffusion and enhancement are not typical of ischemic stroke, which usually shows cytotoxic edema without enhancement.

A rare but important consideration is a primary CNS germ cell tumor, particularly germinoma. These tumors typically occur in younger patients and arise in the pineal or suprasellar regions. Occipital lobe involvement is exceedingly rare. The patient’s age and location make this highly unlikely.

The most compelling diagnosis, integrating the imaging, clinical course, and exclusion of systemic disease, is glioblastoma. The two adjacent masses in the left occipital and posterior parietal regions, with heterogeneous enhancement, restricted diffusion, and transcallosal extension to the splenium, are highly characteristic of GBM. The rapid progression from transient visual symptoms to persistent quadrantanopia suggests a malignant infiltrative process with evolving edema and mass effect. The elevated LDH and stress hyperglycemia are consistent with a metabolically active tumor. The absence of systemic metastases supports a primary CNS origin. The transient episodes may represent transient ischemic attacks from tumor-induced vascular compression or microhemorrhages, or possibly subclinical seizures not recognized by the patient. The splenial involvement is a red flag for aggressive tumor spread along white matter tracts, a hallmark of GBM. While PCNSL remains a strong differential, the bilateral, asymmetric, and heterogeneous nature of the lesions, along with the lack of homogeneous enhancement and the patient’s age, make GBM the most likely. The negative systemic imaging, while reassuring, does not rule out GBM, which rarely metastasizes extracranially. The transient nature of the initial symptoms, followed by permanent deficit, is consistent with a malignant glioma evolving over days to weeks, rather than an inflammatory or infectious process, which would typically show more systemic signs or a more gradual course.

In summary, the leading diagnosis is glioblastoma, with primary CNS lymphoma as the most important differential. The clinical and radiographic features strongly favor a high-grade astrocytic tumor, and the absence of systemic disease, infectious signs, or inflammatory markers supports this conclusion. The next diagnostic step—biopsy of the lesion—will be definitive, but the constellation of findings points inexorably toward a primary malignant glioma.
