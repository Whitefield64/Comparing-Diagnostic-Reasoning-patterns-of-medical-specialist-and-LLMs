### 1. Primary Central Nervous System Neoplasms  

#### 1.1 Glioblastoma (World Health Organization grade IV astrocytoma)  

**Epidemiology & Typical Presentation**  
Glioblastoma (GBM) is the most common primary malignant brain tumor in adults, with a peak incidence in the sixth decade.  The classic presentation is a relatively rapid evolution (weeks to months) of focal neurological deficits, headaches, seizures, or cognitive change.  Posterior‑fossa involvement is uncommon; supratentorial lesions predominate, frequently arising in the frontal or temporal lobes, but occipital involvement is well described, especially when the visual pathway is compromised.  

**Imaging Features**  
GBM characteristically appears as a large (often >3 cm), irregular, ring‑enhancing mass with central necrosis and peripheral contrast uptake.  The rim of enhancement is usually thick and heterogeneous, reflecting proliferating tumor cells, neovascularity, and breakdown of the blood‑brain barrier.  Diffusion‑weighted imaging (DWI) often shows **restricted diffusion** in the solid, hypercellular peripheral component, while the central necrotic core may demonstrate facilitated diffusion.  Perilesional T2/FLAIR hyperintensity reflects vasogenic edema and infiltrative tumor cells extending beyond the contrast‑enhancing border.  Mass effect and midline shift are common, but early lesions may lack obvious shift.  

**Arguments For GBM in This Patient**  

* **Age and sub‑acute onset** – The patient’s age (54 y) fits the typical GBM demographic, and the evolution from transient visual disturbances to persistent quadrantanopia over three weeks mirrors the rapid progression often seen with GBM.  
* **Contrast‑enhancing, well‑circumscribed lesions** – While GBM is classically described as infiltrative, a subset of tumors (especially those with a dominant necrotic core) can appear relatively well demarcated on MRI, particularly when surrounded by a pronounced edema halo.  
* **Restricted diffusion** – The presence of diffusion restriction within the enhancing portions is compatible with the hypercellular tumor periphery of GBM.  
* **Perilesional white‑matter signal extending into the splenium** – GBM is notorious for infiltrating distant white‑matter tracts; involvement of the corpus callosum, particularly the splenium, is a known pattern of spread.  

**Arguments Against GBM**  

* **Size and multiplicity** – GBM usually presents as a **single dominant lesion** with satellite foci; the presence of two distinct, adjacent masses of 2 cm and 1 cm each is somewhat atypical, though not impossible.  
* **Lack of marked mass effect or midline shift** – The MRI notes only modest sulcal effacement and no hydrocephalus, whereas GBM of this size often exerts a more pronounced mass effect.  
* **Absence of constitutional symptoms** – GBM can be associated with headache, nausea, or early seizures; the patient reports none of these. However, the absence does not exclude GBM.  

**Conclusion**  
GBM remains a plausible primary neoplasm, especially given the patient’s age and the imaging hallmarks of enhancement, diffusion restriction, and infiltrative white‑matter signal.  The relatively small size and well‑circumscribed nature make it slightly less typical, but the heterogeneity of GBM presentations warrants its inclusion near the top of the differential.

The **absence of a detectable systemic primary tumor**, together with **modest edema** and **lack of severe mass effect**, further weakens the metastatic and high‑grade glioma hypotheses.  Conversely, the **patient’s age** and the **presence of diffusion restriction** are shared by both high‑grade gliomas and primary CNS lymphoma, keeping those entities in the differential, especially because high‑grade neoplasms can occasionally present as relatively small, well‑demarcated masses early in their natural history.

---

## Most Probable Diagnosis(s)  

1. **Tumefactive Demyelinating Lesion (Tumefactive Multiple Sclerosis)**  
   *Rationale*: The imaging pattern (large, well‑circumscribed, heterogeneous enhancement with restricted diffusion and relatively limited edema), the extension of signal abnormality along the splenium, and the clinical presentation limited to a visual field defect are hallmarks of tumefactive demyelination.  The patient’s age, while slightly older than the classic MS cohort, does not exclude this diagnosis; tumefactive lesions are known to present in the fifth decade.  Moreover, the acute‑on‑sub‑acute visual loss without systemic symptoms aligns with a focal demyelinating process rather than a systemic malignancy.

2. **High‑Grade Glioma (Glioblastoma or Anaplastic Astrocytoma)**  
   *Rationale*: The patient’s age, rapid symptom progression, and diffusion restriction also fit a high‑grade glioma.  Although the lesions are relatively small and well‑circumscribed, early GBM can appear this way.  The involvement of the splenium raises concern for infiltrative tumor spread.  Definitive exclusion of a glioma requires tissue diagnosis.

3. **Primary CNS Lymphoma**  
   *Rationale*: The diffusion restriction and corpus callosum involvement are characteristic of PCNSL.  However, the heterogeneous enhancement and the presence of two adjacent lesions reduce its likelihood compared with the other top contenders.

These three entities together account for **>90 %** of the diagnostic probability in this clinical context.  The remaining possibilities (metastatic disease, infectious abscess, vascular infarct) are considerably less likely given the negative systemic work‑up, lack of infectious signs, and atypical imaging features.

## Suggested Diagnostic Work‑up to Refine the Differential  

1. **Lumbar Puncture with CSF Analysis**  
   * **Cytology** and flow cytometry for lymphoma cells.  
   * **Oligoclonal bands, IgG index** to support demyelination.  
   * **Infectious PCR panels** (HSV, VZV, CMV, TB, fungal) to exclude occult infection.  

2. **Advanced MRI Sequences**  
   * **Perfusion MRI** – Tumors (glioma, lymphoma) usually show elevated relative cerebral blood volume (rCBV); demyelinating lesions have low rCBV.  
   * **Magnetic Resonance Spectroscopy (MRS)** – Elevated choline and reduced N‑acetyl‑aspartate (NAA) are typical for neoplasms; a **lipid‑lactate peak** without marked choline elevation favors demyelination or abscess.  

3. **Whole‑Body FDG‑PET/CT**  
   * To detect occult primary malignancy not seen on conventional CT.  High FDG uptake in the brain lesions would be more typical of lymphoma or high‑grade glioma, whereas demyelinating lesions often show modest uptake.  

4. **Stereotactic Needle Biopsy**  
   * If non‑invasive studies remain inconclusive, a targeted biopsy of the larger occipital lesion would provide definitive histopathology, distinguishing neoplastic from demyelinating pathology.  

5. **Serologic Autoimmune Panels**  
   * Anti‑MOG and anti‑AQP4 antibodies to rule out NMOSD or MOG‑associated demyelination, which can occasionally mimic tumefactive lesions.
