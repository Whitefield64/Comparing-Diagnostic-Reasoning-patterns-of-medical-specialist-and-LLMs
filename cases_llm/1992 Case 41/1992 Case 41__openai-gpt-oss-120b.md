### 1. Congenital Left‑Ventricular Outflow Tract Obstruction  

#### 1.1 Valvar Aortic Stenosis (AS) – Bicuspid Aortic Valve  

**Pathophysiology** – A congenital bicuspid aortic valve (BAV) is the commonest cause of isolated valvar aortic stenosis in children.  The valve leaflets are malformed, leading to turbulent systolic flow across the aortic orifice.  The resulting pressure gradient produces a harsh, crescendo‑decrescendo systolic ejection murmur that is loudest at the left upper sternal border (LUSB) and often radiates to the carotid arteries and, in younger children, to the apex.  The murmur lengthens as the gradient increases, which fits the description of a “long” systolic ejection murmur that has become louder over time.

**Evidence supporting**  

* **Murmur characteristics** – The harsh quality, systolic ejection timing, maximal intensity at the left midsternal border, and radiation toward the apex are classic for valvar AS.  
* **Age of presentation** – Moderate to severe valvar AS often becomes audible between 2 – 5 years as the child’s cardiac output rises and the valve orifice fails to enlarge proportionally.  
* **Hyperactive apical impulse** – A brisk left ventricular (LV) contraction caused by the pressure overload can produce a displaced, hyperdynamic impulse, as observed.  
* **Normal peripheral pulses** – In isolated valvar AS peripheral pulses remain equal; there is no differential between arm and leg pressures, consistent with the examination.  
* **Absence of cyanosis** – Pure AS is a left‑sided obstruction; systemic arterial oxygenation remains normal unless a concomitant shunt exists.  

**Evidence against**  

* **ECG findings** – The ECG shows a right‑ventricular conduction delay without LV hypertrophy.  Moderate‑to‑severe AS typically produces LV hypertrophy on the ECG (prominent R waves in V5‑V6, left‑axis deviation).  However, early or mild AS may not yet have generated overt LV voltage criteria.  
* **Chest radiograph** – The pulmonary vasculature is “slightly increased” but there is no evidence of LV enlargement or pulmonary venous congestion; early AS can still present with a normal chest X‑ray.  

**Conclusion** – Valvar AS due to a bicuspid aortic valve remains a strong candidate, especially given the murmur’s quality and radiation pattern.  The lack of LV hypertrophy on ECG does not exclude early or moderate disease.
