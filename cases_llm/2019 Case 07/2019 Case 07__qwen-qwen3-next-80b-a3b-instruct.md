# Case 7-2019: A 73-Year-Old Woman with Swelling of the Right Groin and Fever

## Presentation of Case



   *Dr. Alexandria K. Maurer* (Medicine): A 73-year-old woman with a history of chronic lymphocytic leukemia (CLL) was admitted to this hospital because of right inguinal lymphadenopathy, fever, and drenching night sweats.



The patient had been in her usual state of health and had been undergoing active medical surveillance for CLL until 2 weeks before this presentation, when swelling of the right groin developed. During the next 2 weeks, the swollen area increased in size. Drenching night sweats and daily fevers developed; temperatures taken at home were as high as 38.3°C. The patient called her hematologist’s office and was asked to present to the emergency department of this hospital for evaluation.



The patient had received a diagnosis of CLL 27 years earlier. At that time, lymphocytosis developed and an evaluation revealed pelvic lymphadenopathy. The patient underwent resection of the left ovary and a nearby retroperitoneal mass. On pathological evaluation, the ovarian tissue was normal, but findings in the nodal tissue were consistent with CLL. During the next 12 years, the patient had slowly progressive lymphocytosis and stable splenomegaly, and she underwent active surveillance with periodic complete blood counts and physical examinations.



Fifteen years before this presentation, fatigue and anemia developed and a 3-month course of chlorambucil was administered, with improvement in lymphocytosis and anemia. During the next 3 years, the patient received three additional courses of chlorambucil for symptomatic anemia, each of which was followed by a period of remission. Nine years before this presentation, anemia, thrombocytopenia, and splenomegaly developed and fludarabine and rituximab were administered, with improvement in blood counts and splenomegaly. Three years before this presentation, during post-treatment surveillance, fatigue, worsening anemia, and splenomegaly developed and idelalisib and rituximab were administered, with improvement in symptoms. After 1 year, all treatment was stopped because of a diffuse rash that was attributed to idelalisib. Two months before this presentation, the patient had a white-cell count of 19,000 per cubic millimeter (reference range, 4500 to 11,000).



In addition, several years before this presentation, hypogammaglobulinemia developed in association with rituximab treatment and was complicated by recurrent sinopulmonary infections, and intravenous immune globulin (IVIG) treatment was initiated. In the past, the patient had undergone resection of squamous-cell carcinoma from the right leg and of multiple basal-cell carcinomas. Other history included recurrent herpes simplex virus (HSV) type 1 infection of the genitals (for which she received acyclovir as needed), diverticulosis, a burst fracture of the first lumbar vertebra after a fall, and shingles due to herpes zoster infection of the right L2 dermatome. Four years before this presentation, she was treated empirically for  *Borrelia burgdorferi* infection.



The patient had no known drug allergies. Her medications included subcutaneous IVIG every other week, a multivitamin, calcium carbonate, vitamin C, lysine, and acyclovir as needed. She had smoked tobacco for 10 years but had quit 40 years before this presentation. She drank alcohol occasionally and did not use illicit drugs. Her husband had died 1 year before this presentation, and she was not sexually active. She was a retired librarian, lived in Massachusetts, and had not traveled outside the United States. She had no pets or exposures to cats. She enjoyed gardening and took daily walks outside. Her family history included heart disease in her mother and skin cancer in her father.

## Differential Diagnosis

The clinical presentation of this 73-year-old woman with chronic lymphocytic leukemia (CLL) and new-onset right inguinal lymphadenopathy, systemic symptoms including daily fevers and drenching night sweats, weight loss, and intense FDG-avid lymphadenopathy with central necrosis on PET-CT presents a complex diagnostic challenge. While the core biopsy confirms a monomorphic infiltrate of small mature B lymphocytes with a CLL immunophenotype, the acute nature of her symptoms, the dramatic metabolic activity on PET-CT, the presence of central necrosis, and the development of systemic B symptoms over a 2-week period strongly suggest that this is not merely a progression of indolent CLL. The question before us is whether this represents transformation of CLL to a more aggressive lymphoma, an infectious or inflammatory process superimposed on CLL, or a secondary malignancy. We must carefully weigh the evidence for and against each major diagnostic category, considering the patient’s immunocompromised state, prior treatments, and the imaging and histopathologic findings.

The most compelling and statistically most likely diagnosis is Richter transformation (RT), the transformation of CLL into a high-grade lymphoma, most commonly diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL). RT occurs in approximately 2–10% of CLL patients over their lifetime, with a median time to transformation of 5–7 years from diagnosis—though it can occur decades later, as in this case. The clinical hallmarks of RT include rapid lymphadenopathy, B symptoms (fever, night sweats, weight loss), elevated lactate dehydrogenase (LDH), and new-onset cytopenias, all of which are present here. The PET-CT findings are classic: intense FDG uptake in the inguinal nodes, with SUVmax three times that of the liver, and central photopenia consistent with necrosis, which is a well-documented feature of RT, reflecting rapid tumor proliferation outstripping vascular supply. Although the biopsy describes a “monomorphic infiltrate of small mature lymphocytes,” this may reflect sampling error or the presence of a mixed population in which the large-cell component was underrepresented in the core biopsy. In fact, in many cases of RT, the small-cell CLL component dominates the histologic picture, especially if the biopsy is taken from an area of residual CLL rather than the transformed focus. The necrosis and intense metabolic activity strongly argue that a large-cell component is present, even if not yet morphologically apparent. The fact that the patient had multiple prior lines of therapy—including chlorambucil, fludarabine/rituximab, and idelalisib/rituximab—further increases the risk of RT, as prior DNA-damaging agents and targeted therapies may select for clonal evolution and genomic instability. The absence of a rash or other signs of idelalisib toxicity at this time makes drug reaction unlikely, and the prior rash was clearly attributed to idelalisib and resolved upon discontinuation.

However, we must consider whether the biopsy result is truly representative. It is possible, though less likely, that the biopsy sampled only the CLL component and missed the transformed area. In such cases, a repeat biopsy from a different node or an excisional biopsy may be necessary. Nevertheless, given the clinical context, RT remains the leading diagnosis. The prognosis is poor, with median survival after transformation typically less than one year, even with aggressive therapy, making prompt recognition critical.

An alternative, though less likely, possibility is that this represents an aggressive variant of CLL itself—namely, CLL with high-risk cytogenetic features such as del(17p) or TP53 mutation, which can present with rapid progression and B symptoms even without full transformation. While CLL can occasionally present with systemic symptoms and bulky disease, the development of necrotic lymphadenopathy over two weeks is atypical for even high-risk CLL. Moreover, the intense FDG avidity—threefold higher than liver—is far beyond what is typically seen in untreated or progressive CLL, which usually demonstrates only mild to moderate uptake. In CLL, SUVmax rarely exceeds 10, and central necrosis is exceedingly rare without transformation. Therefore, while clonal evolution within CLL may contribute to her rapid decline, it does not fully account for the imaging and clinical picture. The presence of necrosis pushes the diagnosis beyond mere progression.

Another important consideration is infection, particularly in an immunocompromised host. This patient has profound hypogammaglobulinemia due to long-term rituximab exposure and is receiving monthly IVIG, yet she remains susceptible to opportunistic and atypical infections. She has a history of recurrent HSV-1, shingles, and sinopulmonary infections, indicating impaired humoral and possibly cellular immunity. The inguinal lymphadenopathy could represent an infectious process—such as tuberculosis, fungal infection (e.g., histoplasmosis or coccidioidomycosis), or less commonly, nocardiosis or atypical mycobacteria. Tuberculosis is a classic mimic of lymphoma, especially in immunocompromised individuals, and can present with necrotic lymphadenopathy, fever, night sweats, and weight loss. The CT findings of necrotic nodes with surrounding fat stranding are consistent with tuberculous lymphadenitis. However, the intense FDG uptake is more typical of malignancy than infection, although some granulomatous infections can be FDG-avid. The lack of pulmonary infiltrates or cavitation makes miliary TB less likely, but extrapulmonary TB with isolated inguinal involvement is possible. The patient has no known exposure to TB, has never traveled outside the U.S., and has no history of prior positive tuberculin skin test or interferon-gamma release assay. Moreover, the histopathology shows a monomorphic infiltrate of small lymphocytes without granulomas, necrotizing or otherwise, which argues strongly against TB. If granulomas were present, the diagnosis would be far more compelling, but their absence makes infection less likely as the primary driver.

Fungal infections such as histoplasmosis are rare in non-endemic areas and typically present with pulmonary findings, hepatosplenomegaly, or disseminated disease rather than isolated inguinal adenopathy. The patient has no history of residence in the Ohio or Mississippi River valleys, and her exposure to gardening, while potentially increasing risk of soil-borne fungi, is not sufficient to override the histologic findings. Similarly, fungal lymphadenitis usually presents with more diffuse lymphadenopathy and often with systemic signs of immunosuppression beyond just hypogammaglobulinemia. The absence of skin lesions, mucosal involvement, or organomegaly beyond the spleen further argues against disseminated fungal disease.

Another infectious consideration is cat-scratch disease (Bartonella henselae), but the patient has no cats, no history of scratches or bites, and no conjunctival or hepatosplenic involvement. Her lymphadenopathy is inguinal, not axillary or cervical, which is atypical for cat-scratch disease. Similarly, syphilis, especially tertiary or neurosyphilis, can present with lymphadenopathy and systemic symptoms, but the patient is not sexually active, has no mucocutaneous lesions, and no history of positive serologies. The prior empiric treatment for Lyme disease is intriguing, but she has no rash, no joint pain, no neurologic symptoms, and no evidence of persistent infection on serology or PCR. Lyme lymphadenopathy is typically regional and not intensely FDG-avid with necrosis.

A less common but important consideration is Kaposi sarcoma, particularly in the setting of immunosuppression. However, she has no skin lesions, no mucosal involvement, and the histology does not show spindle cells or vascular proliferation. Her prior history of skin cancers (squamous and basal cell) is unrelated to HHV-8-associated malignancies.

Another possibility is a secondary malignancy, such as metastatic carcinoma or another primary lymphoma. The patient has a history of multiple nonmelanoma skin cancers, but these are unlikely to metastasize to inguinal nodes with such intensity. The lung nodule with mild FDG uptake could represent a primary lung cancer, but its low SUV and small size make it unlikely to be the source of the massive inguinal adenopathy. Furthermore, the biopsy shows a lymphoid infiltrate, not epithelial or mesenchymal cells. A second primary lymphoma—such as follicular lymphoma or marginal zone lymphoma—could theoretically arise, but the immunophenotype is definitively CLL, with CD5+, CD23+, CD200+, and weak surface immunoglobulin expression. The absence of CD10, BCL6, or cyclin D1 expression rules out follicular lymphoma or mantle cell lymphoma. The morphology and immunophenotype are too specific for CLL to represent a completely unrelated lymphoma.

Autoimmune or inflammatory causes of lymphadenopathy must also be considered, though they are exceedingly rare in this context. Sarcoidosis can cause bilateral hilar and inguinal lymphadenopathy with necrosis, but it typically presents with bilateral symmetric lymphadenopathy, pulmonary involvement, elevated ACE levels, and non-caseating granulomas—all absent here. IgG4-related disease can cause lymphadenopathy and fibrosis, but it usually affects multiple organ systems (e.g., pancreas, salivary glands, retroperitoneum), has a characteristic lymphoplasmacytic infiltrate with storiform fibrosis and obliterative phlebitis, and elevated IgG4 levels—all of which are not present. The monomorphic lymphoid infiltrate is not consistent with the polyclonal plasma cell-rich infiltrate seen in IgG4-RD.

The patient’s history of prior surgery for a pelvic mass initially thought to be ovarian but later confirmed as CLL suggests that her disease may have been present in extranodal sites for decades. The presence of splenic parenchymal calcification and multiple calcified mediastinal nodes on CT is also noteworthy. Dystrophic calcification in lymph nodes is uncommon in CLL but can occur after prolonged inflammation or prior treatment. In the setting of CLL, it may reflect prior necrosis or fibrosis, but it does not explain the new, rapidly progressive, FDG-avid inguinal nodes.

The absence of cytopenias beyond the known chronic anemia is notable. Her white count is elevated at 19,000, which is higher than her baseline but not dramatically so, and platelets and hemoglobin are not reported as acutely worsened. This suggests that the transformation, if present, is not yet causing overwhelming bone marrow infiltration, which is consistent with early or localized RT. The fact that the spleen is unchanged in size and has lower FDG uptake than the liver argues against massive splenic involvement, further supporting a localized transformation rather than systemic disease progression.

In summary, the constellation of findings—rapidly enlarging, necrotic, intensely FDG-avid inguinal lymphadenopathy in a patient with long-standing CLL, new-onset systemic B symptoms, and a biopsy showing a monomorphic CLL-like infiltrate—is most consistent with Richter transformation, likely to DLBCL, with possible under-sampling of the large-cell component. The biopsy, while showing small lymphocytes, does not exclude the presence of a small but aggressive clone that is driving the clinical picture. The necrosis and intense metabolic activity are hallmark features of transformation and are rarely seen in indolent CLL progression. Alternative diagnoses such as infection, autoimmune disease, or a second malignancy are possible but lack supporting evidence and are inconsistent with the histopathology and imaging findings. The immunocompromised state increases the risk of opportunistic infection, but the monomorphic lymphoid infiltrate and lack of granulomas or organisms on special stains make infection an unlikely primary cause.

Given this, the most probable diagnosis is Richter transformation of CLL to DLBCL. The next step should be additional molecular testing—fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) for del(17p), TP53 mutation, and MYC rearrangement—and possibly a repeat biopsy or excisional biopsy of a different node to confirm the presence of a large-cell component. If transformation is confirmed, therapy with R-CHOP or a similar regimen should be initiated promptly, though outcomes remain guarded. The presence of prior fludarabine and idelalisib exposure may limit future treatment options, making the diagnosis even more critical to confirm rapidly. While other diagnoses must remain on the differential, the clinical, radiologic, and pathologic evidence overwhelmingly supports Richter transformation as the leading diagnosis.
