### 1. Endogenous Cushing Syndrome (Hyper­cortisolism)

#### 1.1 Overview  

Cushing syndrome (CS) denotes chronic exposure to supraphysiologic glucocorticoid concentrations, whether due to exogenous administration or an endogenous source. In pregnancy, the differential diagnosis of new‑onset hypertension and hyperglycemia must always include CS because the glucocorticoid excess can mimic, precipitate, or exacerbate gestational hypertension, gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM), and the metabolic syndrome. The hallmark biochemical abnormality—excess cortisol production—produces sodium and water retention, potentiates catecholamine‑mediated vasoconstriction, and antagonizes insulin action, thereby generating the triad of hypertension, hyperglycemia, and central obesity that is evident in this patient.

The markedly elevated UFC (1 865 µg/24 h) is diagnostic of cortisol excess. In a non‑pregnant adult, a UFC > 3–4 times the upper limit of normal already confirms hypercortisolism, and values > 10 times normal are typical of overt Cushing syndrome. Pregnancy does not abolish the utility of UFC; although total cortisol production rises ~ 50 % during gestation owing to increased corticotropin‑releasing hormone (CRH) from the placenta and decreased cortisol‑binding globulin (CBG) clearance, the magnitude of elevation seen here far exceeds the physiologic rise and therefore strongly supports an endogenous source.

#### 1.2 ACTH‑Dependent Causes  

##### 1.2.1 Pituitary Corticotroph Adenoma (Cushing Disease)  

Pituitary corticotroph adenomas account for roughly 70 % of endogenous CS in the general adult population and remain the most common cause in women of reproductive age. In Cushing disease (CD), excess ACTH drives bilateral adrenal hyperplasia and cortisol overproduction. Typical clinical features—central obesity, facial rounding (“moon face”), dorsocervical fat pad (“buffalo hump”), skin changes (thin, violaceous striae), proximal muscle weakness, and neuropsychiatric disturbances—are variably present. Importantly, CD can be relatively “silent” in early pregnancy, allowing conception before the classic manifestations become severe enough to impair fertility.  

**Why CD fits this case**

- **Facial rounding, periorbital edema, ruddy complexion**: Classic Cushingoid appearance.  
- **Absence of overt Cushing skin changes** (striae, bruising) does not exclude CD; many pregnant patients develop only subtle cutaneous signs because the skin is already distended by the gravid uterus and weight gain.

Classic sources include small‑cell lung carcinoma, bronchial carcinoids, thymic NETs, pancreatic NETs, and medullary thyroid carcinoma. EAS typically presents with a more fulminant course: severe hypertension, profound hypokalemia, metabolic alkalosis, and very high cortisol levels (often > 5 times normal). The rapidity of onset (within weeks) and the difficulty in controlling blood pressure and glucose are hallmarks.

### 12. Detailed Pathophysiologic Correlation With the Patient’s Findings  

#### 12.1 Hypertension

The resultant insulin resistance manifests as polyuria, polydipsia, and fasting glucose in the 140–180 mg/dL range, as well as a 1‑hour post‑load glucose of 346 mg/dL. The patient’s BMI of 28 (overweight) and a 6.8 kg gain in less than five months are consistent with cortisol‑driven fluid retention and fat accumulation.  

#### 12.4 Ruddy Complexion

#### 12.5 Potassium Supplementation  

Although the case does not explicitly state hypokalemia, the prescription of potassium supplements suggests low serum potassium, a frequent accompaniment of ACTH‑dependent CS due to cortisol’s activation of mineralocorticoid receptors (cortisol binds the mineralocorticoid receptor with comparable affinity to aldosterone; normally, 11β‑HSD2 converts cortisol to cortisone in the renal tubule, but overwhelming cortisol concentrations saturate this enzyme).

Importantly, the presence of CS complicates antihypertensive and antidiabetic therapy in pregnancy. Labetalol is generally safe, but high‑dose β‑blockade can mask hypoglycemia. ### 14. Therapeutic Implications of Each Etiology  

#### 14.1 Pituitary Cushing Disease  

- **First‑line definitive therapy**: Transsphenoidal surgical resection of the adenoma. In pregnancy, surgery is generally deferred until after the second trimester (≥ 20 weeks) to minimize teratogenic risk from anesthesia and to reduce the chance of miscarriage. However, in cases of severe, uncontrolled hypertension and hyperglycemia threatening maternal or fetal health, early surgery (even in the late first trimester) may be justified.  
- **Medical management**: Options (e.g., metyrapone, ketoconazole, pasireotide) are limited in pregnancy due to teratogenicity concerns. Metyrapone has been used with relative safety, but data are scarce.  
- **Radiation therapy**: Contraindicated in pregnancy.  

#### 14.2 Adrenal Adenoma (ACTH‑Independent)  

- **Surgical**: Laparoscopic adrenalectomy is the treatment of choice. In pregnancy, the timing is similar to pituitary surgery; the second trimester is optimal. If the tumor is suspected to be malignant, earlier intervention may be necessary.  
- **Medical**: Steroidogenesis inhibitors (e.g., ketoconazole, metyrapone) can be employed as bridge therapy, but the safety profile during pregnancy is uncertain.  

#### 14.3 Ectopic ACTH  

- **Oncologic management**: Requires identification and resection of the primary tumor (e.g., bronchial carcinoid). In pregnancy, the management is challenging; surgery may be performed if the tumor is resectable and maternal/fetal risk is high.

#### 14.4 Pheochromocytoma

- **Absence of features suggestive of ectopic ACTH** (no pulmonary symptoms, no rapid weight loss, no severe hypokalemia).  

Adrenal adenoma remains a viable second‑most likely diagnosis pending ACTH measurement and adrenal imaging. If ACTH is low, the diagnostic focus would shift to the adrenal gland. Nonetheless, the preponderance of evidence favors a pituitary source.
