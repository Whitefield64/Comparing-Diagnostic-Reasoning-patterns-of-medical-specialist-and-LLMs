# Case 36-2006 —A 35-Year-Old Pregnant Woman with New Hypertension

## Presentation of Case



   *Dr. Andrea L. Utz* (Endocrinology): A 35-year-old pregnant woman (gravida 2, para 1) was admitted to this hospital at 19 weeks and 6 days of gestation because of the recent onset of hypertension and diabetes.



Three weeks before admission, at a routine prenatal visit, her blood pressure was 150/100 mm Hg. On the same day she saw her primary care physician, who recorded a blood pressure of 172/102 mm Hg. The results of a physical examination were normal. Urinalysis showed glucose (4+). The results of other laboratory tests are shown in . The next day, the blood pressure was 180/100 mm Hg. The blood glucose level 1 hour after the oral administration of glucose (50 g) was 346 mg per deciliter (19.2 mmol per liter). Treatment with labetalol, glyburide, and potassium supplements was initiated. The results of fetal ultrasound examination were normal for the gestational age of the fetus. Three weeks later, despite increasing doses of labetalol, the patient's blood pressure remained in the range of 180/110 mm Hg and her fasting blood glucose level ranged between 140 and 180 mg per deciliter (7.8 and 10.0 mmol per liter); the patient was admitted to the hospital.



The patient had gained 6.8 kg in weight during the pregnancy. She had recently had polyuria and polydipsia and increased facial puffiness; her complexion was chronically ruddy. She did not have headaches, proximal muscle weakness, bruising, flushing, abdominal pain, edema, palpitations, diaphoresis, edema, or changes in vision. Her menses had been regular before pregnancy, and she had had no difficulty conceiving with either this pregnancy or a pregnancy 3 years earlier, during which she had had mild, diet-controlled gestational diabetes. She had been mildly overweight, with a body-mass index (the weight in kilograms divided by the square of the height in meters) of approximately 25 for several years. She did not smoke, drink alcohol, or use illicit drugs. She was married, with a 2-year-old daughter. Her mother and maternal grandmother had type 2 diabetes mellitus, and many family members had hypertension.



On physical examination, the patient's weight was 80 kg and height 170 cm, with a body-mass index of 28. The blood pressure was 180/100 mm Hg, and the pulse was 88 beats per minute; other vital signs were normal. Her face was slightly rounded, there was mild periorbital edema, and her facial complexion was ruddy. Extraocular movements and visual fields were intact. The thyroid was normal in size, with no palpable nodules. There was no increase in supraclavicular or dorsal adipose tissue, hirsutism, or evidence of bruising. The abdomen was gravid, nontender, and without striae. Proximal muscle strength and reflexes were normal. There was no peripheral edema.



The dose of labetalol was increased to 800 mg three times daily; extended-release nifedipine (30 mg daily) and insulin (66 U per day in divided doses) were added. Both the blood glucose levels and the blood pressure fell, and the patient was discharged on the third hospital day; the results of laboratory tests were pending.



One week later, the patient was seen in the neuroendocrine clinic. A repeated 24-hour urinary cortisol measurement showed that the level was 1865 μg. A diagnostic procedure was performed.

## Differential Diagnosis

The clinical presentation of this 35-year-old pregnant woman at 19 weeks’ gestation—with new-onset severe hypertension, marked hyperglycemia, weight gain, facial puffiness, ruddy complexion, and markedly elevated urinary free cortisol—demands a systematic and nuanced differential diagnosis that integrates the unique physiological adaptations of pregnancy with the potential for endocrine, metabolic, and vascular pathologies. The constellation of findings—particularly the combination of hypertension, glucose intolerance, central adiposity, and facial plethora—strongly suggests a disorder of cortisol excess, but the timing of onset in early pregnancy, the absence of classic stigmata of Cushing’s syndrome (such as purple striae, easy bruising, or proximal myopathy), and the presence of a prior history of gestational diabetes necessitate careful discrimination among several overlapping entities.

The most compelling diagnostic consideration is **Cushing’s syndrome**, specifically **pituitary-dependent Cushing’s disease** or **adrenal Cushing’s syndrome**. The 24-hour urinary free cortisol level of 1865 μg is profoundly elevated—far exceeding the upper limit of normal in pregnancy (which may rise to 2–3 times the non-pregnant upper limit due to increased cortisol-binding globulin and placental production of corticotropin-releasing hormone). In non-pregnant individuals, levels above 150–200 μg/24 h are diagnostic of Cushing’s; in pregnancy, values above 500–700 μg/24 h are considered highly suggestive, and values exceeding 1500 μg/24 h are virtually pathognomonic for autonomous cortisol secretion. The absence of classic cutaneous findings does not rule out Cushing’s in pregnancy, as many of the stigmata (e.g., striae, bruising, hirsutism) are masked by the normal physiologic changes of gestation, including skin stretching, increased vascularity, and fluid retention. The ruddy complexion and facial puffiness are consistent with the cutaneous manifestations of cortisol excess, and the rapid development of severe insulin resistance—evidenced by the need for high-dose insulin and persistent fasting hyperglycemia despite oral agents—parallels the profound gluconeogenic and anti-insulin effects of cortisol. The hypertension, refractory to multiple antihypertensives including labetalol and nifedipine, is also typical of mineralocorticoid excess secondary to cortisol’s off-target activation of the mineralocorticoid receptor (due to saturation of 11β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase type 2 in the kidney during cortisol excess). The patient’s weight gain, though within the expected range for pregnancy, is disproportionate and centralized, suggesting an endocrine rather than purely mechanical etiology.

However, the differential must also consider **gestational hypertension with superimposed gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM)**, which is common but cannot account for the severity of the findings. While GDM occurs in up to 10% of pregnancies and gestational hypertension in 5–8%, the combination of severe, refractory hypertension (BP >180/110 mm Hg), fasting glucose consistently >140 mg/dL, and urinary cortisol >1800 μg is incompatible with either entity alone. The patient’s prior pregnancy had mild, diet-controlled GDM; this episode is dramatically more severe and accompanied by systemic signs not attributable to pregnancy physiology. Moreover, gestational hypertension typically presents after 20 weeks and is not associated with hyperglycemia of this magnitude unless there is an underlying endocrinopathy. The lack of proteinuria, edema, or other signs of preeclampsia further argues against this diagnosis. Although preeclampsia remains a critical consideration in any pregnant woman with new-onset hypertension, it is characterized by proteinuria (usually >300 mg/24 h), often by 20 weeks, and frequently by thrombocytopenia, elevated liver enzymes, and evidence of end-organ damage—all absent here. The absence of edema and the presence of hypercortisolism make preeclampsia highly unlikely.

Another important consideration is **primary hyperaldosteronism (Conn’s syndrome)**. This condition can present with severe, refractory hypertension and hypokalemia (though potassium levels are not provided, supplementation was initiated, suggesting possible hypokalemia). However, hyperaldosteronism typically does not cause hyperglycemia of this degree, nor does it cause facial plethora, central obesity, or elevated urinary cortisol. Aldosterone excess may lead to glucose intolerance through potassium depletion-induced impairment of insulin secretion, but the magnitude of hyperglycemia here—requiring insulin doses >60 U/day—is far beyond what would be expected from this mechanism. Furthermore, urinary cortisol is not elevated in primary aldosteronism; in fact, aldosterone levels are typically measured directly, and suppression of renin is the hallmark. The elevated urinary cortisol is a critical diagnostic clue that redirects the focus away from primary mineralocorticoid excess and toward cortisol-mediated mineralocorticoid effects.

We must also consider **adrenocortical carcinoma** or **large adrenal adenoma** as a cause of autonomous cortisol secretion. These tumors often produce large amounts of cortisol and may present with rapid onset of symptoms, as seen here. In pregnancy, adrenal tumors may be discovered incidentally or due to hormonal overproduction, and they can grow more rapidly due to increased placental estrogen levels, which may stimulate steroidogenesis. The absence of abdominal mass, flank pain, or other signs of tumor bulk does not exclude this diagnosis, as adrenal masses may be retroperitoneal and not palpable until very large. The degree of cortisol excess here is more consistent with a tumor than with pituitary disease, though pituitary Cushing’s can also present with severe manifestations, especially in the setting of pregnancy-induced CRH stimulation. The next diagnostic step—presumably a dexamethasone suppression test or adrenal imaging—would help differentiate between pituitary and adrenal sources. However, in pregnancy, dexamethasone suppression testing is complicated by the fact that dexamethasone crosses the placenta and may suppress fetal HPA axis, so it is often avoided or modified. Corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH) stimulation tests are also contraindicated due to the risk of precipitating uterine contractions. Thus, imaging becomes paramount: adrenal CT or MRI (with appropriate shielding) would be the next step to evaluate for adrenal masses.

A less common but critical consideration is **ectopic ACTH syndrome**. This is typically caused by a neuroendocrine tumor (e.g., bronchial carcinoid, small cell lung cancer, pancreatic islet cell tumor) producing ACTH independently of pituitary regulation. While ectopic ACTH syndrome is rare in pregnancy, it can occur and is associated with very high levels of cortisol and ACTH, profound muscle wasting (which is absent here), and often hypokalemia and metabolic alkalosis. The patient’s lack of weight loss, absence of respiratory symptoms, and presence of central obesity make this less likely, but it cannot be excluded without measurement of plasma ACTH levels, which were not provided. If ACTH is elevated, the next step is to determine whether it is pituitary- or ectopic-derived—using high-dose dexamethasone suppression or petrosal sinus sampling, both of which are technically challenging in pregnancy. However, the absence of signs of malignancy (no weight loss, no cough, no diarrhea, no flushing) makes this a lower priority than adrenal or pituitary causes.

Another important, though rare, differential is **familial glucocorticoid resistance** or **glucocorticoid receptor mutations**. In this condition, patients have high cortisol levels due to resistance to its negative feedback, leading to compensatory pituitary ACTH hypersecretion. However, these patients typically do not have the clinical features of Cushing’s syndrome because their tissues are resistant to cortisol. This patient clearly has signs of cortisol excess, making this diagnosis unlikely.

We must also consider **pseudo-Cushing’s states**, which can occur in pregnancy due to physiologic hypercortisolism. The placenta produces CRH, which stimulates maternal pituitary ACTH secretion, and placental 11β-HSD2 is not fully functional in early pregnancy, allowing some maternal cortisol to act on fetal and maternal tissues. In some women, this leads to mild elevations in urinary cortisol, but not to the extreme levels seen here. Furthermore, pseudo-Cushing’s does not typically cause severe, refractory hypertension or insulin resistance requiring high-dose insulin. The degree of cortisol excess here is orders of magnitude beyond physiologic adaptation and is incompatible with a benign pregnancy-related phenomenon.

A very rare but important consideration is **ACTH-independent macronodular adrenal hyperplasia (AIMAH)**, which can present in middle-aged women with variable cortisol secretion and often has a familial component. It may present with intermittent or progressive hypercortisolism and is often associated with adrenal enlargement on imaging. The absence of family history of adrenal disease makes this less likely, but it should be considered if imaging reveals bilateral adrenal enlargement.

The patient’s strong family history of type 2 diabetes and hypertension raises the possibility of **polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)** as a contributor to her metabolic phenotype. PCOS is associated with insulin resistance, obesity, and increased risk of gestational diabetes and hypertension. However, PCOS typically presents with menstrual irregularities, hirsutism, and acne—all absent here. The patient had regular menses before pregnancy and no signs of androgen excess. While PCOS may have predisposed her to insulin resistance, it cannot explain the severe hypertension, facial plethora, or the extreme cortisol elevation.

Finally, we must consider **medication-induced or iatrogenic causes**. The patient was not on any exogenous corticosteroids prior to admission, and there is no history of topical or inhaled steroid use. The use of labetalol and glyburide is appropriate for her presentation and not causative of her symptoms. The weight gain and glucose intolerance are consistent with the progression of her underlying endocrinopathy, not with drug side effects.

In summary, the leading diagnosis is **Cushing’s syndrome due to autonomous cortisol secretion**, most likely from an **adrenal adenoma or carcinoma**, with **pituitary Cushing’s disease** as a close second. The extreme elevation in urinary free cortisol, the severity and refractoriness of hypertension and hyperglycemia, the characteristic facial findings, and the timing of onset in early pregnancy all point strongly toward this diagnosis. The absence of striae, bruising, or muscle weakness does not exclude it, as these features are often attenuated in pregnancy. The next diagnostic steps—adrenal imaging (MRI preferred in pregnancy to avoid radiation) and measurement of plasma ACTH—will distinguish between adrenal and pituitary etiologies. If ACTH is suppressed, an adrenal source is confirmed; if ACTH is elevated or normal, further evaluation for pituitary adenoma (e.g., MRI of the pituitary) is indicated. Ectopic ACTH syndrome remains a remote possibility and should be evaluated if ACTH is elevated and no pituitary or adrenal lesion is found.

This case illustrates the critical importance of recognizing endocrine disorders that mimic or are unmasked by pregnancy. The clinical presentation, while initially appearing to be a severe case of gestational diabetes and hypertension, is in fact a classic example of Cushing’s syndrome presenting during gestation—a condition that, if left undiagnosed, carries significant maternal risks (including preeclampsia, cardiac failure, infection, thromboembolism) and fetal risks (intrauterine growth restriction, preterm birth, stillbirth). Early recognition and intervention are paramount. The patient’s response to insulin and antihypertensives, while clinically reassuring, does not address the underlying pathophysiology. Definitive diagnosis and treatment—whether surgical resection of an adrenal tumor or transsphenoidal adenomectomy for pituitary disease—will be required to restore maternal and fetal health.
