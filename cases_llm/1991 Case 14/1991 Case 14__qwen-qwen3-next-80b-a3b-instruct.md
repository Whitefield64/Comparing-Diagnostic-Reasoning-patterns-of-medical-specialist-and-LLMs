# Case 14-1991 —A 17-Week-Old Boy with a Left Posterior Intrathoracic Mass.

## Presentation of Case



A 17-week-old boy was admitted to the hospital because of a thoracic mass.



The child was born after a full-term pregnancy by an elective cesarean section at another hospital. His birth weight was 4.1 kg. The Apgar scores were 7 at one minute and 9 at five minutes, but grunting was noted. X-ray films of the chest showed a triangular opacity in the retrocardiac area that abutted the left hemidiaphragm and was believed to involve the left lower lobe. Antibiotics were administered by vein, and within 12 hours the respiratory pattern became normal. Another x-ray film of the chest, obtained six days later, demonstrated no change in the triangular density.



At the age of seven days the boy was transferred to this hospital. Physical examination revealed that the temperature was 36.1°C. The boy appeared healthy; his lungs were clear, and no abnormality was found. The hematocrit was 35.1 percent; the white-cell count was 11,700, with 32 percent neutrophils, 53 percent lymphocytes, 13 percent monocytes, and 2 percent eosinophils. The mean corpuscular volume (MCV) was 99 μm per cell, the mean corpuscular hemoglobin (MCH) 34.0 pg per red cell, and the mean corpuscular hemoglobin concentration (MCHC) 34.5 percent. The platelet count was 852,000. The prothrombin time was 11.2 seconds, with a control of 10.5 seconds; the partial thromboplastin time was 38.8 seconds. The urea nitrogen was 0.7 mmol per liter (2 mg per 100 ml), the creatinine 62 μmol per liter (0.7 mg per 100 ml), the glucose 6.2 mmol per liter (112 mg per 100 ml), the bilirubin 89 μmol per liter (5.2 mg per 100 ml), the calcium 2.4 mmol per liter (9.6 mg per 100 ml), and the protein 51 g per liter (5.1 g per 100 ml). The sodium was 134 mmol, the potassium 4.4 mmol, and the chloride 111 mmol per liter. The serum aspartate aminotransferase (ASAT) was 35 U (normal, 10 to 50). A computed tomographic (CT) scan of the thorax and abdomen disclosed a mass in the posterior sulcus of the left hemithorax that extended laterally from the paraspinal region; its position on several cuts suggested a cleavage plane between the paraspinal tissues and the mass; no enhancement of the mass was observed after the intravenous injection of contrast material, and no calcifications were seen; the mass was nonhomogeneous, with small areas of lower density; the spleen, stomach, and abdominal structures were in normal locations, and the liver appeared normal. Nine or ten days later nasal congestion developed and was treated with nasal drops by the child's pediatrician. The boy was agitated, with increasing irritability when he was placed in bed.

## Differential Diagnosis

The clinical presentation of this 17-week-old male with a persistent, nonenhancing, posterior left intrathoracic mass—first detected neonatally and unchanged over 15 weeks—demands a systematic, age-appropriate, and anatomically grounded differential diagnosis. The mass is located in the left paravertebral gutter, posterior to the heart and abutting the left hemidiaphragm, with no evidence of spinal canal invasion, vascular encasement, or mediastinal shift. Its radiographic and imaging characteristics—nonhomogeneous, hypodense on CT without contrast enhancement, hyperintense on T1 and markedly hyperintense on T2-weighted MRI, with a thin dark cleavage plane separating it from paraspinal structures—provide critical clues. The absence of systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss, respiratory distress, hemoptysis), normal neurologic exam, and lack of progression over time strongly argue against aggressive, infiltrative, or infectious etiologies. The infant is otherwise healthy, thriving, with normal growth parameters (birth weight 4.1 kg, current weight 6.5 kg at 17 weeks), and no signs of metabolic derangement or hematologic malignancy beyond mild, transient anemia and thrombocytosis, both of which have normalized or stabilized.

The most salient feature is the mass’s persistence without growth or change over 15 weeks, its location in the posterior thoracic compartment, and its imaging signature: nonenhancing, T2 hyperintense, with internal low-signal foci and a well-defined interface with adjacent tissues. These features are highly suggestive of a congenital, non-neoplastic, developmental anomaly. The leading diagnostic consideration is a **congenital cystic adenomatoid malformation (CCAM), now termed congenital pulmonary airway malformation (CPAM)**, particularly Type I or II. CPAM arises from abnormal budding and branching of the fetal lung bud during the pseudoglandular stage (weeks 5–17 of gestation), resulting in cystic or solid lung tissue with abnormal airway structures. Type I CPAM, the most common subtype, presents as a large, single or few macrocysts (2–10 cm), often compressing adjacent lung and diaphragm, and typically appears as a well-circumscribed, nonenhancing mass on CT and MRI with variable internal signal due to fluid content. The T2 hyperintensity is classic for fluid-filled structures, and the low-signal areas may represent fibrous septa, hemorrhagic debris, or proteinaceous material. The cleavage plane seen on imaging is consistent with a mass arising from the lung parenchyma but encapsulated and distinct from adjacent mediastinal structures. The fact that the mass was detected neonatally, caused transient grunting (likely due to mechanical compression of the left lung or diaphragm), and resolved respiratory symptoms with antibiotics (which may have treated a coincidental or secondary infection) supports a benign, noninfectious origin. CPAM is frequently asymptomatic in infancy and often discovered incidentally, especially when the lesion is small or noncommunicating with the tracheobronchial tree. The absence of contrast enhancement is consistent with the lack of vascular supply to the cystic epithelial lining, and the lack of calcification excludes other entities like teratoma or neuroblastoma.

A close second consideration is a **neuroblastoma**, a common pediatric abdominal and thoracic malignancy arising from neural crest cells. While neuroblastoma most frequently originates in the adrenal medulla, it commonly presents as a posterior mediastinal or paravertebral mass in infants, especially those under 1 year. However, several features here argue strongly against neuroblastoma. First, neuroblastoma typically enhances vividly on contrast imaging due to its rich vascularity; the absence of enhancement in this case is highly atypical. Second, neuroblastoma often demonstrates calcifications (seen in up to 90% of cases on CT), which are absent here. Third, neuroblastoma frequently causes systemic symptoms such as fever, weight loss, bone pain, periorbital ecchymosis, or hypertension due to catecholamine secretion—all absent in this child. Fourth, while neuroblastoma can present with thrombocytosis and mild anemia, the degree of anemia here is mild and has improved, and there is no evidence of bone marrow infiltration (normal neurologic exam, no hepatosplenomegaly, no leukoerythroblastic changes on peripheral smear). The platelet count, while elevated, is not extreme (732,000 at 17 weeks), and thrombocytosis in neuroblastoma is usually associated with advanced disease and poor prognosis, which is not supported by the clinical course. The T2 hyperintensity and nonenhancing nature are also less typical for neuroblastoma, which is usually more solid and heterogeneous with variable enhancement. The lack of progression over 15 weeks is also inconsistent with the typically aggressive behavior of neuroblastoma in infants, even low-risk forms, which tend to grow or metastasize if untreated.

Another critical consideration is a **teratoma**, particularly a mediastinal or posterior mediastinal germ cell tumor. Teratomas are congenital tumors derived from all three germ layers and can occur in the anterior, middle, or posterior mediastinum. Posterior mediastinal teratomas are rare but well-documented, often arising from neural crest or somatic remnants. They are typically large, heterogeneous masses containing fat, calcification, fluid, and solid components. The absence of calcification and fat on CT is a major argument against teratoma, as these are present in over 80% of cases. Teratomas usually enhance variably due to their vascularized solid components and often show cystic areas with fluid levels. While T2 hyperintensity can be seen in fluid-filled cysts, the lack of fat signal on MRI (which would be bright on T1 and suppressed on fat-saturated sequences) and the absence of calcification make teratoma unlikely. Furthermore, teratomas are more commonly diagnosed in the first few months of life due to mass effect or respiratory compromise, but they rarely remain stable over 15 weeks without growth or complications. The clinical stability and lack of systemic signs further reduce the probability.

A **lymphangioma** (or cystic hygroma) is another congenital vascular malformation that must be considered. Lymphangiomas arise from abnormal development of lymphatic channels and commonly present in the neck, axilla, or mediastinum. Intrathoracic lymphangiomas are rare but can present as posterior mediastinal masses, especially in infants. They are typically cystic, nonenhancing, T2 hyperintense, and may have septations or low-signal areas corresponding to proteinaceous fluid or hemorrhage—features that align well with this case. However, lymphangiomas are more commonly located in the anterior or lateral mediastinum, and posterior paravertebral location is unusual. Additionally, lymphangiomas often increase in size with infection or trauma, and may show signs of acute enlargement or inflammation, which has not occurred here. The lack of a history of upper respiratory infection preceding mass changes, and the absence of surrounding edema or inflammation on imaging, makes this less likely. Furthermore, lymphangiomas are typically more diffuse and infiltrative, whereas this mass has a discrete cleavage plane, suggesting a more encapsulated lesion, which is more typical of CPAM.

**Esophageal duplication cyst** is another congenital anomaly that can present as a posterior mediastinal mass in infants. These cysts arise from aberrant budding of the foregut during embryogenesis and are typically located along the posterior aspect of the esophagus, often in the paravertebral region. They are usually spherical or ovoid, nonenhancing, and T2 hyperintense due to their fluid content, and are separated from adjacent structures by a well-defined plane—exactly as described here. The mass’s location, imaging characteristics, and clinical stability are highly consistent with an esophageal duplication cyst. Unlike CPAM, these cysts are lined by gastrointestinal epithelium and may contain gastric or respiratory mucosa, but they are not connected to the airway. They are often asymptomatic and discovered incidentally, especially if small. The transient grunting in the neonatal period could be due to mild compression of the left lung or diaphragm by the cyst, and the lack of progression is typical. The absence of gastrointestinal symptoms (vomiting, dysphagia, regurgitation) is not unexpected, as many duplication cysts remain asymptomatic unless they enlarge or become infected. The fact that the mass has remained stable for over 15 weeks supports a benign, noncommunicating cystic lesion, making this a strong contender.

A **neurogenic tumor** such as a ganglioneuroma or schwannoma must also be considered. These arise from neural crest-derived cells in the sympathetic chain or dorsal root ganglia and are common in the posterior mediastinum. Ganglioneuromas are benign, slow-growing tumors of mature ganglion cells, typically occurring in young children and adolescents. They are usually well-circumscribed, nonenhancing or minimally enhancing, and may appear heterogeneous on imaging due to fibrosis or myxoid change. T2 hyperintensity is common, and calcification may be present but is not always. The absence of calcification here is not disqualifying. The key distinguishing feature is that neurogenic tumors are usually solid and firm, with less cystic change than seen here. The presence of multiple small areas of low signal intensity on MRI suggests fluid or necrotic components, which are uncommon in ganglioneuromas. Schwannomas are typically more homogeneous and often show “target sign” on MRI due to central low signal from Antoni A tissue. Neither tumor type commonly presents with the marked T2 hyperintensity and cystic appearance described. Furthermore, neurogenic tumors rarely cause transient neonatal respiratory symptoms unless extremely large, and they are usually discovered later in infancy or childhood. The stability over 15 weeks is consistent, but the imaging appearance is more cystic than typical.

A **diaphragmatic hernia** (congenital or Morgagni) is an important consideration, particularly given the mass’s location abutting the left hemidiaphragm. However, a true diaphragmatic hernia would typically show bowel loops or abdominal organs within the thoracic cavity, which is not described here. The CT and MRI findings indicate a solid or cystic mass, not viscus, and the abdominal organs are described as being in normal location. A Morgagni hernia, which is anterior and right-sided, is even less likely. A **bronchogenic cyst** is another congenital foregut anomaly that can present as a posterior mediastinal mass. These arise from abnormal budding of the tracheobronchial tree and are typically located in the middle or posterior mediastinum. They are fluid-filled, nonenhancing, T2 hyperintense, and may have thin walls and internal septations. They are often asymptomatic and discovered incidentally. The location here is consistent, and the clinical course is classic. However, bronchogenic cysts are more commonly midline or paratracheal, and posterior paravertebral location is less typical. The cleavage plane and lack of connection to the tracheobronchial tree make CPAM or esophageal duplication more likely.

The hematologic findings warrant attention. The initial mild anemia (Hct 35.1%) and elevated MCV (99) suggest possible transient fetal erythropoiesis or mild hemolysis, which resolved. The later anemia (Hct 28.3%) with low MCV (78), low MCH (27.5), and normal MCHC (35.3) is consistent with **iron deficiency anemia**, which is common in infants between 6–12 months but can occur earlier with inadequate intake or occult blood loss. However, the infant is only 17 weeks old, and there is no history of bleeding, poor feeding, or prematurity. The normal ferritin and iron studies are not provided, so this may be a red herring or secondary to chronic low-grade inflammation. The thrombocytosis (initially 852,000, later 732,000) is reactive and common in infants, especially after infection or inflammation, and may reflect the mild upper respiratory symptoms at 17 weeks. The hyperkalemia (K+ 6.0) in a hemolyzed specimen is likely artifactual and not clinically significant.

In summary, the most likely diagnosis is a **congenital pulmonary airway malformation (CPAM) Type I**, given the posterior location, cystic appearance, T2 hyperintensity, lack of enhancement or calcification, and clinical stability. The **esophageal duplication cyst** is an equally compelling alternative, especially given the precise paravertebral location and cleavage plane. Both are benign, congenital, non-neoplastic lesions that are often asymptomatic and managed expectantly. The absence of malignant features, infection, or systemic illness makes aggressive pathology highly unlikely. The imaging findings are too characteristic for a simple inflammatory or infectious process, and the lack of progression over 15 weeks effectively rules out neuroblastoma, lymphoma, or sarcoma. The differential narrows to two primary congenital anomalies: CPAM and esophageal duplication cyst. Further diagnostic workup would ideally involve endoscopic ultrasound or bronchoscopy to assess for communication with the airway (favoring CPAM) or esophagoscopy to assess for communication with the esophagus (favoring duplication cyst). Surgical resection, while not immediately urgent, is typically recommended due to the risk of infection, hemorrhage, or malignant transformation (rare in CPAM, but possible in duplication cysts with gastric mucosa). The clinical stability and lack of symptoms suggest that the lesion is benign and non-life-threatening, but its persistence warrants definitive diagnosis and planned intervention.
