# Case 16-1995 —A 35-year-old man with dilated cardiomyopathy, repeated ventricular tachycardia, and pulmonary lesions

## Presentation of Case



A 35-year-old man was admitted to the hospital because of recurrent ventricular tachycardia.



The patient had been well until seven months earlier, when ventricular tachycardia occurred and a diagnosis of dilated cardiomyopathy was made; no cause was identified. Radiographs of the chest showed ill-defined opacities in the right upper lobe. A tuberculin skin test was negative, a test with mumps antigen was positive, and a test for human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) antibodies was negative. Microscopical examination of right and left ventricular myocardial-biopsy specimens revealed myocyte hypertrophy, without evidence of myocarditis. Because of recurrent bouts of ventricular tachycardia that were uncontrolled by several agents, an automatic, implantable cardioverter–defibrillator was implanted 27 weeks before admission. The patient was discharged with atenolol (25 mg twice daily) and disopyramide (200 mg twice daily).



Thereafter, he had approximately 1 discharge by the cardioverter-defibrillator monthly and occasional brief clusters of discharges, including 13 such episodes three and two days before admission. On the evening of his admission, sustained ventricular tachycardia developed below the detection threshold of the cardioverter–defibrillator, and the patient returned to the hospital, where successful external cardioversion was performed after the intravenous injection of antiarrhythmic agents had failed. He was admitted to the hospital.



The patient was a part-time student. He was a native of a Caribbean country and had immigrated to Boston 10 years earlier. He had had several positive tuberculin skin tests in recent years, with normal x-ray films of the chest, and had discontinued treatment with isoniazid because of side effects. He had had occasional night sweats in recent months, without fever, chills, or weight loss, and for several weeks had had increasing exertional dyspnea and a mild cough productive of yellow-brown sputum. There was no history of the use of tobacco or alcohol, risk factors for HIV infection, hemoptysis, paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea, chest pain, or syncope. His father had received inpatient treatment for tuberculosis in earlier years. There was no family history of heart disease or cancer. The patient's medications consisted of furosemide and potassium chloride, in addition to disopyramide and atenolol.



The temperature was 35.9°C, the pulse was 65, and the respirations were 14. The blood pressure was 95/60 mm Hg.



On physical examination the patient was thin and appeared well. No lymphadenopathy was found. The head and neck were normal. The lungs were clear. The heart was enlarged, with a regular rhythm; no murmur or S    sound was heard, and the jugular venous pressure was not elevated.



The urine was normal, as were the hematocrit, white-cell count, platelet count, erythrocyte sedimentation rate, and prothrombin and partial-thromboplastin times, as well as the levels of urea nitrogen, creatinine, glucose, bilirubin, calcium, phosphorus, protein, albumin, globulin, electrolytes, magnesium, and amylase; the aspartate aminotransferase level was 81 U per liter, and the alkaline phosphatase level was 120 U per liter. An electrocardiogram showed a normal rhythm at a rate of 71, with a PR interval of 248 msec and diffuse, nonspecific ST-segment and T-wave abnormalities. Radiographs of the chest again revealed patchy, ill-defined opacities in the right upper lobe; the remaining lung regions were clear. There was probable right hilar lymphadenopathy. The heart was slightly enlarged, and an electrode wire was visible in the right ventricle.



Disopyramide and atenolol were discontinued, and a loading dose of propafenone (150 mg three times daily) was begun. During the next several days a few bouts of ventricular tachycardia occurred. The implantable cardioverter–defibrillator was reprogrammed to a detection threshold of 140 beats per minute, the dose of propafenone was reduced (150 mg twice daily), and metoprolol (50 mg twice daily) was initiated. Thereafter, the patient had only two brief bouts of tachyarrhythmia, with prompt reversion to normal rhythm by the cardioverter–defibrillator. He remained afebrile at all times, and the blood pressure generally ranged between 80 and 90 mm Hg systolic. A cardiac ultrasonographic examination showed that the left ventricle was diffusely hypokinetic, with an estimated ejection fraction of 33 percent; the right ventricle was markedly dilated and severely hypokinetic. There was Doppler evidence of mild mitral and pulmonary regurgitation and severe tricuspid regurgitation. The right atrium was markedly dilated, and the inferior vena cava and hepatic veins were enlarged. A small pericardial effusion was detected. In comparison with the findings on examination seven months earlier, there was marked deterioration of left ventricular systolic function, increased tricuspid regurgitation, and new mitral regurgitation.



A computed tomographic (CT) scan of the thorax, obtained after the oral administration of contrast material, showed multichamber cardiac dilatation, without an obvious pericardial effusion. A single left prevascular lymph node, 1 cm in diameter, was noted, with mild right hilar and subcarinal lymphadenopathy. Multiple alveolar nodules, approximately 10 mm in diameter, were distributed throughout both lungs, especially in the posterior and anterior segments of the right upper lobe, where the nodules were confluent and centered about the bronchovascular bundle, with thickening of the bronchial walls and segmental air-space disease. No endotracheal or endobronchial lesions were observed. An ophthalmologic consultant found no evidence of an ocular abnormality. Skin tests with tuberculin (5 TU) and with candida and mumps antigens and a serologic test for syphilis were negative. Microscopical examination of three sputum specimens revealed no fungi or acid-fast bacilli. Several sputum cultures yielded  *Staphylococcus aureus* and a few colonies of  *Enterobacter aerogenes.*



A diagnostic procedure was performed.

## Differential Diagnosis

The clinical picture presented here is complex and multifocal, involving three major organ systems: the heart, the lungs, and the immune system, with overlapping features that challenge a unitary diagnostic framework. The patient is a 35-year-old man of Caribbean origin with a 7-month history of dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM), recurrent ventricular tachycardia, and newly identified bilateral pulmonary nodular infiltrates with hilar and subcarinal lymphadenopathy. The absence of classic systemic symptoms such as fever, weight loss, or hemoptysis, combined with negative HIV serology, negative tuberculin skin test (TST), and negative sputum acid-fast bacilli (AFB) staining, initially obscures the most common etiologies of pulmonary nodules and cardiomyopathy in this demographic. However, the constellation of findings—particularly the combination of progressive cardiac dysfunction, granulomatous-appearing pulmonary nodules, prior positive TSTs, and a family history of tuberculosis—demands a highly nuanced differential diagnosis that spans infectious, inflammatory, infiltrative, and neoplastic etiologies, with careful consideration of immunologic context and geographic exposure.

The most immediately compelling diagnostic category is **tuberculosis**, despite the negative TST and sputum AFB findings. The patient’s history of multiple prior positive TSTs, discontinuation of isoniazid due to side effects, and a father with a history of inpatient tuberculosis strongly suggest prior exposure and likely latent infection. The negative TST at presentation is not reassuring: in advanced or disseminated tuberculosis, particularly in the setting of severe malnutrition, immunosuppression (even if not HIV-related), or overwhelming disease burden, anergy can develop, rendering the TST falsely negative. This phenomenon is well documented in miliary TB and disseminated disease. The pulmonary nodules described—10 mm, bilateral, confluent in the right upper lobe, centered on bronchovascular bundles, with bronchial wall thickening and segmental air-space disease—are classic for **tuberculous granulomatous pneumonia**, especially in the setting of reactivation disease. The nodules are not cavitary, which is consistent with early or non-necrotizing granulomatous disease, and their distribution aligns with lymphatic spread from hilar nodes, a hallmark of post-primary TB. The presence of hilar and subcarinal lymphadenopathy on CT further supports this. The myocardial involvement is less commonly recognized but not unheard of: tuberculous myocarditis, though often subclinical, can lead to granulomatous infiltration of the myocardium, resulting in arrhythmogenic substrate, conduction abnormalities, and progressive systolic dysfunction indistinguishable from idiopathic DCM. Myocardial biopsy in this case showed myocyte hypertrophy without myocarditis, but this may reflect sampling error—the granulomas may be patchy, and the biopsy may have missed the foci. Moreover, the absence of inflammatory cells on biopsy does not exclude granulomatous disease, as granulomas may be fibrotic or necrotic in a way that is not immediately apparent on standard H&E staining without special stains (e.g., Ziehl-Neelsen, AFB culture, or PCR for *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*). The patient’s night sweats, though mild and without fever, are a classic constitutional symptom of TB, and the elevated AST and alkaline phosphatase may reflect hepatic granulomatous involvement or drug-induced liver injury from prior isoniazid, further supporting a chronic granulomatous process. The negative sputum cultures for AFB are not definitive: in pulmonary TB, sputum AFB is positive in only 50–70% of cases, especially in paucibacillary disease, and the patient may have extrapulmonary or lymphatic-predominant disease. The fact that *Staphylococcus aureus* and *Enterobacter aerogenes* were cultured from sputum likely represents colonization or secondary bacterial overgrowth in the setting of impaired mucociliary clearance from underlying granulomatous inflammation, not primary infection.

A closely related differential is **sarcoidosis**, a systemic granulomatous disease of unknown etiology that classically presents with bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy and pulmonary nodules, often with cardiac involvement. Cardiac sarcoidosis is a well-recognized cause of unexplained ventricular tachycardia and DCM, particularly in young adults. The granulomas in sarcoidosis are non-caseating, and myocardial infiltration can lead to conduction system disease, arrhythmias, and progressive heart failure. The patient’s age, lack of other systemic symptoms (e.g., uveitis, skin lesions, hypercalcemia), and absence of elevated ACE or lysozyme levels (though these were not measured) make sarcoidosis less likely but not impossible. The key distinguishing feature from TB is the lack of caseation and the absence of systemic symptoms such as erythema nodosum or parotid enlargement. However, cardiac sarcoidosis often presents in isolation, without pulmonary or cutaneous manifestations. The bilateral pulmonary nodules, especially if centered on bronchovascular bundles and associated with lymphadenopathy, are classic for sarcoidosis. The fact that the patient had a positive mumps skin test suggests preserved cell-mediated immunity, which argues against immunosuppression and makes sarcoidosis more plausible than TB in some contexts, but this is not definitive: many patients with TB retain anergic responses in certain antigens while responding to others. The absence of ocular disease (confirmed by ophthalmologic exam) and normal calcium levels make sarcoidosis less likely, but cardiac sarcoidosis can occur without other organ involvement. The myocardial biopsy showing only hypertrophy and no granulomas is a significant counterpoint, but again, biopsy sampling error is common in sarcoidosis—up to 30% of cases require multiple biopsies or endomyocardial biopsy from multiple sites to yield diagnostic granulomas. The presence of a small pericardial effusion and severe tricuspid regurgitation with right heart dilation may reflect right ventricular involvement, which is more common in sarcoidosis than in TB. The progressive deterioration of cardiac function over 7 months, despite antiarrhythmic therapy, is also consistent with the insidious, infiltrative nature of sarcoidosis.

Another important consideration is **fungal infection**, particularly **histoplasmosis**, which is endemic in the Caribbean and the Ohio and Mississippi River valleys. *Histoplasma capsulatum* can cause a chronic pulmonary granulomatous disease with nodules, lymphadenopathy, and, rarely, cardiac involvement. Disseminated histoplasmosis can present with fever, weight loss, and hepatosplenomegaly, but the patient is afebrile and lacks these systemic signs. However, chronic or subacute histoplasmosis can mimic TB, with pulmonary nodules, hilar adenopathy, and negative sputum AFB. The pulmonary nodules described are radiographically indistinguishable from those of TB or sarcoidosis. The negative serology for syphilis and absence of other markers of immunodeficiency make this less likely, but histoplasmosis can occur in immunocompetent hosts. **Lymphoma**, particularly **Hodgkin lymphoma**, must be considered in any young adult with bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy, pulmonary nodules, and unexplained cardiomyopathy. Lymphomatous infiltration of the myocardium can cause arrhythmias and systolic dysfunction, mimicking DCM. The pulmonary nodules could represent lymphangitic spread or intrapulmonary lymphoma. The absence of lymphadenopathy on physical exam is not reassuring, as CT clearly shows lymphadenopathy. The negative HIV serology and lack of B symptoms (fever, night sweats, weight loss) make lymphoma less likely, but night sweats alone are a classic B symptom, and weight loss may be subtle or unreported. Amyloid deposits in the myocardium cause thickening, diastolic dysfunction, and arrhythmias, and can also deposit in the lungs, causing nodular or interstitial patterns. The patient’s age, lack of monoclonal protein (not tested), and absence of renal or peripheral nerve involvement make this less likely, but isolated cardiac-pulmonary amyloidosis can occur. **Metastatic disease** is an unlikely but important consideration. Between the two, tuberculosis is more likely given the patient’s Caribbean origin (high TB burden), prior positive TSTs, family history, discontinuation of isoniazid, and the radiographic pattern of nodules centered on bronchovascular bundles with lymphadenopathy. The negative TST is likely due to anergy from chronic infection, a well-known phenomenon in disseminated TB. The myocardial biopsy’s failure to show granulomas may reflect sampling error, or the granulomas may have been fibrotic and not easily discernible. The progressive cardiac deterioration, despite antiarrhythmic therapy, is consistent with ongoing granulomatous infiltration. The presence of mild pericardial effusion and right heart failure may reflect concomitant pericardial or pulmonary vascular involvement.

Sarcoidosis remains a strong contender, especially given the classic radiographic findings, but the absence of systemic manifestations, normal calcium, and lack of ocular involvement make it less likely than TB. The positive mumps skin test, while not diagnostic, suggests preserved immunity, which is more typical of sarcoidosis than TB in advanced stages.

The clinical, radiographic, and epidemiological evidence strongly favors TB, despite negative initial tests. The next diagnostic step should be bronchoscopy with bronchoalveolar lavage for AFB smear, culture, and PCR, as well as transbronchial lung biopsy for granuloma detection. Failure to recognize this could lead to inappropriate management focused solely on antiarrhythmics and device therapy, while the underlying life-threatening infection progresses unchecked.
