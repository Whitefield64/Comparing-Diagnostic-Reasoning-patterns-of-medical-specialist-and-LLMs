# Case 16-1995 —A 35-year-old man with dilated cardiomyopathy, repeated ventricular tachycardia, and pulmonary lesions

A 35-year-old man was admitted to the hospital because of recurrent ventricular tachycardia.

Thereafter, he had approximately 1 discharge by the cardioverter-defibrillator monthly and occasional brief clusters of discharges, including 13 such episodes three and two days before admission. He was admitted to the hospital.

### 1. Cardiac Sarcoidosis with Pulmonary Sarcoid Nodules  

#### 1.1 Rationale for Consideration  

Sarcoidosis is a multisystem granulomatous disease that classically involves the lungs and intrathoracic lymph nodes, but cardiac involvement occurs in up to 25 % of patients on autopsy and in 5 %–10 % clinically. The disease can present with a spectrum ranging from isolated conduction disease to fulminant ventricular arrhythmias and dilated cardiomyopathy. In a young adult, especially of Caribbean or African descent, sarcoidosis is a well‑described cause of “lone” VT and progressive ventricular dysfunction.  

Pulmonary sarcoidosis frequently manifests as bilateral, ill‑defined, perilymphatic nodules, commonly in the upper lobes, with bronchovascular bundle thickening and hilar/mediastinal lymphadenopathy—findings that parallel the CT description in this case: confluent nodules centered on the bronchovascular bundles of the right upper lobe, patchy ill‑defined opacities, and mild hilar/subcarinal lymphadenopathy.  

Cardiac sarcoidosis can produce a dilated phenotype through extensive myocardial granulomatous infiltration, scarring, and loss of contractile tissue. The biopsy of ventricular tissue in this patient showed myocyte hypertrophy without overt myocarditis, which is not inconsistent with sarcoid involvement because endomyocardial biopsies have a low yield (often < 30 %) due to the focal, patchy nature of granulomas. The presence of a small pericardial effusion, severe tricuspid regurgitation, and right‑ventricular dilatation may reflect advanced infiltrative damage extending to the right heart—a pattern described in sarcoid cardiomyopathy.  

#### 1.2 Evidence Against  

- **Negative tuberculin skin test and negative fungal stains** do not exclude sarcoidosis, but the lack of a positive serum angiotensin‑converting enzyme (ACE) level (not reported) and the negative mumps skin test (used historically to assess delayed‑type immunity) provide no supportive serologic clues.  
- **Absence of classic extra‑pulmonary sarcoid manifestations** (e.g., skin lesions, uveitis, hypercalcemia) reduces the pre‑test probability, though up to 30 % of patients have isolated cardiac disease.  
- **Lack of non‑caseating granulomas on any tissue sample**: the only tissue examined so far is myocardial biopsy (negative) and sputum (negative for acid‑fast bacilli and fungi). However, the diagnostic yield from endobronchial or transbronchial biopsy can be low in early sarcoidosis.  

#### 1.3 Likelihood  

Given the combination of upper‑lobe nodular disease, mediastinal lymphadenopathy, refractory VT, and progressive dilated cardiomyopathy, cardiac sarcoidosis remains a leading hypothesis. The lack of definitive histologic confirmation is a known limitation, and the next logical step would be a targeted transbronchial or mediastinoscopic biopsy, or advanced imaging (cardiac MRI with late gadolinium enhancement, FDG‑PET) to seek active granulomatous inflammation.

### 2. Tuberculous (Mycobacterium tuberculosis) Cardiopulmonary Disease  

#### 2.1 Rationale for Consideration  

The patient’s Caribbean origin, prior positive tuberculin skin tests, a family history of tuberculosis, and a documented history of isoniazid therapy (discontinued due to side effects) raise the suspicion for active or reactivated TB. Pulmonary TB classically presents with upper‑lobe cavitary lesions, but “ill‑defined” infiltrates and nodular disease can also be seen, especially in early or miliary forms.  

Cardiac involvement in TB is uncommon but can manifest as tuberculous pericarditis, tuberculomas, or granulomatous myocarditis, potentially leading to arrhythmias and ventricular dysfunction. In rare cases, myocardial tuberculomas cause localized scarring that precipitates VT. The presence of mild right‑hilar and subcarinal lymphadenopathy could represent tuberculous lymphadenitis.  

#### 2.2 Evidence Against  

- **Repeated negative sputum acid‑fast stains and cultures** (three specimens) make active pulmonary TB less likely, though the sensitivity of sputum microscopy in non‑cavitary disease is modest.

#### 2.3 Likelihood  

While TB cannot be completely excluded, the cumulative evidence (negative sputum, atypical imaging, lack of granulomas on biopsy) makes it a less probable explanation for the cardiac findings. It remains a diagnostic consideration, particularly if later tissue sampling reveals caseating granulomas, and empiric anti‑TB therapy may be warranted if the clinical picture deteriorates.

   - **Three‑sputum cultures for mycobacteria** (including liquid culture) and **bronchoalveolar lavage** for mycobacterial PCR, given the high stakes of missing treatable TB.

## Management Implications of the Differential  

### 12.4 If Cardiac Sarcoidosis Is Confirmed  

- **Immunosuppressive therapy**: High‑dose corticosteroids (e.g., prednisone 0.5–1 mg/kg/day) followed by a slow taper, possibly combined with steroid‑sparing agents (methotrexate, azathioprine, mycophenolate mofetil) to reduce recurrence.  
- **Arrhythmia control**: Continuation of the ICD is mandatory; anti‑arrhythmic agents (e.g., amiodarone) may be added if VT persists despite immunosuppression.  
- **Monitoring**: Serial cardiac MRI or FDG‑PET to gauge response, repeated echocardiography for functional recovery, and periodic pulmonary imaging to assess nodular regression.  

### 12.5 If Tuberculosis Is Identified  

- **Standard anti‑TB regimen** (rifampin, isoniazid, pyrazinamide, ethambutol for 2 months, followed by rifampin + isoniazid for 4 months).  
- **Adjunctive corticosteroids**: May be considered for pericardial or myocardial involvement to reduce inflammation and prevent scarring.  
- **Monitoring for drug‑induced cardiotoxicity**: Some anti‑TB drugs (e.g., quinolones, macrolides) can affect QT interval; careful ECG surveillance would be required.  

### 12.6 If Fungal Infection Is Diagnosed  

- **Antifungal therapy** tailored to the organism (e.g., itraconazole for histoplasmosis, fluconazole or voriconazole for coccidioidomycosis).  
- **Consideration of drug interactions** with the patient’s anti‑arrhythmic regimen and the ICD.  

### 12.7 If Idiopathic Dilated Cardiomyopathy Is the Final Diagnosis  

- **Standard heart‑failure guideline‑directed medical therapy** (ACE inhibitors/ARBs, beta‑blockers, aldosterone antagonists) with careful titration given low blood pressure.  
- **ICD remains essential** for secondary prevention of sudden cardiac death.  
- **Evaluation for cardiac transplantation** if ventricular function does not improve despite optimal medical therapy.

## Conclusion  

In a 35‑year‑old man with rapidly progressive biventricular dilated cardiomyopathy, refractory ventricular tachycardia, and a distinctive upper‑lobe, peribronchovascular pulmonary nodular pattern, the differential diagnosis must prioritize systemic granulomatous diseases that can involve both heart and lung. Cardiac sarcoidosis offers the most coherent unifying explanation, fitting the radiographic, electrophysiologic, and hemodynamic data while acknowledging the known limitations of endomyocardial biopsy. Tuberculosis and chronic endemic fungal infection remain credible alternatives, particularly given the patient’s epidemiologic background and prior positive tuberculin skin tests, but both are weakened by repeated negative microbiologic studies and atypical imaging features. Idiopathic dilated cardiomyopathy, while possible, fails to account for the pulmonary findings and the marked right‑ventricular involvement.

The next decisive steps involve obtaining tissue diagnosis from the lung (or mediastinal nodes) and employing advanced cardiac imaging modalities (MRI, FDG‑PET) to detect active myocardial inflammation. These investigations will either confirm sarcoidosis, uncover a treatable infection, or solidify the diagnosis of idiopathic cardiomyopathy, thereby guiding targeted therapy—immunosuppression, antimicrobials, or intensified heart‑failure management—and ultimately improving the patient’s prognosis.
