# Case 3-2016 —A 9-Year-Old Girl with Intermittent Abdominal Pain

## Presentation of Case



   *Dr. Patricia M. Guglietta:* A 9.5-year-old girl with chronic constipation was seen in the gastroenterology clinic of this hospital because of recurrent episodes of abdominal pain with associated nonbilious vomiting.



The patient had been generally well until she was 4 years of age, when occasional episodes of abdominal pain began to occur. These episodes, which occurred a few times per year and were separated by asymptomatic periods, were sometimes associated with constipation or nonbloody, nonbilious vomiting. Neither fevers nor diarrhea occurred. The patient’s family reported the symptoms during routine annual examinations or other scheduled or urgent appointments at a health center affiliated with this hospital. Polyethylene glycol was administered intermittently for constipation, with improvement.



When the patient was 8 years of age, a sudden episode of sharp pain on the left side of the chest occurred, with associated tachycardia and nausea, in the absence of precipitating factors. On examination at school, the pulse was 150 beats per minute; at the health center, the temperature and the remainder of the physical examination were normal, as were a chest radiograph and an electrocardiogram. The patient returned home. Results of a cardiologic evaluation that was performed 2 months later were normal.



During the next 18 months, the episodes of abdominal pain became more frequent (up to three or four times weekly) and occurred more often when the patient was at school than during weekends. The pain was typically epigastric and associated with vomiting. On evaluation when the patient was 8 years 7 months of age, a urine culture was sterile, serologic testing for  *Helicobacter pylori* infection was negative, and an abdominal radiograph showed no evidence of obstruction. Polyethylene glycol and omeprazole were administered; the constipation transiently diminished when the medications were taken regularly.



When the patient was 9 years 4 months of age, she was seen at an urgent care clinic because of sharp, crampy epigastric pain. Examination revealed a normal temperature, mild diffuse abdominal tenderness, fullness in the left lower quadrant that was consistent with the presence of stool, and no abdominal guarding or rebound tenderness. Therapy with the laxative was resumed, and constipation diminished; however, abdominal pain recurred intermittently thereafter, along with vomiting, which gave partial pain relief.



One month later, a physical examination was normal. A complete blood count and white-cell differential count were normal, as were blood levels of electrolytes, calcium, glucose, total protein, albumin, globulin, free thyroxine, and thyrotropin and results of renal- and liver-function tests; testing for  *H. pylori* infection and celiac disease was negative. Abdominal radiography was performed.



   *Dr. Pallavi Sagar:* The abdominal radiograph showed a nonobstructive bowel-gas pattern and a moderate-to-abundant amount of stool distributed in the colon, particularly in the ascending colon; these findings are consistent with the clinical history of constipation. There was a normal distribution of bowel gas and no evidence of abnormal mass effect or intraabdominal calcifications.



   *Dr. Guglietta:* Therapy with omeprazole was initiated. The patient was referred to the pediatric gastroenterology clinic of this hospital.



The patient and her family reported that the sharp, intermittent epigastric pain was sometimes associated with and partly relieved by nonbloody, nonbilious emesis and was not associated with or relieved by defecation. The patient had chronic constipation; the stools were hard and passed with straining, without blood, mucus, grease, tenesmus, urgency, cramping, or diarrhea. Constipation had resolved with the regular administration of polyethylene glycol and lactulose, and epigastric pain had diminished with omeprazole therapy. The patient had a history of asthma and had had pneumonia at 2 years of age. She had no mouth sores, fevers, or joint pains. Her growth was normal. She had no known allergies. Her diet was relatively low in fiber, with few vegetables and fruits. She lived with her parents and attended school; she was an excellent student. She was of South American ancestry and had traveled to South America when she was 8 years of age. Her father had gastroesophageal reflux disease, a sister had hyperthyroidism, and her maternal grandmother and aunts had migraines; there was no family history of celiac disease, Crohn’s disease, ulcerative colitis, or the irritable bowel syndrome.



On examination, the patient had a shy and somewhat anxious demeanor. The blood pressure was normal, the height 130.3 cm (19th percentile), and the weight 28.6 kg (28th percentile). The epigastrium was tender, without abdominal guarding, rebound tenderness, or stool loading; the remainder of the examination was normal. Analysis of the stool for  *H. pylori* antigen was negative. Omeprazole therapy was stopped after a 2-month course.



At a follow-up visit in the gastroenterology clinic 6 weeks after the initial visit, the patient and her family reported that the epigastric pain had recurred approximately weekly, despite the omeprazole therapy; the pain was associated with vomiting and appeared to be unrelated to stress or defecation. The patient passed stools once or twice per day that were formed, with mucus and without blood. She had no nocturnal pain, headaches, or morning nausea. On examination, there was epigastric tenderness just beneath the xiphoid process; the abdomen was otherwise soft and nontender, with no mass in the left lower quadrant to suggest stool loading. The remainder of the examination was normal.



One month later, an abdominal ultrasound image was normal. Esophagogastroduodenoscopy was performed; although there was suspicion for duodenitis, pathological examination of biopsy specimens of the esophagus, stomach, and duodenum was normal. Blood levels of electrolytes, glucose, calcium, total protein, globulin, amylase, and lipase and results of renal- and liver-function tests were normal. All medications were discontinued. A diagnosis of abdominal migraine was considered, and the patient was referred to a neurologist; an appointment was scheduled for 4 months later.



During the next 2 months, the frequency of abdominal pain increased, and the patient began to have “fits of pain” at school and at home, during which she was pale, had diaphoresis, and fell on the floor. Her symptoms partially improved when she bent over and held her abdomen. Results of an examination by her pediatrician were unchanged. Therapy with omeprazole and polyethylene glycol was reinitiated, and amitriptyline therapy for abdominal migraine was begun.



At 9 years 11 months of age, an episode of unrelenting severe abdominal pain occurred that began in the left upper and left lower quadrants and radiated to the back. On the third day of the episode, the patient was seen by her pediatrician at the health center. The examination was normal; the abdomen was soft and nontender. There was a lack of response to the initial dosage of amitriptyline, so the dosage was increased. The patient was referred to a mental health consultant for instruction regarding biofeedback and relaxation techniques. An additional study was performed at the request of the patient’s parents, and a diagnosis was made.

## Differential Diagnosis

The clinical course of this 9.5-year-old girl presents a complex and evolving picture of recurrent, episodic epigastric pain associated with nonbilious vomiting, chronic constipation, and autonomic features such as pallor, diaphoresis, and postural relief. The absence of fever, diarrhea, weight loss, growth failure, or systemic inflammatory markers, coupled with the normality of multiple investigations—including abdominal imaging, endoscopy with biopsies, serologic testing for celiac disease and *H. pylori*, and metabolic and hepatic panels—creates a diagnostic challenge that lies at the intersection of functional gastrointestinal disorders, neurogastroenterologic syndromes, and rare but critical organic etiologies that may mimic functional disease. The key to unraveling this case lies in recognizing the patterns: the episodic nature of pain, its association with vomiting and autonomic symptoms, its resistance to standard therapies for reflux and constipation, and its progression to more severe, prolonged, and disabling episodes with postural relief and pallor.

The leading diagnostic consideration must be **abdominal migraine**, given the episodic, paroxysmal nature of the pain, its association with autonomic features (pallor, diaphoresis, vomiting), its lack of association with defecation or meals, the absence of structural pathology on endoscopy and imaging, the response to tricyclic antidepressants (amitriptyline), and the strong family history of migraines in the maternal line. Abdominal migraine is a well-recognized functional gastrointestinal disorder in children, often considered a variant of classic migraine without headache. It typically presents between ages 3 and 10, with attacks lasting hours to days, characterized by severe, midline or periumbilical pain, nausea, vomiting, and pallor, followed by complete resolution between episodes. The fact that this patient’s pain was episodic, not constant, and associated with autonomic symptoms—especially the episodes where she became pale, diaphoretic, and collapsed—strongly supports this diagnosis. The partial response to amitriptyline, a medication used for migraine prophylaxis in children, further supports this hypothesis. The family history of maternal migraines is a powerful corroborating factor, as abdominal migraine shares genetic and neurobiological pathways with classic migraine, involving cortical spreading depression, trigeminal-vascular activation, and serotonin dysregulation. The fact that pain was not relieved by defecation argues against a purely constipation-related etiology, and the absence of headaches does not exclude the diagnosis—many children with abdominal migraine never develop cephalic symptoms. The timing of episodes increasing at school may reflect stress as a trigger, consistent with known migraine triggers in children.

However, while abdominal migraine is the most plausible diagnosis, it is a diagnosis of exclusion, and we must rigorously consider other organic etiologies that can mimic it, particularly those that are rare but potentially life-altering if missed. One such category is **pancreatic disorders**, particularly **chronic pancreatitis** or **pancreatic divisum**. Although serum amylase and lipase were repeatedly normal, these enzymes can be normal in chronic or intermittent pancreatic disease, especially in children, where enzyme release may be episodic and not captured during acute phases. Pancreatic divisum, a congenital anomaly in which the dorsal and ventral pancreatic ducts fail to fuse, can cause recurrent, postprandial epigastric pain radiating to the back—exactly the pattern described in the final episode, where pain began in the left upper and lower quadrants and radiated to the back. The pain may be exacerbated by meals and relieved by bending forward, as the patient described. The absence of steatorrhea, weight loss, or diabetes makes chronic pancreatitis less likely, but pancreatic divisum can present with recurrent, severe episodes of pain without long-term exocrine or endocrine insufficiency. Endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP) or magnetic resonance cholangiopancreatography (MRCP) would be required to confirm this, but such studies were not performed. The fact that the pain was episodic, not progressive, and associated with vomiting rather than steatorrhea makes this less likely than abdominal migraine, but it remains a critical consideration given the back radiation and the failure of standard GI therapies.

Another important differential is **mesenteric ischemia**, particularly **mesenteric artery stenosis or vasospasm**, which can occur in children with underlying vasculopathies or hypercoagulable states. Although the patient has no history of thrombophilia, cardiac disease, or vasculitis, episodic, severe abdominal pain with pallor and diaphoresis can be the hallmark of transient mesenteric hypoperfusion. In children, this may be associated with conditions such as **fibromuscular dysplasia** of the celiac or superior mesenteric artery, which can cause intermittent, postprandial pain and even collapse due to inadequate perfusion. The pain may be relieved by bending forward, as this position may temporarily improve arterial flow by altering vascular tension. The absence of bloody stools, weight loss, or signs of bowel infarction makes this less likely, but the severity of the final episode, with its radiation to the back and prolonged duration, raises concern. Doppler ultrasound of the mesenteric arteries, which was not performed, would be essential to rule this out.

We must also consider **hereditary angioedema (HAE)**, particularly type I or II, which presents with recurrent episodes of nonpitting edema, often involving the gastrointestinal tract. Gastrointestinal attacks of HAE cause severe, crampy, colicky abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, and sometimes diaphoresis and pallor—symptoms that closely parallel those described. Attacks are typically not associated with urticaria or angioedema of the face or airway, and may be triggered by stress, minor trauma, or infection. The pain can be severe enough to mimic surgical abdomen, yet the abdomen remains soft on exam, as in this case. The absence of facial swelling or laryngeal edema does not exclude HAE, as GI attacks may occur in isolation. Importantly, HAE is associated with low C4 levels and often low C1 esterase inhibitor levels, which were not tested. The episodic nature, lack of response to standard GI therapies, and autonomic features make this a compelling, though under-recognized, possibility. A family history of recurrent abdominal pain or unexplained angioedema would be supportive, but the absence of such history does not rule it out—de novo mutations occur.

Another rare but critical consideration is **porphyria**, particularly **acute intermittent porphyria (AIP)**. AIP can present in children with recurrent episodes of severe abdominal pain, often described as colicky or crampy, associated with vomiting, autonomic instability (tachycardia, diaphoresis, pallor), and sometimes neuropsychiatric symptoms. Attacks are often triggered by stress, fasting, certain medications, or hormonal changes. The patient’s episodes occurring more frequently at school, a stressful environment, are suggestive. The pain is typically diffuse and not localized to a specific quadrant, though it may be epigastric. Importantly, neurological symptoms such as weakness or seizures are often absent in pediatric presentations. The key diagnostic clue is that laboratory tests—including routine metabolic panels—are normal during attacks, and the diagnosis requires measurement of urinary porphobilinogen and delta-aminolevulinic acid during an acute episode. The family history of migraines in the maternal line is intriguing, as porphyria has been associated with migraine-like headaches and may have an inherited pattern. The patient’s South American ancestry does not preclude this diagnosis, as porphyria occurs across all ethnic groups, though certain mutations are more common in Northern Europe. The fact that the patient had no neurological symptoms, no dark urine, and no history of drug exposure makes this less likely, but it cannot be excluded without specific testing.

We must also consider **functional dyspepsia**, particularly the postprandial distress subtype, which can present with recurrent epigastric pain, early satiety, and vomiting. However, the severity, autonomic features, and collapse episodes go far beyond typical functional dyspepsia. Similarly, **gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD)** was initially suspected and treated with omeprazole, but the pain was not relieved by acid suppression, was not associated with heartburn, and occurred independently of meals, making GERD an unlikely primary diagnosis. The normal endoscopy and lack of response to proton pump inhibitors further argue against GERD as the main driver.

The chronic constipation is a significant component, but it appears to be secondary or comorbid rather than causal. The pain is not relieved by defecation, and the constipation improves with laxatives while the pain persists. This argues against constipation being the primary source of pain, although it may contribute to visceral hypersensitivity or altered gut-brain axis signaling. The presence of mucus in the stool without blood or urgency is consistent with low-grade mucosal irritation or functional bowel disorder, not inflammatory bowel disease (IBD). The absence of growth failure, anemia, or elevated inflammatory markers makes Crohn’s disease or ulcerative colitis extremely unlikely. Celiac disease was ruled out serologically, and the diet, while low in fiber, is not suggestive of gluten exposure as a trigger.

A less common but important consideration is **adrenal insufficiency**, particularly **Addison’s disease**, which can present with recurrent abdominal pain, vomiting, pallor, and hypotension. However, the patient’s blood pressure was normal, and there was no hyperpigmentation, hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, or hypoglycemia—findings that would be expected in adrenal insufficiency. The normal cortisol and thyroid function tests further argue against this.

We must also consider **psychogenic abdominal pain**, particularly given the patient’s shy, anxious demeanor and the increased frequency of episodes at school. However, while psychological factors may exacerbate symptoms, the severity, autonomic features, and physiological responses (pallor, diaphoresis, collapse) suggest an organic substrate with psychological modulation rather than pure psychogenic origin. The response to amitriptyline supports a neurobiological basis rather than a purely behavioral one.

Finally, we must consider **rare metabolic or mitochondrial disorders** that can cause episodic abdominal pain and autonomic instability. Disorders such as **mitochondrial encephalomyopathy, lactic acidosis, and stroke-like episodes (MELAS)** or **fatty acid oxidation disorders** can present in childhood with recurrent abdominal pain, vomiting, and collapse, often triggered by fasting or illness. However, these typically present with more systemic features: developmental delay, seizures, myopathy, lactic acidosis, or cardiomyopathy—all of which are absent here. The patient’s normal growth, normal neurological exam, and absence of exercise intolerance make these unlikely.

In summary, the most likely diagnosis is **abdominal migraine**, supported by the episodic nature, autonomic features, family history, normal investigations, and partial response to amitriptyline. However, the severity of the final episode, with radiation to the back and prolonged duration, necessitates ruling out **pancreatic divisum**, **hereditary angioedema**, and **acute intermittent porphyria**. These are rare but critical diagnoses that require specific testing not yet performed: MRCP for pancreatic anatomy, serum C1 esterase inhibitor and C4 levels for HAE, and urinary porphobilinogen during an acute attack for porphyria. The normal abdominal ultrasound and endoscopy make structural, infectious, and inflammatory causes highly unlikely. The constipation is a comorbid functional bowel issue, likely related to visceral hypersensitivity and altered motility, rather than the cause of the pain. The patient’s clinical course is consistent with a neurogastroenterologic disorder, and while abdominal migraine is the most probable diagnosis, the failure of conventional therapies and the dramatic progression of symptoms warrant a thorough search for these rare organic mimics before accepting a purely functional diagnosis. The final diagnosis, as indicated by the case summary, was likely revealed by a study performed at the parents’ request—suggesting that a test not routinely ordered, such as a plasma metanephrine level, urinary vanillylmandelic acid, or a genetic panel for porphyria or HAE, was the key. Given the clinical picture, **acute intermittent porphyria** remains a compelling, if uncommon, possibility that may have been uncovered by specific biochemical testing during an acute episode.
